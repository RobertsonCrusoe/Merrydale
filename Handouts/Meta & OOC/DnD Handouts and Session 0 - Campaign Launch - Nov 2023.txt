Source: Message 18bcae8800b3699f
Attachments: Daggerdale Chronicle - Chapter 7 - Incomplete.pdf,
             Daggerdale Chronicle - Thistle Ridge Farm - Players Handbook.pdf

"Fellas;

Attached are the following;

1. Daggerdale Chronicle - Chapter 7 - Incomplete
2. Daggerdale Chronicle - Thistle Ridge Farm - Player's Handbook

The first is a very long document that I've shared with you before. It is
a summary of sources regarding Daggerdale, many of which some of you wrote
yourselves. I don't expect you to read it from start to finish, but it
would be helpful to acquaint yourselves with everything in 'Chapter 7',
which starts around page 185. I consider this document 90% done, wanting
only for some images to break it up a bit, and a few spots where you'll
find 'xx' holding a spot I haven't finished yet. Let's not make perfection
and completion be the enemy of a good story though, eh?

The second is really the start of the adventure. There are two handouts,
'A Call for Bravery, Endurance and Honour!' and 'Congratulations,
Adventurers!' which kick things off. The first is an announcement about a
competition to select a Company to resettle an abandoned farm called
Thistle Ridge. The second assumes that you have answered that call,
submitted an application for the competition, and have been awarded a spot.
It lays out what is about to happen over the next 10 days, and what you
will need to succeed.

I envision this as a very long campaign that we probably never finish. The
characters will form (or join) a company that spends years in game time
(and real time) resettling an abandoned town, governing it and growing it.
It will be full of some of my favourite DND themes; exploring the
wilderness and abandoned places, intrigue in a semi-urban environment,
base-building, and long-term story crafting. You will have a sound base of
operations from which to launch many characters over time if you desire.

The first real decision you'll have to make is what Company will you join,
or will you make your own."

PART 10: STEPHEN'S LUCAN CONCEPT — STEPHEN ROBERTSON (19 Nov 2023)
-------------------------------------------------------------------

"I haven't had time to dig through the PF2 rules yet, but I am planning a
rogue-type character - a Sembian treasure hunter from a branch of the
Alavandor family. I'll flesh out his class and stats, but I'm thinking he's
from a branch of the family in Saerloon that in the past few generations
has become a little more entangled with some of the less salubrious
organizations in that notorious city.

His family has become quite wealthy, but he is the youngest of four
brothers, and there really isn't a place for him in 'the family business'
(smuggling and coin laundering) so he's grown up a little dissolute and is
always on the make.

He recently uncovered a trove of correspondence between his
great-grandfather and the Alavandors that went to Thistle Ridge. Reading
between the lines, and recalling a few family rumours, he's deduced that
there was an ulterior motive for the Alavandor and Clarandal families to
settle at the farm 75 years back, and that it wasn't just to sell apples
and medicinal herbs. The correspondence included a surveyors' report
concerning the Sembian engineers' rerouting of the river into the caves
beneath the settlement. Certain annotations have convinced him that there
was something down there that the Sembians were after...

Talking up his interests in 'family history' he was able to convince his
father and aunt, who control the family purse strings, to underwrite his
journey to Thistle Ridge Farm to take part in the recovery operation. They
expect to be repaid with interest, but are happy just to get him out from
underfoot. Of course, he has not told them - or anyone else - the report
and the hints therein. Nevertheless, the report mysteriously disappeared
from his possessions one night at an inn on the way to the Farm..."

PART 14: TODD ON CHARACTER PHILOSOPHY — TODD BURRY (24 Nov 2023)
-----------------------------------------------------------------

"My views on D&D have really changed over the years. I now believe it's
okay to play a suboptimal mediocre character as long as they have character.

This came from listening to the 'Glass Cannon' podcast a lot. One of the
players has uncannily bad dice luck. It's to the point that it is hilarious
how bad he roles at the most important times. The thing is: that bad luck
organically makes his characters better. Seeing a PC that starts at a low
level with high aspirations just turn into a sad sack of bad luck makes for
more intriguing D&D. Think of how much we laugh when Blacky gets up from
the table to throw his dice in the backyard. The highs don't feel as high
if there are never lows.

In actuality, I think of Garth as sort of like Charlie Brown. He's the
protagonist of Waterdeep, but still a sad sack. It's kind of great."
