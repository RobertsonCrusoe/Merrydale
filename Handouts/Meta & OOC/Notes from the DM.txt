==Party Composition==

After playing several sessions of 4ed, I have learned that the party really has to have some sort of balance to be viable and have any sort of endurance.  To that end, I am recommending the following characters/roles to be made;

*Tank; This could be a paladin, a warden, or a fighter.  Straight up fighters seem to do the best job of this role, when they are made and played properly. 

* Hicksy's two cents - Swordmages with finely tuned ability scores seem to be capable of tanking as well; and play in a fashion similar to wardens.  Wardens seem to be more capable of controlling multiple opponents and rather than hitting as hard as fighters they are far more capable at affecting the monsters through push/pull/slide tactics as well as debuffs and wreaking havoc with a monsters ability to navigate the combat.  Fighters are by far and away the easiest class to play if you wish to make a straightforward tank but by no means the only one.  Paladins are underpowered at the moment and struggling with an efficient marking system to discourage monsters from attacking other party members.  At this time, to make the Paladin tank an effective one, it really comes down to the player and having only one point of focus; that being marking enemies every possible opportunity and controlling the battle with an aggressive use of the bullrush attack.  Paladins should be getting a shot in the arm at the end of July with the new Divine Power book which is rumoured to give \"tankadins\" a second \"mark\" as well as adding a significant number of powers to their current anemic collection of powers and abilities.

*Healer; This is pretty self explanatory.  Thus far, it really only looks like clerics do a good job of this.  For this campaign, a cleric of Amauantor would not be out of place, nor would Chauntea.  However, pretty much any good faith is acceptable.

*Damage; These are the characters that people have been lining up to play so far.  This includes wizards, rogues, rangers, warlocks, and other characters that hit hard, but can't take too much punishment.

*Leader; In a small party, you can pretty much do without a leader-type.  These include Warlords and Clerics who give the party bonuses in combat.

* Hicksy's two cents - Leaders/Controllers almost always go hand in hand with healing and there are some really interesting and useful abilities that Warlords, Bards, Druids and Shaman have.  A well-played Warlord can give a DM fits with the buffs and tactical positioning advantages they can provide to the party.  Bards; should not be confused with the irritating hound's breakfast of a class they once were.  A bard can provide enough temporary hitpoints in a single encounter that a party simple mows down whatever it's meeting.  They can also add a ridiculous amount of buffs and debuffs each round. Bards, Warlords, Druids and Shaman don't get enough love as far as I'm concerned.  They're finesse classes and they can be pretty spectacular if used intelligently.

In my experience with the game so far, it seems like 6 is the golden number for characters in a party.  The following composition seems to work OK, but slight variations are likely viable;

*1-2 tanks
*1-2 healers
*2-4 Damage
*1 leader max

* Hicksy's two cents - Ideally we have guys stepping up and saying what they want to play.  At the moment, things are dreadfully quiet.  Once we know what the new guys want to play, we can start building a party around them

==Skills==

This will be a largely wilderness adventure.  As such, at least a couple of the PC's will need the Nature skill, and one tracker (Perception Skill).

The Endurance skill will pay dividends, especially if lots of you guys train it.

Perception (which replaces the old skills of Spot and Listen) is a valuable skill for a number of reasons.

There will be need for social skills as well; Diplomacy and Streetwise will both be used.

Basically, every skill will have a use, and you will need all of them at some point.  Do try to round out the Skills in your party.
