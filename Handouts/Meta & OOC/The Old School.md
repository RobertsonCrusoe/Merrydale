== Introduction ==

In September I intend to run some Old School Dungeons & Dragons adventures.  These will be tied together to one another somewhat loosely, and each one should be completed in a single 4 to 6 hour session.  All characters will have as a central motivation the desire to be \"adventurers\" in the classic D&D sense: to break into ancient tombs and ruins with the sole purpose of slaughtering the inhabitants and looting the contents for their self-enrichment and personal glory.  There will be a central town with the appropriate services and shoppes, and a reasonably straight-forward classic D&D economy.  We will be reveling in plenty of the tropes and clichés of the game with no irony whatsoever.  This is adventuring for adventure's own sake, as it was in days of old.

== A few notes on character creation ==

thumb|left|\"I have 1d8hp and Str 13.\"
thumb|right|\"I could pay to have her raised, but then I'd have to share all this loot.\"

Given that this style of D&D play lends itself to a high mortality rate, particularly at the lowest levels, it is imperative that all Players have a solid stable of characters from which they can quickly call up a replacement as needed.  Adventures are designed with at least 6 characters per party, so each Player will field a minimum of 2 characters per adventure.  It is recommended that each player field at least one front-line warrior (Fighter, Dwarf, etc) along with any rear-line character.

*There will be a few pre-generated stock characters available for players to use if they prefer to not roll their own
*Characters are rolled up using 3d6 with the results assigned to abilities in order rolled (Method I)
*Characters start at Level 1, regardless of the level of the rest of the party
*While it is entirely unnecessary to write any form of character background prior to play, it is a good idea to at least have a basic concept and physical description in mind.
*Motivation should be kept simple:  to seek adventure and treasure by sword and spell.
*Memorable, imaginative and well-played characters will be rewarded with longevity and the DM's favour.  Forgettable or poorly-played characters will not earn their second chance.
*Death is the only exit.  There will be no Casey Priests of Full-Plate Armour Stealing.  If you don't want to play a particular character anymore, have the courtesy of dying in battle so that the others may loot your cowardly carcass.
*This style of play is more about imagination and creativity than character abilities and rules.  Almost everything is left uncodified and up to DM discretion, so the rewards go to those who try, not necessarily to those who succeed.  It is different than 4E in that very little is explicitly spelled out in a stat-block or chart.  If you can imagine your character doing it, then he is free to try and do it.  In this style of play there are really no gimped or flawed characters, no matter what you may think the character sheet says.  The grid on the map is a guide, not a hard-and-fast limit.  It is all about imagination, not rules.
