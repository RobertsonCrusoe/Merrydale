We found ourselves drawn to the most expensive hotel in town, which was perched atop what is known as the Casino Platinum.  Though traffic in and out of the Casino was tremendous, it was handled quickly and courteously, and we found ourselves checked in promptly.  We were forced to change our currency to the house platinum coins, though, and lost very little in the exchange.  The little that was lost was well worth it for admission to such a singular place.  The floor of the Casino is a bustling place where there is more gambling happening in this single room than there is in the entire Busker

s travels through a spinning, numbered wheel.  An elegant game, it drew the finest gamblers and the retinues that you expect from such excellent sport.  I was drawn immediately to this table like a vulture to carrion, but I had to wait to play; Cedric and I were both in dire need of a bath and change of clothes.

If I have been in a better appointed room in any inn or hotel in all of my journeys, I cannot remember it.  The room where I now write this from is without a doubt the finest of accommodations.  But, at twenty platinum per night, it should be.  It is quite a trick that the Casino plays on it

s guests; turning all your money to platinum, you begin to treat one of their coins as if they were a gold or a silver, not a five-piece!  But, here we are, in a state room on the twelfth floor, enjoying life to it's fullest.
Cedric and I spent a few hours in the room recovering from our travels.  I took to the tub room first, and had a bit of sport there. In each room there is a platinum bell that you can ring, and servants will come to you as quickly as they can, from some place outside the rooms.  I wasted no time in conjuring, with bell, a small host of bath attendants.  In no time, I had a tub that was nearly as hot as I like it (though not quite; most servants can not bear the temperature of water that I prefer).  The host stood by, and I singled out one particularly comely young wench to help me with my robes.  When I was disrobed, I playfully pushed her into the tub, and she made quite a splash.  The rest of the host looked on and laughed, some uncomfortably.  I was about to proceed into the tub after her, but the small host was aghast, and protested as politely as their horror would allow.  For, these youngsters had seen the naked body of Chadrazan Kossur, and knew me to be of an infernal lineage.

For a moment, I thought I would conjure up a few pets to hold the host of servants captive audience to my romantic intentions towards this young lady, and even conceived of summoning forth a goodly sized infernal octopus, or some such thing, in the tub in order to hold her down.  But, I thought of my gentle friend in the next room, and thought what a shame it would be if I were to spoil our accommodation by spoiling the flesh of this girl, and I relented with a smile and some jest.  The host departed quickly and awkwardly, and they are the only people in this Casino that did not hold out waiting palms to me.

Another perk of the Casino is our private balcony overlooking the very active coloseum next door.  Cedric and I spent quite some time seated upon this balcony as the sun went down, wrapped in fine bath gowns from the hotel, and drinking fine drinks, toasting this and that.  Life is good.

We both agreed that tonight we would have some fun, and that tomorrow we would get down to business.  Cedric was well into his cups by the time we set out for the gambling hall again, but I kept my sobriety.  I wished to test that roolet table with the best chances I had.  These proved not good enough.

Every man has his vices, and I have several.  The feel of a struggling lover, the taste of a thick red wine, the coppery smell of spilled blood; these are all things that are fond to me.  But roolet, now there is a real diversion!

While there were many men playing the table, and most betting heavily (with platinum being the only currency bet, of course), I guess I got a little caught up in my habit, and drew more attention to myself than I should have.  I played fifteen coins each round, and lost, in merciless unbroken succession, nine rounds.  Had I the coin, I would have played a tenth, but, as I write this, the Casino has every coin I have come to the City with.  The funny thing is, I don't feel the least bit upset about it.  Easy come, easy go.
Westgate, Day Two
Today we got down to business, and it was a long day indeed.  Longer for Cedric, who is laying here, still in quite a bit of pain from the nearly fatal skewering he received today.
Our first order of business was to pay a visit to this paper known as the
Free Thinker
.  We hired a boy named Prit to guide us through the City, and the boy has earned almost as much as we are paying him.  He brought us to the paper without delay.

At the paper, we find a very busy place where men are cranking out papers on some modern machine, and excited writers shout and hurry about in the nonsense of their vocation.  He met the
Associate Editor
, one Mr. Daniel Thompson.  He was a little put of by me, but Cedric effortlessly convinced the man to tell us of the whereabouts of the editor, Mr. William Spedicut.  As it turns out, Mr.Spedicut is attending some form of political debate at the parlaiment house presently.

Westgate has remained in this political limbo ever since. Anyway, the voting process and all the murder, deceit, and wickedness that it bred didn

t actually change the City much.  But, three years ago, for whatever reason, it was declared that the voting population would include many people of common blood, but uncommon purse, and thus the population became too large to influence with murder, and the candidates for succession turned in their assassins for orators, and the modern age of the City began.

I have clipped an article from the archive of the paper, which describes best, the present political situation.

At any rate, almost three years ago it was announced that merchants bringing in over a thousand in yearly income would also be able to vote in the succession elections in three years time.

Almost immediately this moved the electoral candidates from the bribery business to the oratory business. Every day, as anyone can remember, the candidates could be seen hollering on the important corners the most outrageous promises and veiled threats.

It was at this time that a few like minded individuals headed by one James Darter became organized and began shouting back at the candidates with a most skilled style of banter that would not but discredit the candidates.

Because of the influence of the candidates this began to land those dissenters in the pillories.

It was this very paper that exposed this scandal, in an article that this author wrote that described the dissenters

behaviour as harmless raillery rather than treasonous behaviour. I am proud to say that this article popularized that point of view and led to the beginning of the Raillery Party.

Through the lucky misfortune of the Campion family, the Raillery Party was able to purchase a noble title for William Darter thus allowing the Raillery Party to enter into the election as a candidate.
It didn't take long for the other candidates to realize that the Raillery Party was distinctly from them and that although they had a vast majority of the support the vote would be split between the lot of them.
At this time the other candidates decided to unite into a single party heade'd by Victor Dhostar, the previous ruler of Westgate.
They took to calling themselves the Octogenarions for the three chief noble families that had each ruled Westgate for at least eighty years
the Thalavars, the Dhostars, and the Ssemms.
