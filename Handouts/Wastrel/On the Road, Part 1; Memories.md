How long had he been on the road now?

Edward tried to number the days on the fingers of his outstretched hand, but the jarring bumps from cart he rode skewed his count at odd intervals, leaving him exasperated and second-guessing himself. A tenday from Suzail out to the border towns, several more since then to the downstream ferry into Elturgard, at least a month then by his count, a portion more spent at the mining camp to work off the debt from that unexpected detour... bah, he was never one for mathematics. He rested his face in his hands, arms propped up on his legs, and let his mind settle for a moment as he shrugged off the heavy wash of numbers that bombarded his mind. They fell like seeds to the base of his skull, sprouting winding and twisting trees of confusion that clawed upwards through his brain, leaving nothing but frustrated headaches in their wake. He was not a clever man, not by any stretch, and numbers and metrics grander than the span of a foot or the number of days to a month were, as far as Edward was concerned, less than important.

The old man next to him glanced aside, noting his passenger's strange movements, switched the cart-horse's rump with a casual flick and settled back into his seat. \"What's yer worry, partner?\" he asked through stained lips. \"Yeh late fer somethin'?\" Edward lifted his head, an apologetic smile prodding the corners of his mouth. \"No sir, not late. Not yet, at least. Not for awhile, I suppose.\" He looked to the right, noting the passing scenery, tall, thick trees unaware of the mountains that crept up behind them from some unseen horizon. \"How long have we been on the road?\" Edward asked absently, his eyes lingering on the deep forest before turning aside to his squinting companion. The man looked past him at the sun, still hung low in the sky and muttered faintly himself. Edward wondered for a moment if words in the man's mouth were to disgusted to make the journey past the blackened teeth and worm-like mouth, but the old farmer's reply brought his attention back to bare.

\"Little more'n half a day, by my reckonin'. Give or take an hour, of course, this close to Elturgard. That damn second sun sets the sky funny, sometimes I find myself misreadin' that sorry thing instead'a tha real deal, and then I get an earful from the wifey fer bein' late to supper.\" He cracked a wide, dirty smile. \"Not tha' I'm ever in a rush to taste the shit she passes off as a meal, if ye gather my meaning.\"

Edward allowed himself a small chuckle, uncomfortable, for the sake of politeness.

\"But naw, I keed a'suredly, love me woman ta bits, ye can relate, eh?\" A bony elbow jabbed Edward in the side, elicting a second laugh, more uncomfortable than the last. \"Young, clean-chinned feller such as yerself must have a lady-friend of his own waitin' fer 'im, yeah what?\"

\"No.\" Well, yes. Rather, well... that depended on a lot of things.

A third jab followed by several seconds of jolly, hacking laughter and a sharp slap on the shoulder. The old man then fished a tarnished silver flask from his pocket, unstoppered it, sniffed, winced and then took a sharp tug. He offered it to Edward.

\"No sir, I don't drink. Thank you though.\" A bit early, Edward noted.

\"Naw, take a swig lad, yer momma ain't here to paddle ye, much as I'd like to see 'er. Takin' from yer pretty face, must be fetching though. You noble-born, lad?\"

Edward took the flask that the old man boisterously forced into his hand, making sure to dump a portion of the liquid over the side of the wagon as the driver leaned opposite to spit. He pretended to wipe his mouth with the side of his hand and passed it back. \"No sir, farmer by trade.\" He said, his discomfort reaching an all-time high.

\"Where'd ye hail from?\" Another deep pull from the flask, punctuated by a wet belch that sent the wagon-horse's ears flat.

\"Cormyr, sir.\"

\"Hoo-wee, long way from home. And ye say yer a farm boy? Sure don't talk like one.\"

\"Well, my family owned land east of Arabel - if you know it - for a long time, but my grandfather was a generous man. Sold off land debts and donated the wealth until all we had left was our home, although the neighbours appreciated it well enough.\"

The old man nodded sagely. \"A man of true nobility. A toast to yer granpappy, then!\" As if the man need an excuse to knock back more of the flask's content. Edward smiled and raised his fist despite the thought.

\"So what're ye doin' all the way out here, m'boy?\"

Edward glanced at the road stretching out before them, noting the slowly growing outline of rooftops small on the horizon. \"I, ah. A pilgrimmage. I suppose. If that's the word for it, I mean.\"

The old man continued nodding, leaving Edward worried that perhaps something had come loose. \"A journey of the soul! Ha-ha!\"

The old man was muttering and gesticulating now with a savage exuberance, telling a tale of his youth that Edward was only half aware of, being deep in his own thought at the time. Deeply lost in his own recollections, remembering the momentous days leading up to this placid state of existance he had acquired over the last few months. Long roads and hard seats, little sleep and much talking, same as always across every step of the journey he had made and was still making, as though he were merely repeating the same encounter over and over, caught in the same play with different players: old fellows, hard fellows, scrawny fellows, each one tossing words in one of his ears and out the other, him responding with the same hesitant replies, stripping away hours and boredom only to reveal more of the same underneath. Every. Single. Day. Edward had yet to find what he was looking for, what he had been tasked to find, and already so much time had been wasted. Wasted.

In his head, Edward rolled back the countless days and nights, to the evening that started it all, the point at which life for him had finally begun. Metaphorically, of course. Spiritually.

He remembered the arrows that pierced his flesh, probing deep and deadly, stealing the life out of him. As though he possessed one to begin with, ever caught in routine, especially back then. For him, life was just the hours between sleeping, filler, purposeless and plain.

One through the ribs, twisting as it entered, slipping between the heart and lung but not so much as touching either.

The second in his abdoment, dodging the liver, dull tip tumbling between the coils of his gut as though it were afraid to touch them.

The third below the sternum, along the crook of his stomach and stopping an hair's breadth before the spine.

Those three arrows, at the same time so poorly and so expertly fired, dodging anything of imporantance in their clumsy intrusion, like an unwelcome thief stepping through a room full of delicates, careful not to disturb anything for fear of being caught.

They had found him in the morning, the arrows snapped and deeply set from his mindless crawling. All Edward remembered of the recovery was the initial, agonizing removal of the projectiles, the rest of the experience being a long unconciousness peppered with fevered dreams from dirt-clogged wounds gone septic. On occasion he'd wake, when the gaggle of local young women outside, trading places in shifts, would wail louder and longer than usual. He was later told that they brought regular offerings of sweet cakes and soups to hasten his recovery. The chaplain had assured the mob that Edward would receive them despite his condition, and so they continued bringing them. Upon his awakening, the considerably-chubbier-than-before chaplain had explained to Edward the happenings of the last few days and tended to him with the assistance of some of the more driven young ladies, often clawing and clamouring at one another for the opportunity, much to his own red-faced dismay.

A miracle, his survival, he was told, over and over by his caretakers. Some kind-eyed lord or lady of creation had cast his or her blessing upon Edward that night, surely, otherwise the young man would be dead. Many cited Tymora as his unseen benefactor, from the sheer luck of the arrows' harmless - relatively - effect. Edward had decided then, once strong enough, to make his way to Arabel and pay respects at the Lady's House, her very own seat of power during the tumultuous Time of Troubles, when the gods themselves walked amongst mortals. Leaving his home, the only home he had ever know, traumatizing several young woman in the process who had, in Edward's last moment amongst his friends and neighbours, thrown themselves upon him with vows of marriage and celibacy until his eventual return. Edward presently winced at the memory, his face warming instictively at their blown kisses and waving purple sashes. An unhealthy obsession that he seemed to instill in most girls, despite his quiet and unresponsive demeanor (or perhaps even moreso because of it), as though he were the carrier of some love-struck plague.

In his mind's eye, Edward was in Arabel once more as he walked up the temple steps, awed into silence by the grandoise structure before him, touched in the past by the very presence of Lady Luck herself. As though on cue, the great doors opened and a woman emerged, wreathed in fine fabrics and clinking jewelery of gold and silver. Coin-shaped icons and symbols decorated her curvaceous body, and Edward knew immediately that this was a woman of high standing, if only from the airs she carried as she strode, smiling, towards him, servants flowing in her wake with heads dipped in deference. Edward knelt in a similar regard as she approached, head bowed, waiting for her to pass. Those awkward moments of waiting were unexepectedly brushed away by the swishing approach of her flowing garments, not quite robes but something near, all together not leaving much to the imagination. Expecting her to pass, Edward was instead caugh off guard, terrified even, by the sudden feeling of smooth, warm hands sliding up either side of his face, gentling bringing his head upwards, a pair of lips gently caressing his forehead before the priestess drew herself back with that beautiful smile still adorning her face.

\"The Lady's blessing upon you, young man. I have felt your approach.\"

He was dumbfounded, searching for words, her lingering kiss burning a hole straight to his addled mind and setting his face afire. The priestess laughed at the sight of him, a perfect sound, not at all condescending but... affectionate, Edward decided.

\"I...ah, um, wha...\" He ventured a reply, clumsily.

She nodded as though any of it had made sense, and replied. \"She is most aware in these lands. You have come to pay forward your gratitude, Edward.\" The way his name rolled off her tongue turned his legs to jelly. It was good that he was already kneeling, else he'd have had to suffer a long tumble down the stairs behind him.

\"I...I have some gold on hand. If I owe, I mean, that is, or...or...I...\" He managed to stammer helplessly, fumbling at his hip for his coin pouch. The priestess bent low once again, laying a hand on his own, drawing it away to grasp herself from the pouch a single gold lion. Edward wasn't aware of that though, eye's unwavering dead ahead on the ample and barely-concealed bosom she had presented him unknowingly (or perhaps otherwise) during the graceful motion.

\"Do not be absurd,\" she murmured, a the hint of a sly smile dancing at the corners of her full lips. \"The Lady does not seek recompense for her favour. She accepts only what is offered, darling, and is more than happy to accept what you have offered here this day.\"

Edward nodded, hypnotised, eyes unable and unwanting to leave her delicate features, attention fixed on her every word.

\"What I have offered, yes...\" he mumbled blankly, watching breathlessly as she grasped the gold coin between two slender fingers an flicked it forward, sending it arcing, spinning, through the air towards him. Edward lurched clumsily and caught it with two shaking hands, gazing admiringly at it as though it were a priceless treasure.

\"Your service is accepted, Edward Albrecht, Aspirant of Tymora. Hold close that coin, not as a symbol of your faith for Her, but as a symbol of Her faith in you.\"

He looked up from the coin with an incredulous expression. Service? Aspirant? His what? Who? When had he agreed to anything?

\"I don't understand -\" the priestess cut him off with a single finger laid across his lips.

\"What she wants of you? So simple, my darling. All she requests of you is a deed of true virtue.\" She smiled as though that statement had laid every question answered, so simple that misunderstanding was rendered an impossibility. She continued in a sing-song voice. \"Travel to the capital and out of Cormyr, westward, to the coast and all the sordid things roosting therein. Those in need, the misbegotten, the forgotten, the sad downtrodden. Find a cause to champion, for the good of Her and us all, and see it to its conclusion. Be a beacon, a bastion, and a brother of justice and truth.\"

She held his face with both hands once more, leaning dangerously close, their eyes scant inches apart. \"I shall await you one year hence... ready to induct you into the Order of Her Ladyship. Make haste.\" Edward leaned forward, instinctively, unable to stop himself, but she drew back the moment her final words danced from her lips.

Edward rose to his feet, shaking hand clutching the coin so hard that, despite the smoothness of its edge, blood began to trickle down his wrist. The priestess and her entourage began their descent once more, the lady herself pausing to whisper in his ear as she went by. \"Fortune favours the bold, young Edward. Remember that.\" And then she was gone in a flourish of silks and smiling supplicants, leaving Edward to stand alone, halfway ascended to the temple, clenched fist held at his waist, mouth drawn tight, ears still red, breath coming fast, then slow, then fast again. In the frenzied pounding of his heart, young Albrecht knew, finally, the plight of the many young woman he had unintentionally plunged headlong into the waters of adoration. This was love, he knew it, a new feeling for him and a powerful one at that. A smile split his face, uncontrollable, as he looked down at his bloody palm, golden coin still sparkling between the runnels of red liquid marring its perfection.

I shall await you one year hence...

The memory of her voice sent shivers up his spine, a memory that, since then, he had clung to desperately each night as he lay, dreamless, anxious with excitement. Edward was experienced in passively spurning such advances, and he knew the tone a woman struck when... a certain something was on her mind. One year hence... He would remain pure, free of temptation and baser things, and save himself for her. And yet Edward didn't even know her name. Tossing and turning in his head every night, obsessed with not knowing, matching names to that perfect face, each one discarded, unfit to contain all the glory that beautiful priestess radiated, as though labelling her was a unspeakable blasphemy in both thought and deed.

\"...titties as big as two melons, shit ye nat boy, hoo hoo I tell ya, if the wifey hann't been 'angin' near at the time I'da been outta me trousers and hittin' the water faster'n a mutt chasin' a bone, yeah hey?\"

A sharp jab in his side brough Edward back to the present. The sun hung far overhead, beaming into his squinted eyes as if to chide him for his wandering daydreams. The old man had been talking to himself for hours, unconcerned or unaware of Edward's vapid disregard for the no-doubt-hi-larious tales he had. The distant specks of civilization, the bridge and rooftops, had grown to looming size, mere minutes away from engulfing the wagon they rode. The cart hit the bridge's planks with a rough crunch that did nothing but worry Edward. He was not much of a swimmer, even when not encumbered by his father's heavy blade and armour. Regardless of the structural integrity of the construction, they crossed the span with little trouble - having to stop a moment as several young women sauntered past with brimming jars and pots balanced precariously on their head and shoulders, tossing up catty smiles and what whistles they could manage at Edward (which the old farmer acknowledged as his own, flexing and winking much to their disgust). Upon arriving at the center of the village's thoroughfare, the point at which the road they had travelled met up with the Trade Way - a name that bespoke a veritable font of creativity, Edward noted - and continued northwards along the Sword Coast.

He thanked the farmer profusely as he dismounted the cart, shaking the old man's hand and wishing him luck at the 'morrow's market, Waukeen-bless-you-why-thank-you-lad-good-luck-yourself and other pleasantries passed between them that extended until the old man's wagon out-ranged the sound of either of their voices. Edward sighed and looked around himself, taking in the crowded buildings surrounding the plaza with an appreciative eye. Quaint and friendly, much like his own home, albiet a mite better-travelled, a fact the worn cobblestones underfoot attested to.

Here he would find something to champion, a virtue worth defending. Edward's smile couldn't have been more sincere as he glanced over his shoulder, the flickering speck of the second sun still looming over distant Elturgard, like a gold coin winking in the distance, measuring palpably the progress he had finally made. He grasped the coin slung around his neck, bound in two strips of leather lest it spring loose.

Fortune favours the bold, the priestess had told him, and so Edward would be the boldest of them all.

For her.
