Beneath Luskan's midnight sky, a man was being beaten. Three men loomed overhead, thrashing him with legs and fists, and it was all he could do to keep his head covered and legs tucked under his belly. He coughed and gagged and screamed, overwhelmed by pain, unable to keep his anguish bottled up. The three men, thick-set and shabbily dressed, much like their victim in that regard, continued the beating, beads of sweat growing on their foreheads, rolling down past their bared teeth, flung from their faces by hissed breaths of exhaustion. As far as they were aware, the only witnesses were themselves and the rats.

In that regard, they were incorrect.

The man at the end of the avenue had not yet stepped into plain sight. His broad, tattered cloak poorly concealed his prodigious form, but it kept the wind off his back, and the shadows leaning alongside his dilapidated surroundings seemed to take to it well enough, leaving nothing showing from the darkness but the moonlight's glitter off his beady eyes. He had been leaving this place, these people, when the sound of the scuffle had reached his ears from across the thoroughfare. Nothing new, not for a place like this, and especially not for a man like him. He clenched his fists, rough nails digging into rough palms, and flexed them open, cracking his fingers against the side of his thumb, one-by-one. He shifted his shoulders, adjusting the baldric slung across his torso, feeling the reassuring weight of the heavy blade slung under his arm. He took a step forward, then paused, half-extended into the alley, frozen. What was he doing? Everytime. The prone man at the end of the alley had ceased uttering his groaning, his head bouncing off the pavement under each heavy-heeled blow. Something glistened wet in the light, oozing from some unseen wound on the underside of his head.

A smart man would walk away. A smart man would mind his own business. Memories flourished in the small of his mind, his mother's thick, gravelly voice grating in his ear. \"Bigglesbee,\" she would say, tapping the ashes from her pipe into the outstretched hands of one of her husbands,\"ya're the dumbest of all me boys. How ya've gan and not gotten ya'self skewered on some pigsticker is any sane person's guess. Quit tha gamin' and night-foolin' and take yarself someplace respectable double-fast, since ya're someday liable to bite off more'n yar big mouth c'n chew and then ya'll be linin' the Mirar like the twenty-three brothers that came b'fore ya.\" She turned her wizened prune of a head and hocked a brown spack of phlegm onto the shirt of the husband who had just brought her tea. The woman took the cup and shoo'd him off, sipping it from one shaky, claw-like hand. Biggs locked eyes with the man for a moment and saw the raw expression of resignation on his face, turning after a moment to saunter off to the kitchen with the demeanor of an old, broken horse. Biggs had often wondered which one of the men that had once composed his mother's crew was his father, but looking around at the sad throng of elderly men tending the house as though they were servants - which in retrospect, they truly were -, the young man had decided that none of them were particularly inspiring role models, and dropped the line of thought entirely.  

\"I know ya think ya're doin' good 'round har,\" she continued, puffing furiously on her pipe,\"but one headstrong oaf with big hands and a small sword ain't doin' shit but stirrin' shit up, gat me? I ain't want ta bury another'un a me boys, so screw yar head on tight. Take to the road. Ya're wasted here. Ain't no good souls worth wastin' your effort on. Ya're a good boy, an' that's yar problem. Good boys don' belong in Luskan.\" She paused for a long moment, having seemingly concluded her lecture. Biggs got up from his chair, but his mother waved him to sit down once again. He did so, and after several more uncomfortable seconds and several more thick pulls of pipesmoke, the crone shook her head and coughed. \"Now git.\"

And git he did, Biggs thought none-too-sourly, still eyeing the group of thugs at the end of the avenue.

The kicking had slowed in pace, now little more sporadic bursts of violence that elicted little reaction from the man laying in the slowly-expanding pool of liquid. The faint rise and fall of his chest was the only indication of his survival. He should leave. Should continue down the way, Biggs told himself, out the gates, down the road, out of sight of this town and every awful thing it contained - mother not included, gods bless her - until all that was left before him was a better life stretched out on the horizon. The people had their own problems, their own reasons for doing what they did. Maybe the man owed them money. Stole something from them. Touched one of their wives. Was such a beating, so blisteringly vicious, adequate recompense? Was this fair? Was violence the only fair answer to violence? It was all Biggs had ever known. It was the rule he had lived by for all his miserable life, the only rule that anyone did in this cesspool. The only one that made sense. He was no scholar, no philospher, no judge of men's character.

Biggs could only do what he felt was right, as was always the case. Ever a slave to his own idealistic concepts of right and wrong, be they childish or otherwise.   

And so he strode into the darkness, the sound of his sword sliding from its sheath sending a warning to the men he fast approached. A warning none of them heeded. Chuckling, they drew their own blades and approached him with the casual confidence that superior numbers always inspired. \"

What're you looking for, feller?\" the closest one asked with a grin, stepping within range of Biggs' blade as he did so, not waiting for an answer before lunging low. The blow would have split his belly wide open, but Biggs turned quickly to the side, grasping the man's arm with his empty hand and hacking it loose with a heavy chop from his own blade. The man screamed and slumped into the gutter as Biggs stepped over him to meet the next two thugs. Biggs whipped the severed arm at the closest one's face, sending blood spraying from the man's broken nose, sidestepping into a parry that deflected the next man's clumsy chop, allowing Biggs to slip under his arm and haul him over his wide shoulder and, with one hand around his waist, drop the man head-first into the cobblestones with a satisfying crunch. Biggs swiftly rose to his feet and turned his attention to the man with the broken nose, still cradling his face, leaning against the wall to the left. Snapping his elbow into the throat of the fourth man approaching from his flank, sending him crumpling to the ground, wheezing and spewing vomit as he suffocated, Biggs stepped towards bloody-faced man and stabbed, his blade sliding smoothly between ribs and into the vitals. Biggs twisted the blade, splitting the man's heart and snapping ribs, and watched as he slid down the wall, an expression of wide-eyed fear permanently engraved on his pale face. With a kick and a tug, Biggs pulled his sword loose, cleaned the blade on the man's tunic, and turned to approach the prone man at the end of the avenue.

Dead. The blood pouring from the side of the man's battered head had already begun to thicken in the night's cool air. This man's fate was sealed before Biggs had stepped around the corner. Without a sigh, without a grimace, he rose to his feet and looked over his shoulder, his regard lingering on the bloody mess behind him. A fair exchange, one life for four? Some would debate, some would argue, some would say yes and some would say no. Biggs merely grunted.  

\"Fair enough.\"

He turned south and walked.
