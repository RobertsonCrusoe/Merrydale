And just as the Oni made his exeunt, did the Lady Saharel make her entrance.  She appeared just as we suspected; an apparition within the summoning circle, raven haired, floating about and saturn in expression.  She addressed us, but to be perfectly honest, I wasn’t paying attention to her words.  While her ghostly lips moved, I was having a discussion with myself in my head.

“By all that’s dead and gone, there she is!”

“Indeed.  Who would have guessed that the children’s tale that so ensorcelled the mind of the skinny mayor would actually have form, incorporeal though it be?”

“Not I.  I did not truly expect a ghost here, but rather a priest of Oghma to spring from behind the stones, with a sheet over his head, making moaning noises and proclaiming answers to any question, for a fee.”

“Precisely what I was thinking, old boy!  And yet, there she is.  There mustn’t be much to eat in the after-life; she’s as waif-like as that bag of bones you’ve been heaving on top of for the last few months.”

“Are we sticking to the order, wizard?”  This was Baird, and his curt interrogative snapped me out of my disbelief.

Now I must back up a bit.  A day or two ago, at breakfast, we were discussing what everyone wanted to ask the Lady.  There were variously phrased requests for the whereabouts of unlimited wealth, ale and ass, and I’m sure the pup wanted to know who had whelped him.  Baird and Mattrick had the only viable question, which was “Where are the Horns of Eltab?” They have come all this way looking for these artifacts in order to help in their war against Thay.  The politics of this and my place in it are not well known to any but me, but suffice it to say, I thought they could do some of my dirty work for me, by way of making two neighbours weak.  And so, I promised that Baird would have the first question of the Lady, and if needed to further their quest, Mattrick would have the second. Tyriol would have the third, and the rest of the team in no particular order, would go until I asked the final question.  We didn’t get that far.

I shall try to record this as accurately as I recall;

*Baird:  Where are the Horns of Eltab Brugukarg?
*Ghost: In the remains of the Tower Tyranis.

*Mattrick:  Where is the Tower Tyranis?
*Ghost:  You will find it by finding the Monastery of Sundered Faith.

*Pup:  Who is my daddy?
*Ghost: Nooooooo!!!!  AAAARRrrrrrrrr......

And screaming and clawing, off she went into whatever goes for a dungeon for ghosts.  She had been abducted by a black gauntleted hand with gemstones in each knuckle plate, and though I was chuckling and slapping my knees on the inside, I presented a face full of concern and surprise to my help.

“Such are the terrible perils of divination.  I wish I would have been proven wrong when I warned against such when we set out.”  I said diplomatically.  The troops were dismayed; Tyriol wore an expression of horror on his pretty face, and I think Ender actually threw one of his knives after the absconded spectre.  Luc hung his head and let out a smoky sigh, and Arya, no doubt longing to know the whereabouts of a shiny unicorn, was in tears.

I should admit that I felt some of their disappointment.  I had spent more than a couple of sleepless nights deep in thoughts that alternated between thinking up a question to ask the Lady, and reprimanding myself for being so foolish.  Tales of all-knowing ghosts are usually reserved for children’s stories, and when they are not, they should not be dealt with lightly.  I had planned to ask nothing that the ghost could not possibly know, and nothing that would have earth-shattering consequences.  I was simply going to ask the whereabouts of the closest unearthed Netherese ruin which I could come back and plunder.  But, I could also live without the answer.  Like I said; these are not matters to trifle with, and I did not count on one thing or the other.

What I did not expect was that the answer to Tyriol’s question would be a matter of inter-sphere importance.  This is a troubling development.  Ironically, if I myself had asked Tyriol’s question, verbatim, I would rightfully expect the exact same answer.  However, it troubles me that his father’s identity is a matter worth abducting a famous ghost to keep secret.  I will dwell on this another time, however, as there is still much to record, and I have burned too many candles in this lengthy record all ready.

The tale of our battle with the Mercykillers and the death of Baird will have to wait.  My pen is tired.
