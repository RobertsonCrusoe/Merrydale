== On the Differential Economics of a Scholarly versus a Worldly Education ==

There is a contrast, it would seem, between the life within Trygalle House and the life without. The tutors of the house expected much of Wiam, and he gave it too, but there was a general feeling of reciprocity - and what the tutors took they gave back ten-fold.

However on the mainland, Faerûn may expect little from you or it may expect much, but in either case, what it expects it gets, and it gives not even one one-hundredth back.

== On the Ecology of Justice and Wickedness in the City of Splendors ==

Wiam spent his first few months out of Trygalle in Waterdeep as a Barrister's aide, and it was there that he first glimpsed the taint of the outside world.

Even at the hands of Tyr, justice was meted out in disparate proportions. When the debtor’s prisons were too full one could see chorus lines of bodies hanging from the gallows in Dock Ward. Of course many of those poor were in such straits because they could not sue for payment from their noble employers. And such was the natural ecosystem of the city with the guildhalls driving it all. The poor that took away from the guild coffers are choked out, but only enough so that the nobles can run their ventures and pay the more well connected guild masters.

The 'splendor' in the city seemed to be nothing more than a veneer over the callous hearts of those that owned power, while everyone else wallowed in the muck and died. However many those beggars did not just die. Rather, many let darkness into their hearts turning to wickedness and hate.

== On the Ubiquity of Darkness in the World and Its Inevitability ==

The City of Splendors did not hold much for Wiam so he left it after not so long a time. He traveled south, down the Sword Coast, then east to the Shaar, before finally traveling up around the Inner Sea. He traveled for the remainder of the first year, as he wanted to see the hearts of man in the world. And what Wiam saw was no different than in Waterdeep. All about Faerûn was murder, was tyranny, was strife.

Wiam saw the darkness about, but could not see a cause so great as to affect so many and so far.

So Wiam put his symbol aside and set out to travel again, this time as a beggar. He circled Faerûn for another year looking through the eyes of those who were the most tempted to the shadows.

And he saw it this time. A little here, a little there at first: Dark stares in a tavern beckoning him closer; words mumbled in languages that should not be known; distant drumming at midnight. As he traveled more as a beggar Wiam let a little piece of darkness, no more than a speck, into his heart so that he could learn see more, praying that his will was hardened enough not to be overcome.

== On Free Will and the Rejection or Otherwise of Fear ==

And he saw more as those men of darkness beckoned. Those men welcomed him in every hamlet, every village, every city. Those men were drawn to him everywhere by their dark master. And that master was Bane.

The Black Lord Once Dead Now Returned had taken that little speck of injustice and fear that lay within the heart of all downtrodden men and turned it to his own evil purpose, even while the churches of good looked to the skies instead of into the eyes of other men.

And when Wiam came to this realization, Bane came to him through the form of an asp:

14
You have watched me all these years.
I have watched you.
I am the speck inside your heart,
and I will erupt and become your master.
Kneel before your master now
so that I may see that you fear me.


And Wiam did fall to his knees trembling, for he was no god. But Wiam had been taught to be strong all those years ago, and although he had opened himself to weakness for a time he was still strong inside.

14
I have seen your speck in my heart,
and in the hearts of the good men you have overcome.
But I will push you out and cast you off,
and the men that have made you their master will cast you off,
for I will show them how to be good,
then your speck of darkness will turn into my speck of light,
and that speck of light will swell ever larger until it blinds you.


Hearing this, the asp’s eyes grew wide as Bane was cast out of it, but as he left Bane made the asp lash out, sinking its fangs into Wiam’s heart. Then the asp died, being drained of the poison that sustained it. And Wiam was left trembling and dying in the darkness and the cold.

But Wiam did not die.

== On the Paradox of the Forgiveness of Masters by Their Servants ==

As the sun crested the eastern horizon the asp turned to dust, and his poison seeped out of Wiam as tears, scarring Wiam’s flesh as they fell down his face. And Wiam cried:

14
My Lord, Father of Light,
I forgive you for standing by
while the world turned to wickedness.
I know that it is not your fault.
For I know now that you only
see through man’s eyes,
feel through man’s heart.
And if man does not wish to see,
then he cannot use your power.
But yea, I will make man see,
I will restore sight to you my Lord.


And with that the morning sun washed over Wiam, warming him and filling his heart with strength and joy. Wiam’s personal journey was over, and it was time to seek out friends.
