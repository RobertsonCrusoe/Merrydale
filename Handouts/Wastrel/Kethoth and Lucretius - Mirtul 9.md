Luc and Kethoth sitting around the campfire while some sleep and Ender and Tyriol are off scouting the camp of the black armoured company ahead of them. Roughly one day's journey out from Spellgard.

\"Well, my capable general...What do you make of our merry band so far? It seems we have done well enough as a team in our few tests so far.\"

Kethoth crouches down by the fire, and blows at one weak part of it, to little effect. He smiles up at the dragonborn, and holds out his hand, palm up, as though he is going to blow a kiss at the fire. Instead, a small flame blows out of his mouth, and stokes the fire up a bit. He winks at the dragonborn, and then returns to his seat, a bare and blasted rock. He rips a hunk off of a roll of dry bread, and hands the rest to Luc.

\"A general sits in his tent and decides how many of his troops he’ll send to slaughter that day. Nay, I am no general, just a traveller like us all.

\"I fear, Kethoth, that we have not been tested yet at all. I accompanied several caravans from Tymanther to Loudwater and all I saw was danger and strife. To tell the truth, I was taken on for my size and appearance, but I fear I was not ready for much of what faced us. How is it that only the most hardened soldiers are fit to trade a bit of cloth?\"

Lucretius continues; \"I've been told that this land has not always been this way, that before my world appeared much of the land was safe. Most of the long lived races still remember this world as it was and seem saddened by this knowledge.

\"Didn't you live through the Spellplague Kethoth? Tell me, why does this ravage seem to fill you with such glee?\"

\"The world has not changed for better or for good, my friend. It has only changed.

\"It has traded one form of danger for another; the world is a bigger place all of a sudden, and there seems to be more wilderness between the periodic bits of civilization. Before, it wasn't so. Faerûn was covered in roads and populous towns; trade flourished, magic and education likewise flourished. But there was some rot right at the middle of it. 

\"There will always be danger in the world, no matter what happens. To tell you the truth, I don't know if I prefer the old dangers that a mature civilization creates, or the baser sort of troubles that we experience today. The world is a dangerous place no matter what happens to it.

\"It will all change again, though. I've seen it more than once. I once walked in the Dales before they began to clear away Cormanthor, and I recall the smell of the streets of Waterdeep when there were only 20,000 people living there. I remember when Thay and Aglarond were peaceful neighbours.

\"It all changes. That is the nature of all things; to change. To grow for a while, then to die or decay or otherwise shrink back, and to grow again. I don't doubt that Men will carve their roads and trade networks again before long. I don't doubt that they will again drive off the elves and other Fey. Your people, too, will carve their place in the world, and in relatively short order. 

\"And then they will die off again for a time. Kethoth will watch this happen, and not marvel at it any more than I do the thawing of snow and ice in the spring, or the colouring of the leaves in autumn.\"

\"You think much of yourself. Maybe you can pull your weight on the battlefield next time then.\"

Lucretius turns away and starts sharpening his sword.
