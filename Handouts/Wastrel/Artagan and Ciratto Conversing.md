It is only noon hour on the 9th of Tarsakh, 1480 DR, but as the door to a small room on the upper floor of the Green Tankard Inn in Loudwater opens up, it admits two men who look as though they have been up all night.

First through the door is the larger man, a pair of axes through his belt and a bow slung over his shoulder. He enters cautiously, looking behind the door, and crosses the room to brush aside the curtain in the small window, ever so slightly. He surveys as much as he can see from the tiny opening he has made, and satisfied, settles down on one small cot with a bit of a groan. Grimacing, he runs his hand over a jagged rend in his leather jerkin just under his ribs, the leather creaking in response.

The smaller of the two men comes in only after his companion has checked the window. He runs his hands along the casements of the small dirty window in the small dirty room. Nodding to himself and smiling, he opens the window and takes a deep breath. “What were you saying about snake money, Arty?” the assassin asks the ranger in a gravelly tenor.

Artagan, who is now sitting on the cot beside his piled up gear, looks up from under the brim of his hat to squint into the sunlight of the glorious day outside, flaring his nostrils with a deep draught of the clean air.

“Sorry…what, Ciratto?” Artagan says after the two of them have paused for the air.

“The snake money, Art. The first snake-money; you know, from the slavers in that swamp south of Zelbrosse…” Artagan watches the assassin as he continues his circuit of the room, doing he-only-knows what with chests and door-jambs and such. Artagan removes his broad-brimmed shiny-creased leather hat, and sets it on the pack beside him. He then un-slings his bow from his back and begins to examine it.

“Yeah” Artagan says, pausing long enough for his companion to actually gesture for him to continue. “Well, I have to think…hold on…” and with this he trails off. His brow furrows, either from concentrating on his weapon or thinking back to the events in the slaver’s camp, or both. Ciratto continues to examine small details of the room as his friend collects his words, in his now-familiar deliberate fashion. As he contemplates the slavers, he sees to his weapon, waxing the string, and oiling the ash-and-yew beams with oil from a small jar. “The snake money…the slavers in the swamp…”

“The slavers in the swamp…”

In his mind, he is back in the swamp, waist-high in the foul water, creeping along in the nearly-pitch-black night, with Ciratto beside him, gliding through the water as silently as the small bugs on the surface that scatter before them. Up ahead, they can see men in the torch-light of the small ruin-become-slaver camp, and with a few silent gestures, they separate. Ciratto vanishes off to the left, while Artagan continues to stalk along straight ahead, making for a gap in a weed-covered ruined wall.

Through that gap, and a good distance behind it, there is a torch burning, and Artagan can see a man walking towards the gap. His heart skips a beat and a contemptuous scowl plays across his face, for none to see. In his thoughts, now the memory of his thoughts, the man he sees walking towards him is no more than a skulking fox, sneaking away from the livestock, guilty and retched. “You won’t get away from me” thinks Artagan, as he touches the small piece of antler tied about his neck with a sinewy piece of leather.

To his left, there is some commotion. Ciratto has done what he does best, and somewhere in the dark, a man is dead. Artagan’s prey notices, and hurries his pace towards the gap; the same gap that Artagan is making for. Artagan quickly but silently emerges from the water, and taking a few steps to his left, he is out of line-of-sight to his quarry. He hastens to the weed-covered wall, and slinging his bow on his back, and drawing out his paired axes, his scowl deepens, and his face becomes an array of contoured lines radiating out from between his eyes. He puts his back to the wall, and turns his head over his left shoulder to listen to the approach of the doomed man. As the heavy footfalls of the man announce that he is only a few paces on the other side of the wall that divides them, Artagan crouches down to two-thirds his height, and tenses up, much like the bow-string he has just retired.

The slaver steps through the gap in the wall; coming from the lighted side of the wall, to the dark void of the far side, he is unable to see the blackened axes or the butcher wielding them less than a pace away. The man steps fully through the breech, and Artagan can see his face; he is squinting into the darkness beyond; looking straight past the ranger a step in front of him, into the gloom where another cry announces that Ciratto is still at work. The torch light illumes the right side of the man’s face, and it is this dim light that allows Artagan to see the terror and surprise on his quarry’s face as a whirlwind of axe-sweeps erupts in front of him.

“Surprise strikes to the vitals, first, boy” Artagan can hear his uncle saying in his mind, as the world slows down, his battle-senses firing. The first blow would be fatal, with enough time; it opens the man’s throat, and a gout of blood dances towards Artagan’s face, though it is easily blocked by the subtlest nod of the head, the brim of his hat keeping the blood from his eyes.

“The belly next, Arty. They always reach up to stop the blow that just landed. Always.” Hands go up to stop the blow that just landed, to assess the damage, and Artagan’s left hand continues the assault, coming in lower and opening up chain and flesh in one brutal swipe. The sickening noise of rending chain and escaping gasses from the gut can be heard across the swamp. The look of surprise and terror turns to disbelief. 

“A tough one will start to guard, if you haven’t done for him yet. But he’ll still guard the areas you just attacked. Go lower.” This message plays in his mind as the momentum of the belly-blow continues; the ranger turns on his heel and sinks low as he does, and the axe in his left hand arcs around fully to sink into the knee of the slaver, who has indeed brought up his sword to fend off another attack to the vitals. The deep, loud report of the stroke, similar to that of a woodsman’s axe striking a thick tree trunk, is loud enough to signal to Ciratto that Artagan is not in need of help.

“Three strokes should do it for most men; they should be bleeding from two lethal blows, and laying on the ground for you to either finish off, or move on. Some chaps just don’t quit, though, and those need another round. Paired strike, boy; one high, one low.” Artagan returns from his spinning crouch to stand still, only for the smallest portion of a second, to look into the man’s eyes. He’s still got some fight in him. The axes nearly touch behind Artagan’s back as he crosses his arms, and prepares for the final lethal attack. In an effort to parry both axes, the blood-covered slaver manages to miss both of them, and both axes arc out from behind the ranger, one sweeping across the bridge of his nose, taking flesh and cartilage with it, the other returning to the belly wound, where it sinks deeper into that gaping wound to tear it’s way out through the man’s side, more gas-belching gore escaping from a widened rend in the mail shirt.

The dying slaver staggers back, and though one hand tries feebly to stop the flow of blood from his throat, his other hand continues to bring the sword to bear, in one last defiant gesture. In his mind, Artagan could now see the beautiful young Otra, and imagining the filthy paws of this jackal on his new-found charge, his rage found a here-to-fore unknown pinnacle; Artagan was in the air without even thinking; leaping high to bring the axe in his right hand down with the benefit of trajectory and gravity, it sunk to the shaft inside the man’s skull. The sword clattered from the dead slaver’s hand, and the lights went out. Artagan stepped on his mutilated face to help free the axe, and without looking back, he touched the small piece of antler, and stalked off silently into the night after more prey.

“…the slavers in the swamp” Artagan repeated. “They had coin that don’t go ‘round here. Snake money.” Artagan’s bow is now unstrung and propped up in a corner. Ciratto waits for Artagan to continue his train of thought, but then abandons the wait. Ciratto starts checking some of his own gear, adjusting straps and testing the edges of blades, while Artagan begins to remove his beaten-leather jerkin and greaves. From the same bottle of oil he used for the bow, he begins to apply a light coat to the leather armour, working it in with a worn strip of cloth.

At length, Artagan finds his tongue again. “Well, then that little bastard Zark had a pocket full’o’em too. Naj’ran coins.”

“Mmhmm…” replies Ciratto. He looks up from his gear to stare at Artagan for a moment, waiting once again for a response from his friend. After it is clear that the ranger is sussing it out in his head, and that the response won’t be quickly forthcoming, Ciratto shrugs and heads back to the window, and just stares intently outside.

Artagan now has a patch of leather, a needle and thread in hand, and he sets about patching the hole in his armour. He stops, and squinting hard says “Right here in the Fisher’s Friend.”

“Right here in the Fisher’s Friend…”

Artagan is now sitting on a wobbly stool at the bar in the Fisher’s Friend. The sound of the river comes up clear as a bell through the opening in the middle of the bar. He knows that Ciratto is down there, creeping around, plying his trade. He puts his elbow down, and after a moment notices that it is perched in a pool of stale beer, which has now soaked through and reached his skin. He removes his arm from the mess and curses.

“Ha ha! ‘Fraid of a little ale, stranger?” a rough-neck says to him. The man looks about as terrible as he smells. Artagan already had a good look at him when he entered the place, sizing up all the potential enemies in the bar. Now, this brute is standing over his shoulder, and he can smell fish and piss and beer and…

“I said, are you afraid, stranger?!” Artagan ignores the man. He keeps thinking ‘I am just here having a quiet drink, I am just here having a quiet drink, I am just here having a quiet drink…’

The problem is this; he is not just here having a quiet drink. And even though he tries to ignore this man, attempting in vain to stick to the plan and wait for Biggs, Ed and Otra to crash the party before he does anything, he’s just not the right guy to keep out of a fight. Now, there is a brute on either side of him, and they are telling him to leave.

Now they have spilled his beer.

Now one has punched him in the face. Still, Artagan sits unmoved, grinding his teeth. He imagines he is back at the farm, watching Ilyaska’s deft fingers embroider in the light of the hearth. Such lovely fingers…

Now the other one has punched him in the face. Artagan grabs his whiskey, closes his eyes, and tries to think about Ilyaska. But now there is a hand on his wrist, and he can’t raise the glass. He thinks to himself “what can I say to get these men to just leave me alone?”

Now he is elbowing one man in the face, and blood has exploded from the odorous brute’s nose, spraying the bar. The other man grabs Artagan from behind, hoisting him into the air. Artagan swings his other elbow back, and blackens this man’s eye, but not before Mr. Nose is back in the fight, punching as fast as he can, slowly knocking the wind from the restrained ranger with body-blow after body-blow.

Now, they are all on the ground. These things always end up on the ground. There is a frantic flailing of twelve limbs, but now Artagan can hear Ed shouting at Zark, calling him a coward and…is Edward drunk? Better stop playing around.

Now, Artagan is on his feet, and the two bruised wretches on either side of him won’t give up, even when he’s offered a dozen chances. The axes come out, flashing through the air with an audible whoosh, and still the two brutes are determined. They made Artagan the minute he crept in through the back door. “Ciratto should have done this” he thinks to himself. “I can’t fool shit.”

But Artagan thinks back to a scolding he took from Ilyaska a few years ago. “No one ever started a feud by NOT killing someone, Art. Don’t be so damned stupid!” Artagan pauses for a second after flourishing his axes. Even though the two men set against him have likewise upped the ante by brandishing weapons, the ranger lets the paired axes slide through his hands, gripping both handles now close to the head, so that he now has a ratty-looking pair of leather-covered sticks in his hands.

And before his two foes can take one swing with their weapons, Artagan strikes. Again, the world slows down. Again, instructions from the distant past from a long-dead uncle guide him through his strikes. In less than a couple of seconds, both of these men are on the ground out cold, the axe-handles having clubbed each man thrice.

Looking across the room, Artagan can see that things aren’t going well for his friends. Zark has a host of nasty body guards with him, and they are being terribly rough on his friends. When Otra is sent to the ground by the assault of these villains, Artagan touches the bit of leather-strung antler at his neck, and moments later, there are a number of mutilated bodies at his feet, and people are fleeing from the bar through every possible exit, doors, windows and improvised.

Ciratto is already going through Zark’s pockets while Biggs helps Otra to her feet, and Edward sways drunkenly, looking in disbelief at the fist-sized glob of gore that slowly slides off of one of the ranger’s axes. Artagan’s face is a stone-carved rage, and only when Otra is on her feet and waiving off the mountain of muscle that is Bigglesby, does his countenance soften.

“Najarran” Artagan hears Ciratto say. He sees Ciratto flip a serpent-stamped coin to Edward, who fumbles drunkenly, and drops the coin right into the pile of mucky gore that slid from Artagan’s axe.

“Naj’ran” Artagan says. By now, he has sewn his armour up, and is folding it neatly on the cot. He lays out a leather roll on the cot which he has produced from his back pack, and he then removes both axes, and lays them gently on the leather.

“The slavers in the swamp had Naj’ran gold.” The ranger proclaims, looking up from his axes, addressing the wall in front of him. Behind him, his friend pauses.

“We know this…”

“Zark had Naj’ran gold.”

“That too, we knew…”

“Well, we know that Zark is a Jackal, and the Jackals are being paid with Naj’ran gold…”

Ciratto sighs as Artagan’s head drops down again to focus on his gear. He begins to unwrap the handles of the paired axes. Ciratto pads up behind his friend, to look silently over his shoulder. He sees Artagan use an oiled cloth to clean the steel heads. There is something odd about them…

Artagan’s hands go still again, and his head comes up. Ciratto takes one silent step back, as if the ranger needed all the space possible for his thoughts to come together. “Where does this Naj’ran gold come from?”

“Najarra, friend.”

“Where does THIS Naj’ran gold come from? I’ll tell you where; the same place that they plan on taking Moonstar tomorrow.”

The ranger begins to unravel the grimy leather that surrounds the handles of the crude-looking axes, as just as the axes become unwound, so too do Artagan’s thoughts.

“We are planning on ambushing that column, aren’t we, Ciratto?”

“Mmhmm.”

“Why?”

“…”

“Why not just follow it instead?”

After this, there is no noise but the sound of leather winding away from the shafts of two implements of war. Ciratto once again pads up close behind his friend, and looking over his shoulder, he can see the weapons, now naked on the oil cloth. They are being meticulously cleaned and oiled by the ranger. Now, fully revealed, Ciratto can see that each axe is actually made out of one piece of damasked steel, blade and shaft one continuous piece of shimmering silver metal beaten together in a wavy pattern with a darker metal. Artagan always has the axe heads blackened with tar, and the handles covered with leather, so that they appear to be crude instruments. When seen for what they are, as Ciratto alone can now attest, they are of the finest workmanship. Ciratto makes a point to himself to ask his friend where they came from, but perhaps not just now.

Artagan picks up one polished and oiled axes, and Ciratto can see the image of his friend’s face reflecting in the alternating ribbons of polished steel. He can also see the half-obscured, half-reflected image of the piece of antler on the leather around his friend’s neck, and can make out the beast-claw emblem on it. This too, he will have to ask his friend about, but perhaps not just now. Ciratto takes his eyes from the reflection to focus on the weapons themselves.

“They’re…”

“Beautiful.”
