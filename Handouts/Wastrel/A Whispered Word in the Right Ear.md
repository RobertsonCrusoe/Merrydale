Ciratto had been in town for four days now, and felt it about time he was on the move again. Tarsk’s offer had been perfectly timed. A slight smile touched the corner of his mouth as he thought on that. Well of course it had. He had a hand in arranging it.
 
A “subtle” conversation here; 
 
The captain of the mercenary group that call themselves the ‘Sword Coast Six’ had laughed when it was suggested he pull out of town. They had come to town three days ago, up from Baldur’s Gate, expressly to offer services to Thunderstaff Mercantile. Ciratto met him in this same bar, over breakfast, the morning after they arrived.
 
“Little man” the captain had mockingly intoned, leaning forward to set his gaze upon the scruff of a man seated before him. “I don’t like your tone, and your suggestion is preposterous. The Six have come to take this job and take it we will. We do as we please round these parts, no better swords can be found.” The words had not been a boast, but were spoken as fact. A plain simple truth with no embellishment.
 
Ciratto sat back as he spoke, more to be removed from the captain’s breath than from being cowed, but let the man take the action as he would. The fool’s smug grin spoke volumes on that measure.
 
“The ‘Six’ does have a reputation.” Ciratto agreed. “Indeed, I find it odd you would come, without contract, all this way?” Ciratto feinted discomfort now and his tone was both apologetic and complimentary.
 
The Captain seemed appeased, and his pride loosened his tongue.
 
“Our reputation has been well earned, and thus we never travel without contract. I bear diplomatic documents of a relevance that someone of your status could hardly fathom. The Six will hire on with the Thunderstaffs, on the ‘dangerous’ Delimbiyr Way, for fun. As I said, the Six do as they please.” His mocking of the danger ahead was indeed boastful now.
 
The captain continued, rather imperiously “And now it is my turn for questions. It seems you have heard of the Six, and thus I assume your requesting our departure implies your mind is somewhat addled. Thus, I will be generous. Give me your name and you may depart without the whipping you deserve.”
 
As he spoke his attention had returned his breakfast. He forked a large chunk of sausage into his mouth as he finished speaking as if to clarify that his part in the conversation was at an end.
 
The response was but a word: “Ciratto.”
 
The half chewed sausage dropped back to his plate as the captain’s mouth fell open. His head raised, slowly, his eyes wide.
 
Ciratto let the name sink in a moment longer before he continued. He enjoyed this part of the game.
 
“Ah, it seems you know my reputation as well Mr. Billings.” Ciratto sipped his coffee, sitting back comfortably eyeing the captain over the rim of his mug.
 
“Mr. Gerhard Billings of Baldur’s Gate, Captain of the renowned mercenary company The Sword Coast Six. The trouble with renown, Captain, is that you become a person of interest. And people of certain status make it their business to keep an eye on people of interest. I trust that your wife and children are well? Still living with your brother Bertram on Fisher Street?” The questions seemed most pointedly rhetorical.
 
The fear in the captain’s eyes grew at the mention of his family, the threat was ever so subtly implied, but he was no fool. The implication was clear as daylight. He was obviously struggling to bring moisture back to his tongue as his mouth worked without forming words. All colour had drained from his face.
 
Cirrato rose from the table, coffee in one hand.
 
“Good, we are clear then.” He started to walk away, but after two steps turned back to the table. 
 
“Oh, and you can leave the document case at the bar, before you depart. They know you are leaving.” With these final words a flick of his wrist sent something flipping through the air to land on the captain’s plate. He then turned and left the bar.
 
The captain closed his eyes and drew a deep breath. His composure was almost regained, and a thought of hunting Ciratto out with his men began to form in his mind.
 
A hard edge, formed from years of combat found his facial features. He opened now determined eyes and looked down to his plate.
 
The blood on the ear was dry, and the earring was unmistakable. It belonged to a one-time member of the Sword Coast Corsairs. It belonged to his lieutenant.”
 
By noon that same day folk in Secomber were already gossiping about the hasty departure of the “Sword Coast Five”. 
 
A whispered word in the right ear there.
 
The surprising and sudden departure of the Mercenary Company from Baldur’s Gate a couple days previous had left Thunderstaff Mercantile and Sundries without a guard. A void Ciratto had a desire to have a hand in filling. He soon learned that in Secomber, “When in doubt, search Meriweather out”. 
 
Trouble was, Meriweather was apparently short on swords himself and would be looking to hire on a crew. Watching the man made his position obvious, he fretted and grumbled as he lovingly brushed the manes of his prized mounts. 
 
Word of the arrival of a worthy group of adventurers to town that very morning reached Meriweather surprisingly quickly. As did rumours of the likelihood that they would most likely wish to depart Secomber with little delay. Apparently they were in some trouble, desperate even.
 
The yellow smile on Meriweather’s face at hearing this news was unmistakable, even from 40 yards away under a hay mound. 
 
The Grey Vale native Artagan also received fortunate news that day. He would not be required to wait the usual five days for his order of Silver Flakes to be amassed. It seemed that this previously ‘rare’ commodity was indeed readily available now, the normal ‘acquisition fee’ had been waived, and the order was being insured through Thunderstaff Mercantiles and Sundries at the sellers cost. The order would be ready at first light the next day.
