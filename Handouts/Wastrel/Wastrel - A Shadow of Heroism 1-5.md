The very next morning Edgar, Chadrazzan, and I board The Bodiger. The captain is a nice enough fellow who is reminiscent of Zechary Mudge in his salty straightforwardness. Kethoth (as he prefers to be addressed in front of the crew) and I were lucky enough to have our own private cabin to share, although later discovered that it had traditionally belonged to the first mate. I don

t know what was worse that voyage; the hostile glares of the first mate, or Kethoth

s infernal snoring. Thank Corellan for the crate of everquist I brought along. Edgar, unwilling to find reprieve through alcohol, took to staying above decks, or sleeping through the day.

Travel on the Bodiger was slow, much slower than travel aboard The Wastrel. I passed the breezy hot days as best I could. Kethoth was too busy with his scrolls and research to be much fun. While I agree that power is enjoyable, what use is the pursuit of power if one must forsake all other forms of enjoyment? Regardless, at nights I could count on his presence in the captain

s quarters along with Edgar and the captain himself, for it pleased me to spend a portion of each day preparing my favourite dishes from across the realms for the four of us to enjoy. And after we had eaten our fill, the everquist and bloodwine and Calishite brandy would flow and the captain would regale us with tales of the sea. And every once and a while when the bloodwine had loosened his tongue sufficiently, Kethoth would also thrill us with a tale of a tomb robbing
research
expedition. It is easy to forget that he is hundreds of years old until he tells a tale of the events of centuries ago that he witnessed first hand.

When I was not cooking I spent my time writing and painting and lost in wine and the smoke. Time passed satisfactorily thusly until we reached Baldur

s Gate. I can tell you that setting foot on land, I was a little more than eager for some female companionship. Unfortunately, Kethoth was all business. We quickly arranged passage by river barge to Iriaebor. This time the journey substantially shorter, but the cramped accommodations began to wear on Kethoth and I. The crew were most apprehensive of his appearance, and he spent most of the nine hot, cramped, days under cover of his massive cloak. I was envious of Edgar

s ability to take flight at will, and although I bade him not to stray too far, I could not deny him some measure of escape. I decided then and there that travel by commercial barge was not for the likes of me. Better to hire a private barge, or better yet, a coach. Surely with a coach Chadrazzan would attract much less attention.

And so it was with great joy that we set foot in Iriaebor. Kethoth was all business, stating,
Time is our enemy
as a rational reason not to blow off some steam in the first festhall in sight. That man could see enemies in a field of daisies, and treachery in a sunset. To this very day he remains so. I wonder what it is that he is so fearful of. As usual Edgar and I bent to his wishes and the three of us proceeded to book a room in an inconspicuous and boring, if quaint, inn.

The next morning Kethoth summoned forth a mount for himself, and was about to do likewise for me when I beat him to the punch. Forming a mount from shadow-stuff I draped it in a glamour most complimentary to Kethoth

s own mount. He acted a little indignant at my refusal of his hospitality, and then he pretended not to care. But I could tell that he was a tiny bit impressed with the effortlessness and style with which I pulled it all off. We rode our magical mounts far harder than I would have pushed a horse of flesh and blood, and consequentially made very good time. The next morning as I summoned up another shadow-mount Kethoth commented,
Decided to summon a real one today, did you old boy?
which suggested that he was both aware of the true nature of my shadow weaving, and also that he was sometimes fallible to it. We all have our secrets and it was good to know that, unlike my family, he harboured no ill will towards the nature of my in-born abilities.

We made excellent time along The Trade Road, and could have made better time yet if I wasn

t conserving my talents for an emergency. I was afraid of a repeat of that nasty business that happened that time in Cormyr, but we were spared such unpleasantries. At any rate, both the time and the countryside passed pleasantly along the trade road. Edgar was my constant companion, of course, and of this Chadrazzan seemed almost envious. One night when were playing chess he pretended to be ever-so-interested in his books, but I feel that were we to offer to include him in a game of cards his reluctance would be poorly feigned, at best. It is too bad that the old bird has not the fingers to hold a hand of five-card dwarven draw. The next day, Edgar asked him why he had not taken a companion of his own, but his inquiries were met only with a thoughtful silence. After that we played chess less, and drank wine more.
