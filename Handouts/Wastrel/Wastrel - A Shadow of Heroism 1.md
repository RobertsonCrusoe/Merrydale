A Shadow of Heroism: the Memoirs of Cedric Blackchalice-Lliandreal

I was passing the scorching summer days of late Flamerule and early Eleasis in Calimport much as any other young man of means might; sleeping until the sun

s pending retirement made the outdoors more bearable, then partaking of the various delights of Calimshan

s Jewel. Typical evenings began at one of the restaurants close to my villa where Edgar and I would dine on the excellent local offerings and play a nightly game of chess. After dining, it was off to the theatre for a bit of show and beauty or, depending on my mood, it was off to one of the two Great Arenas for some thrills and excitement. Sometimes I would spend hours getting lost in the bazaar district, braving the cut-purses and conmen to catch a glimpse of all Faerun

s offerings laid bare in tents and baskets. There was a most excellent selection of books there that I have rarely found since, and also some of the finest smoke weed known to mortal or god. If my recollection of Calimport is somewhat murky, it is in no small part due to the quality and quantity of my smoking days there. As the drink and smoke took its toll and the arenas and theatres closed my travels and explorations would invariably take me to the harems of the city. What can I say of these? It is true what the Calishites say: there are indeed 101 ways of making love and their harems are devoted to perfecting each and every one of these.

It was in one such place that I was given a message most mysterious to relay to one of my good friends, the famous Maevri of Rashemen, the Open Harper. Maevri

s allies could not at that time contact her directly so they had set about subtly guiding her travels towards Calimport and had set me in her path with a message. The message was a typical offering from that lot; cryptic and vague it said nothing, yet said it with the utmost urgency. The gist of it was that May was to travel to Westgate to receive another cryptic message from another vague source. Color me surprised. I love these cloak and dagger guys.

Still the message in and of itself was good tidings for I had been dearly missing May and the rest of our friends aboard that most famous ship. I think that of that crew, Edgar missed most the company of Captain Mudge, although he is certainly fond of May

s flattery as well. What that bird sees in the old sea rat I will never know. Perhaps I should have done as Edgar, and spent more time at the helm learning from the captain, rather than smoking the nights away with Salazar. Just thinking of my friends started the old Blackchalice wanderlust stirring within my heart and I suddenly knew that no force in Faerun could hold me to Calimport once they had arrived.
And so I set my servants about watching for The Wastrel
s arrival. Days passed without sign of the ship. Edgar and I spent our time getting our affairs in order and purchasing supplies for our pending voyage to Westgate, namely rare spices for my cooking, and a goodly supply of smoke weed. I also spent most nights with Haranna, whose beauty and wit I had come to appreciate above all the other girls in Calimport. It would be years before I would see her fair countenance again.

At last came the evening when an outburst from my faithful servant Dango (not the Dengo that Chadrazzan would come to rue) interrupted my nightly luncheon. Leaving Edgar to supervise the packing of the last of our belongs in the villa, I rushed past the nobles through the elevated streets and hurried down through the commoners below to meet my fellow crew members. Arriving at the docks I saw my friends aboard The Wastrel. Kethoth (as he was then known) was descending the gangplank and just as I was about to wave a greeting an attacker materialized beside the dumbfounded tiefling and began an unsolicited assault upon his personage. Well, I know Chadrazzan has his social shortcomings, but this seemed a little over the top to me. It was then that I noticed that the Wastrel itself was under attack. Some sort of floating island was raining down spells and arrows upon my shipmates. Captain Mudge rallied the troops and The Wastrel tried to outmanoeuvre its magical pursuer. Veiling myself with invisibility, I started to close the considerable distance between the violence and myself. My attention to the naval battle was diverted by a sharp cry from the docks. Kethoth

s assailant had struck a bloody blow against him. Kethoth responded with all the power of his black arts, calling forth every demon at his disposal and interposing them between his attacker and himself. Hoping to aid the wounded conjurer I summoned forth a figment of a fearsome monster and set it upon our foe. Unfazed, the villain continued in his single-minded pursuit of his objective. As if aware that his prey

s demonic minions were finite in their time on Toril the assassin withdrew from the combat, vanishing into thin air. About this time Kethoth must have noticed that The Wastrel was heading away from Calimport with the flying fortress quick at its heels, for he ran down the docks towards his retreating home. His fiendish menagerie pursued the invisible assassin to the best of their abilities, but he was too skilled and fast for them. I completely lost our foe until Kethoth alerted me to his presence approaching me. Reaching into the plane of shadow, I formed some shadow-substance into a humanoid shape and veiled it with the likeness of a bloodthirsty ape. I set the ape directly in the path of our fleeing opponent and it set upon him, delaying him until the fury of Kethoth

s pets might catch him in its toothy maw. Knowing that to stay another instant would mean facing still more fiendish and shadowy foes, our silent attacker chose the better part of valour, and bolted for the pier. For all his skill and speed he was not fast enough, and the hounds of Kethoth dragged his soul back down with them to the infernal planes from whence they came.

Alone on the dock at last, with no sign of The Wastrel in sight, Kethoth and I examined our foe in hopes of gaining some insight into his motivations. I recognized the hue of his skin as akin to those folk from Anauroch I had met on my travels. Other than that there was little we could discern. After quickly sketching his likeness we abandoned the would-be assassin and returned to the safety of my villa. The two of us agreed to discover the fate of The Wastrel in the morning, and over some wine he told me of his travels of late. Kethoth was hiding his worries for his friends under the pretence of fear for his material possessions aboard our ship, but I could tell that his sleep that night was restless for reasons other than concern for his favourite clothes.

Chadrazzan and I agreed that immediate contact with our companions was of the utmost urgency. I suggested the Thayan method for contacting friends afar using a scrying mirror and, lacking such a device, the two of us proceeded to the Mages Guild at once, despite my usual distaste for such places.  It is my firm belief that it is the rigidity of arcane study that leads to the pointless pomp and circumstance of mage interrelations. All that formulae memorization, endless repetition of rote spell-casting, and the labelling and parcelling out of art enforces a certain structure upon those involved. Much as a pickled cucumber up one

s behind enforces a certain structure on a humanoid. It is really quite similar. Anyways, after much hoity-toity, how-do-you-do

s we are able to ascertain that The Wastrel was safe, although still pursued by the island fortress. My message is relayed to Maevri, and the two of us agree to meet our companions in Baldur

s Gate. Chadrazzan and I agree that we should investigate Westgate first, as the harpers seemed to believe that there is information there that is of import to the both of us. May
s contact suggested that we contact
the free thinker
upon arrival there, and we set out to do so at once.
