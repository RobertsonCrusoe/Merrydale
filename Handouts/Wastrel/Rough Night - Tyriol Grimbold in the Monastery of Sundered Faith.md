Steele.

Leirran.

Grennirath? 

No, Grimbold.

Tyriol rubbed his face, tried to shake the soft, luring sensation of tiredness from his mind. 

Reading was not his forte. Most literatary pursuits were beneath his interest. He leafed through the pages, frowned at the chafing sensation of dry parchment against his fingertips. Some of the words escaped him, but these documents were clearly about his father. The Sphinx. Leader of the Mercykillers. How was that possible?

Tyriol slipped the goggles from his forehead and briefly rubbed the spot where they were. The things were not made for his eyes, he had concluded long ago. A flaw in the angle, perhaps a minor imperfection with the lenses. Whatever it was, his eyes were always sore after using them. Granted, he saw everything sharper through the things, but without them, everything seemed so blurry in contrast. He blinked away the fuzziness and continued squinting at the pages.

Tremalkin.

Assumed name? Obviously.

But what did that mean? Were they all related? They must have known eachother. Decades ago. So long. Tyriol's mind wandered to the stories of his youth. Of his great-great-great grandfather, Edgar Grimbold, and the adventures he had gotten himself into. The ranger couldn't recall any mention of his father or the paladin of Shar, but the connection between these names couldn't possibly be coincidental.

His head hurt. He rested his head on the table, tried to gather his thoughs.

\"What are you doing here, boy?\" 

Tyriol nearly jumped out of his seat. The voice came from the shadows, just out of range of his lantern.

It was in no way unfamiliar to Tyriol. Kethoth.

Kethoth? Wait. 

\"You made mention of something before, Sepret. I would ask you something.\" Tyriol said quietly, taking his eyes off the papers before him to stare at the wizard. He had not left the shadows, with only his glittering eyes visible. Typically dramatic of him.

\"I said, what are you doing here, ranger.\" Kethoth repeated. Annoyance twinged in Tyriol's gut.

\"Reading.\" He said in a level voice.

\"Not in this room.\" Kethoth's tone was as unctuous as always, almost verging on a purr. \"I meant here, with the rest of us.\"

Tyriol straightened in his chair and replaced the parchment carefully. What was going on?

\"I don't understand. What do you mean.\"

Kethoth smiled. The light danced off his teeth, leaving a disembodied face grinning out of the shadows. The effect was... disconcerting.

\"What I mean is, why are you still here with us? You are a guide, an escort, correct? You know the region, but where we are going, well... you have no further knowledge to be of use to myself nor the rest of us. So I submit this query - why are you here?\"

Tyriol was at a momentary loss for words, but he caught himself quickly. Pushing his chair back, the ranger stood, fingers resting on the table, trembling with anger that he was hesitant to show.

\"I... I don't... you know why I'm here. What is this? What game is this, Kethoth?\"

\"Game?\" The wizard asked. Tyriol wanted to cut the smug smile right off his face.

\"So what is this. Are we done, then? Is that what you're trying to say?\"

Kethoth merely laughed, and Tyriol found himelf reeling, landing hard against the floor, rolling onto his back. Pain exploded into his skull, and he was no longer where he was.

A dark sky dragged itself overhead, rain pouring down like so many tears. Or perhaps just rain, Tyriol corrected himself - no need for dramatics. Pain lanced through his arms and legs, and the ranger was suddenly aware of the clamour all around him. Screams, the clash of weapons, the all-too-familiar snap of magic and the smell of burnt air. His mind was reeling.

Suddenly, a figure loomed overhead. A dragonborn, red scales smeared with blood and dirt, a cruel looking blade gripped tight in his hands. With a snarl and a swing, the sword angled downwards, to fast to avoid. The split second before he died, Tyriol understood where he was and what was happening. That black and blue armour, the ruined stone around them. He closed his eyes, and heard a ringing connection that caused his whole body to flinch.

And then a hand had him by the collar, lifting him to his feet despite the ache in his limbs. A voice, familiar, but not heard of in a long time.

\"Find your backbone, half-elf. This is a battle!\"

The imposing figure before him had him by the neck. Baird of Kelemvor stared down at Tyriol, and clapped him hard on the shoulder. Tyriol winced, and looked again. 

An arrow had sprouted from the side of Baird's head. 

The rain trickling down the man's plated arm had turned to blood, running down Tyriol's cloak and pooling at his feet. The paladin leaned in close, inches from Tyriol's horrified face, just as a massive sword punched through Baird's chest.

\"Why are you here?\" Baird of Kelemvor hissed between clenched teeth.

Tyriol took a terrified step backwards, stumbled, and fell. 

Raindrops fell down like tears, scattered on his face, and then everything was black.

But he could still see. He twisted and turned and screamed until his throat was hoarse, but nothing would stop his descent. Tyriol was falling, from nothing towards nothing. He looked down, or where he thought down was, and saw a reflection, a face just as crazed and horrified as his own. 

Tyriol felt a snag. His cloak caught on an outcropping of rock, reefing hard at his throat, almost snapping his neck in the process. The ranger dangled there, helpless, coughing and kicking until hands began pulling him upwards, up to safety, away from that face that slowly shrunk into the darkness.

He felt flat stone beneath him and turned around on hands and knees to face his rescuer. Ender stared him in the eyes and smiled, that sarcastic smirk that was always plastered on his round face. Out of breath, panting and shaking, Tyriol looked past the halfling and felt his stomach fold into knots.

Barthas and his demon, battling against his companions in the dim of the cave. His attention returned to Ender, to thank him, but the rogue spoke first, his voice dripping with malice.

\"Useless. Why are you even here?\"

Tyriol tried to muster a reply.

\"I don't understand, I'm here to help!\"

The halfling shook his head and sighed.

\"Tsk. Like I said. Useless.\"

The kick caught him unprepared. He cried out, reached, fingers brushing the halfling's tunic briefly before the blackness erupted around Tyriol once again. Legs kicking, hard and fast, he wasn't aware of the transition, when the shadows pulled back to reveal mortar and stone and wooden stairs, but he was running, panting, gasping for air, ripping at his clothing as a curtain of burning liquid descended upon him, eating through skin and muscle and bone.

Tyriol was melting from the outside in, and all he could think to do was run. Heels connected, legs tangled, and then he was rolling down the stairs, head over heels at a break-neck pace. After what seemed like an eternity of cracks, collisions and hard landings, the ranger landed face first on the cool stone. It didn't burn anymore, and the ground felt so nice, so cold, he couldn't bring himself to move.

A soft voice spoke above him. A woman's. Or women's. It seemed a chorus almost, disconnected and strong at the same time.

\"Why are you here?\"

Tyriol looked up. The ghost of Lady Saharel gazed down at him, eyes pale and unfocued.

\"I don't... understand... what this is supposed to be. Not... no sense. Makes no sense.\" He muttered in a daze. The ranger propped himself up with his arms and looked down. Red, blistered flesh. Clear liquid wept from the wounds. Tyriol shook his head, trying to regain his grasp on whatever situation it was he had found himself in.

\"Why are you here, Tyriol?\"

The voice had changed. He returned his gaze to the ghost, only to find his mother crouched down in front of him. Beneath the bandages that covered her body, she smiled, cupping his face in two blistered hands just as tattered as his own.

Tyriol stared at Maeghuen, mouth agape, scrambling for words. Something to say. Anything. But he knew. Did he? He couldn't make up his mind.

\"I need to know.\"

Her laugh made his heart skip a beat. \"Know what, my love?\"

\"...\" 

\"Him.\"

She giggled again. So uncharacteristic of her. Mother was always so hard.

\"I told you to stay away from him, Tyriol.\"

\"I know, but... I just need to talk to him. To know why.\"

Something changed. He frowned, a terrible feeling growing in his gut. His mother wasn't smiling anymore. The opposite, in fact. Her mouth opened, but no sound came out. Tyriol reached up, on his knees, and brushed his hands against Maeghuen's face.

She screamed. Loud and long, as her skin began to burn. Melting. Bandages fell away, bubbling flesh escaped her bones and by the end of it, all that remained of her was a skeleton that Tyriol cradled in his arms. He was horrified, but the expression on his face remained flat and numb. He couldn't muster the strength to cry.

Footsteps. Behind him.

Tyriol looked up, his mouth pressed into a thin line. A bald elf, tall for their kind, face and pate twisted with scars. He slowly made his way towards the ranger and paused a few feet away. His face betrayed no emotion or hint as to what he was thinking.

\"Why are you here, boy?\" For a moment, the tone and voice, it sounded so much like Kethoth. Tyriol just stared, and finally managed a single blink.

The figure merely crossed his arms, and spoke once again.

\"Dragons. Demons. Vampires. You and your companions have endured quite a bit. They have their causes, their reasons, and so they must. They have purpose. You, however,\" The elf leveled a finger at Tyriol,\"do not.\"

\"I... just... \" Tyriol managed to mumble. That one simple gesture had stripped Tyriol of all his remaining coherence and resolve.

\"Just wanted to meet your father, hmm? The Sphinx, leader of the Mercykillers. Heartless. Possibly insane.\" He leaned in close. Tyriol couldn't even begin counting the mess of scars that criss-crossed the man's face.

\"Just wanted to get to know him, is that it? Maybe you two could get together and work out those abandonment issues of yours! Maybe over tankards of ale, and then after that the two of you could play some kings and killers or go fishing, eh? Perhaps you could even get mama and papa back together, be a real family. Wouldn't that be quaint. I'm sure he could fit all of that in between artifact-mongering and running a guild of cold-blooded murderers.\"

Tyriol couldn't speak. He just stared at the ground. His heart was pounding so fast, it hurt. His chest felt like it was going to explode. Nausea was building in his gut. He wanted to vomit, to get rid of all the aches and pain.

\"I think... it's time for you to grow up, lad.\" He heard knees pop as the figure crouched down over him. Tyriol could feel hot breath against the back of his head. He closed his eyes, clutched the bones tighter.

\"People's lives are at stake, after all.\"

Tyriol jolted upwards in a frenzy, flung his arms aside and grasped at the object plastered to his face. Ripping it away, he saw, amidst the dots swimming in his blurry eyes, the parchment of paper he had been reading. Tyriol rubbed his tired face and gave the room he was in a cursory inspection before setting the paper down on the table before him. The library. The ranger looked at the doorway, half-expecting, half-dreading Kethoth to appear.

He sighed and rested his face in his hands.

\"What am I even doing here?\"
