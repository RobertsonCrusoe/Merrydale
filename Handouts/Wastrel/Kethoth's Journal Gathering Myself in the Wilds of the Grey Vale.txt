==Ches, 1485==

The last few months have been a time of discovery for this devil. I am pleased that there seems to be no sign of my madness recurring, and I have put together a good many things about how I have spent the last eight or ten years, though not how I came to the Gray Vale in the first place.

I emerged from a self-made charnel house a few months ago in the foothills of the Greypeak Mountains, just on the eastern edge of the Southwood. Emerging, I had a fantastic view of the wood below, and the cold and fast-running Greyflow river beyond. In the remote distance to the northwest, I could see the Star Mountains, and though all about me was a vista of no small rustic charm, the Star Mountains are inspiring and majestic. My camp was perched high in the foothills of the Greypeaks, which rise to the west, cold, bleak and indifferent.

The camp I was in had a couple more hovels and evidence of the rough-living of a few dozen creatures of mean habit; the filthy common area was littered with detritus of all unmentionable varieties, and a couple crude cages made of beech boughs held some frozen captives who had not survived the inclemency of some previous night. I was aware almost immediately that creatures were fleeing from the camp, no doubt from my burning rage, and as one crude door opened in another ramshackle hut, I unleashed another gout of flame into the door, leaving one bipedal corpse burning in the doorway, and sending a few more back inside to hide in terror, until the flames and smoke consumed them too. I was in a rage just then, and I brought flame down upon every building, and every significant pile of supplies in the camp. By the time I was finished, I was standing in the midst of a decent inferno, and smiling broadly at the indefatigueable spell fire which I was able to master.

This was when I first realized I had changed. I had employed flame and other simple evocations in my earlier career as a Master Summoner, but never was I able to wield it in such considerable volumes or for such a sustained period of time. Magic has changed, for this devil at any rate, and I am sure that there are academic mages in the learned institutions of the Great Cities that remain, that are spending every waking hour studying the changes, and likely wasting a good bit of time and ink with theories and such. For Kethoth, it is enough to know that Art has changed, but that I still have command of it. I have much again to learn, but it is coming to me quickly already.

To return to my fiery awakening into the world, I should say that I came to my senses after a little while and a lot of flame. I regretted nearly immediately the torching of everything around me when I realized how cold it was outside, and how distant I must be from any warm and habitable place. However, something of the maddened bandit that I had been took over in an instant, and I soon found that I was cloaked in malodourous but warm and serviceable furs from head to toe, and had a variety of things stuffed into a large leather bag that would help me get from one place to the other in this unforgiving wilderness.

Now, I have spent countless nights under the stars on my adventures with the crew of the Wastrel; I am sure I watched Brenan start a hundred fires, and seen Cedric turn every variety of land-going creature into a delicious feast. However, always I was at mentionable discomfort whilst out-of-doors, and though my companions relished any chance to hike up a forested mountain trail in any sort of inclemency, I only every grudgingly followed, and then only if there was a hope of plunder at the end. These days, however, I can tell you the names of every plant and tree that I pass, and to sleep on the ground with nothing but a skin and my bag for bedding is no discomfort to me. I have changed.

These changes and newly-acquired wilderness skills suggested in the back of my mind that it was time to move. The inferno that I had caused would attract attention, and though I half considered hiding in ambush to kill any curious investigators, I did take my leave of the charred remains of my recent, half-remembered life. 

I headed instinctively uphill at that point, keeping off of ridges to stay hidden, and being careful not to leave tracks in conspicuous places. I should clarify, for my own humble benefit if nothing else, that I am no Ranger or professional wilderness tamer of any sort. I am however, capable now of surviving in some mean fashion; I made small warm fires, had plenty of fresh game and some roots and things to eat, and was, for a time, relatively comfortable. I was living in conflict with myself, however; my new wilderness skills suggested hiding in the mountains, while the old Noble-from-Unther craved civilized contact. In the end, after a couple weeks of skulking around in the foot hills, I made my way down into the Valley, towards the river and the road that I knew were the conduits of civilization, in whatever fashion it has in these parts and times.

I made my way indirectly towards the hamlet of [[Zelbross]]. I wanted to head directly towards Loudwater right away, but I thought that after all my villainy in the wilderness, that I had best collect some intelligence first. And I’m glad I did.

I decided it might be best to take advantage of my nocturnal proclivities, and so entered the small settlement late at night. It was warm with the melt, but the moon was shrouded in cloud, which suited my purposes. While there were two men walking the perimeter of the town with torches and what I think was a nearly-empty flask of spirits, they were easily avoided, and I managed to get readily to the village square, where I looked over the various printed news, invitations, weather predictions, and warnings. A weather-beaten broadsheet advertised a generous reward for the capture of the bandit ‘Kossur’, and I must have gone the colour of snow when I read my name. Never in all my wanderings in the many corners of the Faerun did I ever even whisper that name, for fear of the many people hunting after the Son of Kossur who had sewn such wickedness back in Unther. Ever did I go by ‘Kethoth Sepret, Scholar of Skuld, Plunderer of the Ruavin’, and a few other misdirecting titles, but not once did I mention my father’s name. However, on that warm spring evening, standing in the muddy square of an insignificant bywater on the East Road, there it was in bold typeface, tacked to a busy message board right next to an advertisement for the following week’s bake sale.

Fortunately, the likeness of me on the picture was crude. It had the colour of my skin pretty much right, but it included enviably long fangs, and horns that not even my father had. I had a sense that I might be able to evade this infamy if I could only package myself a little neater. 

I took the time to learn something of myself from the broadsheet, which claimed that I had been a bandit of constant engagement from Daggerford to Llorkh for nearly 20 years! The list of my transgressions was topped by cavorting with goblinoids, and ‘perpetuating the forceful breeding of half-bloods with the vilest sort of monsters that go on two legs’. Well, that does rather resemble something I might get involved in, and so I decided most of the charges were probably true. They included murders by the score, countless abductions, thefts, beatings, rapes, and every other sort of violent criminal endeavour, all the while carried out with a host of the ‘vilest sort of monsters that go on two legs.’ 

To get back to my packaging and countenance, I knew I had to have a change and double quick. I had every intention of heading back East, towards Loudwater; a large town I had been to over 100 years before, and which I remembered to be something of a semi-precious jewel in an otherwise barbaric wilderness. However, I wanted to go in better fashion that I could currently present, and so I crept about the village until I found an inn that was seasonably busy. There were a few small caravans encamped about the place, with many of the poorer sort who accompany them surrounding a few anemic fires situated in the dry islands amidst the boundless muck of the settlement. They carried on cheerfully enough, though; the night was warm and they must have been in drink. Any one of them would have been easy prey for Kethoth, but I knew that if I was to go about in a presentable manner, that I would have to find the important men of the caravans, who would no doubt be inside the Inn, resting in a dry bed, perhaps with a local girl.

Here was the trick, though; I wanted to head east, which meant that I had to find someone who was heading west. It would not do to rob or kill some bastard, take his clothes, and then be mistaken for him by his travelling companions at the next watering hole on the road. I crept about the wagons and pack animals for a while, and found one caravan that was carrying copper ingots from a mining company up Llorkh-way, and decided that his coster, the ‘Abbotswine Company’, was the one to exploit. 

I could have asked about the camps outside for the location of the caravan master for Abbotswine, but I didn’t think that would do; there was no need to have my memorable face asking questions about a man who was about to have his fate changed. I decided instead to prowl about the windows of the inn, and gather what intelligence I could. I knew I could make a hasty and violent escape into the night if I was discovered, and be none the poorer for it. But by taking my time, and watching every person I could through every window that offered the opportunity, I was able to espy one very well-fed man who was groping at the local girls, trying to keep the squirming things on his lap, though all got off quickly, and to his amusement, and this man eventually boasted loud enough for me to hear, that he was headed to Waterdeep with a fortune in copper. I watched the man drink his fill, grope his fill, then stagger upstairs to his bed chambre; I had to crouch and crane my neck at the remotest corner of the grimy pane of glass through which I was spying in order to follow the man’s progress to his room, but I marked it well, and made my way quickly around the other side of the building, beneath his window. 

The drunk letch’s window was on the second floor, but I was able to quickly find a rainwater barrel to stack on top of some crates, and thus pyramid my way up to my prize. I guess I must have made a bit more noise than I wanted, because I was soon set upon by the two drunken fair-weather guards whom I had so easily avoided earlier on. They had the poor sense to try to sneak up on me and attempt to bag me themselves, rather than just sounding the alarm, which would have sent me scurrying away into the wilderness again, and this was their demise. For Kethoth unleashed the most powerful magic at his disposal just then, and put them both to sleep as they sprung like a pair of lummoxes towards me. There was nothing for it but to slit their throats, and carry out the rest of my business expeditiously. I mounted the barrel, pried open the window of my drunken quarry, and entered his room. As I was crossing the room, a guard in the corner stirred to wakefulness, and half-stood from his chair, paralyzed with either fear or surprise, and this was his undoing as well. I sunk an arcane blast between his eyes, which had the unwanted effect of rousing his drunken charge, who flailed about in bed awkwardly, trying to gain his bearings. I paralyzed the man with a bolt of negative energy, and though it didn’t kill him outright, it gave the advantage I needed over him. I pounced on the man, and held my bloodied knife to his doughy throat, and promised him he would live if he only answered a few easy questions.

I should say at this point that I have gained some mastery over my tongue, which in my glory days I used most carelessly. I have somehow learned to hold my thoughts, and to listen to others before speaking, and I have learned how to sling falsehoods like I used to sling curses and oaths. And so I lied to this corpulent trader, promising his life, though I knew he must perish. I found out that he was indeed the owner of the company headed west, and that he had already been to Loudwater. In fact, he told me exactly what he had worn in Loudwater, and those clothes I later threw into the small fire in the room. First, though, I slit the man’s throat from ear to ear, and watched as his face contorted through a series of emotions; first disbelief, then rage, and finally a pathetic sort of pleading. 

To sum up this already lengthy account of my first real challenge as a Wizard in the 15th Century, I conjured up a Tenser’s below the window (I would praise Thoth himself if I thought he could hear me, for the preservation of this most useful evocation), threw the two bodies out the window upon it, and stole out with all the wealth and useful possessions and clothing that the merchant had. I had summoned up a particularly powerful disc, and was able to throw my first two victims of the night on top of the pile, and the five of us made our way into the wilderness.

The bandit ‘Kossur’ had struck for the last time.
