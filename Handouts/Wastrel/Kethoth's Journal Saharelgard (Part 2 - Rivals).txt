==13 Mirtul==

Today was eventful enough.

It started with a very agreeable breakfast in the taproom of the Monastery, where I convened my henchmen for a discussion of strategy and such. We compared notes and speculated as to the best plan of approach for the day’s sojourn into the ruins. In the end, we pretty much just agreed to reconnoitre the area, and see what’s what.

As we headed into the ruins, we noticed a number of haphazard structures resembling poorly and hastily constructed archways, and in fact we encountered a group of men trying to build just such a structure, and when we approached them to make inquiries as to the nature of their amateur masonry, we were indelicately rebuked. Anyway, we have put together to the fact that many who have come seeking the Lady are attempting to summon her by way of a portal-like-structure built from the bricks and blocks of her ruins. Kethoth finds this amusing, and will not endorse our party undertaking such an endeavour. I suspect that those who are attempting such construction have not had access to Kuryons analects, and I am not about to share mine.

The ruins are not too vast, to tell the truth, and there has been so much activity of late that the place reminds me of the streets of Waterdeep after the Greengrass Festival; there is detritus of all kinds in the streets, hinting at the activities of the previous night. Here, the stones are marked with blood in many spots, and there are smashed weapons, discarded bandages and potion bottles, and all other forms of adventuring cast-off that take a good deal of the mystique out of the ruins. It is rather like the entrance to Undermountain (if it still exists) at the Yawning Portal Inn, where dungeon delving has become a spectator sport.

At any rate, there are some obstacles to overcome, and at least one clear objective. Though most of the ruins are just that; hopelessly demolished ruins, there are some walls left, one partial tower, and one full tower, which we have been told is called the Sceptre Tower. The Sceptre tower is held fast against approach by all accounts, and we did not test its defenses. There does not seem to be an easy way into it at this point.

At various corners of the ruins are the companies of adventurers that choose to take their pilgrimage here on the cheap, staying outside the expensive lodging of the Monastery. These people keep mostly to themselves, and indeed, if what I watched last night is any indication, they are likely nursing wounds and recovering, waiting for the violence that dark will bring.

I have decided that our objective lies inside the Sceptre Tower, and as we cannot head directly into it, we must head down and find a passage up. I don’t doubt that we will find such a passage, as the Netherese favoured such warrens beneath their cities. It remains to be seen how such a passage might be held against us, but we shall endeavour to find out.

While meandering about the ruins, looking for a way down, we were set upon by a group of large spidery looking monsters that Tyriol says are called Krutchik. “How do you spell such a rare taxonomy, my good ranger” asked I. He could not answer, and I suspected he might be trying to figure out how to spell ‘taxonomy’ in his head. At any rate, the Krutchik proved capable adversaries that nearly had one halfling for their lunch, but between Tyriol and I, we managed to pull his small hide out of the dinner pot. I am forced to wonder tonight if I did the right thing in blasting those creatures from about him.

It took us great effort to dispatch the creatures, but we decided to press on a bit. We found a slimy hole leading into the belly of the ruins, from which the Krutchik seeming issue forth. Many feet below, we espied some of them skittering about in what appears to be their lair, and the more-squeamish of my cadre decided it looked too ‘yucky’ to go down. I relented in my decision, in no small part because Arya looked a little put off by the business. 

At any rate, there was clearly another point of interest in the ruins that merited investigation. The other tower, heretofore referred to as the Broken Tower, is in good enough condition that we figured there would be a good chance of finding a passage between it and the Sceptre Tower.

We approached the Broken Tower with caution, and rightly so. We did indeed find a passage heading down, and it had clearly been used recently. Down below we found a long, circular passage that ringed a large chamber in which we heard some voices. We found two doors into the chamber, and decided to storm it simultaneously from both sides. Well, this turned into something of a chest beating contest which we shamefully backed down from. I say ‘we’, but really I mean the rest of my hirelings, the dour lizard excepted.

Inside that room, we found four out of five of the Waterdhavians busy at extracting, of all things, an alabaster pillar, from a pile of rubble. I won’t go into the significance of seeing yet another of these ubiquitous pillars just yet. Rather, I will cast some character indictments of my hirelings while my blood is hot. 

As we opened the two doors, and each group of us got partway through our respective doors, we were met with the calm and confident demeanour of the Waterdhavians, who met our intrusion as something of an annoyance, and little more. They are a tough-looking lot, to be sure, and two of them are dragonborn like Luc, but they don’t realize that they have made an enemy of a Son of Set.

Their leader, who would not give me the decent and small honour of his name, insisted that if we didn’t leave immediately, that they would ‘deal with us’, which we all took to mean that they would round us up, have the two dragons flash-fry us with their fiery breath, and serve us up warm. I was not shaken by the subtle threat, and in fact attempted to employ tact and diplomacy, asking if there was not some common goal that we could work at together. They would have none of this, and as the Aglarondans, the thief and the half-pup recoiled from the doors and were halfway up the steps, Luc strode into the room! 

Well, this devil was pleased as you may. I thought that he was striding headlong into a battle of his choosing, and I was overjoyed for it. This was the time to take them, when they are at some labour, and not as prepared for battle as we clearly were (or were supposed to be, rather.) However, Luc was just making his way across the room towards the rest of us, choosing to walk through the midst of our foes, rather than skulk around through the circular passage that had delivered him to the rear of our foes in the first place. I was proud of the lizard just then, and for the first time I truly admired him, if I didn’t exactly like him. At the very least, he got close enough to all of them to get a good look at them and gather some intelligence, the clever bastard, and he also asserted that he would not be intimidated by them, though the rest of his colleagues had.

We left the four of those villains in that chamber, and headed up the stairs. It was tormenting me tremendously to do so. Behind us were the adversaries that had previously been a complete nuisance to us, and had just escalated into hostile and threatening enemies. Only Kethoth saw things thusly, though, as I would soon learn.

At the top of the steps, heading back into the cracked-open ruin of the Broken Tower, I took a good look around for number five, the scout that had so terrified my own two scouts a couple days ago. I knew he would be watching, and he was. My blood was boiling, and I could hear Set in the bounding rhythm of my pulse. A very long and complex calculation ran through the mind of the Kossurian just then, and I will try to summarize it here, for my own defense at least;

The five adversaries that we have been following since we left Loudwater have indeed showed up at our very destination, and have, by no coincidence whatsoever, but by some design unknown to me, uncovered a Netherese alabaster pillar similar to our own. We know that these items play a significant role with the Lady Saharel, likely as tools for her enslavement, or perhaps even her destruction (not that I care.) When we offered to ally ourselves with them, we were met with much more than a friendly rebuke, and were, in fact, overtly threatened to stay away. In this endeavour, they perceive that they can have no allies, which places associates us rather contrawise to them. That they are here seeking these pillars, and that we are in possession of just such an artifact, means that we are at odds, and given that they have threatened us, now on more than one occasion, I surmise that we are on a course leading to violence. 

With it established that these are our enemies, and finding one of them alone, offering us the best chance we are going to get to ambush them, notwithstanding the marvellous opportunity we just passed up to get the drop on the other four, this was indeed the time to act. The enemy scout and I made eye contact before anyone else saw him, and he started to slink away, but I shouted to him, and implored him to come out so that we might talk. 

“Come out, my good man! We are fellow travelers in a dangerous place! Perhaps we can be of some assistance to each other!” I was saying. I was being polite and proper, and I made a decent enough show of it that the man crept back to the edge of the ruined balcony above us, but I think Baird knew what I was on about. I suppose I was a bit anxious; Sets Blood throbbed in my veins, and it was veritably speaking to me. I had endured quite enough of having my cadre bullied about by these men, and I wanted to tear that scout apart and drink his blood. Sets Blood was veritably singing in my veins, a deep, low song of murder, and I think it showed in my tone enough that Baird figured it out.

“What are you doing, Ketho…” he was saying as I pointed a finger at the scout and growled a low growl and I think I might have hissed at him too. My eyes flared and the blood surged through me so that I thought I might black out. The scout howled in agony as Sets will rampaged in his mind. I don’t know exactly what happened to him after that, because I was immediately set upon by Baird, who was now yelling at me to stop. I should say that the paladin made only a half-hearted effort to stop me, which is well enough; there is no way I could free myself from his iron clutches if he held any conviction for his actions and was determined to restrain me. But his efforts were enough; the scout made off during my distraction, and my ears are still hot from the rage of being man handled by that confounded paladin.

An old colleague of mine used to say never to bring along a fool-knight, which is what he called all paladins. I, however, have often gone with the ancient wisdom that one keeps their enemies close, especially when they have no friends. But tonight I am rethinking this. I could have murdered Baird on the spot. For a moment I considered putting my entire retinue to sleep, cutting off Baird’s head, and blaming the scout and his colleagues. This would have served the double purpose of getting rid of one paladin, and steeling Mattrick’s resolve to eliminate the Waterdhavians, if only I could convince the lot of them that it was the scout and not I who did for the paladin, and I am sure I could have pulled it off. However, I let it pass, and I’m glad I did. I have need of Baird yet, but we shall have to have a talk about priorities, objectives and such in the future.

But let me continue with the scout and the Waterdhavians. After Baird and I stepped back from one another, and the scout was out of sight for good, I decided it was best to get going immediately. We had blown our initiative, again, and it was time to put some distance between us and my quarry. We could not face them at that moment, divided as we were, and so we hoofed it back to the Monastery. As we parted, we heard their leader shout after us the words “the next time I see you, you are all dead!” That about seals it, I guess.

On our way back to the Monastery, I think for the first time on this journey, I was truly enraged, and said not a thing until long after we had returned. As I sit here writing this I can understand how Baird and some of the others might have interpreted my actions as rash and probably a bit out of character, but they lack the wits of a Son of Set, and did not calculate things out such as I have. Now there is no doubt about our relationship with the Waterdhavians; we are sworn enemies now, and when next we meet there will be blood, and I can only hope none of it is mine.
