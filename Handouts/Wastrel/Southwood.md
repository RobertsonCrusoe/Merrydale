\"Wot'cher mouf, cretin... I 'eard sumfin.\"

The silence buzzed with tension. 

Loeber, until recently picking wax from his scruffy ears, quirked his head in obvious confusion.

\"Eh, speak up sum?\" The bugbear drawled.

The statement preceeded a muffled crack that echoed through the trees. Melancholy lowing followed.

Frosted air pouring from his nostrils like a grumpy dragon's smoke, Grikh trembled before his ignorant comrade. Trembled with rage. Disdainful of the significant difference in size between them, he glared at Loeber, a rather imposing bugbear draped in tattered leather armour two sizes too small. The goblin's short temper had gotten the best of him again. Winding up another bony fist, the goblin jabbed his furry minion straight in the gut. 

Loeber slumped to his knees, hands still pressed against his bloody nose, clearly on the verge of tears. Taking advantage of the bugbear's temporary incapacitation, Grikh shuffled up to one of the trees surrounding them and wagged one ear from side to side.

Nothing. 

He was certain he had heard something, though. With a grimace and a spit, the goblin sighed and returned his attention to Loeber, who was busy rubbing fistfuls of leaves against the blood that continued streaming from his face. The snot and tears, pure theatrics, made it difficult for Grikh not to pity the lump.

\"Awwww, now, don'cha weep, big baby. I 'ardly touched you!\" Grikh cooed, his voice carrying a slight tone of frustration. He went up beside his comrade, the top of his head barely clearing the bugbear's thick shoulders, and gently began rubbing his back.

Mustering his words, Loeber hiccupped loudly and spoke.

\"My nose, it's all done and wrecked!\"

\"Wut.\"

Grikh leered at his companion. 

\"I 'ardly see anything wrong! Yer crying over nuffin'! Yer supposed to be da muscle, and 'ere yer busy bawlin' over a sore snout!\" The goblin paused a few, and then shrugged. \"Dere's not even dat much blood, see? It's mostly yer drippins.\"

\"It ain't 'cause it hurt, awright?\"

\"Fffft,\" Grihk hissed with obvious disdain.

\"You've gone an' damaged me feelings, is wot it is!\"

Grikh moaned aloud, kneading his forehead with a thumb and forefinger. He was always shanghaied into guard duty with this insufferable lout. Always asking stupid questions, always eating everything in sight, always never not...not following simple orders. Grikh would be mentioning this to the captain. They were scouts after all - integral to the safety of the clan. No one else wanted to drag their lazy feet up and down the length of Southwood, searching for humans and elves and all the other dirty long-legged freaks. It was a duty that Grikh took serious-like, and he was not at all pleased with being teamed up with this failure of a bugbear for the duration of his shift, time and time again. He shot a passing glance at Loeber beside him. The shaggy creature had begun hiccuping in his despair, and was now trying to wipe some thick spittle off of his chest with several leaves, already covered in snot themselves. The result was an embarassing mess, which Loeber failed to notice.

With a deep sigh, Grikh focused again on locating the sound that had caught his attention earlier. He scanned the trees from his position behind Loeber's shoulders. A faint rustle once again reached his ears. He whispered sharply to the bugbear.

\"Hush ya git, dere's a skulker about.\" 

Grikh directed his meatshield's attention towards a small clump of trees several meters distant. A small portion of green cloth was visible, caught up on a branch. Loeber nodded, brushed his bloody paws off on the dirt, and picked up a grim looking morningstar twice as long as Grikh was tall. The goblin reached for his own knife, and smiled for the first time in days.

<nowiki>* * *</nowiki>

Stealthily as a bugbear is able, Loeber crept towards the bundle of cloth hanging from the tree. Excitement and terror churned in his gut, shooting through his body like wildfire. He looked over his shoulder at Grikh. The goblin was pressed against a nearby tree, fingering a dagger as sharp and wicked as his own toothy grin. He nodded, and Loeber paused for a moment to muster his courage, close his eyes, and then, with a loud snarl, sidestepped the trees and swung hard, burying his morningstar deep into the trunk of the tree.

He opened one eye, then both.

Loeber was... perplexed. A forest green cloak, now partially obscured by the girth of his weapon, lay unattended and tangled in the branches of the tree. Something shiny pulled at his attention. A clasp or buckle or something like that. It was leaf shaped, and familiar. Where had he seen a leaf-shaped buckle before? In a forest, no less. At least it belonged somewhat, what with the other leaves hanging around... 

His attention was refocused by a faint whistle, soon followed by a loud 'thunk' and a wet gurgle. Loeber made a double-take - Grikh had finished testing the sharpness of his dagger, laying discarded hilt-up in the dirt near his kicking feet, and was now pinned to the tree by means of an arrow through his throat. Eyes bulging, the goblin croaked his final message to his long-time companion.

\"You idiiiiioooouuuhh...\"

And then Grikh was dead. 

His friend. Best friend.

\"Boss! Who! Where!?\"

Loeber began to whimper as panic set in. He turned to pull his weapon free, frantic tugs slipping his sweaty hands, and caught sight of a figure adjacent to him. Bow drawn with an arrow mere inches from the bugbear's head. Startled, adrenaline pumping, he finally wrenched the morningstar free and caught the ambusher in the torso halfway through the swing, sending him sprawling to the ground. Rage and terror fuelling his impressive frame, Loeber lifted his weapon overhead, intending to bring it straight down on the prostrate ranger's head.

\"POINTY-EARED WHELP!\" he shouted, heaving the mace in a downward arc at the cringing youth.

Thump, into the dirt, leaves flew upwards and scattered as the ranger managed to roll himself to the side. Loeber growled and heaved his morningstar up and down again and again, pounding holes into the forest floor as the ranger managed his scrambling evasion towards where his bow lay, string tangled in the branches of a tree.

\"QUIT YER SQUIRMIN!\" Loeber bellowed.

Swords sang as they slid out of their sheaths, but Loeber was bigger and stronger. The contact between his wild swing and the two longswords sent them both spinning away. The bugbear heard a satisfying crack, and saw the elf tucked against the tree nursing a wrist with one hand. Their eyes met, and Loeber saw fear. He could taste it, delicious and irony...like blood. So nummy.

Loeber paused to lower his weapon. With one finger, he dabbed the back of his tongue and pulled his hand away. 

Blood?

The bugbear turned in time to see the second arrow punch through his brittle armour and into his ribs. His heart spasmed painfully. 

Oh.

\"Dat's... not fair...\" Loeber tried to say, but all energy left him then. To tired now, too tired to even see. 

Everything faded out of sight.

<nowiki>* * *</nowiki>

The bugbear slumped backwards, landing on Tyriol in an unceremonious heap. 

Already gasping for air, the rancid creature's weight further squeezed the last bit of wind from the half-elf's lungs. He coughed, swallowed spittle and blood, and promptly began to choke. Bitter thoughts raced through his mind as he heaved against the monster with both hands, ignoring the agonized protests from his broken wrists. Squirming and struggling, gagging on fluids, Tyriol could only think about the humilating death he was about to suffer. What would they write on his grave, he wondered grimly as stars appeared before his eyes. 

A slender figure bent over him and heaved against the corpse. The goblinoid rolled off the ranger's chest, and suddenly he could breathe again. She watched him for several moments with a smile on her lips. Finally catching his breath, Tyriol grasped his mother's outstretched hand and allowed himself to be helped up, wincing as he did so.

\"That was messy, my dear.\" She chided. Maeghuen sounded very amused.

Tyriol frowned, not so much from the agonizing sensation of several shattered ribs digging into his lungs, but but rather from the fact that his mother seemed terribly entertained by the whole ordeal he had just suffered. He looked down at the bugbear. Disgusting creature. Tyriol's gaze travelled towards the one he had killed.

\"You have to admit, that was a good shot though.\" He gestured at the goblin that was now decorating the tree before them.

\"Mmmm, if you say so.\"

\"I do say so.\" Tyriol replied, somewhat hurt. His mother took a hold of his tattered cloak. He had left hanging in the tree as a decoy. Lips pursed, eyes slightly narrowed. Tyriol knew that face. She was bothered by her son's feckless disregard for his uniform.

\"You shouldn't have come out this far - I told you your route two days ago, and I expected you to maintain it.\" She tossed the ruined cloak aside.

\"I'm sorry, I supposed I just got bored.\"

\"Bored?\" The tone she used was dangerous. The young ranger knew where this was going.

\"I...well, it was quiet in the northern reach. I was worried something may be happening, so I went a bit off path and ended up here.\"

\"You disobeyed me.\"

\"I took initiative.\"

\"You put yourself in deliberate danger.\"

\"I'm no child, mother. You give me abandoned swaths of forest to watch, and expect me to be happy about it? There's no danger, no risk, no -\"

Tyriol was interrupted by a sharp slap across his face. The pain in his cheek seemed to blur out everything else. He clenched his teeth.

\"No what?\" Maeghuen's voice took on a dangerous edge. She was angry, but his mother's angry was the cold, quiet sort. \"No excitement? No fun? No adventure? This isn't a game, Tyriol.\"

She waited for a reply. He didn't trust himself to speak.

\"What would have happened if I hadn't come? If I had been but a few moments late? Gods, Tyriol, you have no concern for anything but your own desires! You have a duty to Loudwater!\" 

Tyriol looked aside, unable to meet her gaze.

His mother sighed and stepped forward, sliding a pale hand against his cheek. Despite the callouses on her fingertips, it felt smooth and cool against his skin. Soothing, almost. 

\"You have a duty to me. You're the only good thing he ever gave me.\"

Her voice had grown softer with that last bit.

\"...\"

\"I'm sorry.\" Tyriol replied after a moment. It was all he could say.

Apparently, though, it was enough. Maeghuen wrapped her arms around her son and pulled him close. Despite the fact that her head barely reached his chest, he felt tiny in her embrace. So small, like a child again. She was right. The aches in his bones cried out as they stood there, but Tyriol tried to ignore them. He looked up, and squinted against the afternoon sun as it pushed through the canopy. Tiny bands of light illuminated the scene around the two as they stood, capturing the moment. The moment when Tyriol made up his mind. 

So small. This place, this forest, this town. Everything was so... small.

And Tyriol had grown too big for it.
