The harbour in the City of Splendours is a busy place most of the year, but it is never quite as energetic and chaotic as the opening weeks of the trading season. The boats that have been coming into the harbour the last couple of days are covered in ice, but they have arrived, and the season has opened with their arrival after a doubtlessly-perilous journey up the ice-floe-ridden Sea of Swords.
 
This morning, the early Tarsakh sun shines down on a harbour that is noisy, almost maddeningly so. For starters, the birds have come back with the boats. In fact, they always seem to show up a tenday ahead of the bravest mariners. The skies are packed with them, and they fill their air with their noisome protests, arguments and implorations. Following the arrival of the birds is the arrival of the young boys wearing rags and sporting crude sling-shots who are paid a penny for a dozen of the dead birds. Exactly whose soup these winged scavengers show up in is widely guessed at, but not confessed to on any provender in the City.
 
The birds and the boys all must dance around the main actors on this stage; the men who are loading and unloading boats and wagons. There are hundreds of them this morning. Rather, there are some thousands, if one was to survey the docks from one end to the other. These are men of all colours, shapes, and sizes, though there are some common uniforms; the rust-coloured tabards and hats of the cooper’s guild are on the backs and heads of hundred men who are making barrels right on the piers to match the cargoes they will bear. The Fellowship of Salters, Packers, and Joiners is represented by several hundred men and women who are likewise working their trade this morning, adding to the noise with their hammers and saws, and adding to the visual impact of the scene with hundreds of yellow tabards in various stages of repair. Watermen in light blue tabards, gloves and boots pilot small boats full of cargo from ships awaiting a berth. The Fellowship of Carters and Coachmen add dots of deep-blue to the mosaic; their smart hats and pristine coats being of uniform hue. Pink-clad Fishmongers, brown-clad Shipwrights, and black-clad Watch punctuate the busy, colourful crowds. And of course, the ever-present rag-clad guildless men work the worst possible jobs for the worst possible pay.
 
Amid the fast-paced spectacle and deafening noise of all of this, two men stand at the edge of the Fishgut Alley Long Pier. They have the bearing of important men, but even they must give way, to their visible ire, to the activities of the day; stepping out of the way of a pair of Carters who are rolling barrels down the pier, and then dodging the other direction to avoid a hand-wagon being pushed the opposite direction which bears the leavings from the Fishmongers further up the way. When they are allowed to settle into place again, it is plain to see that one man is more irate than the other.
 
The really agitated man is dressed in the finery of a noble, and he conspicuously bears the crest of his family on his brocaded over-coat; he is a Thunderstaff, and although he does not appear to be old enough to be the patriarch, he is most certainly the man who is actually running the family business. His blue eyes are narrow and mean, and dart around missing nothing. His face and his jaw are broad and strong, and beneath the rich garments that he wears, the muscular frame of the man is betrayed; he has the strength to deliver lethal strokes with the longsword at his side, no mistake. One red-dyed-leather-gloved hand pushes away a lock of his curly-blonde hair which has settled there after his quick-footed dodging of various workers. “You’re not listening to me, Bleskos” he is saying to the other man beside him, at the very moment that a small boy collides with him; the boy is looking up and aiming a sling-shot at a gull hovering close-by over head, and with one eye shut to aim, and his lips bit together in concentration, he does not notice the large noble that he has just stumbled into. In fact, in the surprise of the collision, neither the boy nor Thunderstaff notice that the shot was discharged, and put a neat hole in the brim of the noble’s hat.
 
A chuckle from the other man makes the noble furious, and before the boy is able to plead his case, he is kicked mercilessly over the dock and into the frigid waters below.
 
“Oh, that wasn’t very nice, Arkos” says the other man. This man may be called ‘priest’. He is dressed in the rich grey and blue embroidered gown of a cleric of Torm. Beneath the rich gown, the man is wearing brilliant chain mail armour. He has a large shield on his back and a longsword at his side to complete the accoutrements of a man who is clearly a battle-priest. His arms and neck are as thick as the noble next to him, and his hair likewise blonde and curly.
 
“Damn that rat, Bleskos! I’ve had enough of standing around waiting and dancing on this cunt-smelling pier.”
 
“I’m not sure what cunts you’ve been sniffing lately, my brother, but perhaps you should lay out a few more coins for your entertainment, eh?”
 
“That’s enough. We’ve got business to discuss.” Another group of Carters is marching down the pier towards the men, but this time, Arkos shoots them such a poisonous glance that they navigate their unruly load of goods around the two men, to the ire of those coming the other direction.
 
“Business?” The cleric makes an amused and incredulous face. “Brother, you run the business. I am but a humble servant of the Cloth. Surely you have someone else to count our coins…”
 
“I don’t need coins counted, I need them delivered!”
 
“Perfect! I see some idle Carters not two piers over. Shall I fetch them?”
 
“Quit fucking about. I’m serious.”
 
“No, you are irate. As am I; how long will this” and the cleric makes a disgusted gesture towards the boat they are both watching “go on for? Surely that ice will melt on its own.” The two Thunderstaff brothers are watching a half-dozen men dangling on ropes over the side of a large Amnian-built schooner hammer away at sheets of ice that are frozen to the hull of the ship.
 
“Aye! It’ll melt off in time, but that rotten cunt Madras, coin-clipping Sembian that he is, charges even the Families by the hour now; this dock is costing us money as we stand. And the Ebon Shark is nearly half-again her weight in ice right now; the Carters won’t unload her until the ice is off; new law from Madras, our beloved ‘Reformer of the Dock’…fucking Sembian cunt!”
 
“Goodness. Perhaps that explains the smell down here.”
 
“Perhaps. Now, my brother; let us make use of you such as father intends. I have need … rather, we have need of the church’s help. That dimwit Meriweather has sat idle for a tenday now; our goods are rotting in Secomber where they are worth half of what they are in Loudwater.”
 
“Arkos; you have mistaken me for a logistician, or wagonner. What can these,” the man pulls mockingly at his habit, “do for our struggling Family?”
 
Arkos jabs a thick finger into his brother’s chest, hard enough to knock over a good-sized man, but not hard enough to make Bleskos even wince. “Those robes you wear are going to start paying us back, today. How much has our Family dumped down that bottomless pit that is the hidden coffers of our Faith?”
 
“’Dumped’, brother mine?”
 
“Fucking shat down the jakes, then! I’ll be blunt and to the point, because I have things to do today that don’t include a wordy dance with an idle priest.” Bleskos feigns a wounded look. “Never mind that! Just listen…
 
“We’ve got this man, Meriweather. You’ve met him.”
 
“I have had the displeasure.”
 
“He is stranded in Secomber, because he claims he can’t get enough good men, women or children to accompany him on what he claims is a dangerous road.”
 
“A most perilous journey, and a prudent course of inaction, I am sure.”
 
“You’re fucking right it’s prudent, but you aren’t sure of anything yet, priest. Shut your bishop-sucker until I’m done! Meriweather is no fool…”
 
“You just a moment ago called him a ‘dimwit’…”
 
“I called you the dimwit. Listen. Meriweather is a lot of useless things, but useless isn’t one of them. He gets our wagons to Loudwater ten times out of ten. If he says there’s a problem, there’s a godsdamned problem. The part he didn’t have to tell me is this; there hasn’t been so much as a skiff or even a canoe come down the Delimbyr yet this year. We should be buying up dried weed and cheap earthenware by now. Something is wrong in Loudwater.”
 
“Well, my mercantile-brother. I shall pray for the deliverance of that valuable metropolis hourly until our purse is again weighty.”
 
“You’ll do more than pray, priest.” At this moment, a few yards down-dock from the two brothers, a group of small boys is pulling something up from the water; a shivering boy is being hauled up on stolen ropes from the lethal waters below. “You’ll find us a man to head up there and open the damned road.”
 
“Shall I? What sort of man? A ditch-digger to dry up the road? Perhaps a lumberjack to clear the brush?”
 
“Quit pissing on me, Bleskos. You know what kind of man I need. I want someone reliable. Duty bound, even. Surely you know a man such as that.”
 
A smile broadens across the priest’s face. “I do indeed, brother. I do indeed.”
 
“Tell me about him.”
 
“Oh yes. Strong as a bugbear. Sturdy like a brick whore-house. And the very picture of Duty.” The priest pauses in feigned reflection, his hand stroking his profound double-chin.
 
“Come on then!”
 
“Yes; a seasoned campaigner. He helped organize the defences of a small town up north somewhere last summer. Liberated the place, he did. He liberated a good amount of treasure from the monsters that besieged the town, too. And he donated every penny to the widows and children of the town…” The priest shakes his head in a disapproving fashion. “Shepherd is his name.”
 
“Like a fucking sheep-dog?”
 
“He is the very sheep-dog you are looking for, my brother. I shall send for him today, after I have seen to my divinely appointed duties.” Another broad grin.
 
“Leave those cunts-for-hire until after high-sun, you rutting jackal. Send me Shepherd, straightaway.” With this, Arkos takes off his hat with a look of agitation on his face impossibly more severe than that which he has been wearing all morning. He puts a thick finger through the hole in the brim. His brother gives a silent chuckle at the condition of the hat, and motions down the dock to the group of boys that are now gathered around the drenched and shivering boy sitting in their midst. They are all looking at the pair of Thunderstaff men, and their eyes grow wide as they see Arkos look their way.
 
Arkos takes one angry step towards the boys, who turn to flee, only to be stopped short by a pair of large horses pulling a huge wagon. The boys, undecided as to which is more dangerous – the enraged noble or the dauntless hooves of the animals – jump into the harbour to save themselves.
 
The Thunderstaff men are forced to move aside, but they watch with amused interest as the horses and wagon clomp and roll over the shivering boy who stayed sitting oblivious to his danger. The boy is alerted to his peril too late, and simply curls up and covers his head in desperation. Seeing the boy unharmed, Bleskos laughs and says “By Torm, he’s got someone looking over him today!”
 
“Aye. He does.” Arkos strides up to the boy, who is now struggling to get to his feet to get away from the oncoming noble who is slapping a ruined hat across his palm in a threatening gesture. He is too slow, once again, to avoid the large boot of the noble, which once again sends him into the water, this time with mates.
 
Bleskos is in stitches over this; it is unclear what entertains him more; his brother’s temper or soaking-wet urchins. Arkos looks back at his guffawing brother and yells “Send me Shepherd!”
