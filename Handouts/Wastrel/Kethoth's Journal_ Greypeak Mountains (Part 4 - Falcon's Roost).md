=Into the Mountains=

29 Mirtul

It is the very end of fourth watch, and I have been up all night.  As I write these very words, the curious whistling of Zamomel Stass is fading in the distance.  I have been up all night talking to this amazing character, and though I am tired from the events of the last few days, I am too innervated now to sleep.

The sun is starting to come up, and I can see that Radha is just slightly uncomfortable with the grey in the east.  We shall have to do some research at the library and put her story and fate together.

From the beginning, Kethoth...

This morning followed a very long night for us.  The loss of our friends and the destruction of our nemesis has cost the troops strength of body and spirit, but we remain in the swift current of events which washes us ever onwards, and there is no time to mourn or celebrate.

Kor provided what passes for a triumphal feast for the people of these hills when we returned, and promised us a horse each for our service to his settlement, and we are grateful of that.  I hope I am able to settle that debt with him, and perhaps others.  He is mistaken if he thinks a horse and a trinket will pay for my service.  I am still putting together a scheme to make this man and all he has, mine.  If I am to be honest to myself at least, I must confess that I tremendously enjoy it up here, in the hills and at the ranch, and I rather like the women there.  Kor I can’t abide, though I may have to for a time.  He’s probably easier to manage alive than dead.

In the dining hall this morning, I laid out our plans for the next leg of the journey.  Reports have come down from Falcon’s Roost that the army we defeated, the Army of the Red Hand, is but the van of a much larger force that is trapped on the other side of the Roost.  This larger army, styled The Hand of Doom, is of a size to threaten most of the Grey Vale, and it is unlikely that it can be held at the Roost, even if we bolster the ranks of the fatigued garrison there.

I should back up a bit, first.  I will point out presently that it is unlikely that the fire that I caused in the Smear has anything to do with an army of thousands that has been assembled since at least some months before the fire in question.  It is possible that the fire was the motivating force for The Red Hand, but even that is unlikely.  It is more likely that we have been co-opted by the villainous Cerulean Circle to do their dirty work.  Fortunately for all involved, the orcs are directly in my path towards the Monastery of Sundered Faith.

Here I interject that half of the intelligence we receive comes from the scouts, who seem to have a good rapport with Tyriol, and know to distrust me immensely.  No doubt they have been given this bias from Maeghuen, who has me closely pegged, though not as close as I shall soon peg her.  The other half of our intelligence comes from Cerulean Circle, and to me via Volund.  I will be clear here; Volund is as trustworthy a sword-mage as you will find, and that is meant only partially as an insult.  Those that direct him, and us through him, are not to be trusted, and I am not quite sure how the steps of our final dance will go.  I intend to immolate the Cerulean Circle, for a number of reasons, but Volund is not on the menu.  I guess I’ll have to wait and see which way the river of fate drags us.

So at breakfast, we compared our various notes and agendas, and our path was clear; we were off for Falcon’s Roost.  There, we could gather more proximal information, and choose a course.  We may stay and help them defend the Roost, which suits me fine.  If those lummoxes a century ago could hold it, certainly we can too, especially with the additional help we have.  We may try to slip through to the Monastery.  We shall see.

==New Recruits==
We have increased our company by two, for this journey;

Paetr Helgorod, a soldier and descendant of a long and accomplished line of dwarves will be coming with us, at least to the Roost.  He is a soldier on permanent garrison there, and I am glad of his company.  I had the pleasure of watching this stout warrior in the second wave of the Red Hand assault, and he is not to be under-sold.

Hrogan Goringrimm, a dwarven priest of Ohgma who is in fact from the very Monastery we are heading to.  Hrogan has agreed to accompany us back to the Monastery, which serves both parties.  Hrogan is a capable healer, and a very interesting and aged dwarf who's company I enjoy tremendously, despite his generally dour mannerisms.

I would never have guessed that a pair of dwarves would be of any use beyond a circus or a quarry chain-gang.  Wonders never cease in this age.

We had to leave behind our mounts and much of our expedition gear.  The path today has been mostly upwards through the rocky foothills, and we are now well within the mountains themselves.  We are carrying about half of our gear on a Tenser’s Disk, which is costing us dearly every day, but I will not be without the means to crack open tombs or other places that are standing between me and wealth.

We had one encounter with some orcs that are scouts for the Hand of Doom.  We were able to dispatch them easily; my troops are now a fine-honed instrument of warfare, and I am able now to levitate above the field of battle and rain down fire and other destruction with impunity.  I will have to work on my levitation, however; today when I tried it in combat for the first time, it was much like a great wind lifting me upwards, with the accompanying flutter of clothing and other loose gear that you could imagine.  It is a bit distracting, but makes for a spectacle.

We took one orc alive, and I had the pleasure of interrogating him.  The sensibilities of this group, now without Mattrick, are much reduced, and there wasn’t so much as a hushed protest when I took a shovel from one of my trunks, stood on the wrist of our prisoner, and drove the spade through his hand, taking half his fingers off (though we did busy Luc with gathering up booty to keep him out from under foot).  The orc gave up everything he knew after that, which includes the following details;

•	Huu’rom ak Murul – This is translated into the orcish dialect of the giant tongue as Huu’rom the Blue, and this is the orc in charge of the entire army.

•	Uthark – This is some sort of sub-boss who is in charge of a small company of scouts, of which we have killed about a quarter.

•	Mord'ja – This is the wyvern-riding orc that we drove off in the second wave of the assault at Korh’Murron.  He was in command of the Red Hand, but he may have been mustered into the larger Hand of Doom force.

After the brief battle and the interrogation, we rounded up a number of valuable weapons; it seems that this entire army is as well armed as the Red Hand, which I found better armed than any orcish army should be.  There is something more behind this uprising.  It was dark by the time we were ready to go again, and I knew it wouldn’t do to stay here; battlefields yield more than one crop of corpses when they are tarried in.

=Zamomel Stass=

We forced march for about four hours, heading into a deep ravine between two of the great mountains of the Grey Peaks.  We found a dry cave out of the way to set up camp, and settled for the night.

By third watch, Tyriol was forced to awaken us.  His musical voice chimed us awake, and we could hear whistling approaching us, and soon, we could see a small light winding through the trees.  We had been discovered by Zamomel Stass.

The name ‘Zamomel’ rung in the back of my head like directions to a half-remembered tavern in a strange city.  I knew that the name should be familiar to me, but the last century’s havoc in my mind continues to plague me.

Zamomel is a proper wizard, and no mistake.  He carries himself with a profound and confident dignity which I have been trying to refine since waking up this century.  He may well be as powerful as he seems, too; the world is a dangerous place, and for a lone wizard to be strolling beneath the moon, whistling and lighting the way for any fiend to find him, is testament to his confidence.  We will not pick a fight with this one.

It is too bad Zam found us when he did; I would have liked him to meet Mattrick, whom he claims he has been sent to find and help.  He claims that the original author of the plan to send Baird and Mattrick on a quest for the Horns of Eltab, the Simbarch Seriadne, was in fact an evil malaugrym.  He was not clear on how a malaugrym might profit by this errand any more than a legitimate Simbarch, which makes the story less likely.  He also stated that the Horns are a legend at best, one with which he was very familiar, however.  He encouraged us to go on our chosen path, and to pursue the Horns, and had the cheek to suggest that we surrender them, if we find them, to him.

It isn’t every day that a wizard as mercurial as myself sits opposite me and says “good sir; why don’t you risk your neck to retrieve some priceless artifact, and then if you wouldn’t mind dropping your drawers and bending over that I might roger you in turn with each horn once you have surrendered it to me?”  This fellow has more nerve than Calishite with a gold tooth, but I was forced to appreciate his tact.

He then delivered his coupe de grace, stating that he could be reached at the Dripping Dagger in Waterdeep, of all the beer-soaked hovels in all of Faerûn.  Just how he knew I planned on heading that direction I am unsure.  I suppose it isn’t the closest-guarded secret.  But just once, I would like to get out in front of our enemies and other interested parties, and grab them by the nose-ring for a change.  I am sure we are many weeks away from having the initiative in any of our dealings.

I told Zam that I would consider his offer.  He further padded the offer with a promise of great reward, the mention of which made our burglar shift in his bedroll on cue.  “If we deem this the right thing to do, sir” I responded “A reward will not be part of the equation, but I do thank you for your proffered generosity, and I do hope to hoist a glass with you in the City of Splendours in any event!”

==Epistemolgy and the Kossurian==
The two of us were the last awake, and as I have mentioned, this entry begins where our conversation left off, and it left off on a sour note, even if the note was rung in my head only.  Our last bit of conversation went thusly;

Kethoth:  “My inscrutable friend!  As a diplomat of sorts from a land I have had long and friendly ties to, I wonder if you might give me some bit of news of an old friend?”

Zam: “Certainly, good antiquarian!  Of whom would you wish news?”

“I wonder about my old friend, Cedric Blackchalice.  He is but half-elven, but I don’t doubt that he had the wits to survive the Spellplague, if he was able to keep from drowning these long years beneath the oceans of flesh he used to swim in.”

“Blackchalice, you say?  I’ve never heard of any of those, much less by the curious name ‘Cedric’.”

“You must be mistaken; his family once owned every other warehouse in Velprintalar.  I was in their estate for a month, and to this date have not had better wine or song.”

“You mean ‘Veltalar’, or perhaps you are referring to the old city, which we now call ‘Old Velprintalar’.  Those warehouses are now a mile from the ocean.  The city is much changed since you were there last, my venerable colleague...”

He gave me a look that was a mixture of sympathy and fascination, which I returned with a short nod.  “No matter, sir. I am sure I will find him.”

We were quiet then for a bit.  To be honest, I was doubting my own memory. Though I am generally skeptical about Zam, I don’t doubt that if he knew a thing about the Blackchalice Family, that he would tell me something.  I was forced at that instance to wonder if Cedric was just some sort of dream I had.  This is not the first time I wondered this.

Many months ago, when I began to awaken from the madness and depravity of the Bandit Kossur, I had occasion to ambush and interrogate a hapless dragon-man.  This was the same lizard, Tuncred, I have written about previously, but I should now record the conversation we had, his last in this world, as I recall it.

I had the enormous bipedal lizard in my grasp, magically weakened and in caught in my fatal glare.  I demanded him to declare that Set was alive in me, and he did, and at the time I was gratified.  However, with all the interrogations I have been part of lately, I know I could have made the dying creature claim anything to save his life, and so I had some small doubt then, but it was swollen with my conversation with Zam.

Certainly, I don’t look to be several hundred years old; I could pass for middling thirties, as the open-thighed mayor of Loudwater so often purred in my ear.  For just a moment after talking with Zam, and recalling the forced verification of the very God I claim to be a descendant of, I wondered if I might just be a daft youth who has been struck on the head in a battle with some goblins, at the very point which my ‘memory’ begins.

That would be a funny tale, to revisit.

The ‘Bandit Kossur’ is brought a slave girl to rape by his goblin ‘minions’.  He quickly finds the notion of this contemptuous, and begins to assault them with fire and curses, as he awakens from one personality into the clarity of another.

Perhaps from the goblin perspective, the story goes like this; we have captured a lovely slave girl to breed, and plan to bring her to the biggest hut in the camp to begin her gruesome violations presently.  What’s this?!  Who’s this devilish burglar in the boss’ hut, and why is he talking madness about being some sort of child of the gods and our master?  He must be completely fu...and then flames cut short their story.

Something to consider.  There is no evidence thus far that I am who I think I am.

Ah...Tyriol awakens.  Time to lift camp.

==Musings==
Mirtul 28 (later at night)

It was an uneventful though pleasant day in the mountains.  We continue to wind our way up a path that we are often forced to scale or locate; it is not easy going.  The way is steep and dangerous, but the beauty of these cold mountains makes for a pleasant backdrop, even if the immediate company spoils the foreground.

Anyway, before I pass out from exhaustion, I must record a couple of other things that Zam and I talked about.

For starters, we both had a good chuckle about Lady Saharel.  He is of the same opinion about omniscient ghosts as I, and apparently he did not endorse the mission of his two country men to find the Horns at all, much less by way of a ghost-story.  However, we both had to agree to the legitimacy of the Lady, and likewise agree that mixing undead wisdom with the affairs of the living is dangerous for all parties.  It has proven so for three of my party, and Lady Saharel herself.

I told him exactly what the Lady had said to Baird and Mattrick.  Ender coughed conspicuously when I did this, clearly disapproving of my candidacy in this matter.  However, it would not do to lie to this man about Baird and Mattrick, who can still answer for themselves under the right circumstances, revealing my lies, and at any rate, my honesty paid out.  Zam said that he wasn’t surprised that the Lady was leading us to the Monastery of Sundered Faith.  He claims that the Fat Oghman had some sort of premonition about the Spellplague, and put a repository of knowledge in the mountains in case the Font of Knowledge in Waterdeep became compromised.

It comes as no surprise that Kennyth would be prepared for the Spellplague.  What does come as a surprise is that there would be an expedition from the other side of the Sea of Fallen Stars, that has to come all this way to find a lead in an obscure library, for an artifact that is, relatively, in the back yard of the very location the quest set out from.  Add to this curious and circuitous tale one Zamomel Stass, who has come looking for the original expedition, and one is forced to wonder what is really at stake.  Zam knows much, much more than he is letting on.

If Kennyth is as thorough in ordering the library in the mountains as he was the Font, I am sure I will find a good many answers to some of these questions.  The first on my mind is “who is Zamomel Stass?”

=Ambushed in the Mountains=
Mirtul 29

The sun will be coming up soon, but I must jot down a few words quickly, before I rest.  Tonight, we are safely lodged in the smaller barracks of .  It was a long day of travel, fraught with perils, but we have arrived, Tyriol has given his mother a tender hug, and I am planning an expedition to destroy a bridge in the morning.

We nearly didn’t get this far with all of our company.  We were the target of a most sinister ambush by a large group of  scouts, with their devious leader, Utark, coordinating his forces effectively.  This ambush was around noon hour, as we were winding our way up a particularly difficult canyon.  I knew the lay of the land was bad, but I have decided to give Tyriol some latitude in picking our course and other matters in his field of proficiency.  Today, my trust in him was misplaced.

Our ambush was laid with a choice bit of bait; a pair of wardens, whom we espied about 30 yards ahead of us after a sharp bend in the canyon.  These two were being danced upon by the boots of several ‘Hand orcs, and half of my soldiers bolted into the trap before you could say “there’s two fools born every minute to replace the fool that was just killed in an ambush”.  In fact, when the first of several rock slides, triggered by hiding orcs on both rises of the canyon, crashed into my party, Radha and I were the only ones left in the rear, now divided from the rest of the party by a huge rock slide.  I wasn’t sure if my men had been crushed by the rocks at first, but I heard the sounds of combat on the other side of the obscuring mound, just as I noticed more orcs on both rises, now taking aim at Radha and I.

I again took opportunity to levitate, and was able to match the ranged assault against my lovely half-vampire and myself with ease.  Radha is able to muster the elements themselves from time to time, with no particular regularity I must add, and in this battle, our seemingly vulnerable rear was secured and lending aid to our beleaguered front liners in little time.

On the other side of the rock slide, the battle was a bit more dire.  Lucretius decided to mount one of the steep slopes to take on a nest of orcs that were hurtling rocks and starting slides to assail those in the valley, and he found himself biting off even more than his prodigious reptilian maw could chew.  As I levitated over the first debris field, I noticed that Tyriol was in the thick of things, coming to the aid of his fallen kith, and I am sure his mother would be as proud of him as she would be infuriated with me for it.  I actually saw him skewer an orc with a blade, which is a new tactic for him.  This change in his fighting style also reflects the dodgy situation into which my men had foolishly charged.

I would like to say that I floated to the rescue of my men, flinging fire and arcane missiles as I drifted dauntlessly towards my foes, but that isn’t really how it happened.  I was drifting about, again with the agitation of a wind blowing right up my pant leg, and I knocked down about six or eight of the monsters.  But in truth, the day was won by Radha, who seemingly opened a small portal to the Abyss, and let destructive Chaos tear our enemies apart.  She was even able to fly herself into position, and Lucretius out of harm’s way with her ever-improving command of the destructive forces she toys with.  She managed to pitch Utark off of his cliff and smite him with a bolt of lightning before he hit the ground, quite dead, and from there, we mopped up the rest in short order.

I was tempted to reprimand the fools who rushed into the trap, but I think the knocks they took will be a better lesson.  All save Lucretius will think again, and my words are wasted on him as it is.

==Discussion with Lucretius==
There is one last  thing worth noting in this encounter.  The leader of the pack, Utark, was the same sort of Shaman of Tiamat such as we battled against at Kohr’Murron.  The first time I fought these characterless monsters I thought little of it, setting them alight as I would any other viusiteb (translated from the Untheric; there is no word in Chondathan that means both ‘enemy’ and ‘victim’).  However, after my dream the other night, I found myself enraged at this creature for invoking the Five-Headed-Whore, and after the battle was won, I crossed the ravine to his corpse, and stared down at it with palpable contempt.  I was lost in my thoughts for a minute, and was brought to by the rumbling voice of our resident dragon man.

“Orcs that serve the Bitch-we-do-not-name are bitter foes indeed, Captain.”

I turned on him with a snarl and a finger pointed at his ugly nose.  “Tiamat is among a long list of bitches I will name.  Tell me again, Quintus Lucretius Cato, what dealings your people have with her.”

“You know it well by now, wizard.  I have told you three times in three days that we worship her sworn foe, Bahomet” he replied as he made an awkward and foolish looking religious sign as he invoked his dragon ‘god’.

I wanted to say “how the hell can you tell one blasted reptile from the next, and why shouldn’t we just exterminate the lot of you like the infestation of our world that you are” but that wouldn’t do.  Luc would probably have calmly illumed the problem of heritage that I present in every new town and tavern, and he would be right.  I hate him all the more for it.  I hate him as well because I know that he is not lying about Bahomet and Tiamat too; he despises the one as much as he loves the other, and though I have nothing but indifference for Bahomet, I doubt I hate Tiamat even as much as Luc, in both meanings of that singular statement.

The fact that he and I share a common enemy is more than I can bear.  My line has been in conflict with that Whore for centuries longer than the scaly invaders from Abeir have even been on this world.  That they would drop their civilization on top of mine, and take up the mantle of hating the same enemies as us, makes the situation all the uglier.  I should be smart about it, and simply take advantage of the obvious blessing of having two foes who are foes, but it is a blow to my pride and nuanced-sensibilities to keep Lucretius in the ranks, and to battle our common enemy together.

Anyway, I have become distracted.  I curse all dragons, regardless of the number legs they go on.

=Falcon's Roost=
By nightfall, after another forced march and much boot-leather worn thin, we made it to the Roost.  This place sits at the southerly, narrow end of Helgorod Valley, the name of which deriving from the ancestors of our new stout body guard.  Two sturdy towers are built on the mountains that flank the narrowest part of the pass, and a thick wall with a soaring and decorated battlement spans the valley, from tower to tower.  This is in fact a very defensible position, and when I first saw the stones of the fortifications by the light of the moon, the scales on the back of my neck shuddered a little, with the anticipation of another great battle.

The garrison here is comprised of a few dwarves and a handful of wardens from Loudwater.  They were all in high spirits when they saw us approach the gates, but any notion that a warm reception was aimed at me was quickly dispelled when the guards came out to embrace Paetr and Tyriol.  A ragged sergeant whisked Tyriol away to see his mother, while Paetr gave us a tour of the fortress, and saw to our lodgings.

==Kethoth Meets with Captain Grimbold==
Most of the men turned in early.  We are tired from a long and dangerous journey, and this is just the kind of place that most men can sleep peacefully in.  The carved-stone barracks hall is seemingly impregnable, and there are ample guards on watch all night long.  I decided to go for a stroll and seek out Maeghuen’s bed-chambre.

The fortress is not terribly large, so finding it wasn’t too difficult.  I rounded a stone corner and nearly bumped into Tyriol, who was backing out of a door quietly, and trying to close it in a fashion that suggested a host of sleeping infants in the room beyond.  Stationed at the door, on the other side of the slinking scout was a dour-faced guard on a crutch, the best man they could spare to keep the devil from the captain’s underthings, I suppose.

“What ho, Sergeant!” I said cheerily to Tyriol as he silently closed the door.  The volume of my voice made him cringe somewhat.  He waved a sort of half-wave intended to tell me ‘good night, captain’ but which betrayed the message ‘my juicy mother is in the room beyond, and doesn’t want to be disturbed, especially by the devilish likes of you.’

“Ahh...” says I with a grin.  “Ms. Grimbold is convalescing, is she?  I’ll not bother her, my good scout.  Off to bed with you, now.  There’s a good man!”

Tyriol just sighed and muttered a good-night to me, knowing full well I was about to march through that door.  He shuffled off in protest, slowly and conspicuously, and when he was out of ear-shot, I turned to the crippled soldier with a grin.

“Captain Sepret, reported as ordered, sir!”  I snapped.  His face wore an expression of complete befuddlement, which quickly turned to indignation.

“You just said you was going to leave the Captain alone...”

“I am the Captain...” I squinted at the chevron on his dirty uniform “...lieutenant.”

“No no no!  Captain Grimbold is what I meant, sir...” he stumbled in protest.

“Ah yes, Captain Grimbold.  It is her very orders that I am acting upon, my good man, and I would implore you not to keep your superior officer waiting a moment longer.”

Confusion.  “But, she told me to keep ‘that devil-faced wizard’ out of her room until morning, sir, and...”

“And I was given the task of reporting to her with vital intelligence regarding myriad matters, including the number of killed and wounded at Kohr’Murron, recent enemy troop movements, and other matters that simply cannot be discussed here, in this very hall; matters that she will be furious to learn of in the light of day, when time has pressed us in, my good lieutenant.”

I smiled, folded my arms behind my back, and rocked on my heals in victory.  I could see the slow grind of ideas in the head of the simple soldier, and before long, he screwed up his face, shook his head, and opened the door.

“Sepret...” I heard her voice accuse as I stepped through the door.  “If you are quite through lying through your teeth to my men, let’s get this done.”  She wields acrimony in a way that makes my loins stir.  This woman hates me, and nothing so awakens the organ of progeny in me than the knowledge that the prey in question will not go quietly onto her back.

“Ms. Grimbold!  My life has been a tormenting void without your musical voice to ear, made even more unbearable by the constant reminder I have of you, in your son, whom I have delivered back to you, unkilled, as promised.”

“Enough, Sepret.  What do you want?”

Oh, that question made me tie her down with my eyes, and my answer was much delayed by protracted day dreaming, but finally I answered as requested.  “What I want is for you to be honest with me.” A single raised eyebrow made me defend myself; “I know; let us dispense with the irony of my request presently, and get to the meat of the matter, shall we?  I absconded with your son, and a host of other adventurers, as you know, at the request of that daft emaciated whore I have been crawling on for the last few months.”

That statement was an exhilarating mix of truth and bawdiness which I knew she would keep to herself; her opinion of me is well known, and any truth I pour could not be credibly reproduced by her, given her bias.  She knows I know this, and we are both served by it.

“Do you know, the silly slut was actually right about there being a ghost in Spellgard?  Sure enough, the tormented soul answered any question put to it!  Amazing!”  I gesticulated in the most animated and insincere fashion possible.

“Get to it, monster!”

“I shall, my plum!  Of all the things in the world that Tyriol, your precious flesh and blood, could have asked the ghost, do you know what he asked?  Can you guess?  Do you have any idea, my sweet...”  I let the question hang in the air.  Of course, she knew the question he would ask, and knew I knew.  There are scholars and kings who do not stimulate me in conversation such as Maeghuen does.

“We both know what he would ask.”

“Ahh!  Indeed!  He crossed through mountains, forests, badlands and unimaginable perils to ask a ghost something that you could answer yourself, unless of course you have such an appetite for elven affection that the inventory of your lust is so long and complicated as to lend uncertainty to the matter.  But it isn’t, is it?”

She said nothing, answering only by way of glowering at me, and sitting up in her bed.  The way the sheets shuffled across her thighs as she did gave me pause, and a wicked grin.  I quickly changed my expression though, affecting that of a scolding parent.

“Do you know what happened when he asked the question, Mae?  The very ghost that was providing the answers was snatched away by some huge and mailed hand.  The answer to that question is so dangerous that a legendary ghost is not able to answer it, and in fact may be suffering some terrible imprisonment on behalf of your discretion.

“I’ll be blunt with you, Mae; I can’t think of a more selfish and irresponsible thing to do, than to send a child off into peril to safeguard the sins of the parent.

“Who is his father, Mae.  We must know now.”

The answer issued forth from her lips after some delay.  She wore an expression of defeat and sadness, and after a slight quiver of her lip, and a heaving of her generous bosom, she answered;

“The Sphinx.  The Sphinx is his father.”

==Decisions at the Roost==

Mirtul 30

In the dark hour of four bells this morning, what passes for a headquarters division discussed the prospects facing Falcon's Roost, and our options.  There was a rag-tag group of Loudwater Warden officers, some local dwarves, including Paetr who as it turns out is a captain here, myself, Tyriol and Volund.  A couple of scouts who came in overnight gave a situation report, indicating that the main force of the Hand of Doom was only two or three days away from a bridge called Warklegnaw's Span.  This is a fortified bridge that is reportedly in a bit of disrepair.  By all accounts, if we are able to destroy that bridge, the main force of the army will be trapped on the other side, and will not be able to cross until perhaps as late as next spring.

A pair of scrolls were produced from the armoury upon which powerful evocations are inscribed.  I have been assured that the spells on the scrolls will be enough to knock down the span, and having examined them, I can attest that they are potent and destructive spells indeed. The bridge being only a day away from Warklegnaw's, it seemed like we might be able to make the bridge before the enemy host, and trap it on the other side.  There was some grumbling about only putting off something that has to be dealt with eventually, but I put an end to that discussion.

\"There are many ways to defeat an army, and I have witnessed most of them in my time.  I can assure you that there isn't an army on Faerûn that can hold up against itself, when time, food and plunder run out.  If we delay the host of the Hand of Doom even a month, I don't doubt that they would face desertion, disease, hunger, in-fighting and general chaos.  Destroying their means of gaining Helgorod Pass will destroy them.\"

I spoke those words with confidence, knowing them to be true.  I haven't exactly been in the General's Tent of any conflict greater than that which I find myself in now, but I know that armies are difficult to hold together when there is abundant food and plunder, and nearly impossible to keep marching when those things run out.  However, the only reason I supported the destruction of Warklegnaw’s Span was because of expediency and self-preservation.

Finally, the grumbling petered out, we were furnished with the scrolls, and were off.

If we are able to make the span tomorrow, destroy it, and make our way back to the Roost the following day, we will be several days ahead of schedule, and on our way to the Monastery.  After hearing the reports from the scouts, I am no longer anxious to face this army, even at the Roost.  If their numbers are half of what is reported, we would all perish under the press of such a force.  I have no intention of throwing my life away in these mountains, and so I am pursuing this diversionary errand in the hope it will preserve us all.

=Helgorod Pass=
Tonight we are travelling light.  We are forcing march and carrying only our fighting gear and a bit of food.  It is quite pleasurable to travel in such fashion, I should remark.  Not waiting on recalcitrant mules, stuck wagons, and ponderous discs allows one to keep a brisk pace, and also enjoy the air and the scenery.  The Greypeakes are mountains from a child’s story book, and it is a delight to cast aside burdens, even if only for a while.
The valley of Helgorod Pass is broad and green, with a variety of plants and small coniferous trees proliferating.  The pass carries us ever upwards, but it is a gentle ascent, and we made excellent time, covering more distance in a day than we have in three days in the steep road leading to the Roost.

Our pleasant constitutional through the valley was interrupted only once.  Our scouts actually managed to get the drop on a small train of supplies being escorted by a group of Hand scouts.  Tyriol reported back to me with a smile from pointy-ear-to-pointy-ear; “Captain!  We’ve got the drop on ‘em!  A small caravan is heading this way, and we should be able to ambush them!”

I know he wanted me to acknowledge his proficiency in his scouting, and I was pleased that our standard operating procedure was finally starting to work as planned, but I withheld praise.  “How many are they, and how armed, and what is their spacing and formation?  Have you brought the rest of the intel with you or no?”  He frowned and turned to skulk off.  I didn’t really want answers to any of those questions; that we had the drop on these hapless vermin was all I needed to know to assess our chances.  “Hold on, boy.  Find a shooting lane and post up.  We’ll take them right here.”  He smiled and sprung off.

Seeing my troops work an ambush is a wonderful sight.  We had ample cover from rocks and pine trees, and when we sprung our trap on the small column, half of them were dead before they could organize a resistance.  They had great lizards pulling large wagons, and these creatures while frightened were the greatest threat my men had to face.  In the end we had a score of dead orcs to plunder, and three wagons full of supplies.  We did not have the time to round up the booty properly, but I did send a message to the Roost to have them come and retrieve the supply trains.

We pressed on, and as I write this, we are camped out under a canopy of enormous pine trees, a warm Kythorn wind is blowing through the boughs while the stars dance above.

As I look to the northeast, the stars and the moon illume the great chain of mountains that reside on the other side of the vast ravine, which shows as a pitch-black slash through the nightscape.  Many miles off, on the other side of one of those shadow-cradled peaks, a great orange glow lights the bottom of the wispy clouds on this lovely night.  Those are the fires of the host against which we are racing, and they are a full day away from the span.  We are but a leisurely stroll down the hill from the span.  In fact, I can see the towers that guard its entrance, and one orc is smoking a pipe which twinkles on a battlement in the night.

Who could find fault in such a night?

=Warklegnaw's Span=

Kythorn 1

We are once again nestled in the barracks of the Roost, and I have authorized a bit of wine and spirit to be rationed out.  Today was one of the most successful days my company has had, and our success has brought security to the Grey Vale for another season at least; Warklengnaw’s Span is destroyed, the great army of the Hand of Doom is waylaid and will break apart soon, and the road is open to the Monastery.  While we are safe in the fortress here, why not let the troops enjoy a little bit of success; it is hard earned, and we have more travails ahead of us.

Let me begin at the breezy evening I was enjoying just 24 hours ago, however.

We arrived at Warklegnaw’s Span right at dark, and though we were well rested, I decided against attacking an orcish fort at night.  We have too many day-lookers in our midst, and there is no point taking them out of the equation.  All night I stayed up and watched the two towers down the hill from us.  The night was perfect for stalking, and I had to resist the urge to creep down the hill and strangle one of our enemies.  I comforted myself by watching the shadows move on the parapets below, musing over whether or not they have any idea that they have seen their last sunrise.

As the eastern sky turned a moiling grey, I quietly woke the troops, though half of them were up already, anxious to get underway.   Tyriol and Ender crept up as close to the towers as they could, observing the two guard posts for a while, until they had enough for us to put a plan together.

==...When a Plan Comes Together==
Our plan was not simple. It involved a necessary division into teams of stealthy fighters, armoured fighters, and ranged combatants.  Our sneaky lot would gain entry into one tower, silently murder its way to the top, at which time Radha would fly Ender across from one tower to the next, under cover of fire from our missile team, while the gate-crashers rushed the doors to check the advance of any troops emerging from the towers to respond to our assault.

When we normally cobble together a plan like this, I spend most of the planning having a discussion like this in my head;

“Kethoth, old chum; what will you do when these nincompoops botch our stealthy entry plan, and we are set upon on all sides by brutes and savages?”

“Why, I’ll levitate up, toss a fire ball or two, and do my level best not to set fire to our burglar if he is too terribly stuck in amongst our foes, my reliable friend!”

“And if some villain realizes that the real threat is fixed aloft, raining death to scores of his minions and chattel, and then makes some ranged assault on your person?”

“Then, I crack my staff, put up a magical barrier, and when that fails, I drop down, and interpose Lucretius between myself and harm, and pray earnestly for our mutual safety.”

“Jolly good!”

This time, however, I needed no back up plan.  I watched our stealth team creep across to the northerly tower, and it was several minutes before we heard from them again.  I expected any second to hear a horn or a gong, or at least the hue and cry, but as each moment pressed on in silence, I thought to myself “by Set’s cloaca, they’re sticking to the plan!”

When the doors to the upper parapet of the north tower sprung open, and hell was unleashed, I ran, such as I do on one good leg, to my appointed position, and began to launch death at any defenders I could see.  To my complete amazement, I saw Radha and Ender flying from one tower to the next, and heard the dying screams of the vicious little rat’s victims.

On cue, defenders rushed from the doors of the southern tower to attack my position, and Volund and Lucretius checked their advance, and indeed drove them back and began to slaughter them.  Before you could say “wonders never cease” the parapets of both towers were taken, and the defenders on the ground were down to their last few men.

That is when Ozyrandion showed up.

==Tyriol and the Dragon==

It has happened often enough that small battles turn into desperate struggles for survival in our adventures so far, and this slaughter became poised the same.  As the last of the defenders were being driven towards a drop to their death, and the last defenders on the parapets were mercilessly eviscerated or filled full of arrows and arcane blasts, Ozyrandion, a large black dragon, swept down upon us.  We weren’t prepared for the intervention of such a beast.  In fact, none of us has faced a real dragon until today.  But all the men are still alive as I write this, and Ozyrandion is nursing her pride and more than a few nasty missile wounds.

On Ozyrandion’s first pass, most of my ground fighters were sprayed with her caustic breath.  The healing powers of Hrogan are unmatched, however, and we were all free of the burning torment of the thing’s breath in short order.  As it passed by, Radha and I provoked it with light missile fire, while my front-liners prepared for a pitched battle with the thing.  That never came.

Ozyrandion took another pass at us on the ground, but we repelled it with more concerted and prepared arcane fire, and it decided it would have a better chance picking off Tyriol, who was alone in the tower above us.  I am not sure exactly what happened up there, but it seems like Tyriol saved his prowess and wrath for this encounter, and he tormented the dragon, pass after pass, with his swift evasions and punishing and precise rounds of fire.  After the third pass at our lone scout, the dragon fled, and we cheered in unison as the thing dove beyond sight into the depths of the ravine, in full retreat.  Soon after, Tyriol emerged from the tower, and we were now able to see that he had endured a more dangerous assault than we had guessed; his arms and face were horribly burned from the acid, and one cruel claw had found its mark, leaving an unbelievable wound on his shoulder.  Hrogan fixed all of this with wink and a finger up alongside his nose.

We quickly reconnoitred the other side of the span, but found it held against us.  A few orcs threatened to march out against us, but we didn’t’ give them the chance.  We beat a hasty retreat, and used the scrolls to knock down the vast bridge.  I should say that the only low-point in the day for me was watching that old bridge fall into the mist and shadow of the ravine.  The bridge was about 20’ wide, and hundreds of feet across; its gentle arch was an engineering masterpiece, the likes of which one only finds where dwarves have been busy for centuries.  There was a respectable track worn into the stone from countless decades of use, and though a few blocks had fallen through here and there, I did not doubt that a few master masons would have this thing looking tiptop in good time.

==The Doom of Warklegnaw's Span==
As I read the first scroll, the bridge began to shudder, and some of the loose masonry began to crumble away, and many large blocks dropped into the mist below; the ravine is so deep that we did not hear the report of those blocks striking bottom.  My men backed up with haste as the bridge began to protest.  The entire area around the towers, and the towers themselves, seem to be cut from the same blocks as the span itself, so that it is actually difficult to tell where the road ends and the span begins.  As the bridge shook, so too did the ground beneath our feet.

I took out the second scroll and began to read the incantation inscribed upon it.  Now, the men were in full retreat, leaving only this devil and his borrowed magic to see to the final goal of the day.  As the last words of the incantation were spoken, the bridge began to come apart, and the ground beneath me shook, as though a small earthquake had taken hold.  Dust was billowing up from between the dry-laid stones around the towers and beneath my feet, and as the last word left my lips, there was a great cracking from somewhere a few hundred feet away, towards the centre of the bridge.  It sounded like a peel of lightning, but it was the death knell of Warklegnaw’s Span; the stones fell away from the middle, and the bridge then undid itself from two directions, the falling stones racing from the middle to the two ends with dizzying haste.  I backed away slowly, unable to take my eyes off of the spectacle, and feeling no small amount of remorse for the loss of so prodigious a structure.

As the cascade of stone raced to my feet, the towers on either side of be began to shake a bit more, and ever larger bits of masonry began to rain down, while a dust cloud billowed up.  I then turned and hobbled away as quickly and as dignity and one bad knee would carry me.  The troops were ahead of me, pointing and gesticulating.  Hrogan was just staring blankly at the scene behind me, and Paetr hung his head and wrung his hands.  I understood how they felt, but I didn’t let it show.  As I hobbled out from the cloud of dust, I wore a satisfied look on my face, and was about to say ‘well done, team!” when a new gut-churning cracking noise was heard.  The cloud of dust from the collapse obscured all but the top of the towers, and as we stood there looking on, we saw the top of the northern tower, upon which Tyriol had made his stand against the dragon, vanish into the cloud.

The scrolls had worked.  The span was destroyed, and the army of the Hand of Doom was trapped on the other side.  We did not stay long to enjoy our victory, though.  A slighted black dragon was nearby, and it was time to put some distance between us and it.

==Last Night at Falcon's Roost==
A forced march down the pleasant Helgorod Pass brought us to the Roost by dark, where we were greeted as heroes for the first time.  The solemnity of the destruction of the bridge was shared among the dwarves in the Roost, but only until a cask of ale was tapped, and there was some levity among the men.  They all realized that a disastrous battle had been averted, and that the challenge of building a new and better bridge might be just the sort of thing dwarves life for anyway. They are a curious people.

Most of my men are now well into their cups, which sometimes makes me nervous.  They tend to speak a little too freely after even the first helping.  However, even Kethoth is retiring from his scheming for the night.  I enjoyed a single cup of a strong cider which made my cheeks warmer than usual, and then retired to a chair to simply watch the men as they relived the victories of the day, and some of the defeats of the weeks past.

The exhaustion from being awake for a couple of days and the marching that accompanied it, combined with the generous helping of fresh mountain air and a cup of cider all conspired to put me to sleep in the chair I had taken.  My head nodded once, and then I realized I had to leave.  It would be no good to fall asleep in front of the men, and risk having some waking dream.  I took my leave, wrote these words, and now I retire.

Today was a good day.
