== A Grizzly Encounter ==

“It was my second time going into that fantastic but terrible forest, that I was found by a Grizzly Bear. I had once again hired on that same stout man, Mather Trellheim, to guide me in the woods. He was to take me to a particularly notable stand of Eastwitch Walnut far to the east of the village. On our second day of travel along the trail which follows the Silverwash, we happened upon a set of bear tracks, the size of which could have contained a knight’s shield. These tracks followed the same trail we were traveling throughout the entire day, and I made clear my worries to my able guide. Mr. Trellheim insisted that the tracks were days old, and that following these tracks gave us no more chance of encountering this bear than we risk by simply entering the forest in the first place. I mentioned that I did not know that bears this size were in the woods in the first place (I had not yet been up to Eastwitch Grange, and saw that which adorns the floor of the ‘Trophy Room’).

Towards the end of the day, Mather became uneasy. With the Sun and the Wind on our backs, we found ourselves in the most devious trap I have ever heard of having been devised by a simple mammal. We should have been ambushed outright, and killed without warning if it were not for that wind, I should say. For the smell of the great thing was much greater hint than it’s footfalls, and Mather became alert to it, in time to save his life. To my right, I saw him draw great breath. I turned to look at him, noted a look of terror in his eyes, and saw him instantly crouch and turn, bringing up his spear. Dumfounded, I turned and saw only the dazzling glare of the sun in my eyes. But then a great shadow drew across me, and I blacked out.

I do not know what events followed afterward, but it was not for some time that I awoke, and that was to the stimulating effect of Saltes. It was fully dark, and I thought I might still be in the shadow of some great beast, but my senses were soon configured, and I noted the stars and moon above me. In closer perspective was the lean and tattooed face of some Nameless Warden, who was bringing me to. He said to me the only words I ever heard pass his lips; “Yer lucky, Traveler”. I made to rise, but he stayed me with a simple gesture of his palm. I knew I was safe in the presence of the man, and I lay back and savoured this moment of my life, which should have been forfeit. I inhaled the sweet smell of the campfire, and watched the dancing stars overhead. 

Then, I found that my head was wrapped in thick bandages, and I became suddenly aware of a dull throbbing in my head which became increasingly worse. I became aware also of the burning sensation on the left side of my face. Mather must have noted my discomfort, for he came to me and made me to drink a bitter drought full of grit and flotsam. “He says this will lessen the pain” Mather said to me, and I drank the concoction. It put me back to sleep.

Days later, I awoke again to find myself in the care of Dr. Miller, in the village. The good Doctor explained to me that the Wardens give those numbing drinks as much to quiet unwanted travelers, and get them out of the forest, as to lessen their pain. He explained to me also, that the wounds on the side of my face would scar me for life. It is these same scars, the path of three of five claws, that one finds upon my face now, that shall always remind me of that terrible place. Two more marks are found on my left arm, from elbow to shoulder, from that singular pass of the murderous bear’s paw.

Soon after my waking, Mather came to me. He apologized for my ill luck, and gave me back a portion of his fee. Said I to him; “That must have been a most clever bear, and enormous at that!”. Said he to me; “It was a clever bear, though not so big. I reckon she used that same wind which gave her away to me at the last instant, to keep just slightly ahead of us all day. She then came upon us after circling back, and used the sun to hide herself from you. A clever bear indeed.\"

“But it must have been enormous; that same bear’s tracks were the largest I have seen, and I shall always see one of its tracks upon my face! Surely, my head is but three-fifths the size of its paw!” I argued to him.

“Indeed, you are lucky that it was aiming for my head, which is half-a-head shorter than your own. Were it aiming for you, I should think your head would have been sent a-wind without your shoulders” retorted he.

“But you say it was not a large bear?!”

“It was not a large bear” replied he, and made his leave.. And he was right. For in those parts, it was not a large bear.

-Notes by Jacob Woolson,
Cultural Surveyor and Professor,
Lord’s University.
