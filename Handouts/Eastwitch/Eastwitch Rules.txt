There are a few new rules found below that will certainly come into play over the course of an Eastwitch campaign. These rules are specific to this setting, and help to resolve some situations that will likely arise in a setting that is dominated by Wilderness. The DM has the ultimate say over whether the rules are used or not.

== Hunting ==

Because Eastwitch is a setting that is surrounded with great wilderness, there will be many opportunities for the PC's to hunt, in order to simply feed themselves, or maybe even to turn a profit. The skins of most animals are worth at least a bit of money or have trade value, and some creatures (such as Grizzly bears and large wolves) have very valuable pelts.

Hunting is divided into two types; large game and small game hunting. At the beginning of a day, the hunter should declare what type of game he is going after, and tell the DM. Then different hunting methods and details should be given to the DM, who will then determine, secretly, the DC needed to successfully hunt. What happens next depends on what type of game is being hunted.

===Small Game===
Small game hunting is simplified and requires few rolls and no combat. Basically, the hunter uses his Wilderness Lore (WL) skill as if he were foraging. For every two points that the character's WL check is above the DC (which only the DM knows), enough meat to feed one person is hunted. The DM will determine what type of animals were taken (for purposes of skinning; see rules below), and how many. No combat rolls are needed, and a single round of ammunition is expended for each animal taken. Hunters can also hunt with close combat weapons, or even their bare hands, but it is more difficult. There are three different ways to hunt small game, detailed below;

====Roaming====
This is when the character is dedicating his efforts entirely to hunting small game. He chooses an area of land he will hunt, and moves through it, following tracks, sounds and any other signs of the game he is seeking. Thus, his movements throughout a day of roaming will be very inconsistent, as he goes from clue to clue, and place to place, looking for the best spot to take the game he is looking for. This type of hunting offers the best chance of success, but also offers a greater chance of encountering dangerous game (orcs and such) in the process.

In a single day, a character may effectively hunt an area that is equal to his total overland movement distance for a day, divided by 4, giving a total in square miles. Thus a character with Speed 15 would hunt 3 square miles, with Speed 20 would hunt 4 square miles, and with Speed 30 would hunt 6 square miles. These numbers are also modified for terrain and other conditions. The larger the area that can be hunted, the lower the DC will be. The type of terrain and its remoteness from the village will also effect the DC.

A typical DC for a day of small game hunting while roaming would be 10.

====Travelling====
This is when the hunter is moving in a relatively straight line through the wilderness that he wishes to hunt. The PC's may often employ this method, as they travel from Eastwitch, into the Wilds, and back again. It offers less of a chance of taking any game (as the character doesn’t go far out of his way to pursue signs of game), but it does allow greater distances to be traveled overland in a day.

The main factor that affects the DC of this type of hunting is how fast the hunter is moving as a ratio of his total speed. It is best if the hunter can move at half speed (he has time to stop once in a while to look and listen), and worst if he has to travel at full speed. Other factors may also affect the DC, such as other non-hunters travelling with the hunting PC, and the abundance of game along the path he is following.

A typical DC for a hunter moving at half-pace over a trail that is not often hunted would be 12.

====Sit and Wait====
This is the favoured hunting style for older hunters from the village who don't want to travel long or far. The hunter basically sits quietly in one spot and waits to see what comes to him. While close to the village, there is less of a chance of this technique being successful, but once a hunter is a few miles out of the village, this can be a good way to hunt, and also avoid running into creatures that might hunt you in return.

A typical DC for a hunter waiting just outside of the village in one spot would be 14.

===Large Game===
Hunting large game is a little more involved. The character first has to find game to hunt, and then must engage it (using the combat rules), and bring it down. Unlike small game hunting, large game hunters can team together to have a greater chance of bringing down an animal. Large game hunters can use the methods detailed above (roaming, travelling and sit-and-wait), which usually have a DC of 2 higher (given the greater abundance of small game in the Eastwitch area). They have other means of hunting as well. Group hunting should be resolved with a single WL check, using the WL of the character organizing the hunt. Modifiers to this check are given below. One example of group hunting (dogging) is described below, but characters may come up with other plans that the DM will work out a DC for.

Once the game is encountered, the DM will indicate how far away it is, and a regular encounter will ensue from there. The character may attempt to get closer to the creature using whatever means he has, but regular rules for surprise will be used, and most animals will flee when faced with a human.

====Dogging====
This is when at least two hunters act together in order to scare game towards a 'shooter'. Typically, a 'shooter' or shooters stay in one position, preferably a line, while another hunter (or more) moves through the terrain in a loud manner, in order to push game towards the shooters. This loud hunter is called the 'dogger', as this job is usually done with loud, barking dogs. If successful, game should be scared towards the shooting line, and brought down. A group of hunters using the dogging method rolls a single WL check, using the WL skill of the hunter who organizes the hunt (who may not necessarily be the dogger, shooter, or best hunter in the group). This method of hunting offers the best chances for a large group of hunters to bring down large game, but it also offers the best chances for encountering dangerous game.

===Hunting Specific Animals===
A hunter may wish to hunt for only specific creatures on a given day of hunting. The DM is notified of this, and then adjusts the DC of the hunt according to the scarcity of the chosen creature. This typically adds at least +2 to the DC. However, as with all hunting checks, the actual DC is known only to the DM. This rule is applied to both small and large game.

===Skill Check Modifiers===
These are all criteria that affect WL skill checks related to hunting. Only one synergy bonus can be applied to the roll. Only one Attack bonus modifier can be applied to the roll. To get the bonus from an extra hunter, that character must make a WL check DC 10. The bonus for multiple hunters can only be added to rolls for large game hunting. To get the trained dog bonus, the animals must be properly trained, and the dogger must make a Handle Animal check at DC 10 for each animal. Dogs can also be used to hunt small game, but only confer a +1 bonus to the WL check.

===Skinning Animals===

Because trading in animal pelts may be one of the primary means for characters to make any money in some adventures, it is necessary to detail this. Properly skinning an animal means that the character will be able to use the pelt to craft some garment, or that it may be sold in the village for it's true value. Characters with 4 ranks or more in the Profession skills of Hunter, Guide, Tanner or Leather Worker need not roll to properly skin any hide; they are assumed to have enough ability to retrieve any skin without damaging it. Other characters may make a wisdom ability check of DC 10 for small game and 15 for large game in order to retrieve the pelt without damaging it. Even damaged pelts will be worth something, but not the full value.

==Warm Clothing==

Eastwitch is located in a very temperate, continental region. Therefore, the summers are warm, but the winters can be deathly cold. During the winter months (from about late November to late March), the temperature ranges from 40 degrees F at the beginning and end, to -10 degrees F in the dead of winter. The rules in the DMG on page 86 deal with the dangers associated with these temperatures.

To overcome the danger of cold, characters have to bundle up, donning thick and heavy clothing. Below, warm clothing has been classified by five levels; very light, light, medium, heavy, and very heavy.

Examples of Warm Clothing: For quick, generic statistics for warm clothing, assume the following;

*Very Light clothing is equivalent to the explorer's outfit (page 108 in the PHB)
*Light clothing costs 15 gp and weighs 12 lbs.
*Medium clothing costs 20 gp and weighs 16 lbs.
*Heavy clothing costs 25 gp and weighs 20 lbs.
*Very Heavy clothing costs 30 gp and weighs 25 lbs.

Many gamers enjoy and respect fine detail, however, and can use the following guidelines to calculate the level of protection that is offered by individual pieces of clothing. Each of the items on the list below offers one level of protection.

*Linen fullcloth
*Hide armour
*Woolen outer-clothes (a complete set of breeches and chemise, or a dress, for example)
*Woolen socks and mittens, good quality boots and light headgear combined
*Woolen or light fur cloak
*Woolen or light fur surcoat

Each of the items on the list below offers two levels of protection.

*Woolen fullcloth
*Heavy fur surcoat
*Heavy fur cloak.
*Woolen socks, linen socks, fur mittens, heavy fur hat, and high quality boots combined.

The levels are cumulative (layers of clothing are a great way to protect against the cold). One level of protection equals Very Light clothing, two levels equals Light clothing, three levels equals Medium clothing, four levels equals Heavy clothing and five levels equals Very Heavy clothing.

It is possible that characters will find themselves in situations where they have some cold weather gear on, but not quite enough (such as wearing Medium clothing in temperatures of -30 F). This will offer partial protection. Information on partial protection in the cold is found in the DM section.
