Lyrics by Benjamin Wrightson, 1684
----

==Part I==

:In the autumn bleak of ‘sixty-four
:At the farm up on the Heights,
:Good Ridge Dunlogan hung pipes that play
:For miles on wind-swept nights.

:In the slightest breeze, the pipes would moan
:Through the orchards fair and long;
:That ghostly air still sounds today,
:And sings Dunlogan's Song.

:The few who’ve heard would fain forget
:That weird and woeful wail,
:And if you’d learn their lone lament,
:Turn your ears unto this tale.

:Good Ridge Dunlogan, of Eastwitch born
:Was a stoic soul indeed,
:With an orchard large and sheep to tend,
:And five small mouths to feed.

:His days were long but he seldom frowned
:When labour bent his back,
:His toiling ceased one autumn night,
:When he fell to a ravening pack.

:The baleful beasts bayed, ferociously foaming,
:Their wicked eyes windows to Hell.
:As Dunlogan fought, far away in the gloaming,
:His pipes played their master’s death knell.

:Thus Ruth Ann Dunlogan, young, winsome and wan
:Was orphaned by wolves at fifteen,
:Charged with four sisters, a flock and a farm,
:In a year when the harvest was lean.

:A matriarch maiden, Ruth saw to her sisters,
:But the unwanted charge wore her raw.
:When she turned to her kinfolk, as the fox to the coop,
:Came the wretch Zebulon Kavenaugh.

:At Zebulon’s birth, his mother'd expired
:And his father, struck senseless by loss
:Hideously hanged himself high by the neck
:As his babe bayed beneath Canaan's Cross.

:Thus orphaned, unwanted and scorned by the world
:The boy cringed and crept through his life.
:He grew up as cruel as a midwinter storm
:He grew up as sharp as a knife.

:Fortune recoiled from that evil-eyed son,
:No gesture came gracious or kind,
:Naught came from friends, so friends came to naught
:In Zebulon Kavenaugh’s mind.

:Zebulon courted his young cousin Ruth
:And he hungered for Dunlogan’s land
:He flatter'd with falsehoods, ‘till, in spite of herself
:Ruth reluctantly gave him her hand.

:Lonely and lean was the small ceremony,
:For the Heights are a solid day's ride,
:Kavenaugh's kinfolk cared naught for the nuptials;
:Ruth’s sisters played maids for the bride.

:Five wedding bells chimed as the five sisters wept
:Five mockingbirds sang from their perch
:And high on the barn, old Dunlogan’s pipes
:Conducted the desolate dirge.

:His concern so settled, the registrar rode out
:As soon as the couple was wed,
:Then Zebulon Kavenaugh, with a leer in his eye
:Pulled Dunlogan’s daughter to bed.

:Refusing her husband, Ruth hied from the house,
:To take to the trees in her flight
:But Zebulon Kavenaugh ran her to ground,
:That violent conjugal night.

:The sinister spouse’s degenerate thirst
:Was slaked on his newlywed bride,
:But his lot had been cast 'neath Saint Canaan's Cross
:And Fortune was not on his side.

:The deed duly done, 'neath a mist-covered moon,
:The pipes pining low in the air.
:That unearthly music was joined in lament
:as Ruth Ann wept cold bitter tears.

:But at the wan woman’s wailing, the gale grew fierce,
:The pipes sounded shrill through the Wild,
:And from the dark drew a drove of dastardly orcs,
:To doom the cruel groom and the child.

:Zebulon cowered, Ruth screamed in fear,
:The stench of the beasts choked their chests,
:But a westerly wind met the gathering storm,
:A grim wind of steel and of death.

:A Warden’s black blades begged no pardon that day,
:Not from flesh, nor from bone, nor from blood.
:The screams of Or’uk joined in Dunlogan’s Song,
:As it wailed on the winds through the wood.

:The Warden's dread dance did deliberately slow,
:As his gaze caught Zebulon’s eye.
:With a face quite as cold as a corpse in the snow
:He strode to the sad scoundrel’s side.

:Frantically the fiends fled, fully afraid
:As the stalwart stood guard o’er the knave,
:But he strangely stayed silent as they bore off the bride
:To a fate in their festering cave.

:The rapist was rescued; the lass led away!
:Surely a more terrible theme,
:Cannot been found in the foulest of fables,
:Yet such was the grim Warden’s scheme.

:Zebulon Kavenaugh looked up in dread,
:To look on the conquering Shade,
:And he flinched at the eyes that flamed fierce in the foxlight,
:As the forester made known his fate.

:\"You’ve murdered that maiden,” the Warden spoke softly,
:“You’ll bear the full weight of your wrong,
:And I’ll punish you best by permitting you breath
:So long as the winds play this song.

:\"For your woman’s been won by those wretched Or’Uk
:She’ll be worked as a slave and to breed.
:Such suffering she’ll know to the edge of her grave
:For your sin of ungovernable greed.

:“Hark to the music that haunts the Heights here
:And weep when it whispers its song
:And if ever you tamper with Dunlogan’s pipes
:You’ll die seven deaths before dawn.”

:Thus Zebulon Kavenaugh’s sole gift in life –
:The only he ever received,
:Was the gaunt Warden’s sentence of sorrowful song
:On the night he was wed, and bereaved.

:Cravenly Kavenaugh fled through the night
:His features grown haggard and drawn.
:And from that day forward, he hid on the Heights
:And hearkened to Dunlogan's Song.

== Part II ==

Verses were added in 1690, author unknown;

:The pipes on the barn up at Dunlogan’s Farm
:Still sound their sad song in the south,
:Though Kavenaugh’s form is now food for the worm,
:And the tale turned to rumour from truth.

:Though wise folk will race now away from that place
:Where Dunlogan’s pipes gently play,
:His girl Ruth, it is sworn, still walks in some form
:‘Round the farm of her childhood days.

:In ethereal guise her spectre now flies,
:Through the trees by the house on the Heights,
:And once in a while, her ghostly face smiles
:As she hums to her father’s sad pipes.

:She surveys the lands that Dunlogan’s hands
:Once laboured to nurture and grow,
:And there's a tear in her eye, at the memory why,
:It was lost to them long years ago.

:The dead will know best why they never can rest
:When grief and sorrow run strong.
:And for Ruth Ann Dunlogan, the sorrow unspoken
:Sings softly in Dunlogan's Song.

:Fin.
