== History of Eastwitch Grange==

In 1467 Phin Eastwitch and his wife Marla II came to the present day location of Eastwitch. In that first Summer they built a large log cabin atop the hill where Eastwitch Grange presently sits. In the following spring, having decided that they would put down roots here, they hired a team of skilled stone workers from the City to begin construction of a grand manor. They plant the great Walnut in the front yard that still grows there to this day. 

Construction took seven years to complete. During this time Phin and Marla II subsisted completely by hunting and foraging, and managed to feed the entire work crew by this means. By 1475 the modern day version of Eastwitch Grange was complete. Several out-buildings are also built, including a barn, a house for the hired hands and some sheds.

In those early years (up until Thurston Eastwitch and his daughter Paraclesa III moved back to Waldheim, sometime around 1591) the house was a place of great activity. Many hunters from many parts of the realms came to stay under the roof of Eastwitch Grange, and hunt the plentiful game in the area. The Family kept a score of servants and hired hands, and had established an enormous and fertile farm.

However, a marked decline in activity at the house is recorded in many documents and stories in Eastwitch. After Thurston and Paraclesa III moved to the City, the house became less inviting and kind. Fewer hunters came to the area (as the business owners of the time noted), and many of the hands at the house began to disappear. It is unknown whether they simply left Eastwitch all together, or whether they came to a more sinister end, but they began to vanish none-the-less.

Thurston and Paraclesa III returned to Eastwitch Grange shortly after going to the City. Paraclesa had married, giving birth to a boy named Arlos whilst in the City. A second boy, Phaelos, was born upon her return to Eastwitch. Her husband did not come back with her.

By the time the last Eastwitch, Marla III, was born, the manor was nearly empty. Arlos was living in the City, and the few Eastwitch Family members remaining at the house let go all their hired help. The names of the remaining family members at this time (around 1650) are sketchy, and may have been jumbled about with time and many tellings of this story. It is well known that Phaelos and his sons, Hezekiah and Obadiah were still in Eastwitch, though living as much in the woods as in the manor. It is said that Paraclesa III still lived, and hunted with her son and grandsons. Further, it is told that Merridyth Eastwitch was also hunting with her daughter, grandson, and great grandsons. 

It is well known and documented that Arlos Eastwitch died in the City in 1660. His daughter Marla is known to have married Obanyon Hannoch, and took his name. By this time, none had seen any of the Eastwitch Matriarchs, Phaelos or his sons for many years. The house was empty at this time.

Marla and her husband returned in 1666 to Eastwitch Grange with a great retinue. It is unknown what their intentions were, but they stayed only a couple nights, before fleeing Eastwitch. Many stories still circulate about their visit to their inherited home, but there is little evidence as to the actual events. It is rumored that at least one, perhaps several of their attendants went missing. It is also known that Obanyon, whose family had made its wealth by exploiting the wild places in the realms, hired a guide and went into the Great Wood. It is rumored that he narrowly escaped death in that Wilderness, and that he may have crossed paths with Hezekiah, Obadiah, or maybe even Phaelos or Paraclesa III.

Eastwitch Grange was completely unoccupied until 1739, when Orbul Hannoch returned to lay Noble Claim to the village (this event is detailed elsewhere). Orbul, of course, left Eastwitch and has never returned since. His eldest son, Pol, stayed on with a very small retinue, and has dwelt in the manor since.

Pol has since had many of the fields reclaimed by the plow, and has restored Eastwitch Grange to a livable condition. But the years of neglect and dereliction have had their toll on the place. While the place is inhabited again, it still has the look of a ghost of a building. In the youth, it inspires fear, and in the elderly sorrow.

Pol has done much to restore the economy of the manor, tilling the fields once again. And he leads the occasional hunt. But many note that he has made little effort to make the house warm and comfortable again. As the most eligible bachelor in Eastwitch, it surprises many that he takes no company of women. But others whisper that he lives only partially in the realm of civilization, with one foot in the realm of the Wild. Thus he takes no measure to restore the home to a comfortable state, and takes not civilized bride.

== Structural Features ==

The two most striking structural features of the manor are the thick stone walls and the huge timbers that were used to construct the place. The walls on the ground floor are 5’ thick, and sit on a 10’ thick foundation of dry-laid stone that has barely budged in almost 300 years. The original timbers are still intact for the most part as well. Huge oaks were used to construct the floor and ceiling trusses, and these too have not given in all the years since their harvesting.

As can be seen in the Green Grizzly, the Masons have used thick stair cases at each end of the structure to act as buttresses. Thick fireplaces and contoured archways to the external doors act as good buttresses for the north and south walls.

All doors and windows on the external walls of the manor have been built with 8” of solid stone as jams. Thus, each door and each shutter abuts to this 8” jam, making them very difficult to force open from the outside. The doors and shutters themselves are constructed of 4” thick hardwood (varieties of oak and maple). Thus, these openings are very difficult to force. 

The entire place is heated by 7 large fireplaces. The fireplaces are not the most efficient in design, and though on the ground floor they are 10’ in width, the building is, on the whole, very difficult to heat. There is absolutely no running water in this place. There is a very reliable well not distant from the north of the building, and outdoor jakes are still used by the inhabitants and visitors.

=== First Floor Details ===

The first floor is the most open and rugged of the entire structure. Most of the windows on this floor are extremely old, and have turned nearly opaque. The exterior walls are covered in creeping vines, moss and lichens. The inside is mostly dark and cold. 

====The Foyer====
This is a very remarkable room. It is open to the second floor, which has balconies that surround it on three sides. On the south wall, high upon the wall, there are 6 tall, thin windows that used to cast a good light into the room. They are dim now, however. The floor in this room is solid flagstone that sits right on the foundation. Thus the floor is very cold, and tends to be damp through much of the year. A great chandelier hangs from the ceiling, suspended 15’ from the floor. This is a great wooden structure with the remnants of scores of old candles, but it is rarely light these days. In fact, the only lights left running in this room are a pair of candles at the bottom of the grand staircase. The staircase itself is quite impressive, being constructed of solid stone, surfaced with thick marble which must have been imported into the area at some point in the distant past.

Furnishings in the Foyer are sparse. There is a pair of sturdy oak benches on the south wall, and some small tables and chairs tucked away in the northern corners of the place, but nothing else. There is a thick red carpet which invites the visitor to walk up the grand staircase to the gallery; this was once a very fine carpet which was given as a present from a noble-hunter from the City. But close inspection will reveal that it is nearly threadbare now. Further, it is a little moldy on the underside, and is the source of the musty smell which permeates this room.

====The Kitchen====
This is actually the warmest room of the house. A great fireplace is found on the northern wall, but this is not used anymore. Rather, a more modern cast-iron stove has been installed on the south wall, which serves for food preparation and warmth. The shelves and tables in this room are in a good state of repair, as are the storage rooms. The large table in the centre of the room which formerly served as only a prep table, is now the chief dining table for the current inhabitants of the place. Indeed, most functions are performed in this room now. The kitchen is lit by several candles and a pair of lamps found on the table.

====The Hall====
This large room was formerly used for most entertaining. The cherry wood flooring is polished to a bright sheen from countless foot falls from glorious days past. Two large and ornate glass chandeliers hang from the 12’ ceiling, though these are never lit anymore. The walls in this room (and also in the Dining Room, The Library and the Trophy Room) are lined with hardwood panels 8’ high. These give the room a warmer appearance as they cover the bare stone, and do serve a very small insulating roll. The carpets in this room are not musty, but are rather threadbare. The room is nearly empty at present, as most of the furniture has been stacked in the southeast corner of the room.

The walls of this room are covered in countless antlers from several species of animal. There are several mounted heads from various species of deer and bear. Several skins hang upon the walls as well. These hanging skins are the source of the rather leathery-smell which permeates this room. There are also several pictures hung upon the walls. These are mostly of hunting and wilderness scenes, but one picture in particular is striking; a well-framed picture (4’x 8’) hangs above the northern mantle. This painting depicts a very shapely woman leaning against a walnut tree. She and the tree are mostly a silhouette, with a bright moon over her shoulder. However, her eyes are bright and intense, and a large knife is seen in her free hand. The inhabitants of the house call this painting “The Witch”, though they remember not why.

====Dining Room====
The predominant feature of this room is the enormous table in its centre. This is a solid walnut table, 4” thick, 15’ long and 7 ½’ wide. It is surrounded by several walnut benches, rather than the plush chairs one would expect in such a room. The table and benches sit upon a great carpet which has a very curious design, and made of a wool that does not bear similarity to that woven in this area. Above the table a large chandelier hangs. It is covered in stubs of candles, and is rarely used these days. The walls are lined with several large cabinets, many benches and chairs, and smaller tables. The room could easily accommodate about 50 diners with all these furnishings, but it is likely that 50 people have not eaten in this room all told, in over 50 years. The usual preponderance of antlers, heads, skins and furs line the walls all the way to the ceiling in the Dining room, and this room too has a very leathery, musty smell.

====Library====
This is a very impressive room, and easily the most musty in the whole manor. On three walls, book shelves rise to 8’ in height. These seem to be constructed of solid walnut stock. They are not full of books, but there are easily over 800 books in this room, making this the largest literary collection in Eastwitch. Many of the books are hundreds of years old, and only a rare few are less than 50 years old. The shelves could accommodate a couple thousand more books, but rather are littered with countless miscellaneous items. Scores of mounted small animals, scores of animal skulls, a couple human and humanoid skulls, countless items of pottery of various qualities, a couple water clocks, some fine scales, a multitude of small sculpture, many works of fine silver and goldsmithing, many small boxes, and many other things too numerous to list. The shelves are absolutely covered in this miscellany.

A thick red carpet covers the oak flooring, which is as threadbare as the other carpets on this floor. The room is furnished predominantly by a large mahogany table measuring 10’x 10’, towards the centre of the room. This table is covered with the same sort of clutter as is found on the shelves, and it is surrounded by a dozen well-crafted mahogany chairs. There are several smaller tables throughout the room, and couple plush chairs (which are somewhat dusty and give off a funny smell). A pair of small ladders are used to reach the higher shelves. Upon most of the tables are found many candle holders and lamps. The ceiling has 4 small ceiling lamps.

The upper reaches of the ceiling are decorated with (the seemingly requisite) antlers and hunting paraphernalia. On the western wall, between the two windows, are hung four portraits of women who bear a very slight resemblance to the rest of the Eastwitch family, though there is no plaques beneath them.

====Trophy Room====
This room is packed with great mounted creatures of all sorts. It is a very dark and musty room, though, and the only light reaching it is from the candles in the Foyer, and the candle on the mid-landing of the stairs to the west. There is a path through the furry clutter of this room, which is covered in the largest bearskin rugs that can be found in the manor, and likely in many parts of the world.

There are literally countless sets of antlers on the upper reaches of the walls of this room. The lower, paneled portions of the walls are hung with hundreds of skins and scores of spears, bows, knives and other weapons. Standing around the perimeter of the room are dozens of stuffed bears, deer and other game. The centre of the room is similarly packed with these objects. Many tables are interspersed in this mounted menagerie, and these are covered in smaller trophies and other strange things. At least one bugbear is known to have ended up in the middle of the room as a trophy, and there are several orc heads and other body parts found here and there in the room. If a good look was provided at the multitude of trophies in this room, the viewer would simply be overwhelmed. 

====Stair Cases====
A pair of thick stone staircases are found at the east and west ends of the manor. These extend all the way up to the third floor. On the east stair, the top of the steps come to a trapdoor in the ceiling which opens onto the rampart. The stairs themselves are solid stone on the ground floor, and thick hardwood on the upper levels. Each landing has a candelabra in each corner, though these are rarely lit.

=== Second Floor ===

The exterior walls of the second floor are 2 ½’ thick. The interior walls are sturdy hardwood paneling, and the doors are all sets of hardwood double doors. The ceiling is 10’. Few visitors have come to the second floor of Eastwitch Grange, though there are guest bedrooms here that are used periodically.

====Gallery====
This impressive room is bordered on the south by the grand stair and two great arches, on the east and west each by a pair of arches, and the north wall is covered in portraits of the family. The floor is covered with a great rug from some distant desert culture. This room would be well-lit from the large windows on the south wall of the Foyer, but they are too dim to permit much light these days. There are also a pair of great chandeliers hung from the ceiling, but these too are dim now.

The dominant feature of this room is the north wall, with its many portraits. The largest portraits are those of Phin and Marla Eastwitch, which are found on each side of the family coat of arms which is carved into a solid block of stone, above the mantle. Each of these portraits is 4’x 8’, and each is similarly eerie. The details of these portraits are the subject of ages-old rumors in the village, though each has to be seen by the individual.

====West Hall====
This hall gives an impressive view of the Foyer and Gallery below. It is also hung with dozens of portraits of various people; some who are clearly relatives of the Eastwitch family, as well as favored servants from the past. Visitors are not permitted in the West Hall, however.

East Hall: This hall is decorated with dozens of paintings of wilderness and hunting scenes, as well as some notable guests from the past. There are many smaller trophies hung on the walls in this hall as well.

====Bedrooms====

*Bedroom 4 (North East Guest Room): This is a large room which has 4 old beds in it. It is well furnished with many trunks, cabinets, armoires, desks, chairs, and a large table in the middle. The walls are not as littered as most of the others in the manor, but are tastefully hung with paintings of various sorts. There is one in particular, found on the north wall which has always disturbed visitors. This is a painting of some ancient Eastwitch woman crouching above the carcass of some great beast with a shiny, black armored hide (similar to a spider). Her face has an expression of both rage and terror which is hard to decipher. Her eyes are always thought to observe the visitor, regardless of his location in the room.

*Bedroom 5 (Phaelos’ Guest Room): This room is similar to Bedroom 4, but there are only 2 beds in it. On the south wall, above the mantle, there is a painting of three men walking along some forest path, up a hill of hardwood trees. The two in the rear (closest to the viewer) have their backs turned to the viewer, but the lead one (in the background) is looking over his shoulder at the viewer. A single gleaming eye espies the viewer, and the chops hanging from his face are unmistakable.

*Bedroom 6 (Southeast Guest Room): This room is similar to Bedroom 4. There are 4 musty beds and plenty of furniture, paintings and trophies. This room is decorated with many paintings depicting sites from the City, and was likely used by guests from this place. One decoration of note is found on the north wall; a finely crafted spear with a gleaming head is hung on the wall, which has a severed human hand still clutching it. The severed end of the hand has been capped by a silver fixture, and the hand seems to be well preserved. It has several rings upon it’s fingers, one of which is clearly a signet ring of some important man from a far-off place.

=== Third Floor ===

No guests are permitted upon the third floor, except upon the rampart on some occasions.

====Rampart====
This feature of the manor offers the visitor and inhabitants of Eastwitch Grange the best view of the Great Wood which can be had. The visitor, standing up here, can see with his own eye, pictures that have been recorded in oil on some of the paintings in the house below. Certainly, this large open space offers a fantastic view of over 270 degrees of the surrounding country side. There are two ways to get to the Rampart; from the trap door at the top of the east stair, and from a small door down a single step which leads to the attic. There is a single window beside this door, but it’s shutters have been nailed shut.
