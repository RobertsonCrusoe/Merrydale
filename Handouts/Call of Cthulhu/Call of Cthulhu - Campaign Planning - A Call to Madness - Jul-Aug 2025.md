"My idea for a Call of Cthulhu campaign is starting to take shape. By
necessity, secrecy is key, but I'll give you a couple of things I'm
thinking of, and happy to take your input:

1. Timeline setting is the standard 1920's. I considered the 1890's as
well as the 1940's, 1950's and 1980's, but unless any of you feel strongly
about the setting, I think the default 1920's will work just fine for what
I have in mind. A mixture of Great War trauma, plenty of blank empty spaces
left on the world map, and rudimentary modern technology and ideas.

2. Think about what character you want to play. CoC works best with normal
people. We will roll them all up in a session 0 at some point, so don't
worry about specifics yet, but I'd be curious to know what kind of
investigator you'd like to play.

3. I'm going to set it here in Ontario, using Toronto as our principal home
base. I think it works best since we all know the city well, and have a
good sense of the geography. However that doesn't mean your character has
to be from Toronto or even Canadian.

5. Let me know if there is anything you've already played or are familiar
with so I don't use it. For example, if it was done on the GCP shows I'm
not using it.

6. Happy to hear any thoughts or inputs on what you'd like from a Call of
Cthulhu campaign. I've got a story and a plan, but it is easily adaptable.
If you'd rather play in Victorian London, I can do that. If you'd rather
be more action-oriented we can use the Pulp Cthulhu rules, etc."

PART 5: GUS ON RADIO AND THE 1921 SETTING — GUS MIRANDA (27 Jul 2025)
----------------------------------------------------------------------
Source: Message 1984dd5dcb1243d4

"I'm not going to be too hung up on verisimilitude in a make-believe game
of cosmic horror, but I should specify that my intention is to start us in
1921. The first commercial radio station in the country started up in
Montreal in 1919, and the first in Toronto came in 1922.

It's funny because I was just at Signal Hill in St John's learning all
about Marconi and the first radio broadcasts.

However, amateur and private radio was absolutely a thing. During the war
it had been outlawed in case the Hun was listening, but plenty of people
still hid their transmitters and broadcast anyway. It was a bit of a free
for all. The main problem was receivers, and it wasn't until the Marconi
Company of Canada began selling receivers commercially in 1921 that radio
really took off.

(It really is coincidental that I was being a nerd about radio and Marconi
at Signal Hill just a few days ago, so I have all this on my mind.)

However, I like the idea you've got, and would suggest perhaps a washed out
print journalist for The Toronto World (it went out of business in 1921, so
you'd be fresh unemployed) who might also operate an amateur radio station
from your attic or something like that.)

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Toronto_World"


PART 6: STEPHEN'S CHARACTER CONCEPT — STEPHEN ROBERTSON (27 Jul 2025)
---------------------------------------------------------------------
Source: Message 1984f1e2105323b7

"I'm really looking forward to this.

I'm considering basing my investigator on my great-great-uncle. He was a
missionary in China during the collapse of the Qing Empire and was later
commissioned by the British army to help direct the Chinese Labour Corps in
France. The CLC was a massive effort involving 140,000 labourers brought to
Europe from China. Eighty-one thousand of them came to Canada across the
Pacific, then traveled in secret, sealed trains from Vancouver to Halifax
to ship out to Europe. (On the topic of Middenheim, there was a $500 head
tax on any Chinese who came to Canada back then, so this was all done off
the books). In France, these workers handled everything from trench digging
to munitions transport behind the lines. I don't know many of the details
of my uncle's service, but after he returned to Canada, he worked for the
League of Nations for a spell before going back to China to teach history
in Sichuan.

For my character, I'm thinking of someone whose missionary work had an edge
of the mystical about it - spreading the Gospel while becoming deeply
interested in Taoist philosophy and Chinese demonology. Seeing how the
Chinese workers applied centuries-old spiritual technologies to navigate
the industrial-scale horrors of the Western Front would have triggered a
crisis of faith for him. Returning to Toronto after the war, he seeks out
orientalists and scholars to make sense of the things he witnessed. The
ROM, founded in 1912, would be actively collecting Asian artifacts in 1921,
so he might cultivate contacts with Sinologist curators there. He's
essentially a man caught between worlds - too changed by his experiences to
return to orthodox Christianity, but unable to fully abandon his
theological training."

PART 17: GUS ON NEWFOUNDLAND AND TRAVEL — GUS MIRANDA (1 Aug 2025)
-------------------------------------------------------------------
Source: Message 198668874ce06018

"That's a great choice. I was inspired for this on my trip to Newfoundland
and stories of Newfoundlanders in the Great War and what they did
afterwards. The War Museum here in Ottawa is spectacular. Definitely worth
the visit if you want to come into town one day.

And just last night I was reading about the interior boat travel as it
happens. Steamship service was regular between Ottawa and Montreal on the
Ottawa River, and it used to be said you could walk to Montreal over all
the lumber barges being floated downriver, though this was eventually all
replaced by rail and Highway 17. The Rideau Canal had regular passenger
service on paddle boats between Ottawa and Kingston (and it still does
even today on the Kawartha Voyageur
https://ontariowaterwaycruises.com/cruises/rideau/)

I don't know about ships between Montreal and Kingston, but I'll look into
that. There was definitely lots of boat traffic between Kingston and
Toronto."
