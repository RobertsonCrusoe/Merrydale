A Revealing Conversation
Campaign: Gonzadrel / The Fringe
Source: Email thread, February 1-5, 2025
Author: Stephen Robertson (player-written narrative)
Characters: Liltariel (Stephen), Fishook the Hazardous (Les)

---

[I forget whether Fishook rode in the wagon on the way to the Keep, but for the purposes of this narrative, he did.]

The ramshackle wagon rattled across the blasted heath under a bruised sky, its wooden wheels groaning a mournful dirge against the cracked earth. Alwyn the page sat stiffly beside Sister Pristel on the driver's perch, acutely aware of her hand resting on his shoulder, her fingers occasionally tousling his hair as she complimented him on his splendid hygiene. Bondsman Hurl and Ser Pellias rode abreast of each other in front of the trap while Muttonchop carried up the rear on Nimrod, his pony.

In the back of the wagon, sprawled across a tattered rug like a decadent vizier, Fishook the Hazardous puffed languidly on his pipe. The smoke, defying the wind's efforts to disperse it, clung to the wagon, swirling around him in a dense, fragrant cloud. Liltariel sat hunched atop a rickety crate, her gaze fixed on the desolate landscape. The biting wind that tugged at her cloak carried a disturbing undercurrent—the familiar scent of damp earth and thyme was subtly twisted by a sharp, chemical tang, the faintest whisper of something unsettlingly alien. The strange, almost caustic aroma of the corrupted moor was a stark contrast to the sickly-sweet scent of Fishook's pipe, a cloying mix of amber and opium that clung to the wagon like a shroud.

The wizard considered Liltariel through hooded eyes with a gleam of perverse amusement. "Still so pensive, my woodland waif? Five years amongst these… provincials, and you still haven't embraced their earthy charms?"

Liltariel didn't turn. "Charm?" She spat off the side of the wagon. "You humans infest the land like locusts, devouring everything in your path. Your lives are a flicker, your ambitions base, your emotions…baffling. These Fringe-dwellers build their hovels of rot and decay, defiling the very earth they inhabit." Suddenly, her cheeks flushed with anger, and she turned to glare at Fishook. "And that's not merely a metaphor, wizard. Putranos was inevitable. Gonzadrel's 'charming ways' almost cost me my life. Charm? You humans are a plague, Fishook, a slow, creeping blight upon this world."

Fishook put down his pipe and clapped his hands with a low chuckle of delight. "Such vehemence! You almost make me believe you care about this world, my dear. I, of course, simply find the antics of these rustics diverting. A grotesque tragedy of unforced errors, played out on a stage of their own making."

Liltariel scoffed. "Diverting? You find their ignorance and self-destruction diverting?"

Fishook spread his hands and bobbed his head in the caricature of a bow. "Naturally, Your Spriteliness. How could I not? Their capacity for both is truly remarkable! Uncouth, unwashed, uncultured. They are like gladiators in the arena of the mundane, punishing themselves and each other for the amusement of their betters. And what do they gain from this endless struggle? Nothing, of course! These yokels haven't the sophistication to appreciate the delicate aesthetics of pleasure and pain, the exquisite tapestry revealed in violence and vice. They lack any understanding of the moral flexibility of the Old World, you see. They call beauty depravity and tradition cruelty. Simpletons. It's a wonder I am not constantly weeping with laughter."

Shaking his head in wry amusement, the wizard relit his pipe and inhaled deeply. "But enough about them." He dismissed the topic with a puff of smoke. "Tell me again of your sentence, my dear." He leaned forward, his eyes glittering. "A hundred years is a long time even for you, I would venture. What transgression could possibly warrant such an extended… sojourn ... among my race?" A sly grin spread across his face, a lascivious glint in his eyes. "Did you seduce the daughter of a particularly pious priest? Why, if I had a gold coin for every time…"

Liltariel looked away again, her expression hardening. "A child."

Fishook raised an eyebrow, momentarily taken aback. "A child? You… debauched a human child? Excellent!"

"She… died," Liltariel corrected, her voice flat. "It was an accident."

"An accident," Fishook repeated, a note of something like professional admiration creeping into his expression. "And how did this… accident… occur?"

Liltariel hesitated, then took a deep breath. "I… I lured her into the Fickwood." She then hesitated, her fingers twisting in agitation. "To a spring. She… she was… fascinated by me. By the stories I told her. Of the old magic, of the fey realm."

Fishook leaned even closer toward the elf, undisguised covetousness in his hooded eyes.

Liltariel was no longer looking at the wizard. "She would come to the edge of the wood, night after night, drawn by my voice. I… I charmed her. I wove enchantments, subtle whispers, promises of wonder. I made sure she would return home before dawn. She would awaken, safe but exhausted, with only a foggy recollection of her… dreams. I suppose she spoke of these 'dreams' to her family."

"Dreams, eh?" Fishook echoed. A knowing sneer played over his lips, giving his countenance a predatory aspect. "And these dreams… were they of an… innocent nature?"

Liltariel met Fishook's eyes with a significant look. "You and I both know that innocence is a human construct, wizard." But then her eyes grew unfocussed, and a touch of sadness entered her voice as she continued. "She would dance for me, beneath the moonlight. She sang songs I taught her, ancient melodies of the Fickwood. She gave me her… attention. Something these humans rarely offer to anything beyond their own petty concerns."

"And the spring?" Fishook prompted, his voice low and eager.

Liltariel shrugged. "The spring… it was… is… a place of power. The water… it reflects more than just the sky. It reflects… desires. She… she reached for something she couldn't grasp. A power she didn't understand. She fell. She drowned. It was… unfortunate. But it was just one human child."

Fishook was silent for a moment, considering this. "Just one human child," he repeated again softly, weighing the words. "And for this… your elf-kin banished you? For a century and a day?"

"It wasn't just the death," Liltariel said, her voice barely audible over the groaning wheels of the cart. "It was… the consequences. Her family… and there were others." She paused, wrinkling her nose in annoyance. "What do these Fringe-dwellers call it—a 'posse'? They invaded the Fickwood. There was… a confrontation. More died." She looked at Fishook, her eyes filled with a cold, weary disdain. "My kin blamed me. For everything. As if I had purposefully orchestrated some grand tragedy. As if human lives mattered."

Fishook nodded slowly, his expression unreadable. "And so, they sent you away to learn… what, exactly? The sanctity of human life?"

Liltariel gave a bitter laugh. "They sent me to learn why humans are a pestilence best avoided. They thought a century amongst them would dispel my curiosity. They thought I'd return cowed and grateful after the nightmare of exile among you stone-stackers. That I would bring knowledge of your secrets and a willingness to share. But all I feel is betrayal."

Liltariel paused, irritation coming over her face.

What am I doing? My words are spilling out before I can stop them. Why am I telling him this? This preening peacock with his silks and perfumes, his casual cruelty and world-weary cynicism. He is everything I despise about humanity, amplified and exaggerated. And yet, somehow, the company of this degenerate wizard makes me feel almost… comfortable. Maybe it's because he sees the world as I do, a chaotic mess of self-interest and fleeting desires. His nihilism resonates with my own. He simply chooses to take pleasure in the tragic farce of it all. It's a twisted sort of affinity, but I have to admit I find him more tolerable than most of his kind. A dangerous feeling, this kinship with a creature I should despise. But in this empty realm, under this vast uncanopied sky, it's a connection I can't entirely dismiss. It's an argument for keeping him alive, at least.

"They are all so... blind," she continued, her voice low and intense. "Humans race to destroy their world, while my kind stand eternal watch over falling leaves. Both so certain of their purpose." Her lips curled in disgust. "The round-ears think their brief lives give them right to remake the world, while my people imagine their longevity grants them wisdom. Different lies, nothing more."

She turned to face Fishook directly, and for a moment, her mask of cold disdain cracked to reveal something darker. "They're all just different forms of decay. Fast or slow, violent or gentle – it makes no difference. The only truth is entropy, wizard. The only honesty is in acknowledging that none of it matters."

Fishook nodded as his lips curled into a slow, malicious smile. "Indeed, my fey flower. Indeed. Perhaps there is hope for you yet."
