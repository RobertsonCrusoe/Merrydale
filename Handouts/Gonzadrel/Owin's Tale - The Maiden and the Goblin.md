Owin's Tale: The Maiden and the Goblin
Campaign: Gonzadrel / The Fringe
Source: Email thread "Possible new addition to our party in The Fringe", February 10, 2025
Author: Gus Miranda (player-written narrative)
Character: Owin the Bard (Gus), telling a tale aboard the longboat in the Underdark

---

As the longboat drifts in silence along the water, the cavern's hungry darkness being held at bay by Fishook's light spells, Owin claps his hands, sits up, and addresses the motley group of listless passengers:

"Well, it looks like we may be stranded in these caves for quite some time. Perhaps I'll tell a story? Let me see...

A great number of seasons ago, you may recall, I had been cursed by the Melusine to wander the world, evermore in search of my lost home. As days turned into years, my legs and heart grew heavier with weariness. I was worn down and I felt I could no longer bear it.

Just when I thought I could go on no more, I spied plumes of smoke on the horizon, climbing up into the sky from somewhere in the woods before me. 'A village,' thought I! 'A place to rest my tired bones!'

I stumbled forward through the forest until at last I came upon the hamlet, nestled deep within and hidden well inside a grove of ancient trees. The smell of woodfire curled invitingly through the air, the small thatched cottages a most welcome sight. And yet, a palpable unease hung heavy upon the air.

I came upon the first house, and knocked firmly at its door. 'What ho!' said I, 'well met! I am but a tired traveller seeking shelter for the night. Might I beseech you for a kindness?'

Silence from inside. Yet I could see from the smoke billowing from the chimney that a family dwelt within.

I thought no more of it, and moved on to the neighbour's door. I knocked and knocked and said, 'What ho! Hello? I am but a pilgrim, sapped of all my strength. Might I implore you, please, to kindly let me lay and rest?'

Again the silence from within.

'What is the matter in this town?' I thought, 'that a gentle traveller such as I, can be met with such unkindness.'

I put those thoughts out of my head, and moved on and knocked on the next door.

I knocked and knocked, and so I said, 'What ho! Hello? I am but shattered misery upon these legs! Please be hospitable, and let me rest myself one night beneath your roof.'

Such was the plea I made, for I did not think I could carry on beyond.

To my surprise, the door swung open! I was greeted by a maiden whose beauty rivalled both the dawn and dusk! She glowed upon this world with such sweet and warming light. I stood struck dumb, all memory of the road distantly forgotten.

And yet, I could discern a shadow in her eyes. Some great worry troubled her that night.

'I bid thee welcome,' said she, with a voice as gentle as a summer's breeze rustling in the leaves.

'I wish that I could offer the hospitality of old, but truth be known, our little homestead is besieged. I can not offer you much more than this: a warning to move on, be on your way, think no more of this village and of me.'

Well, I am soft-hearted, truth be told. I had been moved by her great beauty, and the sadness in her eyes.

'Pray, tell me my lady, what troubles this village so much, that all your neighbours have locked themselves tight inside their homes?'

I'd like to think that she in me saw some form of gallantry, or chivalry. But I think desperation, more than likely, truthfully.

'Dear sir, you see there is a creature, foul and mean, who stalks the night and robs our village! It comes upon us in the dark, and takes away our chickens and our sheep. We are afraid that in the winter we shall starve, as the monster preys on us and our livelihood keeps dwindling!'

At this tears began to stream from her eyes, and what could I do but take her in my arms and offer consolation of a kind.

She looked at me, her freckled face wet and flushed. I am but a simple man, and simple men like me can not resist the pleasant pleading of a lady fair and kind.

'I am sorry!' she said, leaving my embrace, 'Our once safe and happy home is almost at an end.'

She turned back to me, and warmly pressed her hand upon my chest. 'We have strong men aplenty in this town, but none have won against this menace. I have prayed for a brave and cunning hero to deliver us from this malevolence. Now here you are! Will you not help us?'

And so I say again, what hardness of heart could possibly resist such a petition?

'I know this is our plight,' said she, 'but I can see that you are wise and kind. I ask you please, if you can see to it to rid us of this evil, then I promise you can have me as your bride!'

I had spent what seemed a lifetime lost upon the roads; known little but hunger and danger and discomfort. My muscles, my bones and my soul were all exhausted and spent. I had prayed to the gods to deliver me from such a miserable existence, and here was their salvation!

And so I agreed to take up this task and rid the village of its plague, and in return take the maiden's hand in marriage and with her, settle in that house.

I knew the menace preyed upon their livestock, so I devised a plan to lure the culprit to the maiden's henhouse where I would lay hidden in wait, and pounce upon the villain.

The night grew darker, and the hours passed. My eyes were heavy with exhaustion as I counted the chirps of the crickets in the distance. Just as I was prepared to end that wretched game, silence fell, and I spied a small figure across the yard. I froze in expectation, knowing that surprise was on my side.

I waited, and waited, and nothing seemed to come. I closed my eyes for just a second, but then, a small flicker of shadow, and a guttural giggle! A greasy hand reached out, opening the coop's door. Before I could react, a goblin, no bigger than a badger swung in and grabbed the plumpest hen, and with a blur of green skin and razor sharp teeth, vanished back into the night!

The rooster began its sunrise call, and I sat alone, covered in feathers and shame, having been outwitted!

I vowed then and there that the next night would be different. I had made a promise to the lady. With her leave, I slept and rested throughout the day, so that I would be ready for the dark's endeavour.

As noon fell into dusk, I was prepared. I had smeared myself with mud and leaves becoming one with the forest floor. I buried a wide-mouthed pot near the sheep's pen, its large opening disguised with grass, hay and leaves.

Again I waited, passing the time counting the cricket chirps, until silence overtook them.

This time I saw it. A dark shape creeping like a shadow out of the woods, making not a sound. With ginger steps, it came up to the pen. I could see its avaricious eyes gleaming with anticipation as he stretched out its hands for the maiden's most prized ewe. And as it almost had it, it gave out a disgruntled yelp as he fell into my pot! I leapt up out of my hiding place and slammed a lid on top, sealing in the monster.

'I got you, you horrible creature!' I cried out, already imaging my maiden's act of gratitude when she learned of my success.

The pot rattled and shook, as the frantic struggle of the goblin made it difficult to contain.

'I will need to pacify the beast,' I thought.

I got myself ready. I would lift the lid, and grab it by the scruff, wringing its neck and the life out of it, as he had done just so to the livestock of this hamlet.

One… Two… Three!

I opened the lid, but with a burst of unnatural strength and speed, the creature leapt out and shoved past me, leaving only smears of its slimy, green blood behind. Before I knew it, it had escaped and disappeared back into the safety of the woods.

I was disheartened, but not defeated. Surely I could expect the lady's disappointment. But I reasoned that perhaps I had given it sufficient fright that it had left and would trouble her no more.

Still, I could not be sure unless I kept watch again that night.

I spent the day in thought, devising a new trap. As evening fell, with the maiden's help, we collected as many flighty fireflies as we could find. (Gods! How beautiful she looked, that evening, bathed in the glowing sunset light. Her chestnut hair cascading on her shoulders, bouncing in delight, as we laughed like playing children hunting down those lantern bugs!)

When we had enough, a handful of glass jars now beamed with light, which we placed in a circle in a clearing close to the edge of the woods. In the centre of the circle, we placed a bowl of sweet honey, letting the light summer breeze carry the aroma into the forest.

I chose a sturdy tree to climb, and hid in its branches, counting the chirps of crickets, for the villain to arrive.

When the silence fell, I knew the monster had not fled, and was still here to terrorise the village.

I watched as the goblin crept out of the treeline, lured by the irresistible temptation of the honey and the lights. Once, twice, and thrice, it scurried back into the woods, fearing for its life, but I kept my cool, and let my trap reside.

Thinking it was safe, it crept once more into the circle, and began to lap the honey. It licked and licked until the bowl was dry, then lay on its back, belly full and its eyes glazed in gluttony. I left it there a moment more, to ease it to complacence.

When I judged the moment ripe, I threw my net upon the circle, trapping it inside!

It screamed and howled and thrashed about, becoming more entangled for its efforts.

I jumped down from the tree, now face to face with my antagonist, I looked it in the eye, and said, 'Horrible creature, why do you steal from these fair people? Their hens and sheep are not for you! They are poor, hardworking farmers, and your robbery has hurt them!'

Hearing my angry admonishment, the creature stopped moving, defeated.

Suddenly, to my surprise, the goblin spoke in misery: 'I was hungry and alone. The people never share. I would have asked to work for my fair take, but no one wants or likes a creature such as me.'

The goblin slumped its shoulders down, wrapped up, entwined within my net.

'Do with me as you please. Slice my neck and let me be,' said he.

At that moment I could not help but see the truth that sat before me: the creature was no different than you or me. There was no malice or brutality.

I took my knife and cut him free.

'Listen here,' I said, 'there is more to life than this. Go on, and let this village be. Trust in the gods, and they will guide you to your rightful place.'

The little goblin stood up straight, and with a nod, I knew that he had promised to obey. He jogged out of the trap, and fled into the woods, never to bother the villagers again.

I returned to the maiden's house and told her the good news: the goblin had been vanquished!

The news spread, and the villagers dropped their barricades and streamed out of their houses. They celebrated and hailed me a hero! We laughed and danced and sang in merriment! I had not felt such joy in years.

As I ate and drank and let the villagers toast to my great deed, the maiden came to me and said, 'You saved us all, dear sir! This dreary town has found new life, and I dare say so have I. As promised, I am yours. This noon we can be wed, and tonight I'll welcome you into my bed.'

I looked at that sweet girl, her beauty undeniable. Her smile was a balm onto my calloused soul, her offered gratitude salvation from the weary road. But my heart grew heavy, for I could not stay.

'My lady,' I said with deep regret, 'Nothing would please me more than to take you for my bride. There is none more fair and gentle I have seen, in all the roads that I have been. But I must see my journey to completion. I pledge to you my heart, and swear to you by all that's holy, that I will come to you again, and on that day we shall be wed, and on that night I'll gladly share your bed. Our lives together we shall live, in happiness and peace!'

She was saddened by my words, but understood what I had said. 'I will wait for you, dear husband-to-be. I shall collect fireflies each dusk, and set them as a lantern at my door, until the happy day that you return to claim me as your own.'

I walked out of the village and took up the road once more.

I am pained to say that as I have wandered here and there, I have lost the sense of where that village lay. I have searched and searched, and retraced my steps, but I seem to walk in circles everywhere. I am cursed to lose my home again and again, no matter where I go, and so I fear the village vanished, and my love, the maiden, now gone."
