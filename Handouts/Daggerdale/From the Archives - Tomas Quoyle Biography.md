Tomas Quoyle is the heart that stops beating at the centre of the Waterdeep chronicle. His wiki entry is six words long — "A young fisherman who perished in 1371. Poor fella." — and that devastating brevity tells you everything about how the chronicle remembers him: as someone who deserved a longer story and didn't get one.

The Fisherman's Son

Tomas was the son of Thom Quoyle, a fisherman lost at sea during a storm in 1369, and Zsarah Mudge, a Dock Ward fishwife who was widowed by that same storm and left to raise her boy alone. His uncle — or at least the man who claimed that title — was Zechary Mudge, an old pirate of uncertain provenance who washed up in Waterdeep half-drowned and half-drunk in a beaten dinghy, was taken for a madman by the Port Watch, and settled into the Quoyle household when Zsarah recognized him (or thought she did) as a long-lost brother. Zeke himself wasn't entirely sure the identification was correct, but "he knew an opportunity when he saw one." Tomas grew up in this household — fatherless, looked after by a mother who had lost everything and an uncle who might not actually be related to him.

He was a member of the Fellowship of Salters, Packers, and Joiners, sponsored by a family friend named Rahannsoz Burihildar. He was, in the most literal and unglamorous sense, a working-class Waterdhavian — a boy from the docks who learned to fish, to fight, and to drink, in that environment where these three skills are more or less interchangeable currencies.

"We Dock Ward Boys Have to Stick Together"

The founding event of Tomas's life — and, as it turns out, the founding event of the entire chronicle — is the rescue of Garth Shanks from the Loviatar cultist. Young Garth, paralysed and being flayed alive by a noble wielding a barbed whip, was saved by a boy he'd never met. Tomas freed him with a lockpick, offered him a bottle of strong liquor, and when the traumatized Garth could manage nothing more than "Why did you save me?", Tomas answered: "You're Garth Shanks, from the Docks right? I'm Tomas Quoyle. We Dock Ward boys have to stick together."

Those words become the leitmotif not just of Garth's life but of the entire Waterdeep saga. They are echoed by Max when he pleads for his life. They are invoked by Garth when he addresses Rychel. They are the vampires' weapon when they trick Garth into killing an innocent boy. The phrase outlives Tomas by years, mutating from a statement of simple solidarity into something that is by turns an aspiration, a reproach, and a cruelty. But when Tomas first said it, it was none of those things. It was just a boy explaining why he helped another boy. Because they were from the same place.

The Character in Action

Tomas appears in the chronicle almost exclusively through action rather than reflection. He has no journal entries, no letters, no meditative prose. What he has is a series of things he did, and they paint a remarkably consistent picture.

At the Bloody Fist tavern, looking for information about the Reprobates, Tomas "without even trying" got into a brawl with a couple of Dock Ward toughs who turned out to be his own relations. Having dispatched them, he hadn't even ordered a drink before being challenged by Sergeant Krag, a half-ogre. Tomas wrestled the enormous creature for five minutes and brought him down. Then Krag reached up from the floor and Round Two began. Tomas lost. Raven hauled him upstairs for healing, then pushed him back down the stairs for Round Three. Tomas lost again, knocked senseless into the ale-stained mud. Meanwhile, Garth bet everything he owned against his own best friend, and won.

That scene is comic, but it reveals something important: Tomas does not know when to stop. He doesn't calculate risk, doesn't consider retreat, doesn't weigh the odds. A fight presents itself and he fights. The half-ogre beats him and he gets up. The half-ogre beats him again and he gets up again. Garth — who knows him better than anyone — bets against him, not out of malice but because Garth understands that Tomas's courage and Tomas's judgment are not the same thing.

In the burning North Ward house, while the rest of the company was looting the lower floors, Tomas chased a fleeing man up through the building. When the man started pounding on a door yelling for "Vuldan," and a beholder appeared behind the door to ask what was going on, Tomas — "fear-stricken" — did the only useful thing available to him: he warned the beholder about the impending explosion and then set his charge right next to its door before going back downstairs. Even terrified, even confronted with an alien horror, his instinct was to do the job in front of him.

En route to sell stolen jade through the Dock Ward night, Tomas carried a decoy while Esperel took the real goods through the alleys. He was accosted by two sword-wielding thugs, threw the dummy rucksack at them, drew his sword, brought one down, knocked the other to the cobblestones — and then was hit in the neck with a drugged dart from a third assailant he never saw. He woke up in a Watch cell. Captain Murlyn — who knew his father — interrogated him as though the whole thing had been Tomas's fault, then released him with a warning to stay out of trouble.

When his mother's house was ransacked and she was assaulted during a burglary, Tomas's first priority was her safety. His second was to pledge his personal services to Thagdul in exchange for protection for her. He didn't tell the others. He didn't ask for help. He went to the Zhent's offices alone and made a deal. The chronicle notes that when Thagdul revealed Yarkul's true identity as a Zhent, Tomas "perhaps wisely did not voice his train of thought — that if Yarkul was a Zhent and he looked just like Thagdul..." He wasn't stupid. He saw the implication. He just didn't have the apparatus to do anything about it except file it away and walk to the pub.

The Generosity

The most revealing single incident in Tomas's story is the rescue of Job from the Dark Market in Skullport. Job was a priest of Grumbar, a childhood acquaintance from Dock Ward. The chronicle is blunt about Tomas's opinion of the man: "Tomas had always felt that Job was too bookish and serious a fellow, since despite his imposing physique, he never had anyone's back in a scrap." Job was not a friend. Job was not someone Tomas liked. But Job was from Dock Ward, and he was being auctioned to slavers.

Tomas paid 850 gold pieces — of his own personal money — to buy Job's freedom. He was then equipped with a mithril flail at a cost of another 300 gold. Job "disappeared without a word of thanks soon after this incident."

Eight hundred and fifty gold pieces. For a fisherman's son from Dock Ward, this would have been an enormous sum — possibly a large portion of everything he'd earned from the Red Herrings' missions. He spent it on a man he didn't like, who wouldn't fight, and who vanished without thanking him. The chronicle presents this not as a grand moral statement but as a quirk of character: "the good-hearted fisherman felt an obligation to the fine folk of Dock Ward, and couldn't let the poor blighter be sold to slavers." That's it. No agonizing, no discussion, no weighing of costs. A man from the Docks was in trouble, and Tomas was from the Docks.

The Drinking

Tomas drank enormously and constantly, and the chronicle never treats this as a problem — it treats it as a feature. He is described as having "prodigious intake." He drank Raven under the table, a nobleman. He frequented the Copper Cup. He followed half-orcs from one bar to another hoping to find women without beards or tusks. When Jafar arrived with devastating news about Thagdul's true nature, Tomas was "half-drunk as usual" at the Crawling Spider, having followed his drinking companions from the Bloody Fist. The drinking is not portrayed as self-medication or escapism. It is simply what Tomas does between fights — the other half of the same restless, undiscriminating appetite for experience that drives everything else about him.

Raven, the nobleman ranger, tried to match Tomas drink for drink on their night out together and ended up in a dark alley too drunk to defend himself properly against Dragon's ambush. It's a small but telling detail — the ranger of Mielikki, the man of the wild with every advantage of breeding and training, couldn't keep up with a fisherman's son from Dock Ward. Tomas's tolerance for punishment, in all its forms, was apparently bottomless.

The Death

The chronicle of Harvestide 1371 records Tomas's end in fragments that have the quality of a nightmare half-remembered. During the raid on the Wavesilver Villa, the adventurers descended into old sewers beneath the basement levels. "Despite having had a dream to warn him of such things, Tomas charges ahead through an illusionary wall." Of course he does. That is what Tomas does. He charges.

He fell into the same pit where Raven had been tortured. The drow Rubikawn — who had pointed a spidery finger at Tomas days earlier and prophesied "you have five days left to live" — jumped down to cut his throat. A chaotic battle erupted. Elbing Hightimber was conducting a ceremony. The cult of Iyachtu Xvim was at work. And then, at the peak of the bloodshed, the Baneson Xvim was manifested as a huge green serpent that consumed the souls of the fallen. Tomas's soul was among them.

The "Dark Days" manuscript records it with liturgical finality: "It was then that good Tomas Quoyle was unwritten from the Book of Life, his soul devoured by Evil."

Not merely killed — devoured. His soul taken. This means, within the theology of the Forgotten Realms, that Tomas is not resting with any god. He is not in Mielikki's forests or sailing with Valkur or at peace. He is consumed. When Garth later writes of nightmares and "stories that have not found the villain slain," when he speaks of wanting to see "the soul of my childhood friend to a proper home, free of the torments that Xvim is certainly exposing him to" — this is what he means. Tomas is not dead in the way that Raven is dead, or Falcon is dead. Tomas is trapped, his soul a possession of a malign deity, and no funeral, no consecration, no will can reach him.

The Psychological Portrait

Tomas Quoyle is the simplest character in the chronicle and the hardest to write about, because simplicity is the thing that psychological analysis is least equipped to handle. He is not complex. He is not tormented by internal contradictions. He does not scheme, or brood, or calculate. He is a fisherman who fights, drinks, helps people from his neighbourhood, loves his mother, trusts his friends more than he should, and charges into danger without thinking first.

The sophistication of Tomas lies not in what he contains but in what he generates in the people around him. Garth Shanks — the most psychologically complex character in the chronicle — is essentially a machine for processing the loss of Tomas Quoyle. Everything Garth builds after Tomas's death — the Unseen Army, the Rooken Network, Vauxbourough's — is an attempt to create a structure that would have kept Tomas alive. The phrase "Dock Ward boys have to stick together" becomes the founding creed of an intelligence empire because Garth is trying to build a world in which that creed actually works, in which sticking together is sufficient to protect the people you care about. He names the company after his mother, but the company exists because of Tomas.

Old Zechary Mudge, at the Thirsty Throat after Tomas's death, tells the guardsman Paris: "Garth's a good lad, and whatever he's got into got me sister's boy Tomas sent to the Bitch Queen's terlet." Even the uncle — the old pirate who may not actually be related — understands that Tomas was collateral damage in someone else's war. Tomas didn't choose to enter the underworld of Waterdeep. He was pulled in by Garth's association with Thagdul, by the debt the Red Herrings owed to forces they didn't understand, by the simple fact that he lived in Dock Ward and the people he stuck with were in trouble.

The chronicle's remembrance of him — "Tomas, we barely knew ye" — is inscribed beneath an image of his dead body, and it functions as both a lament and an accusation. We barely knew him because the chronicle didn't give us time to know him. He was alive for one campaign year — Greengrass to Harvestide of 1371 — and in that time he rescued Garth from a torturer, co-founded the Company of the Red Herring, fought his way through sewers and noble villas and underground slave markets, bought a man's freedom with his life savings, and charged through an illusory wall into a pit where a god devoured his soul.

He was twenty years old, give or take. He had a dog named Captain. He was a member of the Fellowship of Salters, Packers, and Joiners. He saved a boy from the Docks because the boy was from the Docks, and then the world that the boy led him into killed him, and worse than killed him — took his soul and kept it. And the boy he saved spent the rest of his life trying to build something worthy of those seven words: We Dock Ward boys have to stick together.
