DDC - 1398 - 1: Life of the White Saint 
A Record of the Life of Ardelusk Whitegate (1340?-1399) and the rebuilding of Dagger Dale During the Tumultuous Years of Arcane Plague - Original Biographical Notes by 
Senezear Dale. 
Notes from GFD: 
This is an easy document to find. In the libraries of most established houses in the dale, you'll find a book about the Whitegates. About 1/2 of it are from sessions that Ardelusk Whitegate held with Senezear Dale around 1387. What you will read if you find a copy are the compiled and edited notes from Shefton Dale, Senezear's son. I spoke to Shefton Dale up at Tesh Valley Farm when I met with the Tethyamar League. Mr. Dale insisted that the account was completely as his father had written it, but did confess that a portion of it seemed to have been missing, after the entry on Paladin's Grave. 
Such is the nature of collecting old paper 
This bit of the book is worth reading as it mentions the woman Arabelle Needle, who would sew a great deal of suffering many decades later 

But, enjoy the story and perspective of this humble hero before you make yourself mad with the benefit of the reader's hindsight

Liberty Hill 
I came to this place for the first time on the 19th of Eleint, 1372. Maturin and his men were leading myself and scores of other freed slaves on an overland march that had taken us many days. We had been harried by the enemy every step of the way; Yarkul was enraged over the loss of what was then called 'Chillholme Mine'; I think he suspected that the mine was worth more than just star-borne-tablets of some obscure monster goddess. 

Anyway, we spent all night arraying defences on that hill; Boregar had been scouting around us, and he came back in a hurry saying that several companies of Fist were only a few hours away. We set up what spears we could in the ground at the top of that hill, and armed and trained every able-bodied person. I was showing a few of the younger men how to swing a hammer to break a man's knees when Dorian came up to me, smiled, and knocked my clumsy hammer from my hands with the flick of his wrist and the flash of his blade. 

"A hammer is no tool for a true warrior" he said to us. He had a small boy with him, Cederick Blackmane was his name, and I came to know him well in the decades that followed. Cederick had an armload of swords that the Liberators had taken from some of the 3rd Garrison at the mine, and it was all he could do to not stumble over the weight of them. Dorian grabbed the swords and put them in our hands, and spent the rest of the dark hours showing us a few basic strokes. 

When the daylight came, I was never so thankful. All that long night, with the worry of the enemy closing in around us, and the fear of not living up to Lord Dorian's training, I as terrified. I've never been that afraid before or since. It was the worst night of my life. When the day came, I was never so thankful. 

Before the day came, as the sky was turning grey in the east, the Fist came upon us. We were completely encircled by them; 2nd - Garlunk's Roughriders, 3rd - Blacktooth's Hussars, 4th - Sandalwood's Roughriders. The Murderer of Mirabar himself was there, as were the terrible halforcs Garlunk and Blacktooth. There were dozens of heavy horse, a platoon of murderous archers, and a score of heavy foot that slammed into us. 

I will not recount this battle stroke-for-stroke. Others have already done that to such a degree that my account is of small contribution, and perhaps even small contradiction. What I will offer is that the enemy broke with the first rays of the sun, which only a few have mentioned, and then only to say that the halforcs didn't want to fight with the sun in their eyes; the 2nd Fist had ridden into us from the West, and atop their great warhorses, they indeed would have seen the sun first; I recall it glinting off the brutal blade of one such monster. 

No, this battle is a legend already. I only wish to state that this is the place where I first felt like a freed man; I had been taken from my shackles at Chillholme, and I had marched as a refugee for days behind Maturin and the others. But atop Liberty Hill, I fought. 
I fought with a sword, and I drove off my enemies. I was then a Free Man. 
The Farm 
Many years later, I returned to this site. I knew it had become a site of some small pilgrimage; there was a cenotaph there, and many small gravestones had been placed randomly around it. No one knew where anyone was really buried, except the Fist. They lay under three huge piles of stones, which I always assumed had been put there by the elves of Cormanthor, but to be honest, to this day I'm not sure who buried those dead villains beneath so much stone. 

None of that mattered. It was 1374 and I had already had my Vision, and much of what I accomplished had already been done. But this was my greatest accomplishment; castles and towers and knights and all that were fine achievements, but on the site of that place where I first swung a sword, I also hoed my first row. I built a farm there, and raised a family, and Amaunator provided all the sun we needed to 

gather surplus crops all the years I have lived so far. I was already rich in gold from 'Chillholme', but now I was rich in crops and children, and that is a gift far greater than any vein of any metal. 

Both of my sons were born there, and there they learned to swing a blade. There, they learned to swing the sickle as well. 

One day, a small group of people had come from town; they were peddlers in some sort of 

collective. They were selling needles and cloth and that sort of thing, and they went around to many of the farms to do just that. Their leader was a fine-looking woman whom I recognized immediately. I had first seen her on the other side of Anuaroch at some terrible mountain-fortress where the Zhents were unpacking us from our crated journey from the Sword Coast. She had been a seamstress in Neverwinter, I recall. 

Her name as I had known it was Arabella Needle, but she now called herself Arabella Liberty, and the small child that clung to her skirts was likewise called Liberty. She asked me what name I had given this farm, and to be honest, I hadn't thought of it. 'Ardelusk's Acres' I said in half-hearted response. I think I may have even asked, rather than suggested the title. 
She shook her head. "Liberty Hill". 
The woman was not being coy or putting on airs. She had fought with us in '72 on this very site as well. She too had won her freedom here, and she was right about the name. 
The Paladins' Grave 
I did something in 1395 that I later came to regret, though it seemed like the right thing to do at the time. 

Cormanthor had begun to grow unchecked, the times being what they were (and still are as I sit here and tell you this). Many times in the years since my liberation, I visited a place only 20 miles away from my farm, called Anathar's Dell. This place goes by more names than the paladins buried there, but I always called it 'Paladins' Grave'. The story of the two battles there in 1372, before my liberation mind you, are still told with as much colour and detail as though they happened yesterday, though I cannot now think of a soul besides the Proprietor of Quick Axes (a place I don't frequent), who was actually there. 

Anyway, I always thought of this place as a sort of precursor site to my own revered Liberty Hill. There were a score of paladins of the four Orders that dominated that time buried there, and their stories are more or less well documented. What is unknown is that the great cenotaph there, constructed by a repentant Dawn Lord Cassius, had been toppled by the oaks of Cormanthor, and many of the graves were being overrun by the forest. 

I moved that cenotaph and those men to my own farm, and they lay there today. I was roundly criticized for disturbing these graves, but I would not sit by and watch them become lost to us by way of a relentless forest. 

I can recite the names of all those knights by memory, as they appear on that stone. In fact, if I close my eyes, I am standing in the bedroom in the tower on the farm, and looking to the west, I can see the last bit of sun coming through the mountains and reflecting off that ever-polished stone. 

I need to change something I said; I don't regret bringing those men to my farm. Their deeds will never die so long as my line keeps clear that hill, and I am happy to have my children and their children harvest crops under the silent watch of that cenotaph.
