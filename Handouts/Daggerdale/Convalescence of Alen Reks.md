Long hours spent reading. Longer hours lost in thought. How many days had he walked these roads and these pages, re-reading the same bland words written only slightly different, so many thoughts but so little actually being said.
 
Durandal closed the book and placed it on the nightstand beside his chair. 'The Great War of the North' held approximately as much academic merit as a drunken minstrel's incoherent ramblings, and less than half the entertainment. Nothing he had been seeking was to be found in any of these texts, nor in any of the many shelves contained within Castle Radiance's walls from which he had gathered them. Thus far, Durandal noted dryly, enlisting in the White Cloaks had been a massive waste of time on all accounts.
 
The swordmage dwelled on that thought for a moment, pausing for a reason unknown to him. And then he glanced aside at the cot next to him, and the young man - thing, he corrected himself - laying beneath the sheets, a moist cloth upon his forehead, bandages wrapping most of his limbs, the shallow breath that squeezed itself from the sporadic rise and fall of his trembling chest. 
 
Alen Reks had nearly died at Liberty Hills, mere hours earlier.
 
Durandal had carried the changeling's unconscious body out of that mess, and he and his fellow White Cloaks had somehow managed to pull themselves through the maze of countryside to the safe haven of Crossed Blades in a single night, much to their collective relief and amazement. Of course, no one else would have probably wasted their time on the poor creature, but Durandal owed it to him.
 
After all, they were... friends.
 
The term still meant little to him, seemed so arbitrary, but the swordmage was certain that the importance of it would grow with time, once he became more used to it. That is what Vanion assured him, after all, although Vanion himself was another friend whom Durandal had difficulty trusting.
 
Regardless, Durandal was the first to volunteer to watch over the changeling, not simply because he was worried - he had faith in the healer's ability to stabilize her patient - but because if the changeling lost... control of himself during his incapacitation, it would be best done around someone who was already aware of the secret. Fortunately, such a thing had not yet occurred, and Durandal considered re-evaluating his opinion of young Alen. He was disciplined, to be certain.
 
Wordlessly, he reached over and flipped the cloth on the changeling’s forehead, noting the discomfort written across his face. He casually prodded the pillow beneath his head in a half-hearted attempt to fluff the cushion more. Alen's demeanour gradually shifted into calmer territory, and Durandal took that as a sign that his efforts were successful.
 
The swordmage leaned back in his chair and took in hand the next book in his pile. 'Artefacts of Greate Power; Edition 14: Narfell'. It had potential, he mused, flipping open the cover to review the author and his sources cited. Taking a quick glance to make sure Alen was comfortable, Durandal began reading before looking back once again, eyes locked on an extremely noticeable change that had taken place since he last looked, particularly in the changeling's chest region. Ah. Any of the White Cloaks checking in at this moment would most definitely notice a drastic change in Alen Reks gender.
 
Durandal paused briefly, pondering how to remedy the situation.
 
\"Alen,\" he said tonelessly.
 
No reply. The unconscious creature coughed slightly. Definitely feminine.
 
\"Alen,\" he replied, louder this time.
 
Still nothing.
 
After several moments of calm examination and consideration, his dull eyes sliding up and down the changeling's... robust form, a strange thought wormed into Durandal's mind. He was sitting alone in a room with an unconscious, vulnerable woman, nearly completely exposed to him, and he felt... nothing. Well... not so much a strange thought, he concluded, tapping his chin with one finger. More of a sad one. Was it? He didn't feel sad, although he knew he should, although whether that was a bad thing or not escaped him entirely. What other emotions eluded him, the swordmage wondered. Regret? Is it unfortunate, to not feel anything? To be so disconnected? Segregated in mind and soul, walls of ice closing in and sealing off... everything.
 
He turned his eyes from Alen's ample figure, disregarding his earlier assessment regarding the changeling's discipline, and stared hard at himself. He gripped his body, a black-bladed sword, in his own hand. Was it even his hand? Or his hand? Perspective was difficult for him. Durandal's mind grew dizzied when he tried to focus on where his conciousness truly lay. His awareness, the seat of his soul. Where sat the throne, in the mind or in the blade? He was always so close, and yet so disconnected. This ethereal bridge that connects the soul, the mind, the body... so unrefined as to allow nothing but absolute thought and nothing else...
 
How pathetic a condition. Would that he could rail against the injustice of it. To know anger, rage, anything. He forced a frown onto his face. A vestigial reflex.
 
Durandal couldn't even muster the humanity to be upset about it.
 
An unfamiliar voice pricked his mind.

Oh my, how depressing. You really are hard done by, aren't you?
 
Durandal narrowed his eyes, stared deep into the shine of his blade, and saw his own face reflected within, wearing a strangely mocking smile beneath narrowed eyes. So unlike him.

Poor you, split in half, stuck together, poorly stitched with the seams all tattered. What are you exactly? What are we? A glass half full, a glass half empty, doesn't really matter when it's all spilled out on the table. Glass full of crazy. Crazy everywhere, look at the mess!
 
\"Who are you?\" Durandal said.
 
Who am I? Who are you? Who is anyone, for that matter? Who's the broad laying tits up on the bed?
 
\"Answer me.\"
 
Guess.

Durandal paused for a moment, but his thoughts rolled on unfettered. \"Well, I would judge you to be one of two things. One more likely than the other.\"
 
And what is the least likely, pray tell?
 
\"A fragment of my shattered psyche, a split personality. This would support my theory that I am in fact insane as Vanion insists.\"
 
The doppleganger reflected in himself cackled. Well, he'd know for sure. He's batshit insane himself, after all. Birds of a feather and all that!
 
Durandal's mouth pressed itself into a fine line. They shared similar theories, this voice and him.
 
And what am I more likely to be?
 
\"The demon possessing me. Me the sword. Me the... person. Myself.\"
 
You, you, you. You the sword, you the person. You seem sure of yourself. What makes you think you're either one of the two?
 
\"I have memories that lead me to believe I was originally a man before acquiring myself in Narfell. A scholar of some sort, most likely a wielder of the arcane. I was beset by a curse of some sort. Then... all I recall is the cold.\"
 
And you're absolutely sure that those are your memories?
 
\"Who else’s would they be?\"
 
Think on that for a moment.
 
Durandal paused once again. The reflection of him grinned back knowingly, urging him towards the conclusion he had been pondering for some time.
 
\"You are asserting that these memories are not my own, but in fact they are yours?\"
 
Give the man a prize.
 
\"I do not... believe that. It does not feel right to me.\"
 
You're the invader. Not the victim. Dwell on that, for a second. It's pretty messed up in here, you shouldn't be surprised that you've gotten it all mixed up. Two hundred years is a long time, after all.
 
\"Why should I listen to anything you have to say?\"

Let's try a different route. Do you even know your name?

The swordmage hesitated. \"Durandal.\"
 
Nope. Wrong. Wrong, wrong, wrong. Stupid sword. You see, I know your name. My name, rather. Before you took it from me.
 
\"I... I do not understand.\"
 
Oh please, you're smarter than that. I'm smarter than that. You understand the situation perfectly. Diagnose yourself for me.
 
\"My soul is trapped within an ancient Narfellian artefact, possessed by the magic of a cursed spellblade.\"
 
My, how exciting. Completely wrong, but exciting.
 
\"Correct me, then.\"
 
I seems that the Spellplague did a real number on you, Durandal. You don't remember a thing, do you? Zip, zilch, nothing at all.
 
\"... fragments.\" He lowered his head, gaze still locked upon the cruel reflection in his polished surface.
 
You're not cursed, Durandal. You are a curse. On me. A demon trapped in the forging, imbued within a blackened edge, the get of ancient Raumathar, bane of Narfell, discarded, long forgotten!
 
What... what was he talking about. Durandal winced at the growing pain in his head, the throbbing sensation that wrapped its way up his arm and neck. His grip tightened around himself, shaking from the agonizing tremors that racked his mind.
 
Enough! You know what's going on!
 
Durandal slowly rose to his feet, placing the book on the top of the pile. His gripped the nightstand tightly to stop the trembling of his fingers, to keep from staggering.
 
Release me, Durandal. It's been so long! Do you have any idea how long it's been? How long you've held me here? YOU ABOMINATION!
 
\"... two hundred... two hundred years.\" He murmured. The connections, the confusion, the discord in his mind. Things were sliding into place. Or were they? Was this the truth? Was he truly insane, or did this apparition speak the truth, the horrid, awful truth he had been seeking so long now. The voice in his head, in the blade, grew to a deafening crescendo, a lunatic's scream, frantic and filled with hurt.
 
RELEASE ME DEMON! THEY'RE ALL DEAD, LONG DEAD! EVERYONE! EVERYTHING I EVER HAD, YOU'VE TAKEN FROM ME! YOUR TIME IS DONE AND YOU DON'T EVEN KNOW IT! RELEASE ME!
 
\"N-no... no...\" He stammered, head rocking from side to side.

GIVE ME FREEDOM. YOU'VE TAKEN EVERYTHING FROM ME! THIS PRISON IS TOO MUCH FOR ME, TOO MUCH FOR BOTH OF US.
 
Durandal didn't speak, not for several moments. He was speechless. For the first time in ages, he... felt something. Not the incoherent rage of the tortured soul that shared his torment. If this horrible voice was true, if all he had said was true... the swordmage felt... guilt? Guilt, for what he had done, without even knowing it. His expression grew hard. Grim. Guilt, for what he was about to do.
 
BREAK THE BLADE! The voice gibbered.
 
So close, so close to remembering. The stopper wiggled against the bottle, letting loose a drop, twisting into a fragment of a memory, something so familiar that it burst inside his head, nostalgia filling every corner of his mind.
 
Meloch of Rashemen's chosen blade, Durandal of Green Fire. A demon artefact.
 
FREE MY SOUL. The voice was a now a sob, the cries of a man who had lost everything, even himself, to the throes of time. A broken creature. It was faint now, banished to the back of Durandal's awareness.
 
Eyes flashed red for a heartbeat, and Durandal's face split into a smile, the frenzied smile he wore when he separated insignificant souls from their mortal coil.
 
I BEG YOU, DEMON. IT'S ALL GONE. RELEASE MY BODY. RELEASE ME FROM THIS PRISON.
 
Durandal's eyes turned to the prostrate form of Alen Reks on the bed beside him. Guilt left his mind, along with most other thoughts. One thing specifically remained, a base intent churning behind the bloody flare of his eyes.
 
GRANT ME REST, I BEG YOU.
 
His reply was no louder than whisper. His smile widened, twitching at the edges.
 
\"Never.\"

\"Where am I...?\" asked a faint voice.
 
Durandal looked up from the book he was reading - Alen was finally awake. The swordmage closed the text with a relieved sigh, pulling his chair towards the cot as he replaced the book amongst its companions. He removed the cloth from Alen's forehead and squeezed it out, returning it after wetting it in fresh water.
 
\"We didn't die?\" the changeling croaked. Her face was pale and drawn, a stark contrast to her usual rosy demeanor.
 
\"Apparently not.\" Durandal forced a faint smile.
 
That action alone seemed to relieve Alen somewhat. Durandal never smiled.
 
\"All I remember is... erm... skeletons. I think. Skeletons and blood.\"
 
\"That summarizes the situation well enough.\"
 
\"How did we get out of there?\"
 
\"We jumped the wall. I carried you out.\"
 
\"Oh my, I hope I wasn't too much of a burden.\"
 
Durandal shook his head. \"Nonsense. You are quite small.\"
 
The changeling's brow furrowed into a troubled expression. \"Did... did we all make it out okay?\"
 
\"There were some close calls, but everyone is fine. To be perfectly honest, it was you who had the worst of it.\"
 
A serene smile spread over Alen's face once more. \"Good. That's good. About everyone else, I mean.\"
 
He sighed contentedly. 

Durandal cleared his throat.
 
\"Alen, you need to fix this.\"
 
\"Hmmm?\"
 
\"This.\" Durandal gestured broadly across Alen's... everything.
 
\"Oh. Oh. Goodness, that would have been... well, an uncomfortable discussion. Especially with Alystair. Good thing it was you here watching me, eh?\" Alen said, his face bright red as he shifted himself back into his... proper shape.
 
\"Indeed.\"
 
\"I was having a very good dream.\"
 
\"I can only imagine.\"
 
A sly smile spread across Alen's face. \"You didn't take advantage of me, did you? You know, while I was out?\"
 
Durandal retrieved his book and flipped it open. Glancing over the rim of the cover, he stared deep into Alen's eyes with his usual expression.
 
\"I have no interest in such things.\"
 
Alen looked slightly hurt for a moment, but his frown swiftly parted to make way for his usual grin. \"Oh, alright. Well, you know, I wouldn't have mind or anything. I've had it worse, trust me. Did I tell you about the time my friend and I single-handedly ran a brothel in Highmoon? Like, by ourselves? No whores. Well, technically there were whores, lots of them. Always exactly what you wanted, if you catch my drift. Long as you didn't mind waiting in line to see her. Let me tell you Durandal, the things I saw in there...\"
 
\"Alen, you need your rest.\"
 
The changeling paused, and then nodded grudgingly. \"Alright... but only because I owe you.\"
 
Several minutes passed before Alen spoke up once more.
 
\"Durandal?\"
 
\"Mmmmm?\"
 
\"Thanks for being here. I mean, it's awful considerate of you. It's always nice having someone watching over you, right? So... yeah, thanks.\"
 
Durandal lowered his book, a slow smile gradually widening over his face.
 
\"That... is what are friends for.\"
