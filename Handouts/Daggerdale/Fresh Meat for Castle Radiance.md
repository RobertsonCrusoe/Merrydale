The springtime is a hectic time to find oneself trying to get into Castle Radiance.  If you thought the questions at the gates of Dagger Falls were intrusive, you find this round of questioning assaulting.  And protracted.  And intense.  It takes hours to get into the Castle, and there are only about a quarter the number of men in this train as you came to Dagger Falls with.  In the end you are granted admittance, and told to secure proper lodgings immediately.

Entering the Castle, you find a place even more ordered than the town.  The Outer Bailey has a cobbled surface that looks like it was cut by the most exacting of Dwarven masons, and there isn’t so much as a bit of dirt between the cracks, much less the usual mounds of horse droppings and other detritus you would expect in any castle court yard.

It is late in the day when you got here, and you can see that a caravan is getting ready to leave in the morning, and that they have wagons loaded and lined up inside, so as to leave at first light.  There are several wagons that all have loads the exact same size and proportions, and all covered in oiled leather tarps.  Two men are throwing one of these tarps over the last of the wagons in the cue, and you can see that it is stacked with 6 coffins.  You can’t take your eyes off the macabre payload of that wagon and the two men who are hastily covering it, and one of them makes eye contact with you.  He gives you a small nod, says “Amaunator keep thee”, and goes back to his business.

==At the 'Hearth==

Before long you find yourself in the Tethyamar Hearth Inn, putting down money for a clean room and a warm meal.  This place lacks any of the charm or character that you found at Quick Axes, but it is clean and orderly, and there are lots of people who know Bargle.  You are in the tap room when the sun goes down outside; this is celestial event is marked doubly; once by the dimming of the superb stained glass on the upper west wall of the room, and also by the ringing of a lone bell from the Fortress Cathedral. While there is no exodus of parishioners to head up to the cathedral to pray and sing, most of the men in the tap room look towards the dimming skylight, and most mouth a silent prayer.  Those not of the faith keep quiet during this brief prayer; there is none of the cajoling that you witnessed in Quick Axes.

Shortly after the evening prayer, you find yourself sitting with a couple men at a table who are also looking for the same man you are, as well as a man named Grenville, who runs a big forge in one of the upper baileys.  He is asking about the prices of weapons and other commercial things, and giving you some local news while the group of you eat together, when he suddenly stops, and motioning towards the door, he says “There’s the Battlemaster there.”  He nods a ‘good evening’ to the lot of you, and gives a friendly not to Bargle as he gathers up his plate and beer, and heads over to another table.

The man who has just come through the door takes in the taproom quickly, giving salutations here and there, and making his way circuitously to your table, finally arrives and gives a slight nod to your table.

“Well met, new comers.  Bargle Battamond at your service.” right

The most striking thing about Bargle is his long grey hair.  It hangs in two thick tails over each shoulder, and has grown thick and unkempt.  He has a close-trimmed beard and moustache that are also grey.  The man is a little dusky, or almost a hue of red or purple.  It is hard to tell.  He has clear grey eyes and a friendly smile, though his face is covered in a half dozen scars from numerous battles.  He wears a golden circlet around his head, and while he walks about the Castle, he wears a fine suit of chain mail, with his black and white tabard, which has a crest for his organization upon it.

When he is nearby, he almost emanates energy, and those close to him cannot help but stand or sit up a little straighter, and pay attention not only to him, but to everything else around him.  Small aches and pains vanish in proximity to this man, such is his presence, and one cannot help but feel energized while in his company.

“Gentlemen,” the old warrior addresses you like an old friend, rather than the new acquaintance that he is.  “I am General Bargle of the Free Defence League.  I hear that you men are all recently arrived here at the Castle, and looking for work.

“As it turns out, I am looking for some men like you.  I command a pair of teams of adventurers like yourselves, and my organization is looking to expand, and create a third team.  I need a variety of men with a variety of skills, and you lot appear to fit the bill.

“If you’re like any other adventurer up here in the mountains, I am sure you are wondering what’s in it for you, joining the League.  ‘Why even bother?’ I’m sure you’re asking yourselves.  There are plenty of bounties, and no need to organize to pursue them, right?

“Well, that’s only half right.  There are plenty of bounties, but there are more men dying in the mountains trying to claim them than there should be.  Things have gotten right dangerous around here the last couple of years, and though rumour of the lucrative work that is to be found up here for brave young men has travelled through the Dales and other more distant lands the last ten years or more, it is not generally known how many men are dying up here every month.

“Tomorrow a wagon train with some 30 bodies is headed for Town.  We send out the dead once a tenday.  You can do the math, I’m sure.  Now, those bodies represent what the locals call ‘unusual escalation of violence’, but this kind of thing is not uncommon.

“So what am I offering?  I’d like to forge you men into a team, and give you some gear and direction to start you out.  That way, you might have a fighting chance out there.  I’ve been fighting for a long time, and I can tell a promising warrior at the start of his career when I see one.  I see many in you, but I would emphasize the start of your careers.  They will be short indeed if you aren’t organized.

“I want to make something abundantly clear to you men.  I’m not going to sit here and make a hard sell to you.  If you want to go it alone, I will wish you luck and give you any advice you might seek.  Up here, we are all free men.  I wouldn’t have it any other way.  If you want me to close my mouth and move along, I’ll not bother you a moment longer, but if you’re interested in hearing what else I have to say, I’ll buy a round of beer, and we can get to know each other.”
