From the Casebooks of Lucan Alavandor

So, I unearthed a letter from among my great-grandmother's belongings in Saerloon, a relic dated 3540 MC—what Brother Vesuvian confirms is the Mulhorand Calendar equivalent of 1406 DR. The letter, signed with the initials "AA," seems to be the work of Alvund Alavandor, my great-grandmother's elder brother. My aunt corroborated this, recalling that Alvund, once a struggling artist, severed ties with the family after leaving Sembia—or perhaps it was the other way around.

The letter hints at a deal struck between Alvund and a shadowy figure known as the Old Patriarch. As part of this arrangement, Alvund's family and another—presumably the Clarandals—agreed to "take what we were given and vanish forever." This implies some significant transaction or event that necessitated complete secrecy and relocation.

The involvement of this Old Patriarch raises several questions. His influence seems considerable, as he ensured their journey was safe and comfortable, which suggests either substantial resources or power. Or both, I suppose. The requirement for secrecy is evident since Alvund writes that he was breaching just such a condition by writing the letter I discovered.

The Old Patriarch's influence extended to a factory in Luthcheq, Chessenta—apparently marking the boundaries of his reach. This connection, though distant, may provide a clue as to his identity or the nature of his dealings. We know, for example, hat the Alavaxi families that "came out of the Iron Hole" reportedly came from Chessenta.

Notably, the name "Alvund" is also associated with the first Alavandor settler in Thistle Ridge. A fundamental assumption in this case will be that Alvund the correspondent was also Alvund the settler. Brother Vesuvian's records mention that Alvund was married three times, each wife sharing the same surname: Maeliticus. Presumably, Senthia, Abrecia, and Aisling Maeliticus were part of the same influential family, suggesting a pattern or an alliance that warrants further investigation.

At this point, I should report a curious remark Vesuvian mentioned when I was discussing his summary with him.

This name… Maeliticus, is indeed curious... Now, as a Sembian, I suppose I needn't remind you that in Netherese—to imagine we once thought of it as a dead language used only by scholars!—the suffix "-iticus" does suggest a sort of... oh, how shall we say? A character or essence of something. Yes, quite characteristic of those ancient scholars, ever so precise in their linguistic structures. But in Netherese "Mael" would likely derive from "malus" – meaning evil. Not a very auspicious name, not at all. The name takes a much more shadowy hue, hinting at "mal"-evolence, you see? Perhaps due to Netheril's storied affinity for the more... ah… robust aspects of the arcane.

And yet, we mustn't leap too quickly, for we find a rather more... uh, pastoral nuance? Yes, pastoral. In Thorass—the Old Common, that is—"Mael" speaks to marks, significant ones, not merely shadows but... milestones, yes, milestones of history and legacy. Fascinating, truly, how a name tumbles through language and time like a... well, like a leaf in the wind, isn't it?

Alvund Alavandor's marriages present a curious pattern, particularly given the lack of known offspring from his first two unions. The absence of records on the Maeliticus family only deepens the mystery. Whether Alvund left Sembia with a wife and two sisters, a wife and one sister, or just one sister, Aisling Maeliticus is the only one we can confirm was with him in Thistle Ridge. Indeed, Brother Vesuvian's notes indicate that the first two Maeliticus sisters perished before the advent of the Sembians in Thistle Ridge.

We know that Aisling Alavandor established a school and an apothecary, the Phylactery, soon after their arrival in Thistle Ridge. According to the 1440 DR edition of the Yellow Abbey's Booke of Surveys and Measures, this Phylactery was notably successful, particularly in producing fertility medicines. On this point, I should mention another comment of Brother Vesuvian's:

Master Alavandor… I say, this "Phylactery"... quite the peculiar name, don't you think? It has roots in Old Chondathan, a language of ancient wisdom and deep mysteries. Originally, the word was "phylaktērion," meaning "safeguard" or "protection." As you are no doubt aware, this term is derived from "phylax," meaning "guardian" or "protector," if you will. You're no doubt aware of the term prophylaxis. My goodness, I hope so. But over time, the word took on darker connotations, especially among those who delved too deeply into the shadowed arts. It became associated with practices that sought to preserve things that should have passed on, to hold onto power in ways... not quite natural. One wonders why such a word was chosen... unsettling, really.

Why Aisling chose such a name is indeed curious. Indeed, it may suggest that her practices were more than just herbal remedies.

The births of Andarl, Senthia, and Abrecia in rapid succession after their arrival further suggest that Aisling's expertise, combined perhaps with the unique properties of Thistle Ridge, played a significant role. Here, I am alluding to the exceptional fertility noted by Blaze Whitegate, a young magistrate at the time, and the Chaunteans' belief that the settlement was located on sacred ground. The productivity of the gardens, the quality of the fruit, the effectiveness of their medicines, and the remarkable health of their children were all attributed to this sacred influence. The Yellow Abbey's Booke even acknowledges that the success of the settlement's fertility products bordered on heresy, though formal inquiries found nothing overtly occult.

Claudius Clarandal's three marriages, much like Alvund Alavandor's, resulted in no known offspring from the first two unions. However, upon their arrival in Thistle Ridge, Claudius's third wife, Saralla Sakurth, bore the next generation of Clarandals—Tergus and Claudius the Younger. Yet again, the origins of Saralla Sakurth and Minerva Maerimmon remain shrouded in mystery, much like the Maeliticus family connected to Alvund.

This raises an intriguing hypothesis—namely that the two families may have leveraged both Aisling's knowledge and the peculiar properties of Thistle Ridge to overcome whatever challenges they previously faced in having children. The influence of the Thistle family, if any, also remains an open question.

Focusing on the key figures who settled in Thistle Ridge—Claudius Clarandal with Saralla Sakurth and Alvund Alavandor with Aisling Maeliticus—raises a question: Did the Alavandors in Sembia sever ties with Alvund because of his involvement with the Clarandals or the Maeliticus family? The recurring marriages to siblings within the same families hint at a deliberate strategy, possibly orchestrated by the Old Patriarch. It may be that Alvund's ostracism from the family had more to do with the company he kept than the fact of his departure.

It's noteworthy that Tergus Clarandal's journal hints at his father Claudius's ties to Daerlun—an area under Netherese influence—which is in marked contrast to Saerloon's semi-autonomous status as a seaport. Tergus, raised to believe his family was opposed to Netheril's dominance, would have been shaken by the revelation that his father may have served in Daerlun—where he could only have been in the service of Netheril. This contradiction seems key, as it suggests that the motives for the families' exile were more complex than mere political alignment.

So where does this put us? Here is a hypothesis: the Old Patriarch sought to remove these families from Sembia, perhaps due to some unknown factor tied to their anticipated offspring. The fact that both men were on their third marriages to a second or third sibling suggests a union of families not for political power—since they were exiled together—but possibly related to the bearing of children. Could the Old Patriarch have been connected to one of the wives' families, orchestrating their removal to protect future generations from some unknown threat or curse? Or to secure their legacy?

Aisling's reportedly tragic death in 1439 at her own hands raises further suspicions. Was her suicide prompted by the loss of two of her children and a grandson in the suspicious hunting accident that year, or was it driven by a darker truth she uncovered? The timeline and circumstances surrounding her death suggest there may have been something she couldn't reconcile, something tied to the very nature of her family's exile and their connection to Thistle Ridge.

My suspicion is that the tragedy of 1441 has its roots in these earliest generations, rather than something that emerged sui generis from Thistle Ridge.

Let's boil this down:

Facts:
1. Letter Discovery: A letter dated 3540 MC (1406 DR) was found, signed "AA," likely written by Alvund Alavandor, my great-grandmother's elder brother.
2. The Deal: Alvund and a figure known as the Old Patriarch struck a deal requiring Alvund's family and another (likely the Clarandals) to "take what we were given and vanish forever."
3. Migration to Thistle Ridge: Alvund and Claudius Clarandal arrived in Thistle Ridge, each with their third wife—Aisling Maeliticus and Saralla Sakurth, respectively.
4. The Phylactery: Aisling Alavandor established the Phylactery, a successful apothecary, and school in Thistle Ridge, known for producing effective fertility medicines.
5. Unusual Fertility: Thistle Ridge was noted for its exceptional fertility and the health of its children, attributed to its "sacred" ground.
6. Suspicious Deaths: Aisling Alavandor ended her own life in 1439 DR after (?) the suspicious deaths of two of her children and a grandson.

Hypotheses:
1. Family Severance: The Alavandors in Sembia may have severed ties with Alvund due to his involvement with the Maeliticus family.
2. The Old Patriarch's Motive: The Old Patriarch orchestrated the exile of these families from Sembia, possibly to protect or control their offspring.
3. Family Union: The marriages to siblings within the Maeliticus and Sakurth families suggest a deliberate strategy, likely related to fertility or legacy.
4. Netherese Connection: Claudius Clarandal's ties to Daerlun under Netherese influence suggest the families' exile had deeper motives than political alignment.
5. Aisling's Suicide: Aisling's death may have been prompted by uncovering a dark truth related to the family's exile and the influence of Thistle Ridge.

Conclusion: The tragedy of 1441 likely has roots in the complex dealings and alliances formed by the earliest generations of the Alavandors and Clarandals, influenced by the Old Patriarch and possibly tied to a legacy of fertility and secrecy.

I awoke from a dream early this morning, and am writing it down before I lose the memory.

I was in Father's rooms back in Saerloon. On the wall of the study hung a portrait of my great-grandmother, appearing quite different from what I remember. I watched the coin approach-but-not as the sound began to thunder in my ears. Finally, as the sound began to overwhelm me, the coin slowed and fell on its side to reveal the face of a sharp-eyed queen. The coin sparkled, and then there was a sudden flash.
