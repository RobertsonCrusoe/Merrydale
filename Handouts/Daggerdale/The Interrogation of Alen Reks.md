A breathless courier had delivered the message to Alen Reks between laboured huffs of breath.
 
“Captain...Warburton wishes to...speak with...you...Sir.” Most likely Alen Reks had been called a number of things a number of times, with “Sir” being one of the least common titles but Captain Warburton was generally one to fall on the side of formality.
 
The young squire led Alen Reks to Loedegan’s Close in the city, not the first time Alen had been there but he was greeted by the imposing figures of Corran Altair and Drake Sterling standing upon either side of one of the interior doors within the ‘Close.

Neither of the men said anything nor seemed particularly open to discussion when Corran simply opened the door before Alen and waited for the man to pass through.
 
Oh my.

It was just as he had remembered it. Stuffy. Gothic. Intimidating in all the most interesting of ways, with the flying buttresses and harsh masonry. Idly, Alen wondered if the Lady of the Close was still around, haughty as ever, stirring some bubbling pot in the deepest belly of the Close, cackling all the while. It would be fun catching up. Lots of catching up to do, yes indeed. First things first, the changeling-turned-elf thought to himself as he glided up the path leading to the interior's threshold. Corran was there, sterling as ever, and Drake stood across the doorway from the paladin, thumbs through the belt at his waist, doing his best to strike a stoic pose.
 
Alen wasted little time. “Greetings Friend Altair! Sharp as ever, I see. Trim from head-to-toe, good to see, good to see.” He turned promptly and regarded Drake, pausing a moment to collect his thoughts, clapping his hands together before addressing him, bright-eyed as ever. “Hello Drake! HOW. ARE. YOU. FEELING?” He enunciated slowly, but without any sarcasm. Alen Reks knew little of wits, and last he had heard, Drake was in short supply of them since his tumble. More so than before the tumble. The lack of reply from the man forced a melancholy frown onto the changeling's face, and he turned to regard Corran with a sad shake of his head (as if to say such a shame) before making his way past the two and into the Close.
 
“I'd love to say and catch up with you fellows, but I've an important meeting with Warburton himself, or so I've been told. Perhaps he needs my opinion on something, I have plenty of those. We'll see.”
 
He paused again, eyes running over the interior of the foyer, not entirely sure where he should be going. He whirled and regarded Corran a third time, mouth open to ask, finger swishing through the air across the walls, like a dowsing rod in search of water. “Which... erm, where is the Captain...?”
 
Corran was quiet for a few heartbeats as he watched the animated man. Reks reminded him of Bollinger in some ways...and in other ways...quite the opposite. He could not help but wonder what it was that Captain Warburton indeed wanted of Reks but he remembered the instructions he had been given and he simply putted in the direction of the door that he had opened, a little used room that was off of the central antechamber in the 'Close. Corran levelled Alen Reks with a purposeful look and spook in a clear, firm tone.
 
“The Captain...is in there.”
 
Alen clapped his hands together and bowed quickly. “Ah, of course, there. How daft, for me to have forgotten.” In one motion he lifted himself upright, twirled on his heels and made his way towards the door, offering an overlong wave at the two White Cloaks still stationed at the entrance to the Close. Within seconds he was before the room Corran had gestured to, and delivered a quick series of raps against the polished oak of the door before turning the handle and allowing himself in, poking his head through the door with a broad smile on his face.
 
“Hello..?” Alen asked, his busy eyes dressing the chamber from floor to ceiling.

Iolus had run through the scenario dozens upon dozens of times in his head after having put all of it together. If he was correct...and he was quite sure he was; if he was correct, the impact Alen Reks could have made in the war against Netheril was incredible...in fact, the only thing greater than the benefit he was to the White Cloaks was the trouble he could bring down upon himself and the White Cloaks.
 
He kept his teeth together as the door opened and stuck his head through the opening. A muscle twitched in Iolus's jaw...the same way it did whenever he dealt with Bollinger. The room was empty, save a sturdy wooden stool situated in the very middle of the room. No windows. No decoration. Four sturdy walls, a ceiling, a floor and a sturdy stool to complete the set.
 
Iolus was dressed in blazing white robes with red and orange piping around the ends of his sleeves, the hem of his robes and the deep cutting neckline. When Alen looked in, he found Iolus, standing...leaning against the wall, his arms crossed and resting on his chest and after having brought himself to Iolus' attention, Iolus unfolded his arms, extended one and pointed to the stool in the middle of the room. He spoke in a low, but firm voice.
 
“Sit.”
 
Oh. Well. That was not at all what Alen had been expecting. Perhaps an office, plush and posh and beaming with all the symbols of hoity-toity religiosity that these Amaunatari seemed to sharply invested in. But nope. None of that. For walls, a floor, and a stool. Presumably for himself. Alen met Iolus's gaze, the contact sending a surge of electricity through his body, a chill in his spine, a churning of the gut, perhaps? The changeling felt sick, and for several moments he just returned the Captain's stare, the broad smile tightening into little more than a seam on his pointed face. Alen didn't speak, not a word. After several awkward seconds, Alen pulled his head back out of the doorway, not for a moment breaking eye contact, and shut the door on Iolus. He stood there for several seconds, the click of the handle echoing in his head, breath suddenly coming fast and shallow, his head swimming.
 
Alen Reks had been in this sort of situation before. The metaphorical cat was out of the metaphorical bag, and that cat was about to get metaphorically crucified. Wait, no, literally crucified. Instinctively, the changeling ran his hands along his face, clutching fistfuls of hair, panic clearly written on his sharp face. Cogs already whirling in the back of his mind, the changeling was looking about, eyes frantically searching for the quickest method of egress. Back firmly braced against the door, the man set his heels and prepared for a sprint.
 
It was almost as if the anxiety that coursed through Alen Reks had pushed everything else out of his head. Both Corran Altair and Drake Sterling stood there on either side of the door, surprised for a heartbeat that he closed the door on Captain Warburton.
 
“I know that look.” , Drake spoke in his low drawl. A hand was felt on Alen's upper arm. The same hand that had grabbed the Black Saint, held her down and then dragged her off a tower. That was the hand that now held Alen Reks. “That's the look of someone that's thinkin' 'bout runnin'.”
 
Corran intervened and stepped in front of Alen and gave him a stern look before speaking. “I do not know what you did, Reks and by the look on your face...I do not believe I want to know. The Captain told me to expect this. He wanted me to tell you this. That HE is the only way out of this. That if you do not go back into the room, you won't leave the Dale alive.”
 
“Ah, hah, ahaha...” Alen chuckled, eyes slowly lowering to appraise the heavy fist wrapped about his upper arm. He tugged slightly, gauging the tightness of Drake's grip, the expression on his slender face slowly turning from downcast into something of a malicious smirk. To twist his way out of that grip... well, it'd cost him the bone at the very least. That was for certain. The changeling levelled his wide grin at Drake, and was about to reply when Corran spoke up.
 
Alen had indeed panicked, much to his own embarrassment. Let his guard down, to the point where these two thick-necks had managed to get so close... but they were his... errrr, companions. Not that that mattered anymore. They knew. Did they know? Warburton must have, told them. No. Corran didn't, maybe... Perhaps Alen could get out of this.
 
...
 
No. The changeling passed one more glance at Drake, then shrugged his arm. The man obliged and released him, and with a careful series of steps, eyes darting between both of the men, Alen took hold of the latch, swung the door open and slipped inside. The door then clicked shut. There was still time to think. He could get out of this. Just like always.
 
Iolus was relieved when he heard the murmuring of voices and not the sounds of violence immediately following the talking. It had taken everything he had in him to not chase after Reks when he closed the door on him but it was important to show Reks that everything was under control. He had stayed up nights and nights trying to think of every possible course of action that Reks might take but it always came back to “being in control”.
 
When the door swung open and Reks smoothly inserted himself inside the room, Iolus mentally stroked one thing off the list and then steeled himself for what was left to take care of. He was still leaning against the wall when Reks reappeared and rather than point, he stayed silent and simply motioned towards the stool in the middle of the room with a subtle tilt of his head.
 
Alen took in his surroundings with the straightest face he could manage. There was one way in, and one way out. Foreboding filled him up, spreading from his gut like an infection, a septic threat he could barely rein in. The walls were closing in. The changeling laced his fingers behind his back, to brace them against the trembling of his limbs. Warburton was where Alen had left him, stoic and... resplendent, an odd look for the man. Robes seemed an ill fit for a Captain... the image always evoked heavy plate, a spear thrust high, bellows of encouragement flung over one shoulder.
 
Iolus was of a different sort, much different than Lumina; Alen could see that clearly enough. Lumina had been, or so Alen had heard, much of his contact with the woman being second-hand as it were, a fiery leader gripped with piety, often at the expense of strategy. Iolus, however... the gleam in his eye, that calculating look... at this point, Alen did not know which one he would have preferred. The zealot was always easy to predict -- purge the heretic and all that noise. The schemers, though... they were of a different, unpredictable sort. Unpredictable, at least, for minds such as those belonging to Alen Reks. He was a clever creature, but there was a subtle difference between wits and intellect. Such things were abundantly clear to Alen. He was just smart enough to know that he was not smart at all, and admittedly had no head for planning of any sort, with the exception of short-term schemes that paralleled his similarly short-term span of attention.
 
Ah! Speaking of attention. The changeling had been staring overlong at the stool occupying the centre of the room. His eyes flitted back to Iolus, unreadable as ever, who was in turn motioning back at the stool, as if to say sit. Alen politely declined with a proffered hand. “No thank you, I'll stand. You seem tired, however -- by all means, you have it.” His tone had a clearly cautious edge, but at the core of it, Alen was oozing with politeness, offering a smile to punctuate the reply.
 
“You seem a bit tired after all, Warburton. Captain Warburton, rather. Up late thinking, perhaps?”
 
Iolus gave Reks a small and seemingly amused smile before nodding his head slightly in what might have seemed a gracious response.
 
“I will leave it for you. After a while, you might be thankful for it.”
 
He tensed his shoulders for a moment as he pushed off the wall, all while still keeping his arms crossed and he took slow, evenly matched paces across the room, each step heard softly as his boots met the floor. He moved across the room, reaching the wall; pivoting and then making his way back across the room before stopping in the middle and turning his head to look at Reks.
 
“So...who else knows, Alen?”
 
He could have been coy. Played dumb. Who else knows what? What are you talking about, Captain? I'm utterly confused, why am I here again? Do you need something juggled, perhaps?
 
But no. Warburton knew. Alen had made a mistake, somewhere, some when. Agonizing self-doubt churned his mind, despite the tight smile he wore, he was frantically searching through his head, trying to find some context, some explanation, some foundation to work from. What did he know? Had Warburton seen? First-hand, or did someone else know, and had informed the Captain? He... Alen had just started to get used to Daggerdale. He sighed a ragged sigh that caught itself in his throat, close to a sob, pressing a hand to his face to conceal the growing crease beneath his eyes. It was all ruined, again. Oh well. Old habits and all that.
 
The elf strained his expression into a tight grin, head cocked to the side, one finger tapping idly against the top of his cheek. “Who else knows... what? I am at a loss...”
 
Iolus did not presume this was going to be easy but Reks's response was a disappointing one nevertheless. No longer simply looking at the elf with a turn of his head, Iolus turned completely, standing across the room from Reks with only the empty wooden stool between the two of them.
 
Iolus did not speak as he looked at Alen Reks. He simply gave the elf a look that all but shouted, “Really? Are we actually going to do it like this?”
 
It was subtle at first. To his credit, Alen managed to weather that gaze for all of half a minute before he lost his nerve. He closed his eyes, spread his hands wide in a gesture of indifference, and took a delicate step forward. The changeling could never stare overlong without cracking, despite the severity of the situation. It was worth a shot. Iolus knew. He knew Alen knew. Alen knew Iolus knew he knew. There was no recourse. Iolus had forced his hand.
 
Alen had never killed a holy man before.
 
Subtle. A faint sharpening of his teeth, the edges of his ever-widening grin seeming to stretch further than normally possible, pupils dilated into inhuman slits. Alen's handsome face twisted into a disquieting mask teetering on the cusp of outright monstrosity. He advanced another step, slow and sinuous. Alen felt the heaviness of his blade on his forearm, underslung so as to jump into his hand at the flick of his wrist. Alen had it all planned out in his head. He could plant his knife hilt-deep in the captain's forehead before either of them could blink or make a sound. Take those fancy robes, walk out the door with an air of undisputable authority, send those two louts standing outside off on some time-wasting errand... the changeling could be out of the Close within half a minute, and then after that it would be a simple task of vanishing into winding streets... and after that, who knows? Perhaps Featherdale. They'd find his naked body hours later, and by then Alen would be gone, same as always. The captain had made a dire mistake. He thought he had cornered the mouse with his feline cunning. But Warburton had it wrong. Alen was between him and the door. It was Iolus who was the mouse, and he had trapped himself in this room with a killer.
 
He laughed. “By my count, there's two people that... know.”
 
...
 
“You.” Alen pointed a finger at Iolus.
 
“And me.” He pointed at his own horrific visage.
 
He advanced another step, and stopped before the stool. That inanimate object would be the only witness.
 
Iolus had not known exactly how Reks had been doing what he had been doing. Magicks...potions...some other manner of thing that he was unaware of, but he was embarrassed at this moment when he admitted to himself that he had never expected this. His mind was already buzzing with questions, only to answer those questions himself, which only served to create more questions. It was possible that Reks had just taken on an entirely different and utterly alien-like form much as he could seemingly take on anyone's form but this...this was Alen Reks.
 
If that even is his name...which it most assuredly is not.
 
Iolus had indeed planned and although he was surprised by Reks's appearance, he wasn't surprised by the possibility of violence. Reks might act the clown much of the time but accounts from how he handled himself at the Liberty Hill ambush showed that he had a keen hand with a blade.
 
Even as Reks took a few steps closer, Iolus smoothly turned his back on the creature and took two steps towards the wall opposite Reks even as he spoke calmly and firmly.
 
“It's important for me to share with you the instructions I gave Altair and Sterling. Should only one of us leave open the door and attempt to leave, that person is to be cut down. Should two of us open the door, I am to be asked a question by Altair. A question that he believes only he and I would know the answer to. Should the incorrect answer be given...”
 
Iolus turned around once more to face the creature known as Alen Reks.
 
“...both of us are to be cut down. By now, Sterling has ensured that more White Cloaks wait just outside of the 'Close and should they be given the signal, they are to kill the next two individuals that leave the building. I would prefer it if both of us are breathing at the conclusion of this meeting Alen. Your way guarantees both our deaths. My way...well...it's possible, but unlikely.”
 
Iolus's green eyes narrowed slightly as he gave Reks what seemed to be a scolding look before he motioned with a slight tipping of his head towards the stool.
 
“So...as I said earlier...sit.”
 
Alen's shift in demeanour was... abrupt, to say in the least.
 
The captain's words struck him like venom, paralyzing him with a sense of dread, a sudden and neurotic panic that locked his knees and drove the vicious smile from his face, which had now reverted to the usual complement of charming elven features. Iolus had him trapped. Like a fly in a web. He had a contingency for situations Alen hadn't even anticipated... a perpetual flaw of his, Alen was aware. He was a reactionary creature by nature, and so far, his cleverness and... abilities had more than made up for his lack of foresight. But this man... Iolus Warburton had bested him soundly.
 
Shoulders slumped, back arched, head cocked back with his hands plastered against his face, mind racing for a solution, a way out, a loop hole, some final avenue of escape he could worm his way through. But no. There was nothing. And so he laughed. And unsettling chuckle that slowly wound down into an exhausted sigh. The sound of a man utterly at a loss, a man defeated.
 
Alen didn't take the seat. It looked, for a moment, as though the man were going to faint, to collapse to the floor, but instead he merely sunk into a rigid crouch, expression masked by the fringe of his hair, fingers gently prodding against the base of the stool. With a shake of his head, Alen straightened his arm, tilting the thing off balance. It fell, filling the room with a loud crack that jarred the silence between the two men.
 
The changeling spoke, face still pointed at the ground. His voice was little more than a murmur.
 
“Who told you? ... was it Vanion?” Alen grimaced at the thought. “Loyalty seems to be in short supply these days...”
 
The irony of that statement was lost upon him at the moment, melancholy as he was.
 
Iolus stayed where he was, watching Reks and giving him a confident and serene look.
 
“That is the last time you will lie to me Alen. I asked you previously who else knows and you told me no one. Your current statement leads me to believe that Vanion is aware of what ... of your secret.”
 
Iolus walked forward and crouched down a foot away from Reks, meeting his gaze, or would have met his gaze had Reks been looking up.
 
“Lie to me again, Alen and I will end this discussion and then I will end you. Can we move forward in this?”
 
Alen raised his head and focused on Iolus for several seconds before looking away, clearly upset, although his expression not so much one of stoic disdain as it was a child's pout. Despite the gravity of the situation, his attention was slipping.
 
“Pretty much everything I say is a lie. Including that. Especially that. Vanion's a whole bag of secrets. I should know, I've been through his things. You wouldn't believe.” He paused, perhaps considering whether he had crossed a line. He shrugged, and scratched the side of his nose with a casual flick of the finger. Head still cocked sideways, he flitted his dull gaze back to the captain, and continued.
 
“But he's neither here nor there.” He chewed his gums for a moment, eyes wandering. “I'll be honest with you for this conversation. Ask your questions, and then toss me to the hounds. It's all your sort ever does.”
 
This was what Iolus had feared. It was not just Reks that had been dishonest but Creed as well. He was concerned that he would find all manner of things he did not like, the more he dug.
 
“The hounds are the very least of your concerns, Alen. If the Questioners got a hold of you...”
 
Iolus shook his head and gave Reks a look of caution.
 
“Well...neither of us want that. This...situation will go as well as you wish it to go Alen. You are a very unique individual in a place where you could be incredibly valuable, and no doubt have some fun, as you seem predisposed towards...”
 
Iolus stood up from where he had been crouched in front of Reks and turned to walk back towards the far wall, talking as he did.
 
“...or you could cause an incredible amount of trouble and wind up dead at best, a prisoner at worst. You and I need to establish an understanding amongst one another. If this is going to work, you need to be honest with me. I do not care who you lie to from this moment forward...that is entirely your choice...but if this is going to proceed in a productive manner, when it comes to you and I...no more lies. Do we have an accord?”
 
He turned to look at Reks once more as he asked the question.
 
Wait, what? Alen's snort of disbelief shifted into a sharp, high-pitched chuckle.
 
“Hah. An understanding? Work? What are you playing at? You're saying that if I cooperate in whatever it is you've got schemed up in that grumpy little noggin of yours, I don't get to ride the rough end of a hangman's knot?”
 
He laughed again, and for a brief moment Alen Reks seemed almost comfortable crouched in the middle of the stark, unfriendly room. “And then after threats like that, you say what goes down here is entirely my decision? Please, Warburton. Blackmail is bad enough, but don't try to play it off as anything but what it is. You're twisting my arm here. How wonderfully bureaucratic, Captain.” Alen puffed out his cheeks and sighed.
 
“You have my word. Now what do you want from me?
 
Iolus arched an eyebrow as Reks became petulant.
 
“You are a foolish...”
 
He paused as he struggled with finding an appropriate term for Alen Reks.
 
“...individual, Alen. I am not playing at anything Alen. Daggerdale is at war and you could be an extremely valuable weapon in that war. We are not going to be friends or pals. You knowingly, wilfully entered Daggerdale; a place that prides itself on rooting out lies and now you feel wounded because I am twisting your arm? You should be thankful I am not boiling you in holy water right now Alen. I said you would be a weapon, not a slave. I do not care what you do for much of your day, each and every day. Make yourself available to the White Cloaks as you would have normally and from time to time, I will ask you to lie for me. I will ask you to be someone else. That is all.
 
\"It is and will continue to be your decision Alen. Just because you do not necessarily like the results of that decision does not negate the fact that I am allowing you this decision.
 
\"You should be thanking whatever gods you worship that Sergeant Dretch did not discover this first. I will not be sharing this with anyone. No one needs to know that you have been lying since you got here. No one needs to know of your abilities that bear an uncomfortable similarity to the witch, Deadnettle.
 
\"You are in over your head Alen. You have been the moment you set foot in Daggerdale. I am offering you a rope. Were I to approach you in any other fashion than this, you would have tried to run and if that was not available you would have lied constantly. If playing the victim in this makes you feel better and expedites you agreeing to my terms then I am fine with taking up the role of a villain. I would like to think you, most of all are capable of understanding and respecting the lengths I had to take in order to put you in this position.”
 
Alen just shook his head, his sullen expression twisting into one of utter irritation.
 
“You know nothing about me. I've done nothing to warrant this attention. I've helped the White Cloaks, helped Daggerdale. I've done nothing to wrong you, Iolus. And yet somehow you find out... you find out about me, somehow you figure it out, and the first thing that springs into your mind is a way for you to twist it to your advantage, to suit your agenda. You are a villain, Iolus. You want to wield me like a weapon for the good of the Dale? How is that any fucking different from slavery? You're mincing words.” The changeling paused. He wasn't angry. No. Just sad now. Mostly sad. “This is my home too, more than you know. But you and your people have made it abundantly clear that I'm not welcome here. Humans.
 
...
 
\"But you're doing me a favour, eh?”
 
And then he shifted into his natural form. The colour drained from Alen's skin and hair, the features on his face smoothing slightly in a disquieting fashion. He regarded Iolus with eyes like polished obsidian, narrowed with contempt, shimmering with moisture.
 
He spoke softly. “You're the monster here, Iolus. Not me.”
 
The first time Reks had shown his true face...it had unnerved Iolus somewhat but the second time; well...Iolus had seen worse.
 
“You have nothing if not a flare for the dramatic, Alen; but I suppose that had served you well in the past. Tell me. Where would it have stopped? First Troia and then...whom?”
 
Iolus raised an eyebrow inquisitively.
 
“I am quite sure that you taking Troia's form was not the first time you have done something like that and best intentions or no, you would get sloppy. Questions would be asked and you would be put in a position far worse than the one you are in now. Not only would your motives be in question but your association with the White Cloaks would also cause everyone to question the motives of the White Cloaks. I cannot and will not allow that to happen.”
 
Iolus let out a deep breath, his shoulders slumping momentarily before they straightened once again. It did not serve Iolus well to show how much this decision had weighed upon him.
 
“I could have you removed from the White Cloaks but you HAVE helped us AND the Dale. You strike me as a well-meaning but reckless sort and your...unique abilities could be used either as an advantage for us, or damn us all. You needed to be put in check, Alen. Your impersonation of Troia was useful to the greater good but had you been discovered, the good you did there and all the good you have done in the past would have been tainted by the pre-conceived ideas of those that call the Dale their home.
 
\"I am your Captain and you have lied to me. That is unacceptable. Surely you are capable of recognizing the threat you could be were you to be ever turned to the side of our enemy. Work with me on this Alen. I am comfortable with being the monster you need me to be if it means you are loyal to the White Cloaks and to the Dale.”
 
It was done. Warburton had him by the throat. The man could play at being polite and reasonable, but in the end, blackmail is blackmail. And the necessity of it was not lost upon young Mr. Reks. Warburton had conviction. He was willing to do what he had to. For the Dale, for Amaunator, for the people he cared about. Alen was either with him, or he was against him. This, Alen understood.
 
What he didn't understand, not at first at any rate, was the strange revelation Alen found at the back of his mind. The realisation that he envied Warburton. Just a bit.
 
For the longest time, Alen had looked out for no one but himself. Who else would, after all? The Captain was a different creature entirely. His devotion to his cause was unyielding.
 
Perhaps it was empathy, Alen mused. It was... a difficult thing for the changeling to comprehend. A creature capable of being anything, anyone... it was so simple for him to disengage from his life, to start over and pretend to be someone else. Ethics, dreams, personalities, opinions, morals... they were like clothing to him, gone through and discarded at a moment's fancy. But for people such as Warburton... those qualities were a life-time's accumulation. Slow to change, and the very foundation of what a person is.
 
And Alen had no real concept of them. He was just a series of masks, each one ready to be tossed aside at a moment's notice. What lay beneath the layers? Was there even an Alen Reks to begin with? Or was he just an empty suit of skin, going through the motions?
 
What was the point of living if all you do is survive? The conclusion of this train of thought left the pallid creature tapping a finger against his smoothly rounded chin, deeply lost for several minutes.
 
What melancholy nonsense. Alen barked a laugh, sudden and edged with the slightest hint of hysteria. He was no philosopher.
 
Knees cracking, the changeling lifted himself up from the crouch he had assumed. Colour poured back into his skin and hair and pointed ears, rendering Alen's usual disguise once again intact.
 
“I... will work with you on this, Warburton. Captain. It has been too long since I had anything worth doing.”
