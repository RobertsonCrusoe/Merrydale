8 Mirtul, 1483 DR – A Discussion with Administrator Reks about The Slave Orbs and Sundry Other Matters

From the personal notes of Lucan Alavandor

By Beshaba's left buttock, what a strange night it's been. Just parted ways with Administrator A. Reks an hour past, after a supper at Quick Axes that I sure as the nine hells didn't plan. I'd meant to dine with the Captain, but she was indisposed, off doing the inscrutable things immortals do when they don't want to be… scruted. So, I made my way to Quick Axes where I thought I would sup and drink and see what opportunities (or skirts) the evening might throw my way after a tenday of selfless heroism. Before I could so much as scan the room for prospects, however, I found myself caught up in a current not of my own choosing.

"Very good, Master Alavandor," a blonde slip of a serving girl—Hannah, I learned—ran up to me and curtsied as I crossed the threshold. Fourteen summers at most, but she had the poise and curves of a woman twice her age and knew exactly what to do with both, I reckon. Mark my words, this one will run a tavern of her own before she's twenty-five. "A room has been arranged for you and your guests upstairs." This was news to me, but before I could so much as ask who arranged what, or with whose coin, Hannah was already gesturing to an even younger waif perched on the patio railing, who nimbly leapt from her perch to race off through the market and toward Loedegan Close like her skirts were on fire.

On those occasions when it's not his worst enemy, Waukeen knows, curiosity is a merchant's best friend, so I followed Hannah up the stairs. I'll confess the view from behind didn't make me regret my decision. On the upper landing, she directed me to a small suite furnished with three beds, three trunks, a serviceable table and chairs, and a pair of plush seats in the corners that, somewhat undwarfishly, looked like someone in the establishment actually gave a damn about comfort. The room was warm, one wall backing onto the grand fireplace on the other side, where I could hear older patrons swapping lies over their tankards. The place was packed with a lively spring crowd. It would have been a good night to work the tavern and see what coin or favours or bedmates might shake loose, but Tymora had other games in mind for me tonight. Besides, when has Lucan the Lucky ever walked away from a mystery?

"Administrator Reks has instructed me to convey that he accepts your kind invitation to dinner, and hopes you won't find it too forward of him to have chosen his preferred dining room…" Hannah paused to look about the warm and inviting room, and wrinkled her nose just enough to acknowledge that calling this a 'dining room' was a stretch even by tavern standards.

My invitation? I thought, but kept my mouth shut. No point showing my hand when I didn't even know what game we were playing yet.

"He has also instructed me to inform you that he will be along shortly, and to enjoy a drink, or two, while he makes himself presentable. What will you have, Master Alavandor?"

So, there I sat waiting for Reks, while Hannah made sure my whistle stayed wet. I listened to the jovialities outside the door. The balcony level was as full as the rest of the place, and I put my ears to good use. Old men nearby were pissing and moaning about the weather and how winters aren't as hard as they used to be, while down in the main hall some rowdy fellows were singing songs filthy enough to make a Chauntean blush. That's to say, they were trying to make them blush and failing beautifully. I'd missed Greengrass, but it sounded like the festivities were still going strong. The songs were deliciously inappropriate, full of references to spring planting and ploughing and milking teats and grinding grain that had nothing to do with cattle or crops. I enjoyed them tremendously.

A couple of times I stepped out onto the balcony to take it all in. I cut a fine figure in my blue jacket, not having thought to dress down for the evening, and I caught plenty of eyes. The older men gave polite nods, younger lads grinned like they wanted to buy me a drink and hear some stories, and more than a few women gave me the kind of look that told me they knew exactly what kind of trouble I was and were weighing whether I was worth it. Any other night I'd make my case, but I didn't need the distraction while I was waiting to see what game Reks was running. Not yet, at any rate.

I went back inside, helped myself to another drink, and then another. Hannah kept them coming and kept apologizing for Reks taking his sweet time. After the third refill, I decided to poke at her a bit. "You're going to a lot of trouble for this half-elf peacock, my girl. Are you in his employ as well as the tavern's?"

"Not…really…master," she said, and I caught the awkward hesitation. I had her off balance for half a second, which is always satisfying, but she recovered quick enough. "I'm an orphan girl and have lived at the Close for near on four years, you see, and I owe the Administrator and Madame Blackmane a great deal, though they say to think nothing of it. I run the occasional errand for the Administrator, and look after what the Lady of the House tells me to, but I'm not in anybody's employ, save the dwarves of this fine establishment." She said it with just enough steel in her spine to let me know she wasn't intimidated by a Sembian in a fancy jacket asking pointed questions.

Sharp girl. Loyal too. Reks has these orphans well in hand, but they seem happy enough about it.

I smiled. "Of course, my dear," I said, and off she went to fetch me drink number four in what I was sure was not yet an hour. I moved from chair to chair, tested out the beds (always useful to have the lay of the land should the opportunity for a quick tumble present itself) and had squeezed about every drop of entertainment—not to mention the last of my patience—out of the little room, when the door opened and Hannah returned with two other girls carrying what I presumed was dinner. Steaming bowls of chicken-and-barley broth with tender greens, a platter of pan-crisped river trout over mashed turnips and buttered peas, accompanied by a warm and crusty loaf fresh from the oven and a bowl of melted butter for dipping. A good meal by Dales standards, and probably the best Quick Axes had on offer tonight. My stomach was growling loud enough to be heard in Sembia, and I was just reaching for the trout—manners be damned, I'd earned it sitting here like a trained dog—when the door burst open again.

This time it was the Administrator himself, decked out in a smart grey woollen suit with pristine white gloves, his silvered hair slicked back like he'd just stepped out of a Cormyrean portrait. He had this expression of mock surprise and delight plastered on his face, and launched into some elaborate bowing and hand-waving routine that looked like he was taking the piss out of courtly manners. Though with Reks, I'm beginning to realize, you can never quite tell if he's mocking something or just being himself. I doubt even he knows.

He straightened himself and his jacket, took an exaggerated step into the room, looked around and switched to an expression of utter disgust. "What a terrible room!" he proclaimed, turning his nose up at the furniture like it had personally offended his ancestors, before stalking toward the table where I sat. I pushed my chair back to stand and greet him properly, but Reks had already locked onto the dinner like a cat spotting a rat. He bent over the table, hands clasped behind his back, and drew his face close to inspect the food, ignoring me completely. I had to step aside to get out of the way of his little performance. He took a deep theatrical sniff of the meal and then reeled backward like he'd been shot in the back with an arrow. He covered his face with one hand while the other supported his arching back in the most dramatic pose I'd seen since the last time I watched the street players in Saerloon.

"By all the devils in Hell, have they put a troll's chambre pot on my table?" he wailed at the ceiling, frozen in his pose of agony. "The smell of it could soften the morning engorgement of an incubus, and it looks worse than it smells! Children…!" And that's when I noticed a half-dozen orphan faces crowding the doorway, watching their lunatic headmaster with expressions somewhere between excitement and confusion, before an older girl, whom I presumed to be their leader, motioned them inside. They were dressed in simple but serviceable garb, with their young matron wearing a coat of a style that I remember being fashionable in Sembia a few seasons back. They scurried in and about us, starting to clear the table while Reks held his dramatic arched pose like a statue, not moving a muscle as they worked around him. Until a boy reached for the plate with the fish. Then Reks's gloved hand shot out from his face, pointing at the boy and the offending trout. "Window, my boy." The boy and several others sighed, and I caught someone muttering "I hate it when he does this" before the older girl shushed them.

The boy set the plate down carefully—I could see him eyeing that trout with real longing—then sighed, crossed to one of the windows, opened it, and stepped aside. Reks, without ever even looking at the plate, as though its lone unseeing eye might turn him to stone, strode over with his face turned away, picked up the platter with his gloved hand and carried it to the window, his arm outstretched before him. He held it out the window and paused. The children all drew an anxious breath. Then Reks let it drop. The muffled crash from below set off a yelp of surprise and anger and then a string of curses from some poor wretch on the street. Still facing away from the window and his outstretched hand, Reks peeled off his glove with his other hand and tossed it out after the plate.

The young matron then herded the children back out of the room and shut the door. Now it was just the three of us standing in awkward silence. I caught her eye and as she held my gaze, she raised her eyebrows and rolled her eyes as she offered me the most eloquent shrug. I almost laughed. Meanwhile Reks finally straightened up and turned to face the window, conspicuously examining his bare hand as though it might be contaminated, before asking, "Where, Miss Lover Clown, is our host?"

"Begging your pardon, it's Cloverdown, Administrator," the girl said smartly and without hesitation. The absence of ire or insult told me that she had clearly corrected him a dozen times before and no doubt expected to do it a dozen more.

"Is it so, young lady, that an orphan girl can choose any name she likes? Well, why not, I suppose, and anyway, let's dispense with tawdry names that invite wicked musings, shall we? What of our host, and that ogre's spittle-cup that was laid out so offensively?"

A note on Reks. He is not a man to be taken lightly. He's much like the card-sharpers and street artists I knew back in Saerloon—he engineers absurdity, watches us all flounder distractedly about in it, then steps in magnanimously to forgive our confused incomprehension. The trap, the springing of it, and the pardon are all his, while everyone else is kept off-balance until he decides to make his move. It's elegant, in a way, if damnably annoying; the mark never quite knows what just happened, and if they ever did work it out and brought it before a magistrate, Reks could account for every move or statement with a straight face and walk away clean. Say what you will about the man (and I intend to say plenty) but the workmanship is sound.

"Master Alavandor is here, as you know, Administrator. You nearly knocked him over. And you've thrown the fish out the window. Again…"

I gave the girl a nod of thanks and she smiled back with a little curtsey. Reks pivoted on his heels, one foot back, spinning like a dancer, so that he now faced both of us standing there by the now-empty table. He looked Miss Cloverdown up and down slowly and deliberately, one eyebrow raised impossibly higher than the other, before breaking into a broad smile and feigning sudden surprise at discovering me to be standing right next to him. His eyes bugged theatrically and he covered his mouth with both hands—one gloved, one bare—as though I'd materialized out of thin air.

"Master Alavandor, you are as sneaky as they say! When and how did you come to this terrible chambre?!"

I had opened my mouth to respond with some witticism or other when Reks silenced me with a hastily produced finger interposed between us, his attention swivelling back to Miss Cloverdown. "Is there no better room to be had, Miss Slobber Down? It is dark and cold in here, and I can still smell that goblin excrement they had the poor sense to plate and table!"

"Madame Blackmane has reminded you many times of my family name, Master Reks. I'll thank you again for your manners," the young lady said, politely but calmly giving him a practiced curtsey before shooting me an apologetic look. "And this is the room you requested…the one you always request…"

The girl had spine, I'd give her that.

"Very good, Miss Blow Her Down, you are dismissed…for now. Please do bring us a proper meal though, could you kindly? I'm not sure what could be found on such short notice, my apologies to the chef for any inconvenience…"

"We've already brought dinner, as you know, Master Reks," she said, not backing down an inch. "I'll have them bring it in presently…"

So the "proper meal" was already prepared and waiting. I was starting to like Miss Cloverdown quite a bit. Once again, though, I found myself distracted trying to figure out the logic of his ploy, only to conclude that the distraction was the point. I kept my face neutral.

The same children who'd carried off the perfectly good dinner I'd been about to eat came back with baskets and started laying out a new table. First came a crisp white linen tablecloth, which Reks inspected ridiculously closely with a monocle like he was examining a contract for forgery. He drew in this impossibly long breath that started as disapproval, looking down at the young girls smoothing the linen flat. They froze and stared up at him like sinners waiting for Kelemvor's judgment. Then his breath turned into a smile and a nod of approval, and the girls giggled and bounced out of the room like they'd just passed some test. The rest of them unpacked the baskets—fresh plates, cutlery, wine glasses, wrapped cheeses and meats, and three bottles of red wine. Reks actually clapped and hopped up and down when he saw the wine. All the children save Miss Cloverdown fled the room, making enough racket on their way out to draw looks from the patrons outside.

Reks carried on with his foppish gesticulating, admonishing the little matron and the room and the weather, then praising all three in the same breath, until finally Miss Cloverdown escaped and left the two of us alone. We sat down at the table that was much more sparsely laid than it had been moments earlier—one plate of various cheeses, a few cuts of meat and three bottles of wine having taken the place of a fine and full Dales supper. Reks spent half an hour going on about the cheeses and just as long on the wine, though I'll admit the windbag knew his vintages. Two of the three were from my homeland, and the third, conspicuously, was a Clarandal '38. I was intrigued by the offering, but again caught myself speculating pointlessly about his motivations in providing it.

What follows is our conversation as best I remember it. (I was more than halfway to drunk before we even got to the meat of it.)

Reks: Do look, we've drank that first bottle of Sembian without even addressing the quality of that Dwimmerholme Sharp. But a rosy complexion is best for the consumption of one's evening meal, I'd argue. But let's get to why we're here, in such a terrible room on such a night. Why have you summoned me?

Me: I did no such thing, Administrator, as your devious mind knows full well. I'll play along if that keeps you talking, but let's get to the point before I'm too far into my cups to care and in need of prettier company than yourself.

Reks: (feigning hurt, bare hand on heart) You wouuuuuund me, Master Alavandor! If I dress like one of these barmaids would that put a glimmer in your eye? Or perhaps, oho just perhaps, I could put on a blue skirt and blouse, and maybe a little blue makeup, and bounce fey-like upon your lap? Oh, wouldn't that be just the thing?!

Me: It would not, but you're already talking business, unless this wine's gone to my head faster than I thought.

Reks: I think it will take many bottles yet to dull your senses, and I'll do my level best to carry out my duty! But you caught me yes, oho! Business it is. And yes, a bit of a ruse and all that, switching roles of 'guest' and 'host', but I'm all for a good role reversal once or twice in a full moon, if you catch my meaning, Master Alavandor. And I see you do, so let me talk plainly and dispense with my playfulness and whimsy, what ho!

Me: Please do. (I waited for him to start talking. He did not.) Administrator?

Reks: Oh! What was it you were saying, Lucan? Can I call you familiar? Would that be meet, your given name rolling from my tongue in a small and terrible room?

Me: I was waiting for you to start talking, as I am well aware that you have no difficulties in that department. And yes, you may call me Lucan.

Reks: Lucan. Lu…can…LUCAN! Who can? Lucan! Ha ha! Wonderful! I'm happy to answer your first question, which was about where your Captain and that little bit of hobbit-leather that polishes her plates whilst she's away on business went this afternoon with… (he looked around suspiciously and then leaned in like a conspirator to whisper the word)…Invictus… (at this, he half stood in his chair, feigning readiness to bolt for the door, before settling once more with a chuckle and topping up our glasses).

Me: I'm sure I never asked about either of them.

Reks: Indeed, well then I misheard. I mishear all the time, if only on account of hearing so much, and rather, it isn't mishearing that is the problem per se, but … misfiling … misunderstanding … miss…miss…Miss Cloverdown ought to be here to take notes should she not? What indeed happened to that little minx? No doubt spying on me again for that insufferable headmistress Blackmane… (At this, he covered his mouth and made a mocking display of shock). Oh, my stars. I've said too much already. And yet hardly anything at all. I was answering your questions before you distracted me with drink, and promises of missiles I think it was. But yes, I know where Captain Monarch went. I think that nosey friend of yours in the abbey went too. Whatsisname…Vulvavian or some such, I believe?

Me: Vessuvian.

Reks: The very same! Yes, they've been in the Historic Wing, the lucky meddlers! What has Alen Reks, dutiful servant of the dales and accomplished hero seen of the Historic Wing? About as much as you, Lucan, but I know a thing or two, and I can guess what they were looking at, or not looking at, as the case may be. And I'll tell you something Invictus can't, if you'll just make vague promises this instant that we can awkwardly negotiate in terrible faith in the near future. Would that suit you, Lucan?

Me: It suits me perfectly, I promise.

Reks: I love a promise, and a promise for a promise doubly so! So let me reward my new friend's confidence, trust and offer for shared carnal knowledge… (he paused and winked at me. I shook my head in disbelief, which only prompted an even longer affected pause, until Reks winked at me again before continuing) Yes, yes, let us talk about Belladonna, and… (again affecting a mock conspirator's poise) …Verinius!

(I drew a breath and said nothing. He'd caught my attention now.)

Reks: Oh yes, Alen Reks, Administrator and hero of Liberty Hill knows the name Verinius. And Vortellius and Varros as well. But those two aren't as interesting, are they? Indeed, they are not, and we both know why, but will not mention it here, though I'll talk to you in code, if you're a clever clogs, my handsome Sembian. Are you a clever clogs?

Me: I've had just enough wine to be clever and not enough to be stupid, so let's have it, Reks.

Reks: Vortellius and Varros share a similarity RIGHT NOW with each other that they do not share with Verinius. Correct?

Me: Correct.

Reks: They also shared a certain similarity on 1 Kythorn of 1480 did they not? (He poured another glass of wine for both of us, emptying the second bottle.)

Me: They did.

Reks: Indeed. You are a clever clogs. Do you know, I was there on the Table of the Sun, with Hoetmer, Estelmer, Bannit, the Bishop, all the rest. All three orbs right in front of us, and a bent-over priest of…whatever papery god Hoetmer actually whispered to, looking at the orbs and taking notes. Do you know that was the only time he was allowed out of the prison…er…the Historic Wing? I never heard tell of him getting a glimpse of the sky aside from that day. So, what was so special? And why did he make the recommendation he made? You know what he said, do you not?

Me: I think I do. Go on. I didn't know you were there for Liberty Hill.

Reks: You did so! Say you did! You wound me, Alavandor!

Me: I only know what I read in Estelmer's report, the one Fitzdecker provided. She mentions you in passing in her chapter "The Heart of the Shadow, The Heart of the Dale." 25 Mirtul, right?

Reks: Twenty-sixth…but just so… (Reks paused for a while, drained his glass and filled it again. Drained it and filled it again before he continued. He looked genuinely introspective. It was the only time I've ever caught a glimpse of genuine emotion from him, I'm sure.) That was the last time I made my living by the knife, Lucan. I told myself I was better with my wits than my blade, if you'll credit it. Anyway, it was an uncomfortable scene, as you will recall from the reading, and it was much worse in person…much, much worse. The Sister Superior didn't have the stomach for plain gore in print, to her credit. But… (the pain in his face softened and a lightness reemerged)

Me: A painful memory it seems, now passing…

Reks: Not so much passing as changing to the memory of Fitzdecker. You'd like him. We'd have more fun together than we'd be able to tell anyone about. But yes, Fitzdecker asked me the same questions you were just asking.

Me: I've maybe had a bit too much to drink. Got my tongue over my eye-tooth, as my grandmother was wont to say. What was I asking again?

Reks: You were inquiring about the research that Estelmer and Hoetmer did on the Table, and where the report went that they wrote. I never did see that report, and there's no Estelmer or Hoetmer around to speak to it, more's the pity. I know this; Verinius, aside from his temporal and geographical differences from his mates, which we've discussed, also stands apart in capability.

Me: What evidence? (I allowed a small hiccup for effect.)

Reks: Indeed, I see you now lining things up in your mind like a ledger, neat, orderly, just so. And just so, consider this, this and much more; who bore Verinius?

Me: Belladonna.

Reks: The same! Who was the leader of the Dark Garden?

Me: Bella—

Reks: THE SAME! Who survives to this day, despite having been pile-driven by one of my men from fifty feet, imprisoned, tried in front of the entire town, and now living upon the Table of the Sun (albeit accosted daily by my former boss?) Yes, you are correct again, Lucan. Belladonna! Either she was the greatest of the Dark Garden and thus merited the greatest of the orbs, or it was the greatest of the orbs and made her the greatest of the Dark Garden, indeed so great as to survive to this day. Do you know Dretch speaks her name every morning at Matins? Would you like your delicious Sembian moniker on his lips every day before his most sacred prayers?

Me: All right, I'll buy into this tale you're spinning. She was the greatest of them, and her rank could be enough to merit the greatest orb. What else? Because that tells me nothing I couldn't work out on the chambre pot.

Reks: You are dearly right my dear boy. So, what else? What do we know of Belladonna's works? Of the evils she wrought with the orb?

Me: You mean the opening in the Dagger Hills? At the Shadowdam? Or the Soul of Corruption? What even is that? I've never read what was meant by it, and no one will talk about it.

Reks: First, you are correct on both accounts. Verinius alone can open portals. The other two were good for run-of-the-mill mischief, like breeding monsters and commanding the undead. You wouldn't have to go looking for Netherese artefacts to accomplish those things, though, would you? But Alen Reks knows what the Soul of Corruption was. Or what it was to him, and to some others besides. Would you like to hear a bit? You would wish you hadn't if I tell you the full truth of it in the worst details I know, but I'll give you…just the tip, if you'd like a taste?

Me: (scoffing at the image) I think you've got our positions confused, friend. But yes, tell me what you know.

Reks: It was so terrible because it was, for every man, his greatest transgression, or transgressions for those of us that lived a fuller life. I won't tell you what Alen Reks saw, or what the Soul of Corruption said or showed him, but some vignettes I can share; there was a Sun Blade, a third sergeant if I recall correctly who retired after Liberty Hill. He had spent much of his career at the Watergate Customs Court, and worked under one of the more wicked magistrates there. If I remembered their names I wouldn't give them, because some crimes are best left in the ground, but this particular sergeant on many occasions carried out…unusually cruel sentences…handed down by the customs magistrate to hapless and guiltless travellers and visitors to our fine town. For him, the Soul of Corruption looked and sounded like the ghosts of those people, and he said he could hear their pleas, and smell their blood and excrement when foul judgements were handed out. He prayed that he could forget all that the Soul of Corruption showed him, and you can imagine what happened to him once that same magistrate's friends heard talk of 'forgetting'.

For another man, a farmer in the militia, and a stout volunteer for a dire and hopeless battle, the Soul of Corruption was simply the vision of his late wife and daughter, both of whom died under the unaccountable circumstances that so many young women face in the lonely and rural parts of our civilization. Though the farmer fought through whatever transgression arrayed itself before him, and though he tried to make peace with it by confessing to a magistrate within earshot of yours truly, he ultimately took his own life, as have so many others that faced that wicked entity.

I won't dwell on it longer, though; I don't think it has a place in your current story; the ritual to summon it was a scheme hatched by the Dark Garden over years, with time, resources and patience. Your enemy is short on all of those, so I think we can move on, though other souls may not.

Have you tried this cranberry brie? Halfling-made, and they are the only people that should touch cheese, if you want my opinion, and you've asked for it after all.

(We sat in silence for a while. I took several sips of wine and tried the cranberry brie. He wasn't wrong. It was wonderful.)

Me: I hate to dispense with the foreplay, Reks, but how did Verinius get out? And how did it get to Loki? And what else is she going to do with it? And what does any of that have to do with the fate of the "Alavandors" and "Clarandals?" (Oops, why had I said that?!)

Reks: (He took off his one remaining glove and made exaggerated air-quotes with his fingers.) "Alavandors" and "Clarandals," eh?! What ho, my chum, but you've held something back, haven't you? Well, I won't press, because I'm the one singing tonight, but it will be your turn next time, if you live to profit by my loose tongue. (He winked.) But those are very specific, very…unsubtle … questions, and you're right that they lack the requisite petting and heavy breathing to get them onto their backs. All the same, sometimes a guileless rogering is what's needed, so I'll answer what I can.

The first question is entirely erroneous, and you've already answered it, if you really think hard. Maybe when you return to your casebooks and put this into your logical written words, you'll see the truth of it.

Me: I don't think—

Reks: Perfect! No time for thinking, Lucan. That gets you into the worst trouble you tend to find, and when you deign to think again you'll think me right and thank me properly. But back to the rogering, perfunctory and hoggish though it be. The answer to your second question will likely be answered by the first, when it can be answered. Lest you think me a sphinx with the answers to all these riddles, I'll clear that up presently; I don't know exactly what the answer is to the second question, but Loki knows; you should ask her. I expect you'll be hearing from her again. Which brings me to the third question…

Me: What can she do with it? What will she do next?

Reks: That's two questions, but I'll answer them both for a copper, as you've been a proper host. Her pattern here is to put them into the hands of agents and insert them into long and complicated schemes. She tried with Charrah, but you foiled her. She'll use it to go home and find another lackey. She won't risk her neck directly, especially so long as Monarch is above the ground; the Netherese hate and fear the eldar, and Monarch has given them every reason to account their fears warranted. I think you'll have to wait for it to resurface again, if she's allowed to get away with it now.

Me: That leaves us—

Reks: Too little time for further talk about generations and decades and centuries and schemes and treachery. It is a fine spring night, there are girls dancing in the Old Market, and we are down to our last bottle. Let's go share it, Lucan. Who can? Lucan!

I'm going to relieve myself, and then I will meet you on the front patio before our excursion to meet some Chauntean girls. Do hurry!

(Reks straightened himself and his jacket, palmed the last bottle of wine with a practiced hand, slipping it into a pocket that seemed made for exactly that purpose. His pilferage accomplished, he glided out of the room before I could even get my jacket on.)

I was just thinking about how much wine I'd put away on damn near no food when Hannah appeared with a bill. "The room and the meal will be five gold, Master Alavandor. Administrator Reks sends his thanks and regards for hosting dinner."

Of course he did. By the time I made it to the patio, five gold lighter, my erstwhile "guest" was nowhere to be seen.
