==Recent History==
The Close was given to the White Cloaks by Luminous Whitegate after the Saints' Battle, in 1480.  Sister Estelmer of Waterdeep notarized the transaction on the eides of Eleasias, 1480 DR.

As part of the agreement, Hasting Blackmane, steward of the Close, is to be kept on.  She is to name her replacement when the time comes.  

==History and Details==
Built in 1403 as both a memorial to his dead wife, Sarah (Weaver), and as a wedding gift to his new wife, Becca (Weaver, Sarah’s younger sister), Loedegan Whitegate had the Close designed by a Waterdeep architect and mason to mirror the gentle buildings of the well-to-do of that distant city. It was also designed to be a home away from the family farm at Liberty Hill and the cold Castle. Three generations of Whitegates have enjoyed this manor seasonally; usually the children came here after the harvest for school for the winter. There has always been a large retinue of people living and working here. The running of the place is overseen by one family member who takes the title ‘Steward of the Close’.

The manor itself is located along the western wall of the Inner Bailey, (just south of the old Barracks. The three large buildings that were there were purchased by Loedegan, and demolished. A new wall was built across the open area, running from the Barracks wall to the outer wall of the town, completely enclosing the estate. However, this wall was not designed to be defensive. It is made of the white stone that comes from the quarry near Liberty Hill, and is an elegant construction; it is only 12’ high, and the entire face is punctuated by 1’ wide, 8’ high openings, repeated every 4’ through which anyone can look into the grounds, or even squeeze through if they were small enough. The main entrance is an archway a little higher than the wall itself, and though there are a pair of iron gates installed, they have never been drawn closed.          

Beyond the wall is the estate grounds, which is a basically a quadrangle of three buildings built hard-upon the walls. These buildings are likewise gracile in appearance, with many large windows and delicate buttresses. The small courtyard is dominated by a white stone polished sundial in the middle of a small fountain, and white flagstones stretching across it all. Rows of low cedar bushes line the front of every building to break up the overwhelming white facade of the place.      

Directly across from the gate is the manor home itself; a 3-story structure that is certainly the finest building in the entire dale. The face of the building is dominated by its many evenly-spaced windows, and the huge balcony on the second level, which takes up about 1/3rd of the total width of this floor. There are six fine glass doors that let out onto that balcony, and it could hold perhaps 50 or more people if an occasion demanded it.    

To the right of this building is a fine stables, about 16’ high, also built in a similar style, but with fewer windows. To the left is a large staff house, 2 stories tall, and nearly as elegant as the manor. It has nearly as many windows, and a smaller balcony on the second floor.    

All of the buildings have pressed copper roofs which have begun to age with a green patina.

The main building is laid out in the now-fashionable H-wing plan of the gentle homes of the west coast. The central part of the home is a large grand entry, with an enormous stair dominating the centre. There are kitchens and other work spaces in behind. The north and south wings are huge rooms for entertaining, each of which takes up 1/3 of the total footprint of the home; the north wing is a grand ballroom, while the south wing is a huge dining hall with 3 large oak tables, which could sit about 60 people.      

The stone walls of the exterior are hidden inside by complete floor-ceiling panels of ¼-sawn oak. Most of the furniture and all of the other fixtures in the place are likewise constructed of this warm, dark wood, leaving only the white stone of the floors to reflect the face of the building.  

The second floor of the manor is the usual day-to-day living space for the family, with many rooms for dining, entertaining, working, and so on. There is a small classroom here for private lessons attached to the family library (the shelves of which are only about 20% full). Most of the activity in the manor happens on this level.    

The third floor has only 7’ ceilings, making it warm and intimate. This level has 16 bedrooms and many powder rooms. About half of the rooms are guest rooms that have bronze name-plaques on the doors with a number and a name, such as “Dagger Hills Suite”. There is also a small chapel on this floor with a floor-ceiling window looking out over the town. About 12 people could gather in here on the oak benches which face the window.        

The estate is not in disrepair or neglect, but there is an ominous feeling about the place. Most of the rooms are completely empty, though the marks on the floors give away the fact that much fine furniture has recently been taken out of the place. The large tables of the dining room are still there, as they were constructed in that room and would need to be sawn down to remove, but most of the chairs are gone, as are the sideboards and other accoutrements.       

On the upper floors, most of the rooms have also been emptied. The library looks like it was nearly ransacked. There are only a couple of rooms that remain furnished; at one end of the main hall on the third floor is the Office of the Steward, which is a large suite including a bedroom for the steward (presently Hasting Blackmane). At the other end of the hall is the master suite, which theoretically accommodates Luminous when he is in town, which is rarely.    

The rooms and halls of this fine home reply with a lonesome echo, every sound that is made within these walls. The emptiness of the place is a sombre reminder that a great enemy is arrayed against the people of Daggerdale, and that the lords of this dale have made personal sacrifices that few are even aware of, to keep it safe.
