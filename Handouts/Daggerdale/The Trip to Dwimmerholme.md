This is a narrative from the DM about the trip from Dagger Falls to Dwimmerholme, on the 6th of Marpenoth, 1481 DR

800px|right

The morning of the 6th of Marpenoth promises to be the nicest day in a week. There is a thick late-autumn fog over everything, rolling off the river Tesh and filling the valley for as far as the eye can see; for the guards at the North Gate, who can indeed see half-way to Dwimmerholme, up the gentle slope of the Tesh Valley, the fog has the look of a vast lake that has swallowed up the orange and golden oaks of Cormanthor.  

It is twenty miles of good road from the North Gate to the edge of Dwimmerholme.  The road goes through the thick oaks of the voracious forest; the path is marked every dozen or so yards by a stump or two, where the road-wardens, the Sun Dragoons and the Foresters (4th and 8th Companies of the Sun Blades) have cut back the forest in their ceaseless struggle to keep the road open against a forest that wants it closed.  For the first few miles, the track is broad and well-packed.  It travels up from the basin of the valley through with the powerful Tesh flows, climbing a gentle, gradual slope that is punctuated by the odd small farm or homestead.  In the last few years, some people of the dale have begun building and settling outside of the walls of the big villages and the town again.  They are met with mixed success, though the families that have settled outside the walls of the town, though close to it, seem to be doing well.  

Many families take a break from their toils; bringing in the late crops, chopping wood and tending animals, to take in your passing and greet you on the way by.  Curiously, none of these people report any problems with bandits or such, but then, these new homesteads are all a few miles from Town, and within a horn’s call of dozens of platoons of Sun Blades and more than a few rides of Knights.

As the day winds on like the path through the forest, the fog lifts almost completely, though even at noon, there is a hazy look to the day.  However, there is no rain, and the sun, albeit a lithium-coloured smear behind a thin but thorough cloud cover, is a refreshing sight after so many days of rain.

Towards the end of the journey, the trail becomes a switch-back, heading up a now steeper incline at the edge of the valley.  The forest here is all golden, orange and yellow, and the trees are such a great size that there is little underbrush, and you can see for a hundred yards or more through the canopy, which is gently raining it’s colourful, leafy precipitation to the thickening blanket below.

Everything is still damp from the last several days of rain.  The sun and the warmth are drying things out slowly, so that a constant mist rises from the leaves.  This and the warm nature of decay provide a constant rise of aroma from the leaf-covered floor of the forest and the trail; that earthy sweet smell that has a slightly acrid tinge; you can nearly taste it or drink it in.

The mile of the journey are the most rewarding; as you emerge from the climb of the switch-back trail, the forest opens up just as quickly as it levels off; before you are the southern-most fields of the farm; a mile of wheat and barley fields that now stand brownish-gold, tucked neatly into a golden, red and amber nest of oak trees.  Off in the distance, you can see the thin columns of smoke from dozens of fire places rising up over a hill that hides the walls of Dwimmerholme.

All day on your journey, you have gotten used to the sounds of the forest; the steady and constant crisp snap of wet leaves as they land on those below, the muffled ruffling of small creatures that play and forage in the leaves, even the muted thumps of the footfalls of the harts that are bold enough to watch you pass.  However, as you draw to within a half mile from the walls of Dwimmerholme, and are walking along a break in the fields; a small grove of oaks and some maples that divide two larger fields, you hear the unmistakable sound of boots, many pairs, crunching quickly through the forest; the heavy thumps and the sound of snapping twigs and saplings tells you that something is coming your way quickly.  You are headed roughly north, these sounds are coming from the west.

You think that you probably have a few seconds to prepare for whatever it is that is coming, but as you turn to face the oncoming sound, you realize that at least one creature has closed the distance more quickly and quietly than you realized; a small girl, perhaps seven years old, is hastening along towards you.  She is wearing a torn up green and brown dress, and has a rough fur cloak of some kind over her shoulders.  She is barefoot, and moves with speed and grace, though you can clearly see that she has been running from something for quite some time; she is covered in scores of lacerations that the forest has marked her with on some desperate flight.  Not far behind her, you can see the shapes of many men cresting a small rise about 100’ away.
