The Travelling Company — Beliard's Journey to Daggerdale

Source: Email from Stephen Robertson to the group, 8 January 2011. Subject: 'Re: The Dragon's Tale Inn - Arabel (continuity rejig)'. Thread ID 12d66d72523c786c. Also forwarded by Stephen in February 2024 with contextual notes.

Summary: Stephen's narrative covering Beliard Emmarask's journey from Suzail to Arabel and the events at the Dragon's Gate Inn. Beliard encounters a troupe of demihuman performers on the road, including a half-elf who delivers a coded spy message: "In Daggerdale, seek the soldier named Creed." In Arabel, Beliard attends the trial of Carpathia Twist at the King's Court, where she is sentenced to exile from Cormyr for unauthorized hunting. That evening at the Dragon's Gate Inn, Beliard meets Carpathia and Tyrian Grey, and delivers a rousing speech to convince the merchant Denelius Ormath to depart promptly for Daggerdale. The narrative includes Beliard's decoding of the "Words of Silver" cipher and establishes his cover as a Cormyrean envoy. Per Stephen's 2024 notes, Beliard is actually a Sembian freedom fighter working undercover to free Sembia from Netherese influence, not a Harper — his moral compass follows a "Good for Sembia / Bad for Sembia" axis. This narrative was a "continuity rejig" that replaced an earlier version, inserting Beliard into the King's Court scene alongside Tyrian.

Note: The section headed "The Dragon's Gate Inn" below includes Les Blackwell's DM scene-setting for the inn, which frames the action and introduces the three player characters. The poem about the Elfskull Inn was adapted by Stephen from an existing source, not original composition.

The Travelling Company

It had been a long day for Beliard. The journey from Suzail had been relatively uneventful, save for an unexpected meeting with a group of demihuman performers journeying south — a pair of androgynous half-elf twins whom he took to be actor-prostitutes, and a midget duo that styled themselves "The Dwarven Trebuchet and The Flying Gnome." From this motley troupe, he learned that trade from Arabel was beginning to flow eastwards once more over the Thunder Peaks and that he might seek secure passage with one of the merchant caravans that would be gathering at The Dragon's Gate Inn. They had heard that there were at least three such expeditions expected to leave in the next ride.

Beliard smiled at the mention of the 'Gate — the old Elfskull Inn, as many of the locals still obstinately called it. An old ditty came to mind, from the days before the royal decree that the name be changed, after the kingdom had re-established relations with the eladrin of Cormanthor.

    I had a pretty elven lass,
    Her tongue as sharp as her ears,
    But not half as sharp as her hips, me lads,
    And I cut myself on her lips, me lads,
    At the Elfskull Inn!

A grin flickered across Beliard's mouth. He had spent many nights at that establishment in one capacity or another, and the prospect of paying a visit before heading into the provinces was a welcome one. Thanking the group for their news, the bard made as if to take his leave, taking the reins of his horse in hand. He was careful, however, to watch carefully as they turned away towards their colourful wagon. There it was. One of the twins was lagging behind the others, with his — her? — hands clasped behind her — his? — back in a distinctive and familiar manner.

Leading his horse closer to the half-elf, the bard spoke in a low tone. "Your wings are no longer clipped, milady..." The half-elf arched an eyebrow at him, smiling, but said nothing.

Beliard frowned, his hand moving surreptitiously to the hilt of the longsword at his side.

Noting the movement, the half-elf's eyes twinkled. "I fly free, friend. But I am no lady."

Beliard relaxed, letting go of his sword. The half-elf chuckled, then spoke quietly but firmly, in a lilting, singsong voice. "Words of silver. In the merry misty valley, one seeks out the lady soldier, and named not the Martyr's Creed."

Beliard blinked twice, then nodded. The half-elf gave a sly smile, touched the bard lightly on the chest, then moved off to rejoin her companions. Watching the troupe move off along the way, Beliard swung himself onto his horse and spurred the beast towards Arabel.


The King's Court, Arabel

Beliard sat in court, reflecting on the half-elf's words, only half paying attention to the scene playing out before him. Had the damnable thing had been a man or a woman? He still wasn't sure.

He had arrived in Arabel before supper the previous day, taking a room at the Dragon's Gate. As the performers had reported, there were a number of caravans readying for the journey, but none would be leaving until the day after tomorrow at the earliest, so he had gone into town to brush up on local happenings. The best place for that, Beliard knew, was the Royal Courthouse. After several hours spent scanning the latest court documents, carefully inspecting several cases concerning border disputes with Sembia through a fine-looking monocle attached to a silvery chain that dangled from inside his sleeve, he decided to sit in on the final case of the day, Crown versus Twist.

The proceedings were taking longer than expected, and he almost regretted attending. The current trial involved a royal, and he had decided that it would be in his interest to take in the session. The foibles of the aristocracy were a favourite topic in certain circles, and though Beliard didn't expect to be visiting those sorts of parties anytime soon, the misadventures of Ervoured, the Black Prince (and if all went well, the Last King, he thought wryly to himself) were a special interest of his.

The trial had gone long, which was a surprise. Normally, anyone found leading a hunt in the King's Forest would quickly be sentenced to death. However, the composition of the hunting party in question was unusual, to say the least. A half-elven woman (definitely a woman, he thought) was charged with the crime of having two weeks previously led a hunt in the King's Forest.

The woman hardly looked like a huntress, small and weary-looking as she was. There was a hint of great sadness in her eyes, and Beliard knew that this was more than simple remorse for her crime — as became manifestly clear as the prosecution outlined a list of the defendant's previous escapades. She had quite a history, it seemed, and appeared to have been systematically disrespecting hunting rights in the most unlikely places.

    Tymanther — 1 charge: Dismissed.
    Chessenta — 2 charges. 6 months in service. (Beliard shuddered at the idea of Chessentan "service").
    Turmish — 1 charge. Dismissed.
    Nathlan — 1 charge. Dismissed. (Where?)
    Dragon Coast — 4 charges. A total of 1 year in service to the crown.


Words of Silver

The bard's mind began to wander as the list went on... Words of silver. That much was clear enough. What had the message been again?

"In the merry misty valley, one seeks out the lady soldier, and named not the Martyr's Creed."

Beliard mentally parsed the strange phrase.

"In merry valley, seeks the soldier named the creed."

"Merry valley" — merry dale. Daggerdale. The first part had to be "In Daggerdale." But what was the second part? "Seek the soldier, named the creed"? Wait, that second "the" was wrong — "not" means skip the next word — "Seek the soldier named Creed." That was it.

"In Daggerdale, seek the soldier named Creed."

The message decoded, his attention shifted back to the court proceedings.


The Sentencing

...Muranndin, Amn, Velen, Elturgard...

How long had the woman had been at this?

...Najara, Evereska... and finally, the Kingdom of Cormyr.

The charge was for the wilful and unauthorized leading of a hunting expedition with the sole purpose of taking of game in the King's Forest.

Much prancing and prattling on the part of the prosecutor. The man was a performer, Beliard had to give him that. He'd not seen such a show of recounting legal precedent and proclamations of Royal Writ in years. Truly — the man had missed his calling.

Alas, he thought, it will all come to naught. After all, it was impossible to convict this woman without also convicting her five accomplices, the king's nephew Erzoured, the Black Prince, included. Beliard was surprised that the case had even gotten this far. No doubt a magistrate loyal to Irvel had decided to curry favour with the White Prince by making life difficult for the Black. More the fool him.

In any case, there was little chance of this case coming off. A good thing for this Twist woman, considering her "defense":

"I care not for your law. Where the servants of the Beastlord hunt, I hunt. And your King's Forest has seven fewer wolves than it did a fortnight ago."

Beliard's eyes rolled as the prosecutor leaped on this, overplaying his hand. "Do you hear, milud? She confesses to being a servant of Malar!" A fatal mistake. Even were this true — and Beliard doubted that it was, though he supposed he wouldn't put it past Prince Erzoured — he knew that the court could not openly implicate the families of the five young men in Hunt Worship.

From there things wrapped up quickly. Some character witnesses, a few veiled threats, some claims of coercion, and at the end of it all, the names of the young men were stricken from the record, while the Twist woman was sentenced only to exile from Cormyr for the duration of two years. She was to leave the city walls immediately, and was to be beyond the borders within a tenday, on pain of death.

Beliard watched as Prince Erzoured strode icily out of court, followed by several retainer-toadies. The bard would give good odds that whoever had been responsible for this travesty of a court case would be chained to the wrong side of a Marsember canal barge before the end of the week.


The Dragon's Gate Inn

After such an eventful day it was a bit of a surprise, upon returning to the Dragon's Gate Inn, for Beliard to see the selfsame woman sitting alone at a corner table of the taproom.

He paused as he entered, considering her. Why such sorrow in her demeanour? Surely this wasn't contrition. Not with a record like hers. Why, when it came to it, did she lead such a troubled life? Did she have such little regard for her own skin that she was simply trying to get herself hanged?

His stare did not go unnoticed, and with a shrug and a what he supposed was an attempt at a smile, the woman reached out with her leg and kicked a chair back from the table. The bard's entrance had drawn looks from a few others as well, but the few men who had noticed looked away as he settled into the proffered chair, his eyes never leaving those of the strange young woman, and nary a word spoken.

At the next table, a few caravan masters were debating their itineraries. It seemed like there was a growing consensus to postpone the departure. Only one grizzled old miser — Denelius Ormath, Beliard remembered from earlier that day — appeared to be standing firm on leaving as soon as possible. This wouldn't do, thought Beliard. His official business notwithstanding, he now had 'unofficial' business to attend to in Daggerdale. Words of silver, he thought.

The Twist woman suddenly broke off her silent interrogation of the bard, looking past him to a newcomer. Finally, the silence at the table was broken by the sound of another chair being drawn back, and the respectful voice of the young knight Emmarask had met that morning — Tyrian Grey, onetime scion of The House of Achres. "Good evening, Master Emmarask, milady. I would share your company, if I may?"

Beliard waved a hand at the table, and when no objection was raised by the woman, the knight's handsome figure settled into a chair opposite the bard.

[Les Blackwell's DM scene-setting follows]

It is in a large road-house outside the walls on the northern side of Arabel, that Beliard and Tyrian first meet. This should be a busy place; 100 years ago, this 'road-house', which more resembles a small fortress built outside the walls of the great Sembian city, would have been packed with traders, this time of year especially. These days, the world such as it is, the enormous tap-room of this inn is 90% empty. It would have been nothing for merchants to exchange thousands of gold a day in handshakes in this place, but now, the roads are a dangerous place, and travel up the road and into the dales is not what it once was.

Despite that, the world is waking up again, from its long and troubled nightmare. It is recovering from the Spellplague, and trade is one of the first healthy signs of that recovery. And so it is that a few merchants are gathered together in one lonely corner of this place, arguing about when they should set off. A dozen retainers, followers and other caravan add-ons wait sitting or standing at tables nearby, where the merchants, who in this age more resemble warlords, discuss their fate.

At one of these tables sits an interesting trio.

1. Beliard Emmarask, envoy from Cormyr, on his way to Castle Radiance to replace a low-level bureaucrat there, whose service obligations are concluded.

2. Tyrian Grey, the paladin of Ilmater, who has likewise been tasked with going to Daggerdale, where he is to earn his knighthood.

3. Carpathia Twist, guide and hunter, who is leaving The King's Forest for her own reasons, and likewise heading north, to the dales.

Beliard, who is quite astute, has noticed that Tyrian has the vocabulary and mannerisms of someone who has spent much time between various polities and is drawn to him. It isn't long before he figures out that this is one of the Scions of the ill-fated Achres family.

The lot of them will be traveling to Tilverton Gap together, with this larger caravan, which will then likely break up into smaller units to head into the various dales. You are all accompanying a man named Denelius Ormath, a merchant-warlord from Suzail, who is bringing goods all the way to Castle Radiance. He has a contract with the Whitegates, apparently, and has been to the Castle 4 times in the last 2 years. He is bickering with 2 other merchants who want to wait a few days before going. Ormath wants to leave right away so that he can fit in 2 more trips this year, but it is late in the season already for this hope. And besides, as the discussion is focused, there is war raging between Netheril and Daggerdale, so what's the rush?


Beliard's Address

"What's the rush, Ormath?"

Unable to help overhearing the semi-private conversation happening at the next table between Denelius and his colleagues, Beliard clears his throat and begins ruminating, seemingly to himself, but projecting his voice loudly enough that he can be easily heard through the room.

"What's the rush, indeed? Why help the Dalesmen with their little war when we could wait for them to succumb to the Shadow? Why, they'd be doubly grateful for our commerce. Nay — triply! What's that you say? They wouldn't want these swords and shields — good Cormyrean steel? I'll grant it you have a point. But you're too focused on the short term. Take the long view, my friends — it's a seller's market! So what if Netheril takes the Dales? We might sell north! To... Netheril! Ah, yes — yes, my mistake. Well, let us sell east then, to Sembia! What's that you say? Sembia is naught but a Netherese puppet regime? So it is! Politics — one forgets the little things. So be it. Those rascally Netherese. They seem to be just everywhere, don't they? In every shadow, you might say. Well, as the halfling harlot says, if you can't beat 'em, bill 'em! Netheril is a growing market! After a war, they'll need to resupply — and, why, seeing as they'll have eliminated our competition, we'll have them by the short and shadies! We can sell them anything! If they're going to keep up this expansionist policy, they'll need all the materiel they can get, from swords to siege engines! Once we share three borders with them, we'll be their favourite neighbours! They couldn't NOT buy from us. Yes, sir — we can just sit back and let the jack roll in as the Netherese do their thing. What's the rush indeed?"

Turning to address Ormath directly, Beliard's voice hardens. "Now, Master Ormath. Though it pains me to encourage what we can all see to be the less profitable option" — this last now dripping with sarcasm — "I am on His Majesty's business, and intend to be at Castle Radiance by Flamerule's first ride. Sorry scrollpusher that I am, I should prefer to ride with the strength of numbers, and though I do not command the royal purse, I can extend His Majesty's gratitude for any haste you might be able to muster on account."

He pauses for a second, then his eyes brighten. Turning to gesture at a wide-eyed Tyrian, with whom he has exchanged only a few words so far, he continues: "In payment, I can offer the axe and strong arm of this fine young knight, for he tells me that he, too, is bound for Castle Radiance." He claps Tyrian on the shoulder. "This young man, whom I know to be a fine and noble fellow, has sworn an oath of anonymity, and may not name himself until he has found Azoun's Lost Crown!"

(gasps from the room, now fully enthralled)

In a stage whisper, he adds, "I can, however, tell you, good master, that this man is a favourite of the White Prince. His glorious future is assured, and the favour of your service is sure to pay off in spades!"

During this exchange, the knight in question appears to be trying to shrink under his own breastplate. Clapping his aghast companion on the back, Beliard smiles brightly at the room — all eyes upon him — stands, and lifts his glass.

"Ladies and Gentlemen — the King!"
