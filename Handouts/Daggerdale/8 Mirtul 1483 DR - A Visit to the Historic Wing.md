8 Mirtul 1483 DR - A Visit to the Historic Wing
Date: 16 July 2025
From: Les Blackwell
To: Scott Mclaren, Todd Burry, Gus Miranda, Stephen Robertson

8 Mirtul - Journal of Brother Vessuvian, Candlekeep Emissary attache to The Yellow Abbeye of Dagger Falls - My Visit to the Historic Wing

I've had a most unusual day and hasten to make record of it while everything is fresh in my mind. Indeed, some of the morning and afternoon are already hazy in my recollection, and some details I can't summon much to my own frustration. But I will press on as best I can...

I have just parted company with Ambassador Monarch, Master Burgundy and Magistrate Invictus, after our visit to the Historic Wing of the Cathedral. Though the door to this storied institute is but 200' from my office and bedchamber, this was the first (and hopefully not the last) time I've ever been inside.

After Terce (the liturgical hour that sees the largest number of celebrants come to the Cathedral and thus the loudest and most spirited of their ceremonies of the day), I was summoned to Invictus' office, where I waited outside on a plain, flat wooden bench of no real comfort for more than an hour, before his clerk announced I would wait longer yet, until the rest of our party arrived. A little while after that, I was surprised to see Ambassador Monarch walking up the stairs to the mezzanine. Monarch was attended by Master Burgundy, who had a large satchel full of writing things. The mature halfling struggled to keep pace with his employer, her footfalls appearing effortless, despite the impossible distances she closes with each. She did wait patiently at the top of the stairs for Burgundy to make the landing, at which point the thick oak door of Invictus' office swung open, and he marched out. I say 'marched' because the man's footfalls report like the mailed boots of a soldier, his heels clacking loudly on the polished oak floorboards.

"Aw, we are all assembled" Invictus said, reading the impatient face of the Ambassador, who clearly had questions. "You will know the reason for our meeting shortly, if you'll follow quietly and without question. Faith is required, faith and trust..." and his words hung in the air, echoing spectrally through the soaring structure. He had a nearly imperceptible smile which grew as we waited. By way of approval, Jeska simply nodded her head, her assent apparently counting for both of us. "Very well. But Madame Elf..." and he cleared his throat in an awkward pause. Jeska seemed to sense what Invictus was stalling for.

"Master Burgundy, you are excused" she said. "I won't be needing a scribe after all" and with this, Merry bowed quickly and deeply, and hurriedly shuffled off, wiping his brow with a neat kerchief he had produced. I should guess his face wore an expression of relief had I the chance to catch a glimpse of it. But I didn't; the small folk are not over-fond of the Cathedral, and Invictus is a bit of a boogey-man to them. Burgundy is sharp for his kind, and has the preternatural bravery that boils up in his people from time-to-time, when it is needed most, but that wasn't today, and his exeunt was swift.

Without another word, Invictus lead us to the basement of the cathedral, and we followed him instinctively. There was no instruction, no small-talk along the way (which is just as well; I am still groggy from too much entertainment with Master Alavandor two nights previous). Just a solemn and wordless procession through the stone tiles of the cathedral, the percussion of the Magistrate's heels a stark contrast to the hushed discussions of various clergy we passed without salutation.

As we entered the basement, the smell of the place changed; the public spaces like the nave are perfumed constantly by the censers that produce a sweet cedary smell, with a slightly bitter note. The basements smell like every other church basement I've visited in a dozen great churches in Faerun; musty and organic, with the sweet-acrid waft of old bones one moment, and a whiff of an old candle or censer interrupting briefly to deliver something between sage and thyme and sorrow the next. For all that, I am quite at home in these spaces, and the Cathedral Basement and Catacombs are as spotless as the Magistrate's habit.

After winding through the cellars and store rooms, we passed through a wing of the catacombs, where the Faithful have been laid to rest. The catacombs betray the lofty ambitions of the Church here in Daggerdale; the crypts extend perpendicular into the walls, so that three-times as many corpses may be interred. The overall effect is of a stone honey-comb of death, many small openings arrayed neatly on the walls where there is room for many hundreds or perhaps a thousand or so coffins. But only a small fraction of the little crypts are full, and these with plain oak coffins all identical in appearance, and all with neat brass epitaphs declaring the name and history of each inhabitant. The only disorderly thing you'll see in the Cathedral can be observed in some of these resting alcoves; old candles whose myriad-coloured waxes have caked the face of the crypt, and sometimes the box of the inhabitant below. Some still had candles alight, presumably from families who have visited or (more likely) paid the clergy to keep aflame.

I could spend as much time as needed to inventory all there is to see down here and never complain once; the basements and the catacombs are wonderful, but they are all just a prelude to the real wonder, which is the library and reliquary we entered next.

For we then came to a metal door, perfectly round, 8' in diameter, set back behind many layers of archivolts which create an ornate tympanum the likes of which I've never seen. The door itself is intricately adorned in bas relief, causing it to resemble a sort of astrolabe, though some of the markings were confounding for me. Again, I could have spent hours here studying it, but this time was not afforded, obviously. Magistrate Invictus paused at the door and prayed quietly. Jeska too said a prayer in her language, invoking Correllon. I suppose I would have given thanks to Denier, though I don't remember if I did or not.

In fact, the remainder of my story will be murky. Trying to recall the details of the Historic Wing is something I cannot do; some images persist, and those are clear and profound. Although I don't feel the slightest bit groggy, my memory of the remainder is foggy, like so many nights stumbling from encounter to encounter with Jasper and Lucan. I should say I was drugged if I didn't feel so energized by what memories remain. So let me inventory those quickly;

I don't remember the door opening, or entering, but next recall that I was in a little workshop. It was set inside clean stone walls and full of tables, workbenches and cabinets filled neatly with components for making boxes and locks. The place smelled of cut oak, iron and oils. Working diligently away at a tidy workbench was the Magistrate of Locks and Boxes. This man was short and thick, with strong hands and forearms. He wore the habit and a clean leather apron that was full of fine instruments. He was singing when we came in; a low baritone much deeper than I might have expected for a small man, and his song was a pleasing hymn, not too fast but neither dirgelike. He started a bit as Invictus approached him from behind, and gently and slowly placed a hand on the smith's shoulder. A strong and calloused hand gently tapped Invictus' and he turned and smiled. It was then that we could see that he was 'blessed' (as Invictus declared) with a deficit of sight and hearing. A cruel accident from his past had taken much of his face and both ears. He wore a neat white cowl to cover up the shiny scars but made no further attempts to hide them. "Magistrate, I have many fine new boxes for you" he declared, his face aimed high at the ceiling, craning about. His volume was much too loud for the place, and though it made both Jeska and I look uncomfortably at each other (we hadn't heard anything louder than a mouse fart for the last hour or so), it didn't bother the Magistrate a bit.

Invictus laid a hand upon the magistrate-smith's white cowl and tapped gently with alternating fingers. "Mmmmhmmmm...yep...mmmmhm" the blind and deaf magistrate responded loudly to some message Invictus was tapping or otherwise sending into the man's head.

"Happy to help, father!" the smith magistrate bellowed. He then lowered his voice conspicuously and said "tell Brother Keys red-white-7-a-sun-9-star-f-g-sun, and give Sister Stacks this" and he then turned back to his workbench, his hands locating a quill and a small bit of paper with a precision that only decades of working in complete order would allow a blind man to do. He scribbled a small note and held it out, his movements snappy and precise. Invictus took the note, one finger of his hand tapping the smith's as he gently retrieved the note. "You're welcome, Brother" shouted the Magistrate of Locks and Boxes, who then returned to his singing and sightlessly crafting a small lock at the bench in front him.

"We'll forgive the Magistrate of Locks and Boxes for his familiarity, won't we?" Invictus said as we started to leave the workshop. "His life before was difficult, and though his path to salvation is long, he marches on in good order."

The next disjointed memory is of a small chambre full of desks and books, good lamps and fine oak furniture. It smelled wonderful; old leather, parchment, oils, inks and earth. Invictus, Jeska and I were stood then at the desk of a small and skinny clerk, old and sickly looking. He is known as the Magistrate of Keys, and he was the second of the three inhabitants we would meet. His frail form was bent from going about with chains of keys which nearly cover his entire torso and hang to his knees. I don't doubt that there are a thousand keys about him and fifty pounds of chains. He said not a word, and I recall Invictus saying "the man is blessed with only the gift of hearing; he says nor sees a thing, his entire purpose being the clear reception of orders and carrying out his duties." His colleagues make up for his 'blessings' as we would shortly see.

Hearing us enter his office, the Magistrate of Keys stood on shaky knees, and bowed a bald and bare head in our general direction. He then clutched handfuls of the chains and keys, and craned his head to aim what I assume was his better ear toward Invictus. "Magistrate" said Invictus sharply, eliciting a nod from his servant. "We will need you to carry out your duty soon" and he crossed the floor to the old cleric, and bent down and whispered in an ear that the Magistrate of Keys now held both hands about to secure the whispered message. Both men straightened up. Invictus returned to our sides as his servant adjusted the heavy chains about him and then departed the room out another door...

My next memory is walking through the stacks, and this has remained clear in my head. It might be that Invictus has let it stay, or it might be that Denier won't deny me of it, but here it is;

The main body of the Historic Wing is a maze of stacks containing myriad boxes, books and other strange containers and objects. I have a good command of architecture and surmised that the space we then stood in was substantially the entire above-ground portion of the Historic Wing. The ground floor is choked with the maze of stacks, but there are two mezzanine layers above and about the place, with many doors leading to small offices.

The entire soaring space is as bright as day, and the light was pleasing and invigorating. I looked up, squinting at the source, trying to figure it out. There is no opening in the Historic Wing that anyone knows of, and I sought to discover the source, though I couldn't make out anything but the warm and pleasing light in my eyes. "It is his Light, Brother," Invictus said calmly to me, watching me try to figure it out. "Pure and unfiltered, as sure as you were stood on a mountain top." I remain unsure of what magic or device, or divine work provided this light, but I was able to make out the many fine mirrors arrayed about the edges of the mezzanine levels; these redirected light down every avenue of the labyrinth of stacks so that no shadows fall in the place.

We were joined by the Sister of the Stacks, the third of Invictus' blessed servants. The sister, Invictus explained to us, had lived a difficult life in Charity as a brothel-whore. A cruel patron savagely maimed her, leaving little of her face beyond one good eye. Her habit is hooded and cowled, so that only her good eye can be clearly seen. Though impossible to observe and inventory her injuries completely, glimpses of terrible scars can be seen as she moves about, and there is not enough beneath the cowl to suggest that she has a jaw or ears. "She found her salvation at Charnel Priory after she was attacked" Invictus said, watching me process the image of the woman before us. "They healed her body, though she should have died, and healed also her soul, driving out the false promises she and Sharess had shared between them. She remains a devout Illmatari, even though she has been in our service for some time. But let us continue..."

With this, Invictus gave her the little bit of paper that the Magistrate of Locks and Boxes had produced. She studied it with her one good eye and then lead us through the stacks. She brought with her a well-made trolley of oak and steel, its wheels oiled and leather-wrapped to produce little sound as it clattered along ahead of her. Her movements were quick and sharp despite pushing the trolley, and it is well that Master Burgundy hadn't accompanied us. At length, she stopped and turned precisely to look at a collection of oak boxes. She scanned them methodically, then laboriously dragged one from deep within the stack, compared some writing on the box with her note, and placed it on the trolley, evidencing strength greater than we might have expected from a woman of moderate size and build. I began studying the box, and recall it being about 2' on each size, expertly crafted and bound in oiled steel. But my memory then fades...

The final scene happens three times in a stone chambre Invictus calls "The Reading Room". This is a round chambre adorned with good lights and a few stout oak tables and fine leather chairs, though little else besides; no shelves of curious or errant candles. This room is tidy and precise. The first of my three memories of this room finds the 2' box on a table in the room, and the Magistrate of Keys standing before it. With the lock on the box held in one hand, the Magistrate fingered through scores of keys with the other, running a finger over fine markings on each to find one particular key. This he deftly inserted into the fine lock, opened it, removed it, and then exited the room.

"Now, we will look upon the tools of the Enemy" said Invictus gravely. And he opened the lid of the box, which permitted the glowing object inside to cast a light on it's bottom and on Invictus' glowering face. He produced an orb about 1' in diameter, wrapped in bronze, and wrought with many runes.

"This is Vortellius" he proclaimed. "One of three, and the least of them. It was brought here with the others after Liberty Hill. This one was used by the Black Druid, Deadnettle, to empower an aberration to produce unnatural spawn of troll and dragon. But you know this history already, and Mrs. Thickwickle may know it best; these monsters plagued her farm several years ago. What you do not know is that it was cut from her arms by Brother Dretch, hands and all, in the closing moments of the battle. It is quite secure..."

Next, I recall a nearly identical scene; the Magistrate of the Keys opening another box, this one much larger and odd-shaped, though the memory if it is hazy. It produced a nearly identical orb, likewise 1' in diameter, brass-bound and glowing. "Varros is the name of this orb, and it is of middling power. It was surrendered to us after the interdiction of the changeling, Lotus, by an unlikely ally in a moment of unlikely generosity. It was damaged in the process..." and we could plainly see that this orb glowed with a chaotic and oscillating frequency, and that it was cracked and some bands of brass riven.

Finally, we were shown the empty box in the final scene of my memory. Same room, same process and circumstances. But this time, the lock removed and the box opened, we met no glow or treasure. The box was empty, and Invictus full of ire. "Verinius was in this box. The greatest of three. It was taken from the hands of Belladonna after she and Drake Sterling plummeted from the Table of the Sun, ending the Saints' Battle. It was unharmed in the descent, and was secured by Magistrate Harbaugh, after the arrest of the Priestess of Shar who had led the Dark Garden plot. I will disclose that Brother Hoetmer, whom you will have read and read about, warned me about removing Verinius immediately. 'I would not transport it through any farm or Charity Hills' he said in his commonly-cryptic portents. That was enough for me; we had a brace of knights come down from the Castle and move it overland with the guidance of the ranger, Tuugan Swift. It came to us by way of the Castle, where it stayed nearly a year, before being brought to the Wing to be interred with its mates.

"It went missing sometime before last fall, though the date we do not know. If you find out the exact date, we might be able to sort out how it happened..."

And then we were standing in the nave of the Cathedral, rejoined by Master Burgundy, saying thanks and farewells as we shuffled towards the main doors. Then finding myself on the narthex of the Cathedral in the afternoon breeze, I noticed of a sudden that I had a book under my arm. Jeska pointed at the surprise package inquisitively. I examined the common-looking leather-bound tome and discovered it to be "A History of the Subterranean Resting Places of the Faithful Dead in the Eastern Heartlands" and a little note was sticking out of the book. It marked a brief chapter titled "The Catacombs of Sembian Expatriats in Thistle Ridge" which I will make a copy of right away, as instructed. The note was from Invictus and read;

"Masters Alavandor and Hawkwinter have been making inquiries on this subject, rightly so. Please copy the indicated portion and return to my office at your earliest convenience. -M.I"

It was now Nones, and the bells of the Cathedral were summoning the faithful to prayer and service, and we were in their way as they filed past and into the nave. I made a hasty promise to the Ambassador to send along my notes and copy of the chapter as soon as the ink is set, and we parted ways.

I am still struggling to remember the missing spaces between arriving at the door of the Historic Wing to standing on the narthex, but will resign from that endeavour presently; that place or the Magistrate himself have permitted me that which they feel I am entitled to recall, and I am thankful for the memory of the sun-washed stacks in that soaring annex. I can still feel the warmth on my face, and the smell of old books, leather and oak.

-Brother Vessuvian
