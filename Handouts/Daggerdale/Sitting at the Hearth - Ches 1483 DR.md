Sitting at the Hearth – Ches 1483 DR

[Play-by-post narrative exchange between sessions.  Compiled from the "Sitting at the hearth" email thread, 24 February – 3 March 2024.  Contributors: Scott Mclaren (Jeska), Todd Burry (Mari/Ser Rowan), Stephen Robertson (Lucan), Les Blackwell (DM/Dalak Quick-Axe).]


I. Jeska at the Fireside
(Scott Mclaren, 24 February 2024)

Taking warmth from the fire, in the silence of your thoughts, you do not hear Jeska enter the room.

Her voice behind you is like an echo of the flames..

"Dear Friends, you have asked of Myth Drannor, I" ..she almost studders....

"I hold those memories close."

"Dear Mari, Mother to us all, I believe I eclipse you in age by decades, and yet I am but a youth. Myth Drannor.. Myth Drannor is...."

She steps into sight.

"Terrifying... and yet Beautiful.. if you can see Beauty in Horror"

Jeska smiles.

"Myth Drannor is Dark, but it is Warm.. in its way, the deep earth preserves a shield from the true cold of the surface. We Elves have learned to dwell, and maybe thrive, there. My Kin face, (and hold back) the unspeakable horrors of the Deep Dark, and yet we protect the same from the influence of the surface world."

Her pause is noticeable as she sits and warms her hands at the fire.

"When I go out and look at the sky, it is foreign to me.. except at night. I was told stories of stars.. they take my breath.. more beautiful than the streams of silver and gold in the rock I called home."

She sits in silence staring at the fire for a moment.

"Truth is even this fire, so easily set to blaze, is a wonder to me. We have no wood in Myth Drannor.... only enemies, from above and below."

Another pause... and she looks at Sir Rowan...then Jasper... then Lucan.. and finally Mari....

"So they sent me.... to Observe only... but I see there is more, for all of us."

Jeska smiles, and stares into the hearth.. seemingly lost in thought.

III. Mari and Jeska Walk to Quick-Axes Tavern
(Todd Burry, 27 February 2024)

After what seemed an eternity woven into mere moments, Mari stepped closer to Jeska, placing a comforting hand upon her. "Come with me, Governess, we've some women's work to do," she suggested, her voice carrying the lilting melody of her Irish roots.

As they departed the chamber, Mari glanced back, her voice dancing over the crowd, "And boys—put your clothes at the foot of me bed tonight. I'll mend and wash them tomorrow mornin'."

The foyer was alive with the laughter and chatter of revelers who had found their way inside. Since Jeska had transformed the embassy into a sanctuary of sorts, the ground floor had become a communal haven. Meriadoc, busy at a table, was a picture of contentment, selling his first cherry harvest, contributing to the company's fledgling trade endeavors.

"Best put on our cloaks tonight, it's a bit cold out still," Mari advised as she reached for a cloak on a high hook, passing Jeska's to her. The night was not cold; in fact, it had been unseasonably warm, but Jeska understood Mari's intent. They donned their cloaks, shrouding themselves from the gaze of their newfound acclaim.

Their journey, though short, was slowed by the dense crowd of festival-goers. Mari, nimble and swift, stayed true by Jeska's side, making their way through the throng.

"The cherry harvest is coming along nicely. They must be fetching good coin. The rest of the trees in the dales won't be fruiting 'til near end of summer," Mari remarked, trying to lighten the mood with talk of simpler things.

"I'm having a terrible difficulty getting around the tournament battlefield. It's this armor. I can barely move in it. I don't know how my great auntie did it," Jeska confided, voicing her struggles with the cumbersome armor.

Mari's efforts to fill the silence were a gentle attempt to bridge the gap between them, even as the festival's joy bubbled all around.

"You know what? You and I will still be kicking just fine when our companions are old and retired. I bet we'll be finishing each other's words by then too. We can both help take back Myth Draenor together, side-by-each. What do you say?" Mari ventured, her words a mix of cheer and solemn promise.

Jeska's smile, small and comforting, was her reply.

"I'm sorry, Governess Jeska. When there's no talking to do, that's when I feel the need to talk the most," Mari reflected, her words tinged with apology and thoughtfulness.

Arriving at Quick-Axes Tavern, they found it bustling, the air thick with song and camaraderie. The bard's tale of their morning's exploits turned every ear, his melody rewriting the day's graver battles into legends.

Mari, ever the pragmatist, approached the bar after contributing to the bard's hat.

"No ale tonight, sir. I'm wonderin' if we could have a quiet word wit the brothers," she whispered, seeking discretion amidst the revelry.

"The brothers, eh? Look around ye. It's too busy around here. Come back after the competitions when it's quieter will ye?" the bartender replied, his voice a blend of hospitality and haste.

With a discreet lift of their hoods, Mari and Jeska revealed themselves, earning them immediate passage to the brewery below, where the air was rich with the scent of hops and barley.

"Hullo, my brother's in stature," Mari called out to the dwarves. The dwarves looked up. One of them kept working while the other came to greet them.

"Dalak Quickaxe." He wiped his hand on his apron and held it out. Mari tensed up, preparing for the crunching handshake she knew all too well from the Tethyamar Dwarves that had started to settle her home farm.

"Might I just say it's an honor to be meeting you, sir. A true honor. The tales of what you did to rescue the dale from the yolk of the Zhentarim are still being told by the wee folk of Tesh Valley. I can't count how many 'Quick-Axe' beards I have crocheted so my little ones could play 'Quick-Axes' in the hills."

After pleasantries, Mari broached the heart of their visit. "Well, Mr. Dalak, Sir—If you've heard of the Redjack battle this afternoon, I bet you can be guessin' why we be here. I have never seen anything like that Dwarf in my life. Tis, except for the tales I've heard told of the Quick-Axe brothers."

Leaning in, her voice low, Mari sought Dalak's aid with an earnest plea, "Mr. Dalak, Sir. We need your help. What can ye tell us of 'Battle Ragers?'"


IV. Dalak Quick-Axe Speaks
(Les Blackwell, DM narration, 1 March 2024)

[[I'll be catching up on sundry email chains and starting a few new ones here over the next 12 hours or so]]

*Trivia;  Balak Quick Axe died in 1438, leaving just his brother Dalak. The Stonebridge family has run the place ever since, except for the brewery, which is Dalak's passion. He is rarely seen upstairs or outside any longer.*

Dalak walks with a bit of a limp, and often holds the bottom of his stomach as though in pain.  He only seems to be relaxed and comfortable when seated with a mug of beer in his hand.  His 'office' is in the front portion of the brewery, and is made up of a simple and messy desk, a comfortable set of wooden chairs on top of a well-worn rug.  He seats you, and seems happy for the company once he's poured a drink, had a good belch, and set everyone down.

"I knew your boss" he says gruffly, pointing his mug at Jeska.  "Miritar, eh?  Good people, the Miritars.  Don't see them 'round here much.  What are they up to these days?"  He seems genuinely unaware of the nearby politics of not only your people, but his own.

"And you, Miss Goodberry...sorry, you've a husband now haven't ye?  Do you have many young'uns?  I knew your mum, knew her sister better, though.  But forgive me an unpleasant question, but is Lovely Lilly still alive?  I haven't heard from her in years I think.  In '77 I ran a batch of meade I called Lilly's Love, which I used that proper yellow honey your people are so famous for.  I couldn't keep it on the shelf, as they say.  Made meade with it until...I'm not sure how long now that I think of it.  Not too long..." he shuffles about in his chair, squinting into the darkness to the north of the room.  "No, in fact, I still have a barrel sitting empty waiting for more.  If I had to guess, I'd say those dwarves that moved in up North have eaten all that honey, have they?"  He smiles at you both and takes a deep drink, a deep sigh, and a long low belch which he politely excuses himself for.  For a moment, he seems like he might nod off, but then shakes his head and his eyes light up again.

"Were you asking me about battle ragers?  I thought someone would come asking some day.  Well, they used to ask me all the time when I worked the bar upstairs.  How does it look up there, anyway?  Is old Daygle keeping it more or less square?  I know the rooms are square.  His missus is a proper landlord, a Jewel among jewels, ha!" Another drink, another sigh, another belch.

"Yes, well, I did hear something about your match today.  I meant to go, but lost track of the time.  I think I do that a lot.  Daygle tried to put a clock down here, when was it...I can't recall when he wanted to put a clock down here, but I told him it would turn the beer.  'All that ticking while the beer's setting up will turn it' I told him, and he believed me.  So no clock, and no match for me today, I apologize.  But yes, I heard about the arena.  I never thought an arena was something for civilized people, and I only seen one, and that one I pulled my brother out of.  A long time ago, I can't remember when.  So you've caught me in a lie, or rather I've led myself to being caught now that I've said a bit too much, haven't I?  I don't care for arenas, and no, I don't suppose I'll see another."

He seems lost in thought for a moment. He brings his mug slowly to his mouth, and he gets a far-off look in his eyes, and puts the beer down before taking a sip, forgetting that he's not touched it.  He sighs, belches, apologizes and continues.

"As for battle ragers, they make bad enemies and worse friends.  But this villain that your friends ran afoul of, Gorehammer, battle raging isn't his problem, from the description I've heard.  Did you know my old friend, Maturin?  He taught me a good many things, and I can tell you that if had a good look at the mouth and eyes of Gorehammer, that I bet you'd see they was a bit blue and black..."

And thinking back to when he was being carted off the field by his team members, you remember the conspicuous black spittle and lips of the wild-eyed dwarf.  With a silent nod from you, Dalak continues.

"Ahh...I guessed as much.  That's Zerk, not battle rage.  He's addicted to a drug from the East.  From where Maturin used to go.  I can't remember what it was called, or why he felt the need to go back there.  But someone has Gorehammer addicted to it, and it makes him wild and murderous.  Not safe to be around.  Worse when it runs out..."

"But what about your auntie, Ms. Sweetberry?  Janus, from Thistle Ridge?  She's the only other woman besides your mum to break my heart, and this time rather than on account of withholding honey, it was for withholding the finest barley any dwarven brewer ever made into drink in ten centuries.  Up until about '42 I used to make Barley Buck Gold with 4 wagon loads I got twice a season from your auntie.  I think I could even tell you when the last shipment came..." he awkwardly shuffles about in his chair, and sorts through old ledgers, squits, belches, and then puts on some reading glasses that rest precariously on the end of his huge red nose.  Finally, leafing through an old ledger, he seems at first happy to find what he's looking for, then, just as quickly, a shadow seems to pass over him, and he sighs, belches, apologizes, and closes the ledger.

"I'm right sorry, Ms. Sweetberry.  I've lost track of a lot down here, and I've just read a note I left to myself in '41.  About your family.  And I'm right sorry.  She was a proper halfing, your auntie, and sewed and cut the finest barley a dwarf could dream of.  And have drempt of.  For more than 40 years it seems.  My, how time flies.  You've put me in mind of something else I thought I should tell you, just let me..." and he holds up a finger, picks up his tankard, saluting you with it, and taking a deep pull.  He sighs, belches, and before he can apologize, he nods off.  You wait for a few minutes, then a few more, before you hear feet on the stairs behind you.

The dwarven proprietor and bartender, Daygle Stonebridge has come down to check on things, and finding his old friend asleep in his chair, he grabs a blanket from a barrel nearby, and covers the shoulders of the now-loudly-snoring dwarf.  You get the impression he's done this many times before.  He fondly claps his sleeping friends' shoulders, and addresses you.  "Master Dalak will be out for the night now, I'm afraid.  He gets tired this time of day.  I'll see you out..."


V. Mari Reflects; Jeska Responds
(Todd Burry, 2 March 2024; Scott Mclaren, 3 March 2024)

Mari stretched up on her tiptoes, bestowing a tender kiss upon Dalak's forehead. "Have yer rest, me hero," she whispered, her voice imbued with affection and warmth. Turning slightly, her gaze lost in the middle distance, she mused aloud to no one in particular, "The wounds of war oft never heal."

She then directed her attention to Jeska, concern lacing her words. "What d'ye make of what Dalak said 'bout the Gorehammer and this zerk? It chilled me to the bone, so it did, hearin' a dwarf would murder their own kin in such cold blood. Small comfort that he was under the influence of some foul alchemist. The death was no accident, and the battle rager won't stop 'til he does the same to one of us." Mari's face bore an expression rarely shown to others; it was one of deep worry.

"Did ye have a chance to glance at the scoreboard today? The Redjacks haven't lifted a finger towards mercantile or intelligence measures. Not a single endorsement to their name.  And did ye catch how they asked the Standing Stone to yield the competition? D'ye think it's their plan to murder the other teams into submission?"
