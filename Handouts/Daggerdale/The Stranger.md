The bleak winter air cut him deeply, slicing through muscles like frozen fire. Ears throbbed against the howling wind. His throat was raw from the cold. The wanderer's face was harsh and pale as his surroundings, hidden behind a tangle of windswept hair. Lank and sickly, he hunched as though the snowy sky itself weighed upon him. Breath grew solid in the freezing air, and landed softly at his feet.

He gripped a blade like a dying man, black and red across it's breadth.

One foot in front of the other, he trekked onwards. 

The swordsman hadn't seen the sun in days. Occasionally he would glance to the east, the faint glow of hope glimmering between the grey of his eyes. It wasn't coming, he had concluded long ago. The mountains were behind him, the tall ice and the deep, endless snow, where a single step could send a man sinking into a slow, frozen grave.  All that stretched before him now was scrub and tundra. He had found a reindeer, lame and pathetic, half buried by the weather. Its bloated, frozen flesh had sustained his trip thus far, but that had been a long time ago. His stomach ached with the memory of how the eyes had popped between his teeth. Frozen saliva gripped his chin.

The swordsman slowed his advance. Sounds, in the distance. With some effort, he raised his head - the cold had forced a hunch upon him, and his muscles ached from the movement. 

Barking, and several voices. Hunting hounds, dots in the distance, growing larger by the moment. The wind was at his back now. It had carried his scent towards them. The barking grew frenzied. Details grew clearer as he made his way forward - they were on horseback, a half dozen men or so. The pack of dogs, thick-set and wolf-bred, had arrived before their masters. They paced a circle around the him, hackles raised, snarling and snapping at the bitter air. The man returned his attention to the riders before him.

Squat, sturdy men. Thick brows, thin, beady eyes. They wore snow goggles carved from deer bone under hoods made of hide and fur. Forged blades hung at their hips, in their hands were iron lanterns. Savages touched by the fringe of civilization. Nar tribesmen, the swordsman was certain. He was in Narfell, he now recalled. Scant memories shifted uncomfortably in the shadows of his mind. It had been a suspicion of his. Few places were as harsh an inhospitable as the northern wastes. The strange edifices dotting the landscape were a sure sign aswell, abandoned spires that reached out of the ice like so many grasping hands, stripped to the bone by the merciless ice, but the presence of these horse-lovers confirmed his theory. 

He craned his head upwards. Obscured by the whipping snow, constellations above stirred his thoughts, constellations both recognizable and alien simultaneously.

How long had it been? How long had he lay... trapped?

Lowering his eyes to the mass of men and animals before him, the wanderer made to speak. Words came to him, but refused to take form in the rawness of his throat. The rider at the lead saw this and spoke first instead. \"Stranger. You walk the wastes alone. No food, no furs. The dray hounds caught your smell, and grow wary.\" A tribal dialect, a savage bastardization of traditional Damaran. The man paused, as though expecting reply. The swordsman merely stared.

\"Speak your name.\" said the Nar.

He gathered what moisture he could, swallowed several times, and opened his mouth a second time. Cracked and rasping, he spoke a word for the first time in decades.

\"Durandal.\"
 

Yes. That sounded... right.

\"You are not of this place.\" the Nar declared. \"Too tall. Too thin. From where have you come?\"

Durandal licked his lips. He could not recall. At a loss, he gestured behind him. The glacier loomed over the distant horizon. In near unison, the Nars hissed with what he could only assume was anger. Perhaps surprise.

\"A winter ghost!\" a Nar exclaimed. \"Demon from ages past!\" cried another. Definately anger, Durandal noted.

The Nar promptly began arguing amongst themselves. Some with weapons already drawn. Others were pale, gripping their reins with trembling hands. \"The dray smell the evil on him!\" the Nar shouted. \"Slit his throat! He comes from the mountains like the others, come for our flesh!\" 
 

\"I am no such thing.\" Durandal spoke, his soft voice cutting through the clamour. The snarling hounds, sensing their masters' discomfort, had tightened the ring around him.

The Nar that had spoken earlier urged his horse forward, passing the dogs and stopping before Durandal. The mount's shoulder was mere inches from his face. Durandal could feel the heat pouring from the beast's shivering skin. He resisted the urge to reach out and touch it, to take that warmth for himself. The Nar leader spoke once more. \"Then what are you, creature of the wastes?\"

Durandal's eyes glazed over for a moment before he replied.

\"I do not know.\"

The Nar's face curved into a grim frown. Slowly, deliberately, he drew a curved blade from his hip, eyes never leaving the snow-hunched figure before him. A swift movement cut the air, then the sound of ringing steel echoed around them. Durandal found the blade buried to the hilt in his chest. Impassively, he lifted his hand and carefully wrapped several trembling fingers around the Nar's wrist. Warm, shaking flesh, pulse racing. Was it fear? Durandal wondered as he slowly pushed the man's arm aside. Incredulous rage spread across the horseman's face as he examined the bladeless sword he now grasped.

Durandal gazed downwards at the mess before him. He saw his reflection in the shattered blade at his feet, but did not recognize himself. High above him, past the stranger in the blade, hung the sky, obscured by coursing snow and ice. The stars were gone, and Durandal along with it. The swordsman looked up with grey eyes gone red. The thin aura of magic around him exploded into life, sending fire and lightning coruscating and crackling in all directions. Durandal felt his fingers twitch, and then they spasmed. The wind picked up, howling around him in a frenzy that was beyond natural.

Durandal lurched forward like a beast, precise and untamed in a deadly conjunction.
  

His black blade flourished, snicker-snack, and blood sprayed the air red, freezing in a heartbeat's span. The Nar rolled from his saddle and onto the hard earth. Lower half still attached to his mount, the bisected tribesman watched with disbelief as his horse rode off into the crowd of tribesmen, shrieking with terror, his legs and feet still tightly gripping its flanks. Durandal watched this with a pensive stare, his mouth twitching uncontrollably at the corners. Dabbing at the blood that now covered his face and torso, he casually placed a finger on his tongue, savouring the metallic flavour.
 

He wasn't cold where the blood touched him.

Unnatural green light began to dance around the swordsman like a fiendish corona, beating back the darkness, illuminating the rough faces of the Nar raiders. Terror and rage danced across their features. 
Durandal leaned down and gazed at the ruined man laying at his feet. Blood bubbled from his lips as he spat his last words.

\"We...will...will suffer no demons... in t-these lands....\"
 

Durandal felt something stir in his chest, an amusing sensation pressing against the base of his throat.

\"I am no demon.\" he laughed. 

The Nar died with eyes wide open, full of fear. Without another word, Durandal grasped the horned helmet atop the corpse's head and lifted it clear. Calmly settling it on his own head, the man lifted his flame-wrapped blade and gazed along its length. Such grace, he marvelled. Straight and true, honed to perfection. 

With this weapon, he could write poetry in the flesh of the dying and the dead. 

The horsemen were screaming now, racing towards him with murder in their eyes. The hounds were howling in a similar fashion. So faint, however, as though a vast distance muffled their cries. Durandal could hardly take it seriously. They all moved like frost-bitten corpses, so slow, so fragile. The swordsman plunged his blade into the neck of the fastest hound, sending its head spinning off into the night. He smoothly advanced into a guarded stance, deflecting the nearest rider's wild chop, and promptly cleaved him in half, shoulder to groin. Green fire promply exploded from the seams of the black sword, incinerating the rider's remains and the horse as well, leaving nothing but a charred pile of savaged bones to collapse into the quickly melting snow.

So clumsy. A madman's smile split Durandal's face.

Tonight, for the first time he could remember, he would be warm.
