Rychel Voordan is the most elusive character in the Waterdeep chronicle — not because the sources are thin, but because the man himself seems to exist primarily in the negative space left by what he doesn't say, doesn't do conspicuously, and doesn't explain. He is defined almost entirely through how other people experience him, and the portrait that emerges is of someone who operates on a fundamentally different register from every other figure in the story.

The Bare Facts

His wiki entry is almost comically sparse: "A former jongleur, and a stalker without equal, Rychel Voordan is the best urban tracker in Waterdeep (some say the Realms). He is the sworn enemy the cult of Ghaunadar, and was a sometime comrade of Harkas Kormallis." That's it. No backstory, no family, no origin. He is categorized under Scott (his player), the Warders of Waterdeep, and the Red Herrings. The category marker beside his name — the ∞ symbol — sets him apart from every other Chronicle PC, suggesting a kind of permanence or continuity that transcends the usual mortal arc.

Former jongleur. Before he was the deadliest urban stalker in the Realms, he was an entertainer — a tumbler, an acrobat, a performer. The physical skills carried over: his combat style is described in terms of tumbling past opponents, grappling, apprehension, and a vorpal garrotte. He fights like a circus performer who learned to kill. The jongleur background also implies a social fluidity — someone comfortable moving between classes, adopting postures, reading rooms — but unlike Garth, who weaponized this into an empire of aliases, Rychel seems to have simply... stopped performing. He became the opposite of a showman. He became the man who says "Yep. Thanks" and leaves.

The Warder of Waterdeep

Rychel's earliest documented role is as a member of the Warders of Waterdeep, a company that first tangled with Company Dalael in the summer of 1368 DR over the Island of Three Curses. The Warders were an eclectic band — Steele the morally ambiguous elf, Kennyth the Oghman loremaster, Gable the mage, Edgar Grimbold the swashbuckling drunk, Gaebril Tremalkin the aspiring paladin. Among these colourful personalities, Rychel occupies the role of the competent professional. He is the one who already has connections to Harkas Kormallis, Tyr's Champion at the Halls of Justice. He is the one who trains Nero Agundar as a protégé. He is the one whom the Blackstaff invites to Roarke's Tavern when it's time to get serious about saving the city. He is, in effect, the institutional memory of Waterdeep's shadow war against the Drow — the thread that holds together the rotating cast of adventurers who cycle through the crisis.

How He Operates

The chronicle reveals Rychel's methods through specific incidents rather than exposition. During the Hunabar raid, while the rest of the party entered through the front and immediately got into a pitched battle with werewolves, Rychel entered alone through a bedroom window on an upper floor and methodically rifled through a wardrobe, where he found a Beholder Staff. When Jafar stumbled upon him, Rychel simply told the mage to take the prize and get out while he stayed behind to observe.

At the Dripping Dagger, amid a chaotic multi-sided brawl involving wererats, Zhents, drow, and Ghaunadan, Rychel "fought a bunch of ghaunadans, using a vorpal garrotte to behead the things," then "tumbled past the rank and file characters, and took out Yarkul with his cutlass of speed." While everyone else was fighting for survival, Rychel navigated the chaos like it had choreography and went straight for the head of the snake.

And then there's the Haskar Hunabar affair. After the disastrous raid on the Hunabar villa — in which werewolf children were killed — Rychel gave Rufus a letter to deliver to the Hunabars. Rufus didn't read it. When Haskar read it and discovered that Thagdul had the stolen staff, he flew into a murderous rage and nearly killed Rufus. "When Rufus returned to the headquarters, he accused Rychel of having nearly killed him, to which the wily jongleur played innocent." This is one of the few moments where Rychel's manipulativeness is exposed. He used Rufus as a courier for a provocative message, knowing full well what it might trigger, and did it without warning the priest.

Rychel and Garth: The Crucial Relationship

The most psychologically rich material about Rychel comes, paradoxically, from Garth's perspective. Their relationship is one of the great unspoken partnerships in the chronicle, and it's built on mutual respect layered over a power asymmetry that both men understand perfectly.

Garth calls him "good old Rychel" with a regularity that feels like an incantation — as though by saying it, Garth can conjure the stability that Rychel represents. When Garth is falling apart after the Max killing, it is Rychel he confesses to. When Garth begs off the vampire hunt, it is Rychel who sees through the excuse but doesn't press it. The narrative voice, speaking as Garth, describes the calculus with excruciating precision:

"He was old at this business when you were young, and will probably always think of you as the miscreant he helped out from time to time, in the past."

There's a whole social history compressed into that sentence. Rychel knew Garth as a Dock Ward street rat — he was introduced to the Red Herrings through Edgar Grimbold during Harvestide of 1371, when Garth's company was still a scrappy band of thieves in over their heads. By 1373, Garth is running an intelligence network and a legitimate front company, but Rychel still sees the boy from Dock Street. And Garth knows this, and doesn't resent it, because Rychel's judgment comes from a place of genuine regard rather than condescension.

But the scene in the leather wing-back chairs is also a study in what the two men cannot say to each other. Garth desperately wants Rychel to offer to come fight alongside him. Rychel comes to ask a favour — "just spare me a man." Neither asks the other for what he actually needs. "Some men choose to say nothing when they have lots to say, and others say all sorts of needless things when they have but one thing they want to ask. Friendships endure such nuances though."

The Purgation and Its Aftermath

Rychel's finest and most revealing hour is the Purgation of Chessenta Ward. When Garth couldn't face the vampires again, Rychel went back. With Steele — an increasingly unstable partner who was losing his mind to Romulus's mental domination — Rychel hunted vampires through Little Chessenta, enlisting clerics from both feuding Lathanderite temples. They destroyed four vampires publicly, dragging the staked monsters through the streets and pitching them into the harbour before crowds of thousands. "Rychel Voordan simply wanted to complete his appointed task," the chronicler notes, drawing a sharp contrast with Steele, who "revelled in the revealing of the truth to the simple folk."

This is the key to Rychel's psychology. He is not ideological. He does not revel. He has an appointed task, and he completes it. The vorpal garrotte, the cutlass of speed, the bedchamber window — these are tools employed toward objectives. The Purgation was a public spectacle because it needed to be, not because Rychel wanted it to be.

But the Purgation also revealed his limits. He fought Romulus head-to-head and could not land a blow. In a contest of speed, the vampire outmatched him. In grappling — the discipline where Rychel was legendary, where he had apprehended men hundreds of pounds heavier — Romulus made him look like a child. For a man whose entire identity is built on physical capability and professional competence, this was devastating. Garth saw it on his face: despair.

The Deeper Architecture

What makes Rychel psychologically fascinating is the tension between his extraordinary capability and his fundamental loneliness. He is sworn against the cult of Ghaunadar — the drow deity of oozes and aberrations — which implies a personal history with the Underdark or its horrors that is never once explained. His comrade Harkas Kormallis is murdered and his villa burned to the ground; Rychel reports this as "tidings of new mysteries" and carries on. His protégé Nero barely survives betrayal in Undermountain, watches Steele turn traitor, and retires from adventuring; Rychel pivots to assembling a new company. Steele loses his mind to vampiric domination; Rychel asks Garth to have a boy follow him, then goes vampire-hunting with a different cleric.

He endures losses without ever being seen to grieve them. He retires from adventuring after being petrified by the beholder Xulla and restored by Kennyth, feeling "an unfamiliar stiffness in his limbs." Then, when the city needs him, he comes out of retirement and immediately begins recruiting new allies and assembling operations.

The picture that forms is of a man who has organized his entire personality around competence and reliability because the alternative — feeling the full weight of what he's witnessed and lost — is not something he can afford. He is not cold; the despair that Garth sees on his face is real, and the fact that he worries about Steele and about Garth tells you that he cares. But he has, somewhere along the way, decided that the only honest thing he can do for the people he cares about is be useful. He won't give Garth a pep talk. He won't press the case when Garth makes excuses. He won't weep for Kormallis. He will ask for a man to follow Steele. He will pitch another vampire into the harbour. He will line up another stake.

Rychel Voordan is what happens when a jongleur — a man whose first profession was to delight audiences — decides that the world is not an audience to be delighted but a problem to be solved, and then discovers that solving it costs him every person he's ever worked alongside, one by one, while the problems keep metastasizing. He persists not out of hope or ideology but out of a kind of vocational stubbornness that is either the deepest form of courage in the chronicle or the most sophisticated form of avoidance. The sources, characteristically, do not tell us which. And Rychel, characteristically, would not tell us either.
