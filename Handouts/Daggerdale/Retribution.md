\"Silence.\"  Cirdan said sharply to the two children sobbing in grief and fear.  He perked an ear and could hear the wind carrying another of Eremus's epiphanies. \"Or would you prefer I send the both of you over to him?\" He said, pointing to the figure standing over a blood-soaked stump.  The sobbing quieted, but only just.

Kneeling down in front of the children he said quietly, \"Your kind are nothing more than a short-lived plague that has been infecting this region and its inhabitants for far too long.\"  Cirdan had been alive a very long time and he found that it is not enough to merely destroy your enemy through warfare.  If one is to thoroughly defeat an adversary, one must educate their progeny.

\"The trees you and your father cut down had lives that spanned centuries and in some cases millennia, if you can grasp such things.  They had families.  You have a family don't you?\"  He asked the children.

The two children hesitated and looked at each another, not sure how to answer the elf's.

\"Don't you?\"  He asked again, his tone sharper.

The children hesitated once more, one of them looked up into the tree line and back down and nodded his head.

\"Yes, I thought so.  The trees dream dreams too, you know.  Do you have dreams?\"  He asked again.

\"Sometimes...\" was the smaller one's reply.

\"They also had friends.  I know I have friends, do you both have friends?\"  At this the older one started crying some more.  \"No, I don't think you do anymore, do you?  That pain you feel, that grief, that is but a pinprick compared to what I feel and what the forest feels over the loss of but one of their own.\"  Pointing to the lumberyard he continues, \"Look at the slaughterhouse your kind has crafted upon our soil, reap the grief from the toils of your labour.\"

The two children were crying loudly now, beside themselves with fear and grief.  \"Quiet!  Your wailing is taxing my soul.  Try to keep your composure for a few more minutes, we are almost finished here.\"  He said producing an arrow from his quiver.  Cirdan held it in both hands and slowly rotated it to inspect its craftsmanship.  \"Do you know where this arrow came from?\"  He asked.

\"A tree?\"  Said the younger child, barely above a whisper.

\"Indeed, do you know which one though?  Which tree did I part this arrow from?\"

\"I ... I don't know.\"

\"I was very careful to observe your father and-\" scanning his quiver, \"thirty-six other men labouring here.  I carefully took note of which trees they felled.  This one is from a tree your father parted from our world.\"  Handing the arrow to the smaller child, he asked, \"What is written on the shaft?\"

The children turned it over and rotated it to get a good look at the carving.  Upon seeing what was written the one looked back up into the canopy while the other continued to sob.

\"Do you know the name or not?\"  Inquired the elf, taking the arrow from their hands.

\"Y...yes...\" Muttered the younger one between sobs.

\"Who is he?\"

\"My father and I won't let you kill him!\"  Said the older one in defiance, stooping to pick up a nearby stick.  It was too heavy for him to wield comfortably, but he tried to look as menacing as his strength allowed him to.

\"Oh, you misunderstand me, he won't die by my hand.\"  He drew a fine elven blade and threw it to the ground, handle-first, in front of the older child.  Motioning to the rope tied around a tree nearby.  \"Go ahead, cut him down, you're free to go.\"

The children looked back up into the ceiling of the forest, their father was hanging upside-down with feet bound and hands tied behind his back.  A gag in his mouth but muffled whimpers and moans could be heard as he swung some 50 feet above the forest floor.

\"He'll die if we cut him down!  He's too far up!\"  Shouted the older one.

\"I assure you he won't.  I myself have survived several falls from that height and at this time of year the forest floor is soft, almost embracing.  At most he would suffer a broken bone or two and then the three of you can be on your way.\"

\"No!  I don't believe you!\"  He shouted again from behind a tree that he and his brother were trying to shield themselves with.

\"Then he will die up there and it will be your fault.  I was not kind when I bound him and he is probably bleeding and has been for some time.  The only chance you have at saving your father's life is cutting him loose.  Come, he will not die from the fall.  You can save your father's life.\"

The children hesitated and looked at each other silently.  They didn't know what to do.

\"If we cut him loose will ... will you let us go?\"  They asked from behind the tree.

\"Yes, you may leave.  For your sake, I pray your gods will not permit you to find yourself among these boughs again.\"

The older child slowly walked out from behind the tree wielding the knife Cirdan had thrown to him.  Cirdan looked up at the man who was now swaying intensely trying to free himself from his bonds, watching as his son approached the tree his rope was tied to.  The boy reached the tree and brought the knife up to the rope when Cirdan interrupted him, \"There are two ropes to cut.  One for you and one for your brother.\"

The first rope was cut by the older and he looked at the elf as he handed the knife to his younger brother.  Cirdan was smiling.  The younger child had a harder time holding the knife above his head and had to stretch to reach it.  After a few hacks the rope frayed and gave way and sent the loose end off into the tree tops.

Their father fell to the ground with a hard thud and muffled screams.  Upon impact there was a symphony of bones popping and breaking, the man was most assuredly dead.  The two children ran to him crying and screaming at what they had just witnessed.  Their father's neck was broken and bones were protruding from his collar.

Through the sobbing and wailing Cirdan made his voice heard, \"Why did you kill your father, children?\"

\"We didn't!\"  They shouted through tears.

\"I watched you both cut the rope.  You killed him.  You have no friends anymore and now you have no family.  Now the only thing you share in common with the trees is your ability to dream.  However, your dreams will not be pleasant ones from this day hence.  Your short and pathetic life will be one of misery and despair, tell every human you meet of what has transpired here.  Return to Glenhaven, it is but a two-day hike from here.  It is your best chance of survival, as your home here in our forest will be overrun with wolves and bears shortly.\"

The older child wiped his tears away and lunged at Cirdan with the elven blade.  He dodged the child, tripping and pushing him to the ground.  \"I will tell you but once more.  Leave or you both will suffer the same fate.  You have already killed your father, don't kill yourselves with this foolish behavior.\"

The younger brother had already started running off into the forest, the older one got up and looked at his father's body.  Cirdan informed the boy, \"Should you return, you will not find your father's body.  He is being denied a burial according to your customs, now flee.  Your brother will need your help if he is to make it to Glenhaven.  You may keep the knife.\"

\"I will kill you one day, elf!\"  Screamed the boy.

\"Brave child, while that may be true it would be exceedingly foolish.  In a blink of my eye you will be old and frail, weak and bitter.  You are being made an example for those who seek to harm Cormanthor.  But should you I feel the slightest inkling of your pursuit, what has happened here today will befall everyone you ever knew and cared about, without mercy.  I will purge your ilk from history.  The forests will regrow, you and yours will cease to be.  Think on that for the remainder of your short life.\"

The child was stunned, not fully understanding what was said to him.  In reply, he hurled the knife at the elf and ran into the woods after his brother.  Cirdan walked over to where it fell, cleaned it, and sheathed it.  He then nocked the arrow he showed the children earlier and walked over to the fallen body.  Pulling back the string he spoke, \"The sins of the father shall be visited upon the child,\" and released the arrow.  It buried itself fletching-deep into the man's throat.

\"Thirty-six more.\"  He said to himself and started walking in Eremus' direction.
