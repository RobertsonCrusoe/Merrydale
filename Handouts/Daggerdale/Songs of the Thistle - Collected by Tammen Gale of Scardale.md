SONGS OF THE THISTLE

AND OTHER VERSES

A Collection of Ballads, Songs, and Poems
from the Reclamation of Thistle Ridge

Collected and Annotated by
Tammen Gale of Scardale

Year of the Awakened Sleepers, 1484 DR

Collector's Preface

To the reader who has already done me the kindness of acquiring my earlier volume, Songs of the Dagger, I owe both thanks and an explanation. That collection was meant to be comprehensive — a final gathering of the verse that had accumulated around the liberation of Daggerdale and the campaigns that followed. I had thought myself finished. I was wrong.

Within months of setting down my pen, the Thistle Ridge affair erupted — that strange, dark, exhilarating chapter in which a company of unknowns called Chrysalis rode east from Dagger Falls to reclaim a haunted ruin and stumbled into something far deeper than goblins and old bones. The enterprise produced its own verse with a speed and character quite unlike anything in my earlier collection. Where the liberation ballads were composed after the fact, often by bards working from secondhand accounts, the songs of Thistle Ridge were born in the thick of events — scribbled in taverns, sung at feasts, chanted by crowds in the arena, and muttered by madmen in the streets.

The centrepiece of this slim supplement is Mumfrey's Song — the cryptic, bawdy, unsettling composition of Merri Mumfrey, the Town Fool of Dagger Falls, sung on the 19th of Ches 1483 at the Vernal Feast. I confess I do not understand it. Neither, I suspect, does anyone else, though several of the keenest minds in Daggerdale have tried. It is presented here exactly as it was recorded by Brother Vessuvian of the Yellow Abbey, who had the good sense to write it down. Its meaning, if it has one, I leave to the reader.

The remaining pieces are all the work of Beliard Emmarask — diplomat, spy, bard, and chronic composer — whose earlier contributions graced the parent volume. Beliard's gifts are undiminished, though his subjects here are lighter: the famous monkey, the Cormyrean war wizard's humiliation, and a summary of the reclamation's events set to a tune that I am told originates from a Cormyrean theatrical, though Beliard's associate Master Hawkford insists it ought to be sung to a Moonsea shipwreck ballad. I leave the matter of musical interpretation to performers of greater talent than myself.

I have added brief contextual notes before each piece, but I have not presumed to alter so much as a comma of the poems and songs themselves. These are the words of their authors, not mine, and they deserve to be met on their own terms.

Tammen Gale
Scardale Town, Marpenoth 1484 DR

I. Mumfrey's Song

by Merri Mumfrey, Town Fool of Dagger Falls

Recorded by Brother Vessuvian of the Yellow Abbey on the 19th of Ches, 1483 DR, at the Vernal Feast.

Of Merri Mumfrey, little is known with certainty. He wanders Dagger Falls unmolested, entering even the Historic Wing while guards look the other way. He claims to hold meetings with the key figures of the dale's history, and can often be found lying on the floor of the Cathedral with his ear pressed against the Burial Stones of the Departed, muttering to himself. Brother Darred of the Yellow Abbey says that Mumfrey is right about the weather half the time, and the late Brother Phinneas Hoetmer — a man not given to frivolity — took him seriously enough for us to give him consideration.

The song is arranged in seven stanzas, each invoking a group of three, and moves from the ghostly to the monstrous by way of the bawdy. Those who have attempted to decode it disagree on nearly everything except that the first stanza refers to the three child-spirits haunting the ruins of Thistle Ridge. The rest is disputed. The investigator Lucan Alavandor of Chrysalis Company devoted an evening and two bottles of wine to the problem and emerged with theories but no certainties; his colleague Jasper Hawkford sat up all night afterwards and arrived at rather darker conclusions. Their analysis, which is itself a remarkable piece of the Thistle Ridge literary record, is preserved separately.

It is presented here complete and unaltered.


There are spirits spirits spirits one-two-three;
Small but great in wrath and sad betrayal,
Their tragic story is the key to set them all and others free,
And expose for good an ancient false portrayal.

There are maidens maidens maidens one-two-three;
Their warm breath in the frosty air a haze,
They sing and dance and tempt the boys with all they have and little poise,
To consummate in less than ten and fifty days.

There are shadows shadows shadows one-two-three;
They are strongest in the west and bend to thee
These shadows cast a form that stirs a mighty storm
That darkens river, ridge and road and sea.

There are mothers mothers mothers one-two-three
Whose husbands toil in the field and the bough.
They have welcoming rear-ends and sons with lonely friends,
Who come and go before the husbands' turn to plough.

There are devils devils devils one-two-three;
Men think that they have got them by the tail.
We laugh while they are weeping but they dance while we are sleeping,
And in the cold light of day they may prevail.

There are nanas nanas nanas one-two-three;
Whose granddaughters have husbands tall and strong.
These men blow out the candles, more fun than broom handles,
Before returning to their young wives' legs so lithe and long.

There are monsters monsters monsters one-two-three;
Who dreamt a man who doesn't know exactly how to seam,
There's an eye that doesn't close while all the others doze,
And I think we all might be but a monster's dream.

II. The Monkey On the Mountain

by Beliard Kathklan Emmarask

Composed and performed at Quick Axes tavern in Dagger Falls on the evening of the 21st of Ches, 1483 DR.

The first round of the Thistle Ridge Competition was a game of Capture the Flag, played on a specially constructed mountain arena in the centre of Dagger Falls. Chrysalis Company faced the White Cloaks — the favoured team, captained by the seasoned mercenary Alen Reks and featuring the Cormyrean War Wizard Alexandus Ward. What followed was one of the most celebrated spectacles in recent Daggerdale memory: a chaotic scramble of steel, spell, and monkey that ended in Chrysalis's first victory and the War Wizard's lasting humiliation. Beliard Emmarask, who was in attendance, composed this ballad the same day and performed it that evening. It is presented here complete and unaltered.


In Dagger Falls, as snowflakes whirled, two teams of valor met,
Chrysalis bold and Whitecloaks proud, in a challenge fiercely set.
The field prepared, the flags were placed, excitement ruled the day,
For boasting rights and victory, the gods would have their say.

The bell was rung, Ser Rowan leapt  and towards the flag did bound,
While Mari, quick and small and brave, ran out to find her ground.
Lucan, laid low by Oakbough's sting, fell tangled, in dismay,
But healed by Mari's fervent prayers, he rose again to join the fray.

Zelia's arrows flew like hawks, at Ser Rowan aiming true,
Yet he with heart and girth and ire, the Whitecloak flag from her withdrew.
Then up the mountain's lofty peak, with flag in hand, he climbed,
'Midst magic spells and arrows' flight, his valor brightly shined.

Jeska, elf of wit and blade, danced 'round her flag with grace,
Her sharp and stinging verbal barbs led the Dales a spritely chase.
But Tyrell Dale, through melee's din, advanced with purpose strong,
To grasp the flag in eager hands, amidst the battle's song.

He hurled it to his kin in stride, a toss both bold and fleet,
Jannus caught it on the rise, their triumph seeming sweet.
Into the water Jeska plunged, by Tyrell's hand she fell,
Yet her spirit, bold and undimmed, rose with stories yet to tell.

Meanwhile, Jannus held the flag, and to the mountain path he raced,
Dodging slicks of Jasper's grease, with a skillful, artful pace.
Up he climbed, the peak in sight, the summit his to claim,
But Jasper's wind, with forceful might, soon brought him low again.

Then 'midst clash of steel and spell and brawn, a monkey took the stage,
A creature small, yet bold and brave, a true hero for this age.
The primate, swift and nimble, grabbed the flag with cheeky glee,
Then leapt from the mountain's lofty height, as cunning as could be.

Lucan, with his bow in hand, at Zelia and Alexandus aimed,
But Hilltop's healing thwarted him, their vigor she reclaimed.
So he seized the flag anew, and towards his goal, he ran,
He threw it back to Chrysalis' zone, a shrewd and clever plan.

Alexandus, the Whitecloak mage, strode towards the arena's heart,
To thwart the hedge wizard's simple ploys, to block his arcane art.
Yet Jasper, friend to bird and beast, sought the monkey's sly assist,
To disrupt the mage's eldritch plans, and his pride's fall to enlist.

The monkey, quick with jest and jape, caught the war mage unawares,
Its arms were strong, its aim was true, it flung dung into his hair.
Cormyr's son, a wizard proud, was thus by a rustic mageling bested,
His pride was scarred by the monkey's jibe, his temper sorely tested.

Ser Rowan, up atop the peak, with hammer swung in might,
Faced the Brothers Dale in a fearsome fray, a truly gallant sight.
Then Jeska, with her longbow poised, found just the perfect shot,
"Show me your heart, stag," she whispered, her aim forgot her not.

At last, the Whitecloaks, waning, their wills by war were worn.
Tara Hilltop hailed the end, her team's defeat was sworn.
So it was that Team Chryslais claimed their first victory that day,
Their deeds of bravery, luck, and skill, in my song I do convey.

III. Monkey on the Mountain
(known popularly as "The Munk," or among the older and crasser boys, "The Monkeyshit Mage")

by Beliard Kathklan Emmarask

A children's song, composed shortly after the ballad above, which the young folk of Dagger Falls took to singing in the years that followed.

If the ballad of the Monkey on the Mountain is Beliard's account of the battle as it happened, this shorter piece is what it became in the mouths of the people. Within days of the competition, the children of Dagger Falls were singing it in the streets, and by the time of the Ches 24 match between the White Cloaks and the Standing Stone League, over a third of the arena crowd joined in a spontaneous rendition when the War Wizard Alexandus Ward attempted his usual strategy of counter-spelling. The effect on Ward's concentration was devastating; he lost the round. The tune, I am informed, is borrowed from a well-known Cormyrean music-hall number — a detail that would not have been lost on the War Wizard himself.


I went to the Dagger Falls fair,
The heroes all were there,
Jasper the Sly, under the sky,
Was casting his spells in the air,
You should've seen the monk,
He played in the snowy funk,
He leaped and swung, the flag he won,
And that was the win of the monk,
The monk, the monk, the monk,
The monk, the monk, the monk!

'Xandus, mage so grand,
Strode forth with staff in hand,
He aimed to weave a spell to achieve
A win over Chrysalis band,
But then here came the monk,
With a jest and a leap he dunked,
He flung with glee, his poo you see,
And that was the win of the monk,
The monk, the monk, the monk,
The monk, the monk, the monk!

Oh, I went to the Dagger Falls fair,
The heroes all were there,
The monkey so small, outdid them all,
A feat beyond compare,
You should've seen the monk,
His spirit never once shrunk,
With daring deed, and cunning speed,
Oh, that was the win of the monk,
The monk, the monk, the monk,
The monk, the monk, the monk!

IV. Beliard's Favourite Things

by Beliard Kathklan Emmarask

Composed at Castle Radiance, sometime after the conclusion of the Thistle Ridge affair.

This piece reached me through the same channels as Beliard's other later compositions — channels I am not at liberty to disclose. It is a summary of the Thistle Ridge reclamation in its entirety, set to the melody of a Cormyrean theatrical song whose original concerns the enumeration of pleasant trifles. Beliard's version enumerates rather less pleasant trifles — purple worm sieges, gargoyle incursions, and the schemes of the Netherese — with a cheerfulness that borders on the unhinged. His associate, the wizard Jasper Hawkford, reportedly sang it to the tune of a Moonsea shipwreck ballad instead, which may in fact suit the subject matter better. Beliard himself judged it inferior to "The Munk," a verdict with which the children of Dagger Falls would doubtless agree.


Sworn band of strangers who take up the gauntlet
Charity Hills lends a hand as their sponsor,
Alliances forged with complex legal strings,
These are a few of our favourite things!

Bawdy spring revels, new-budding romances,
Brawls with the locals and nights filled with dances,
Political currents with intrigue galore,
Secrets and scandals behind every door!

White Cloaks defeated, a rivalry strong,
Redjacks so brutal, the fights last so long,
Free Defense falters and Chrysalis wins,
These are a few of our favourite things!

When the curse bites,
When the witch stings,
When we're feeling sad,
We simply remember our favourite things,
And then we don't feel so bad!

Seances with spirits and grand mayor's balls,
Hinterland battles with wasps ten feet tall,
Fungus-fey creatures and goblin-filled raids,
These are a few of the quests we have made!

Purple worm sieges and gargoyle incursions,
Netheril's plots and Loki's diversions,
Melegaunt's schemes and the chaos he brings,
These are a few of our favourite things!

When the worm strikes,
When the wasp stings,
When we're feeling mad,
We simply remember our favourite things,
And then we don't feel so bad!

A Note on Omissions

The careful reader will note that this collection does not include the song of Jebediah Greenthumb, the conspiracy-minded Chauntean farmer whose rant about the Whitegate family circulated in Dagger Falls around this period. I have excluded it on the grounds that it is prose, not verse, and that its provenance is uncertain — it was transcribed by one Scribe Elmira Quill, and Greenthumb himself was not available for verification.

Nor have I included the remarkable investigation notes of Lucan Alavandor and Jasper Hawkford, who between them produced the most thorough analysis of Mumfrey's Song that I have encountered. Their work is analytical rather than poetic, and deserves a volume of its own — though I note that Alavandor's uncle's advice on the subject of consummation ("To consummate, why hesitate? Don't make her wait — Penetrate!") has a certain rough meter that might qualify it for inclusion in a less scrupulous anthology.

Tammen Gale
Scardale Town, Marpenoth 1484 DR
