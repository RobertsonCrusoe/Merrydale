"A Theory of the Hauntings of Thistle Ridge, by Magistrate Zoderick Winters, 1445 DR"

By now, every child in the dale has heard of the Ghosts of Thistle Ridge, and more than a few men and women claim to have seen or been close to them in the few years since the Thistle Ridge Tragedy. I was given the task as the 'resident expert' on these matters to investigate the ruins and see what truth if any there is to the rumours. My expertise comes entirely from the study of the small collection of books about the undead that is permitted to be shelved in the Athenaeum, most of which I think could be labelled as 'fairy tales'. A couple have merit, but I have not had access to serious literature on the matter, and for that I am grateful and indebted to the Chaplain of the Athenaeum, who's vigil keeps our minds as clear as the morning sun.

Below, I will give a brief description and chronology of my likewise brief expedition to the ruins, and follow with an analysis and theory about what I found. I hope greater men than I will be able to use this information for the betterment of the dale someday.

The Expedition

This might be a lofty title for four-days' journey from my comfortable bed at the Abbeye, but on the morning of 21 Eleint, 1445 DR, exactly four years to the day after the Tragedy, assembling at the East Gate with a ride of Sunblades and receiving rites and blessings from the Chaplains and the Bishop, I had the feeling I was taking part in something greater than the sum of its parts. The air was cool and crisp on this Autumnal Equinox, and all the Signs and Portents suggested a safe return. As usual, they were mostly right, and remind us that mortals should have the humility to accept the judgement of the Gods, even when our limited intellect perceives them (incorrectly) as mercurial and capricious.

I was attached to the 18^th^ Company 1^st^ Platoon, "Task Masters". This is the Command Platoon of the reserve company, and we were led by the company captain himself, Dexter Broad (I have included a sketch of him to the side), a stout man from the eastern dales somewhere. His sergeant was a woman named Bella Birtch from Lostead, a capable archer, forester and leader. Their company chaplain also accompanied us. This was a younger woman named Hannah Hillborn of Scardale, who had answered a Divine Call to come to come and study at the Abbeye. Being some ten years my junior, I didn't know her well, and I regret that I didn't get to know her in the brief time we were allotted together.

With twenty-four Sunblades, plus the command element and attached retinue, we were thirty in total, with half that many horse and a small train with provisions and tents.

We mustered at the East Gate for Prime, this early hour granting few witnesses to our departure, aside from the clergy and some wives and children seeing us off. The Bishop declared our commission and blessings complete, with Good Auspex. However, as the captain mounted his great warhorse, his son broke from his mother's side and ran to his father, tears in his eyes. He cried about never seeing his dad again, and how we'd all be food for crows. Though there are some in the dale that put too much stock in the augury of children, occasionally they merit our attention and consideration. We didn't think today was such a day, given our Auspex, and off we marched.

We made Gribbons' by Nones and enjoyed their hospitality for the remainder of the day and that night. A great work is underway here, the people of this farm still grieving the loss of their neighbours and fearful that the hobgoblin horde that obliterated them without warning and vanished just as quickly might repeat this treachery outside the walls of this very settlement. They have two dwarven engineers hired, and the terraced redoubt of the central farm is shaping up to be one of the finest strongpoints in the hinterland of our fair dale. The mayor here has chosen to make a smaller, taller, and sturdier fortress surrounded by unwalled farms, rather than a longer and lower walls which we see in the settlements to the West and South of the Falls. I hope they are never tested, but I don't doubt the strength of that dwarf-laid stone, or the resilience of the tunnels and stores beneath.

I took the opportunity to break bread with the local Magistrate, a good man I've known for some fifteen years, Invictus. Invictus is a Cormyrean, and answered the Call to come to the Abbeye when he was only ten years old. He convinced his family to send him on his own to live and study here, and we enrolled at the same time, though he was some eight years my junior. Already lettered in the Cormyrean fashion, he was a lightning-fast study of the Faith and wrote his first Treatise (on the dual nature of pain and the harvesting of the Light it can produce, if memory serves me) by the time he was fourteen years old.

Invictus caught me up on everything he knew about the Tragedy and the condition and status of the Ruin presently. Indeed, he had been assigned to tend the flock in Downriver Chapel the very spring before the Tragedy, and his memory of the days that followed it was keen. I've tried to capture his memories to the fullest of my humble abilities below.

The Account of Magistrate Invictus

"The first sign of trouble came on the evening of the Autumnal Equinox of 1481. It had been raining for two days, and a thick fog was low over the river and wafting through the valley. On a clear day this time of year, we can see the smoke from some of the fires in Thistle Ridge. The Sembians are better at making smoke than proper fires, and you can often see a roiling black plume from the Academy on the Ridge. I confess to having been predisposed to dislike their teachings up there, and the site of the distant smoke would bring bile to my mouth. But now I regret those unchecked feelings, and am humbly reminded that Judgement is serious business which should only be undertaken when Ordered. At any rate, as I've said, it was rainy and foggy, and we couldn't see as far as Eastwatch, much less Thistle Ridge.

"What troubled me was the lack of word from Thistle Ridge the entire day. There was a fine young lady-magistrate up there. Daphne Dale. She had no staff to speak of, and I had become familiar with her elegant hand, as she wrote, sent, and received all messages herself, and she thought most small matters worth reporting to me. Her sense of duty was admirable, and her hand a delight to read. I have the last message she sent to me memorized. It was her usual post-Compline message, this from the 20^th^;

"All's well. Large caravan from Hillsfar here tonight. They are not unpacking to trade with us, and so should make your gates by Vespers tomorrow."

"Vespers came and went with no caravan from Hillsfar. Some of the farmers from Eastwatch came in with a squad of Sunblades to report animals acting strange. They insisted that something big must have happened 'down stream', that spooked them into our fields. They were right of course. Do yourself a favour, old friend, and take seriously any farmer that makes his way to your polished floors, cleans his boots to enter, and reports a troubling matter. They are not excitable men.

"On top of that, one farmer in particular insisted that he had heard a great rumbling sound coming from the Ridge and speculated that an evil druid was playing recklessly with the weather, such as it was, and that's what scared the animals. I instructed him that 'evil' was a superfluous word to describe a druid, but I think my jest was lost on him, and I am reminded that humour is a vice for undisciplined men.

"After hearing all this, I sent word to both the Cathedral and to Thistle Ridge, as well as Dwimmerholme for good measure, announcing my concern for the Ridge. I never heard from Sister Dale again, as you know by now. I was instructed by the Bishop himself to send scouts to Thistle Ridge right away, which I did.

"We had a competent garrison in those days, and four very capable foresters which we sent out before the blessed sunrise of the 22^nd^. By Sext, one came back with an early report that they could see smoke rising from many smouldering fires in the distance on the Ridge, and by nightfall, the other three returned with news that portions of the wall were smashed to bits, many buildings were ablaze or smouldering, and that no sign of life besides some listless goblinkind could be detected from without the walls. They didn't enter the newly made ruin, their pragmatism outweighing the impetuous bravery of youth, and returned to us with no further intelligence.

"On the 23^rd^ our garrison was reinforced by the remaining platoons from the 18^th^ Company to stand our walls, and two platoons from the 7^th^ to "recon Thistle Ridge in force". The latter did so in good order, at first, their Captain deploying ¼ of their force as a feint along the river, while the remainder, leaving their horses, crept into the ruin through myriad breaches in the walls. He engaged in a low-intensity skirmish with goblins that were busy taking and breaking things. He estimated that some hundreds were milling about the place, and that if they could be met piecemeal, that two companies of Blades could dispatch them. However, he was the first to report a greater problem, a Shadow in the Academy.

"Some of his men cornered a small host of goblinkind in a front room of the Academy, and were seeing methodically to their duty, when both sides were set upon by a Shadow, both loosing lives to the hunger of the thing. When his men fled the Academy, the Captain was determined to make entry to retrieve the two fallen Blades that hadn't escaped. Hearing the description of a Shadowy fiend, he brought his Chaplain with him, and the two entered to face it with six Blades to support them. The remainder of the command element heard the end of their brothers-in-arms through terrifying screams and otherworldly horror. The sergeants mustered all that remained of the two platoons (they were exactly eight men, one captain and one chaplain short), and withdrew the ruin. The senior of the two sergeants explained to me on the 24^th^ when they returned that he thought the better of lingering in a place crawling with goblins, and with unknown shadowy enemies to account for besides. He was right, and again, I salute his pragmatism.

"The next two tenday we sent out such rangers and foresters as we thought able to handle the situation with discretion. We needed then intelligence, not goblin heads, to account for the Tragedy. And little by little, over that period, we learned that the central redoubt as well as the fortified Bastion had been destroyed, from their account, from the inside out, and that many walls were similarly sapped from below or behind. A great host of hobgoblins from the north had come and gone as well, and left not a survivor, or even a casualty; indeed, they had dragged off their dead, though dead goblins and some bugbears lay rotting about the ruin.

"A host of goblins had crossed the river at some point before the 21^st^, and they had blocked any attempt by the people of Thistle Ridge to flee. Many brutal scenes of flight-cut-short were discovered by our rangers, all of whom confirm that they had never witnessed evidence of such a comprehensive slaughter in all their days, most especially by goblinkind, who are wont to become distracted with cruelty and abandon their duties quickly.

"Most troubling of all was the lack of evidence for a caravan. It is unlikely that a caravan the size of which was deemed reportable by the lady-magistrate of the Ridge could be carried off without leaving a great deal of sign for our rangers to detect. It is possible that it turned around and went back east along the road, and that its tracks were washed away by the rain, but that would make the people of the caravan the only survivors of this raid. I am not sure I am qualified to jump to conclusions, but here are the facts as I understand them;

"The Caravan arrived late enough the day before the Tragedy to not engage in trade and turn some coins.

"It was late enough that there might be a reasonable expectation that no word of its arrival would leak from Thistle Ridge, and indeed, if not for the habitual missives from Sister Dale, no word would have.

"There is not a shred of evidence that it was attacked and plundered like the entirety of the Ridge.

"It seems to have gotten away when no other living thing managed to do so.

"All of this is suspicious to me, but now, years later, I don't recall if we ever sorted out who was in that caravan, where it went, or indeed if it even existed. I think it did, as Sister Dale was a good woman and perhaps the most dutiful correspondent I've ever had, present company included, old friend.

"But what's all that, besides the musings on an event long-passed, on a matter that may or may not be important? What is important is this; the Grentzeldek were indeed behind the assault on Thistle Ridge. Our rangers tracked them, a full battalion at least, heavy-laden with treasure, headed back to the Grentzeldek. It's been four years since their attack, and no ranger I know of has seen them in our lands since, though they are surely settled just a few miles into the Fey Hills. Whatever they took from Thistle Ridge, it has been enough to slake their thirst for plundering human settlements for some time.

"By the end of two tenday, the goblins had been whittled away considerably by our skirmishing rangers. Before the end of that time, some risked entry into the ruin itself, and a couple never came out. One at least went into the Alavandor Academy quite foolishly, and met the same fate as a squad of Sunblades, their captain and chaplain. Another pair investigated the Oak and Sheild Inn, and it is from this duo that we learn the most...from the one that emerged at any rate.

"Brindelle Oakbough was the young lady who made it out. I forget her partner's name. He was a Dwimerholme forester who came down to help. They were a couple, I think. They found strange goblin witchcraft devices; fetishes and wards, that sort of profane thing, all around the Inn. The Inn itself was one of the buildings most intact, or so the young lady ranger told me after.

"Inside, she described a very disturbing setting. She said that the air was thick, and she thought it was smoky; ash was in the air, and everything was dull and grey. She said she never felt such fear, abject terror, and the urge to flee counterbalanced by an inexplicable rooting of her person in place. She said she watched the entry hall of the Inn come alive; furniture and fixtures, loose boards, debris, just about anything that was unattended. The debris became a maelstrom, combined with the smoke and ash, and it buffeted the duo of young rangers. The Oakbough girl managed to escape and report this back to me. I spoke with one of her colleagues who was nearby at the time, who insists that there were no fires burning in the ruin on the day they were there. I have some serious concerns for what might be inside the Inn, but I guess that's why you're here.

"Over the years, there have been many people come and go, trying to pry into the ruin's secrets and find her treasures. A couple of capable adventuring parties have gone in and never come out, and we know that goblins, bandits and other scourge set up camp there from time-to-time, but none seem to stay for very long. There are multiple entities stalking the ruin that eventually turn out anyone who desires settling it. What exactly is lurking there I'm very interested in knowing, and I am presently at your service, Brother Zoderick, here to aid in any way that furthers your knowledge and mine."

The Ruin

I thanked my old friend for his account and warnings. The description of the 'ash-filled Inn' would prove useful, as I had made a guess about the nature of that place, and the undead therein based upon it.

I found myself fully awake for Lauds the next morning, Invictus already lighting candles in the Chapel in prayer for a clear day for the expedition. I remarked that when I meet Amaunator I will ask him if he really wants his flock praying to him at such an hour, but my old friend repeated his admonishment for humour, and entreated me to pray with him.

By Prime, the expedition was again ready to go, now bolstered by twelve Sunblades from the garrison here, and another twelve capable militia who, I think, were more curious to see the inside of the famous ruin than they were eager to be dutiful. I should have refused them, as not all of them have returned with us tonight.

As it happens, our prayers at Lauds were answered with a clear morning. A light and early frost made the ground and trees sparkle until about Terce, when the warm sun melted it away. By Sext we found ourselves at the foot of Thistle Ridge, having arrived unmolested. I can categorically declare the part of the ruin by the river free of evil spirits. What few structures remain here are indeed in ruins, and show signs of infrequent use by travelers, but are free of anything meaner than large rats.

There is a curious all-faiths shrine at the base of the approach to the ruin, which is excellent condition. I asked Invictus what he knew about it when I spoke to him days later, and he mentioned that he didn't know anyone that knew anyone who knew a thing about it; who built it, when, or for whom?

We made a camp in the old caravan staging area, which is already overgrown with trees more than ten feet tall in places, the ruin looming over us menacingly. As we struck camp, and our scouts began to pick their way up the rubble-strewn switchback trail leading to the ruin, some of the Sunblades and most of the militia were uneasy. The day was bright and clear, but our spirits were already flagging. The ruin atop the limestone ridge looks like a line of jagged, broken teeth sticking out of a dark maw. The leaves were just starting to turn, and the oaks were still mostly full, the effect of which was to create shadows and doubt everywhere we looked. The crows in the trees made a discordant and obscene song that bothered the men, many of whom recalled the words of the captain's boy at the outset of our journey.

Still, Captain Broad was resolved to do his duty, and he steadied his men. He had a warm meal prepared over a large fire for his soldiers while we awaited the scouts, and this gave the men confidence, even a little bravado which I enjoyed.

A little while later, the scouts started to return, all with the same report; there was nothing lurking within the ruins that they could see. Lots of evidence of periodic habitation by creatures of various types, including some human bandits, but no one here at the moment.

Leaving only a small group to guard the camp and train, the rest of us climbed up the switchback. Over the last several years someone or something had cleared away the worst obstructions, making our passage possible if not easy. Finally, we mounted the great pile of rubble that had been The Bastion and looked into the already overgrown ruin of Thistle Ridge Farm.

From our vantage point atop the large rubble pile, we could mark most of the major buildings of the settlement, and the captain and I consulted the map from the Yellow Abbeye, while the troops cautiously descended the other side of the rubble pile, into the now-tree-choked main road of the farm. In a few years, you won't be able to see anything from this position, as the trees are already twenty feet high in the ruin, an observation that was interesting to the captain. When the oaks overtake the area, even the large three-storey Sembian-styled buildings will be obscured, but on that bright afternoon in the autumn of '45, we could clearly make out most of the large buildings on the map.

The Town Hall remained, but the once-proud belltower at it's face had fallen into a crevasse which now dominated the main square, the work of devious sappers being painfully clear to see. Artisan's Hall was burnt down to the first-floor stones, and many smaller buildings had likewise vanished completely. But we could see the Inn, The Academy, Merchant's Hall, and the Garrison, all more or less intact.

The men had mustered on the old street, and a few were already hacking away brush and some large trees to form a clearing from which to operate with less frustration, the streets and buildings being choked with not only the large Cormanthor Oak, but many thorny bushes besides. At length, the Captain split the men into two parties, and we decided to investigate the closest large buildings first. These were the Town Hall (sans belltower) and the Garrison. In short, we found that both somewhat intact; they had been through a serious battle, and small fires had been through portions of both, but they stood. And had clearly been used by goblins and bandits over the years. Evidence of little camps was easy to find. They had also been picked over thoroughly, and we found nothing of value, although we didn't spend long in the search.

I am sad to report that the Shrine to our Blessed Lord of Light has been reduced completely to rubble. Given the state of the buildings on either side of it (somewhat intact), the violence done to our Shrine appears to have been deliberate. The Captain tells me that he suspects this might have been done later than the Tragedy of '41, given the reduced amount of overgrowth in that ruin relative to the rest of the place. We are blessed to be reviled by so many wicked foes.

After the brief exploration of the first two large buildings, and the discovery of the state of the Shrine, we discovered that we weren't alone in the Ruin. The gaping chasm that opened in front of the town hall that swallowed up the belltower showed quite a bit of evidence of creatures going to and from it. Some small humanoid tracks as well as larger beasts were discovered by our foresters, and the Captain decided to 'strongpoint the permitter' of the chasm with nearly half our men, before moving deeper into the Ruin. Indeed, from the old town square and the chasm, we could fairly clearly make out the details of the Academy and the Merchants' Hall, and were anxious to have a closer look. But upon surrounding the chasm and risking sending a couple of our most nimble scouts into the opening, we determined that the floor of the chasm was indeed the old sewer network built by the Sembians, attached to the ancient limestone cave network that permeates so much of this part of the dale. The men were getting twitchy at this point, some thinking they heard or saw movement from this dark passage in the chasm or that, and we were forced to maintain the strongpoint, reducing our available numbers for the remainder of the exploration.

Captain Broad, Chaplain Hillborn, Sergeant Birtch and I were in the middle of discussing how to proceed, squinting through the trees at both of the nearer objectives, when the blood-curdling scream of a child came from the nearby Hall, followed a moment later by a vast murder of crows that took up flight and cacophony as the scream faded. The murder filled the sky with the loud and angry birds, and everyone of us to a man and woman was shaken. The Chaplain immediately began a prayer to fortify the hearts of the men, and some would have broken and ran, I don't doubt, if not for the intercession provided.

The Sgt. looked at her Captain with a worried and eager expression, while the Chaplain began to march the ranks, now deploying a censer to bath our men in holy fumes to combat the fear that had taken hold. The Captain said "We will pick one building only, Magistrate. I am sorry but we cannot do more than that for today. I don't have to ask the Chaplain how long she can hold our spirits; we have campaigned together since '39, and am familiar with her blessings and their limits. Also, our men are split already, some holding the camp, some strong pointing the chasm. Now we will take the rest to the building of your choosing, and see what we find."

His counsel was prudent, and I did not argue and sue for a greater commitment. Days later, Invictus admonished me for not encouraging the Captain to find his Courage and his Faith, as though that would have changed the outcome of the expedition to our favour. "For those with Faith and a Righteous Cause, a single loaf may be divided any number of times needed to feed a host" he reminded me. At any rate, we divided our loaf into exactly three parts, and proceeded to the Academy. The Chaplain wanted to go to the Hall to find the source of the screaming child, but I knew a bit of what to expect in the Academy, and had prepared for it, and so we were resolved to investigate it first.

The Shadow

Finally, our expedition, having cut our way down the westward street, and passing many smaller buildings with blackened openings that challenged our bravery, found itself at the foot of the steps leading up and into the Academy. I was equipped with the original drawings of the building which I had found in the Yellow Abbeye Athenaeum, and found it mostly intact, and true to the drawings. It is a three-story building in the Sembian style. Our scouts went about the permitter of the place, and came back to report it intact all the way about. The main steps were covered in profane goblinkind devices and fetishes designed to keep spirits in, and their kin out. What stood out the most, however, and the thing which gave us the most pause, was a small crude sign that had been nailed to a thick bramble close to the foot of the steps, with a hastily-carved message in the Chondathan reading "DEATH INSIDE, NO ESCAPE, STAY OUT!!!" And perched atop this crude sign was a mean-looking crow that glowered at us until a frightened militiamen threw a rock at it. He missed, but the bird cawed once in retort, and lazily took to the air.

The Captain addressed the assembled troops, announcing that he would make entry with myself and his Chaplain, but that any other that wished to 'secure our redoubt' would be thanked for his or her duty. He was a good man and a great leader. He asked only for volunteers and provided an honourable alternative in securing his redoubt from the place. His leadership inspired all five Sunblades to enter with us, the remaining six soldiers all being militia. He clapped the shoulders of a young militia sergeant, and told him how important his role was, holding the steps of the Academy for us.

I confess that I have seen very little time in the field, and was not accustomed to the stratagems of our brave armed men and women. I had initially taken the Captain's words about the redoubt as an honourable way out of entering the building, which it was, but only partially. We were in a Ruin after all, and even though we were certain of danger inside the Academy, there were still unknown dangers lurking in the Ruin which the Captain had accounted for. We will come to this in a moment.

Finally, there were eight of us ready to make entry. A couple of the men cleared debris and thickets away from the doors, while the Chaplain and I prepared the men with blessings and abjurations. Torches were lit, Light was cast on the shields of the men in the van, and in we went.

The front hall of the Academy is as far as we got, I am ashamed to say. As soon as we entered, we found ourselves amid Shadow, our Divine Lights extinguished completely, and our torches illuminating the large room with about half strength. In the air floated motes of ash and shadow, and there was no colour in the room. I would have said we entered the Shadowfell, if not for the description of the assault of the shadowy fiend and the harrowing tale of the Oakbough girl and her partner at the Inn. We weren't in the Shadowfell, but rather it was leaking into our world through some obscene artefact, and providing the Negative Energy needed for the monsters inside. We were quickly set upon by those monsters.

The first to attack us were shadowy versions of goblins, which our troop repelled with ease. The Chaplain channeled pure energy, obliterating the beasts. However, the first attack was designed to test our abilities, and the second came quickly after, this time focused on the Chaplain. Shadows materialized about her, but the Captain, guessing the probing nature of the attack, was already at her side, and was now swinging a mace he had brought apurpose for these creatures. It was a weapon of some renown (I found out much later) named Nifyl Mastycys, from the profane and ancient Netherese, meaning 'Shadow Eater'. Cpt. Broad and Shadow Eater evaporated shadow-versions of their brothers that had fallen here four years earlier, as they attempted to draw out the spirit of the Chaplain. The shadows raked her and the Captain with their unholy elongated talons, weakening them both, and ultimately drawing out a shadowy version each. These shadow-twins of our Captain and our Chaplain joined the fray against us, and it was at that point that the Captain yelled "Winters, now!" and with that I grasped my holy symbol, bowed my head in prayer begging for deliverance, and channeled forth what positive energy I had saved for this very moment, and in a muted flash, the shadows were gone.

Almost all our troop had been raked and drained by the shadows that had assaulted us, but we had prevailed for the moment. The Captain reminded us that the nightmare creature that created the foes we had just vanquished was still at large, somewhere within the Academy, and that we would march forth to find it, and end this infestation of Shadow. But at that moment, we heard muted thumping from the great doors of the entry, which in the confusion of battle we had failed to notice closing up behind us. We could vaguely hear the sound of conflict from without, but between the thickness of the doors and stone, and the otherworldly nature of the Shadowfell-infected hall, the sounds were muted, like hearing something underwater. The Captain guessed that a great treachery was afoot, and boldly commanded his soldiers to fight though. "The enemy wishes for us to think ourselves trapped, and to focus our remaining strength on escaping, rather than carrying out our duty. We will trust our brothers outside to hold, and see our duty finished!"

But this is where I must shamefully confess my failings, and their terrible cost. I was sure that our men on the steps had been overtaken, and that the door was held against us, to keep us in. Trying it quickly, I indeed found it firm, as though barred from outside, and I panicked. "The enemy has closed our casket! Let us cut our way out of here!" I implored the men. My Faith was tested in that moment and failed utterly. My most powerful prayers and intercessions were spent in the first two waves of the assault, and our troop was likewise weakened. A couple of them began to hack at the doors, despite the Captain's orders to form up. There was a moment of chaos, our ranks in disarray, and just then from the grand stair came a horrific howl, and the noise of a child. Later, those of us that got out disagreed on the noise; some said it was crying, others said it was screaming in pain, but I thought it was laughing.

The Shadow emerged at the top of the steps, the child-noises getting louder, and now the screams of men on the other side of the doors sounded clearer. We thought we heard them yelling for us to cut our way out. Half of us tried the door, while the other half fell in behind the Captain who now mounted the stairs with Shadow Eater leveled at his foe. Chaplain Hillborn was right behind him chanting blessings though her fear, and a couple of the men as well. The rest of us were hammering with futility at the double doors that barred our escape.

The next time I looked over my shoulder to the combat at the base of the stairs, a couple of the men were down, and clumsy shadowy doubles of them were clawing at Hillborn and Broad, while the main Shadow attacked with long appendages from halfway up the stairs, likewise clawing at the Captain. I screamed for him to fall back and help us with the door, and looking back I saw the Shadow raising its obscene appendages to the ceiling in triumph, as a murder of shadowy crows flew down the steps, over it's head, and descended on the Captain and the Chaplain.

At that moment, a hand veritably came through the door, and pulled one of the men through it. The closed doors were an illusion all along, and now understanding this I shouted "There are no doors! Close your eyes to this darkness and deceit and emerge unto the sun!" The men did just that and walked through the illusory door. I remained behind to exit last, and looking back for the last time, saw the Captain fall, as a shadowy copy of the Chaplain who laid dead at his feet dragged him down beside her. As he hit the floor, a shadow form of him emerged, and its malicious silver-limned eyes glowered at me. The multitude of shadows now swarmed toward me, the crows nearly reaching me, before I closed my eyes, walked through the illusion, and emerged into the glory of the full Sun.

From inside, we heard the unearthly wailing of the shadows, but that quickly died down, and we were stood in silence on the steps. The militia had done as the Captain commanded, securing our egress. For them, the doors had never closed, but everything appeared impossibly dark within, and they could see nothing. However, they heard the desperate battle inside, and one clever woman had figured out that we were being fooled by an illusion, and reached her hand in to pull us out. Our delivery was made, ensured by the foresight of the Captain. Unfortunately, the Captain's son likewise had foresight on the matter.

For many weeks now, since the expedition and my penning this report, I have cursed my cowardice, and have begged every morning for forgiveness. The Captain was right; the Shadow intended to separate and defeat us, and I fell for its trickery and was complicit in the deaths (and worse) of him, Chaplain Hillborn and the two Sunblades we left beside them.

What followed immediately after was a reassembly of what remained of our host in the main square adjacent the chasm, and a prompt decision by Sergeant Birtch to march back to Gribbons', which I endorsed.

The Debriefing

Originally intending to spend the night at the base of Thistle Ridge, our change in plans found us packing camp and leaving later in the day than would normally be advisable. We marched well past Compline to make Eastwatch and decided to continue the last mile in near pitch dark to make the gates of the farm.

I met with Magistrate Invictus as soon as I was settled in the keep, and he summoned his clerk to take notes. Below is the transcript of our conversation as his clerk recorded it.

Magistrate Invictus (MI): You look terrible, old friend. Have you looked in a mirror? You carry the mark of Shadow on you. We should pray immediately.

The two knelt and did the Imploration Upon Compline together. MI then examined the hair of Magistrate Winters, which had turned white.

MI: This is not good, brother. Your Faith faltered somewhere in the dark.

Magistrate Zoderick Winters (MZW): Indeed. I will pray at dawn in a coarse habit, brother. I'll beg for twelve Luminous Castigations and twelve Luminous Pardons, and leave the rest to Amaunator's judgement.

MI: Let us stick to the judgement of men on the matters most proximal here, for the moment, good brother. Tell me what shadow you found and how you are delivered without the captain.

MZW: Having heard the account of the Oakbough girl and her plight at the Inn, and suspecting a similar condition in the Academy based on the report from the surviving sergeant which you conveyed, I surmised, correctly, that there was a tear in the veil between our world and the Shadowfell. Indeed, this is exactly what I found at the Academy.

MI: You're quite sure, old friend?

MZW: I'm quite sure, brother. The front hall of the Academy was cloaked in shadow, devoid of colour, and choked with motes of shadow and ash. The air was thin, sounds were muffled, and dread filled every heart. If that wasn't a rivulet from the Shadowfell, it was the damnable Shadowfell itself.

MI: I would guess that you are correct that it is a rivulet, and I expect it is drawn forth by some artefact. Would you agree?

MZW: I would agree, brother. I've never heard of an entrance to that accursed place that wasn't below ground. And we've both heard many stories of devices designed to draw forth elements of the Shadowfell, including the negative energy and environment to sustain undeath and worse. There must be such a device somewhere in the Academy, and, I would speculate, the Inn as well. Both will be overrun with shadow, death and undeath, and covetous of light and life, and a profane desire to snuff out both. But they seem confined to the walls of those places, which is unusual, except in cases where the device has been activated by...I'm not even sure I want to say the words out loud.

MI: You needn't, brother. We are both burdened with the knowledge of how these things are activated, and what price is asked. A pure soul has been taken. Perhaps several souls. But there is hope.

MZW: Hope remains, praise His Light! I will return with a host of Knights, descend to the foundations of those buildings, free the spirits, and burn away the shadow with His Light.

MI: You won't, brother. Sorry. Not you. Maybe none of us.

MZW: Not me? Maybe none of us? What is this talk?

MI: You are marked by shadow, brother. Your Faith faltered, and you fall for the tricks of our enemies again.

MZW: Again, brother?

MI: Yes, again, brother. I have already gotten word from the militia. You gave an account of what happened to Sgt. Birtch, rightly so. I know that you muddled the authority of Captain Broad with your doubt and fear, and your inability to see past the tricks of the enemy. You do so again now. You would rush back with Knights that we cannot spare from the front to face a still-largely-unknown enemy in ruin of small importance on the opposite side of the dale from our greatest threat. It is the same trick you fall for, but at a grander scale. We will pray for Him to wash this shadow from your mind and your heart, and we are assured, just as the sun will rise tomorrow, that He will see you bathed in light. But for the remainder of your night, you must take the rest you've earned.

MZW: I am not so sure...

MI: Your doubt in me is evidence of your infection, brother. Who is a closer friend to you? Are we not something greater than brothers in cloth alone? We now share a terrible knowledge together. I will bear this burden with you, but you will have to abandon doubt, and trust in me and Him. Your part in this is concluded, but for the filing of your report. Let us retire until Prime. My clerk shall keep vigil over you the remainder of this night.

MZW: ...I accept your words as wisdom and a kindness brother. Let us retire.

The Final Report

I write this conclusion some months after my return to Gribbons, and the time needed to recuperate from the ordeal in the shadow-plagued-academy. I am nightly haunted by the memory of that place, but Invictus assures me, as does the Bishop, that these memories are sacred and are a lesser burden than having them forgotten. I am assured of their correctness on the matter.

With the proper encouragement from my brothers and the patriarchs of our Church, I returned to the Yellow Abbeye to complete my report, facing the evidence of my failures for my own good, and recording them here so that others can be strengthened by them. The final part of my recovery has been spent in the Historic Wing, where I had the benefit of the materials needed to make a fulsome report on what I found at Thistle Ridge, and what others may one day face there.

Below I will account for what I encountered at Thistle Ridge in the plainest and fullest manner possible.

The Shadow Infestation

The Inn and the Academy we know have become infested with Shadow. This infestation is itself drawn directly to our world from the Shadowfell and has most of the same qualities. It is dark, and divine light does not work there. It fills the heart with foreboding and doubt, and prolonged exposure or a traumatic encounter can permanently affect us. The effects are sometimes visible to the naked eye, sometimes not, and sometimes, as in my unfortunate case, both.

Mundane sources of light shed about half as much light as they should, and fires and other similar sources of light and warmth tend to burn out quickly.

The motes of shadow and ash that hover in the air seem to be emitted from the nightmare-reflection of the landscape, which is itself a twisted and profane version of the material realm which it mocks. These motes are theorized to be the source of the infection which this realm can cause us, and they can travel through most materials with ease; they are not entirely physical, and covering one's mouth and nose doesn't seem to keep them out of the system.

Channeling the positive energy of Good deities there is muted by the nature of the place, being half as effective most of the time.

Although I lack the theology and cosmology to account fully for this, this shadow and the realm it is drawn from are built of a profane constituent material in opposition to our realm; it nurtures undeath and stifles life.

The infestation is not the Shadowfell itself, but a piece of it drawn to our realm. I speculate that there are one or more artefacts known as Tenebrius Ostium, which are the mechanism by which this infestation is facilitated. These artefacts are corrupted versions of ancient Netherese magic employed by the shadow-tainted and returned version of this civilization. They are activated through the sacrifice of a pure soul that has done an impure deed. I know of one such artefact that was deployed in Daerlun early in the Netherese campaign of aggression against the Sembians last century. In this instance, a Sembian mother was tricked into selling her daughter into slavery, and during the pain of her discovery of this, she was sacrificed to the artefact, which caused an infestation similar to those in Thistle Ridge.

The tortured spirit of the Daerlunian mother sustains the artefact and the infestation, which over time grew a vast number of undead (zombies and skeletons) which protected the infestation, and grew it with every failed attempt to cleanse it. To date, this infestation remains intact but contained by an elite faction of Silver Ravens.

It should be noted that neither myself nor any other author I have read on this matter knows what the 'artefacts' look like, or how they might be unmade. But I will speculate below.

The Denizens of Shadow

I personally encountered an undead shadow creature known as a Civis Tenebrarum. These are native to the Shadowfell, and often find their way into the darker corners of our realm, sneaking through naturally occurring black gates, or, in our case, through the Tenebrius Ostium. These creatures naturally revile the living and seek to turn them into shadowy versions of themselves through terror and violence. They are often created by the ritual that draws them and the shadow realm forth into ours and may indeed be the tortured spirits of the innocents that are sacrificed.

They have the following qualities which hunters of such should be aware;

They are impervious to all the usual assaults and maladies associated with a corporeal body, for they are not from our realm, and possess no corpus in their own. Magic weapons will damage them but are not as effective as they would be on flesh.

They are susceptible to magical Force, and Positive Energy, and those weapons that have been outfitted to damage the incorporeal, such as was the case with Shadow Eater.

If light can be deployed against them, they are greatly damaged by it, but in their shadowy lairs, as we know, light is difficult to wield, except by arcanists.

The claws of these shadows do not rend flesh, but rather the positive constituent stuff of which we are made. They also weaken us, and with enough blows, can draw forth a shadowy version of their victim which will attack us. Shadows drawn from victims are clumsy versions of their progenitor and lack the draining abilities of the Shadow that pulled them forth. However, when the victim dies, these clumsy versions are empowered by the parting soul, and become fully capable Civis Tenebraria.

The worse feature, I speculate, about these shadows is that they cannot be permanently slain in their lair. They may be temporarily destroyed, but are likely to return after a time, and continue to defend their lair. The only way of destroying them is to discover the means of their creation, and to undo, if possible, the events that activated the artefact. I think this will have the effect of destroying both the creature or creatures in the lair, as well as ending the infestation of shadow.

While we have no account of other sorts of undead in Thistle Ridge, I speculate that others could be found. Similar to the artefact deployed in Daerlun, people expiring in the infestation of shadow could become different sorts of undead. We have heard of zombies and skeletons so far, but others are possible or likely.

I will further speculate given the accounts I've heard personally, and the stories that have accumulated about the 'Ghosts' of Thistle Ridge, that there is more than one type of undead already there. The description given by the Oakbough girl fits perfectly with what undead taxonomists would label a 'Haunt'. In this case, the entity, again the victim of some scheme who's tortured soul powers some device, does not manifest as a Civis Tenebrarum, but a more 'classical' Haunt. These are enraged spirits that desire release from their undead prison, and are usually confined, as appears to be the case in Thistle Ridge, to a specific place or building. Indeed, you will have noticed that this is in keeping with the nature of the Tenebrius Ostium which created them, both being confined. Perhaps it is the confinement of the spirit that limits the shadow, rather than the other way round.

Haunts are normally ended by fixing the injustice that created them. This might be a difficult task in Thistle Ridge, where we are unsure of who exactly the spirits are, and how they were created. Uncertainty and doubt plagues every element of our understanding of what happened at Thistle Ridge, what is happening there now, and how to fix it.

Finally, I am obliged to mention the voice that we heard from the Merchants' Hall. This may have been some deception, or a mutual misunderstanding of a good many people who heard the voice, but I think not. Given that this building, too, is more or less fully intact, I expect it contains a third spirit and artefact, similar to the Academy and the Inn. However, I know if no account of anyone who's been closer to it than my expedition, and have but my speculation and a single terrifying howl to record here.

I am grateful for the opportunity provided by the Bishop to embark on this expedition, and for the time and resources needed to recover and craft as fulsome a report that my limited ability can produce. I have been instructed to leave ghosts and shadows to others once I retire my quill from this report, and hope earnestly that what I have laid down here will help whoever ventures next into the ruin of Thistle Ridge.

Mgt. Zoderick Winters

7 Hammer, 1446 DR
