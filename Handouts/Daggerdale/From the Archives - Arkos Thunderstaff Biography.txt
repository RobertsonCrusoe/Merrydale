Arkos "Falcon" Thunderstaff is the chronicle's great secular hero — a man without a god, without a cause beyond his own appetites and loyalties, who somehow ends up dying in the defense of a monastery he had no theological reason to care about, fighting a dragon by himself because it seemed like the thing to do. He is the most purely entertaining figure in the entire setting, and the most psychologically straightforward, and these two facts are deeply connected.

The Gentleman Rogue

The wiki introduction sets the tone perfectly: "Loved by the ladies, admired by the lords, few in Waterdeep haven't heard of the celebrated adventurer, brawler and gentleman entrepreneur." Where Garth Shanks is a self-made spymaster haunted by guilt, and Rychel Voordan is a professional whose competence has calcified into a kind of emotional armour, Falcon is simply vivid. He is a member of House Thunderstaff — Waterdhavian nobility — which means he comes from money and status, and this fact permeates everything about him. He is not scraping for survival; he is choosing adventure, choosing risk, choosing the road, because the alternative is boredom.

His primary trade is commerce. The extended internal monologue about buying Elturelian leather at 10gp, calculating customs levies in Iriaebor, weighing operating costs against the Waterdeep markup, considering a backhaul of Mirabar steel — this is the real Falcon. He is a man who sees the world as a vast arbitrage opportunity. He ran Thunderstaff Mercantile and Sundries with his partners Nathaniel Thorp and Valgard, plying the Sword Coast in swords and sundries. The out-of-character material about Valgard reveals the comic reality of Falcon's commercial life: his drinking companion and fellow nobleman ran up a thousand gold on Falcon's tab in a single night through gambling, whoring, and insisting on the best suite in the inn, at a moment when Falcon had sunk all his liquid capital into a weapons shipment and had about 20gp to his name.

He speaks Orcish. He has a personal insignia. He once got hit by a Sepia Snake Sigil and spent some indeterminate period as "the handsomest coat-rack in Amn." You're warned to watch your healing potions around him. He dreams of building an inn on the East Road, and fears he never will.

The Relationship with Maturin

Falcon met Maturin Horgath in Elmwood, and the two crossed the Realms together in an ultimately fruitless mission to prevent the resurrection of Bane. This friendship is the emotional anchor of everything that follows, and what makes it work is the absolute contrast between the two men. Maturin is the Chosen of Ilmater — a paladin of the god of suffering, a man whose entire spiritual vocation is the willing acceptance of pain on behalf of others. He is grey-haired, serious, driven by duty and faith. Falcon is none of these things. He is driven by profit, women, and the sheer entertainment value of being alive.

And yet Maturin specifically chose Falcon to accompany him on what became the journey to the Greypeak Mountains. Maturin had watched the inscrutable elf Steele be consumed by the darkness of the Waterdeep mission, and "deciding that Falcon could do with a change of scenery and that he could use a warrior of Falcon's mettle," he convinced his old friend to take to the road. Falcon agreed, "never missing out on an opportunity to investigate potential trade routes, to say nothing of playing the 'good friend'." That parenthetical qualification — "to say nothing of playing the 'good friend'" — is vintage Falcon. He frames even loyalty as a secondary benefit of a primarily commercial venture, because admitting to sentiment would be déclassé.

Murtaw's Watch: The Defining Episode

An orc army — the Red Hand of Doom — is bearing down on a crumbling outpost in Helgorod Valley. Maturin and Tremalkin, both paladins, conclude that the position is indefensible and that the rational course is to retreat. The defenders agree. Carts are being loaded. And then Falcon, who has been "wandering through the structure, his brow furrowed in thought," scowls, shakes his head, and says: "This won't do. This is a sure bet, lads and lasses."

The defenders' response is telling: "Whatever, you crazy sumbitch." They have no idea what he's about. But Maturin knows something: "Arkos Thunderstaff never makes a bet he can't win — or fight his way out of."

He walks out alone, whistles to get the orcs' attention, shouts something in Orcish, and when their delegation approaches, he offers them the chance to surrender. "Do you find these terms acceptable?" he asks with a smug smile. The orcs fall on him in fury. He kills all ten of them, wipes his blade on one of their furs, and shouts over his shoulder as he walks back: "Perhaps on the 'morrow then!?"

This establishes the pattern for the next nine days. Every morning, Falcon goes out, offers the enemy surrender, fights thirty warriors, comes back beaten and bloody, and tells the exhausted garrison: "Any day now... they're more scared of us than we are of them. Any day now, lads."

The cyclops episode is the apex. On the ninth day, a cyclops smashes through the defences. Falcon sprints across a crumbling stone span, throws himself through the air holding his sword like a javelin, and plunges it into the cyclops's eye, disappearing in a cloud of fluid and blood. The thing falls on top of him. He survives.

The Dragon, and the Death

The final chapter comes at the Monastery of the Adamant Hearth, where Falcon has visited twice during the long years that Maturin spent trying to save Tremalkin from the corruption of the Sharran blade. When the Sharrans come demanding Tremalkin's return and a priestess delivers her ultimatum, it is Falcon — not Maturin, not the dwarven defenders — who responds: "He's not here... and even if he were, we would not be handing him over to the likes of you or your little friends over there." Then, looking up at a distant speck in the sky: "Nothing to fret about... and... what... is... that?" Tremalkin, without looking up, answers flatly: "It's a dragon."

When the battle begins, Falcon and Maturin are fighting back to back — the trader and the saint, the secular and the sacred. Falcon has just beheaded a Sharran warrior with a backswing when he turns and delivers what may be the most perfectly Falcon line in the entire chronicle:

"I should be able to handle the dragon. You take the rest."

He says this to a man facing twenty armoured warriors. He says it about a blue dragon. And then he fights the thing alone, closing with it again and again, opening over twenty wounds in its sapphire scales, until he manages to drive half the length of his sword into its neck. The dragon tears free, ripping the weapon from Falcon's hands, and its tail strikes him twice — once hammering him into the ground, once sweeping him through the air into the darkness.

Arkos "Falcon" Thunderstaff was found near the outer wall. His neck had been twisted at a frightful angle by the tail of the blue dragon he had fought single-handedly and driven off.

He drove it off. He didn't kill it, and it killed him, but he drove it off. The dragon fled. The man who had no god, no sacred mission, no higher calling than profit and pleasure and friendship, fought a dragon alone and won the only victory that mattered — the monastery stood.

The Psychology Beneath the Smile

Consider: before leaving Waterdeep, he was heard muttering, "If I hear one more thing about a thrice-cursed beholder, bloody drow or filthy Zhents... I'll burn this city down, myself." Maturin recognized in this the same corrosive exhaustion that had consumed Steele. Falcon was not immune to despair; he just expressed it as irritation rather than madness.

And then there's the matter of the inn on the East Road. "To Falcon's regret — one of many, it must be said — he fears he will never build that inn on the East Road. Still, who knows?" That parenthetical — "one of many, it must be said" — is the only moment in the entire body of Falcon material where anyone suggests that he carries regrets. The inn is his version of Garth's dead mother, of Rychel's unnamed grief — the dream deferred that tells you the man has an interior life he doesn't advertise.

What Falcon represents, psychologically, is the radical decision to meet a world of horror and corruption with panache. Not faith — that's Maturin. Not professionalism — that's Rychel. Not control — that's Garth. Panache. The belief that if you must die, you should do it with a smile and a good line, and that if you must fight a dragon, you should volunteer for the job and let the paladin handle the merely human enemies.

The chronicle confirms this reading in the way it remembers him. The three heroes of the Monastery of Sundered Faith are known as The Grey, The Black, and The Bold. Maturin is the Grey — suffering, endurance, sainthood. Tremalkin is the Black — dark faith, torment, self-immolation. Falcon is the Bold. Not the Brave, not the Strong, not the Good. The Bold. It's a word that describes a way of being in the world — forward, unapologetic, confident even in the face of impossibility.

He never built that inn.
