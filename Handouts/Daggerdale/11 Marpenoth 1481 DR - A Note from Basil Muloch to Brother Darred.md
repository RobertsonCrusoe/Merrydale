A note from Basil Muloch to Brother Darred, a copy of which was given to Nathan Gale

11 Marpenoth, 1481


Br. Darred;

I send this correspondence in haste, and beg your forgiveness for abandoning the usual formalities of our all-too-infrequent epistles.

I have uncovered, I think, and through no great effort of my own, but rather through what might be interpreted as lucky happenstance in one instance, and perhaps divine intervention in the other, a fiendish plot in our otherwise sleepy and, heretofore thankfully uneventful little towne.

I enclose for your purposes, and would deign to impose upon you for your expertise in the matter and roundly-acknowledged puissance in translations, a copy of what I suspect is a copy of a most malign and certainly devilish bit of script that I don’t doubt is some way related to that nasty business up in Dwimmerholme, that is so vile that even within our news-resilient walls, such terrible stories have violated the talk, and no doubt the dreams, of our erstwhile uninvolved citizenry.

The means by which I came upon, or rather, had thrust upon me in a most assuredly unexpected and certainly unwelcomed and completely unsolicited manner, such a terrible and, I would dare say, Mephistophelian bit of text, I shall spare you, and thereby spare the delicate and, I would hazard to wager, innocent, conduit through which this missive found me.  Suffice it to say, that intentions in the hearts and minds of all parties that I have first-hand knowledge of in this diabolical affair, are pure and well-meaning, unless I am greatly fooled in this manner.  I think I am not, and will rest upon my reputation with you and your organization, and leave it, succinctly, at that, kind friend.

So, you have now my copy, crude though it most certainly is, because I have never mastered the nuances of the hated language in which this even-more-hated script is written, and I beg that you will complete, and where necessary, and certainly this will be in myriad places that I haven’t your wits to identify, correct me, for our mutual benefit.  I’m afraid, sir, that I have the general meaning of the thing, and this, combined with some very unsavoury recent events in what I have already described as an otherwise sleepy and, heretofore uneventful little town, and this combined with what black rumours trickle from Dwimmerholme, and with the cosmos being what they have been this last moon, suggests to me that there is a plot to equal that of the unfortunate Dwimmerholme in my very sleepy, uneventful little towne.

Kindly sir, put any doubts I have to rest, and do not spare my sensitivities.  I would have the truth of the matter, and sharpish if you can spare the time and consideration for your most indebted correspondent, whose fair weather rescriptions are not to be excused, but certainly admonished after these bitter affairs are put in order.

Indebted,

B.Muloch.}}
