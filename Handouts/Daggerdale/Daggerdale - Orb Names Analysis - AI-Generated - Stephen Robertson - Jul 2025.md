So, upon rereading this handout, I got curious about the names of these orbs. Naturally, I was interested to read what the Omnibot had to say. Quite an illuminating interpretation, I have to say:


1. VORTELLIUS — The Least of the Three

   Linguistic cues: The name Vortellius evokes words like vortex or vorticity, suggesting chaotic movement, sucking forces, or maelstroms. Accordingly, it evokes inescapability — not just chaos but being drawn in and consumed. That's more fitting with the orb's use in spawning dragon-troll hybrids, which are not simply chaotic but irrevocably transformed. The name doesn't just signal disturbance; it hints at entropic finality. The suffix -ellius softens it, perhaps implying diminished grandeur or a lesser status.

   Narrative role: It was used by the Black Druid Deadnettle to empower a grotesque hybrid of troll and dragon — suggesting mutation, corruption, and violence. Its removal required brutal sacrifice (cut from Thickwickle's arms by Brother Dretch), further underlining its dark influence.

   Symbolism: Represents corruptive potential, possibly akin to an introductory or early-stage corruption artifact. The "least" not in danger, but still unholy.


2. VARROS — Middling Power

   Linguistic cues: Varros sounds close to "varus," Latin for "bent" or "crooked." It evokes a sense of twist, warping, or unreliability. The double 'r' gives it a sense of force or severity.

   Narrative role: This orb was surrendered by a changeling (a being of shifting identity), and it is damaged — cracked, glowing erratically, its brass bands "riven." Its chaotic state reflects instability and fragmented power.

   Symbolism: Suggests dangerous volatility — a corrupted thing with lingering power but no longer whole. Its origin via changeling and surrender ties it to deception, ambiguity, and ambiguity of allegiance.


3. VERINIUS — The Greatest

   Linguistic cues: Verinius is evocative of veritas (truth), verity, or vernal (spring, renewal). The name sounds noble, Roman, and final. It implies clarity, authority, and completion.

   Narrative role: It was recovered at the climactic Saints' Battle and handled with the utmost care — transported overland by knights and rangers, warned against by a seer. But now it's missing, introducing a dire mystery. The name does not imply a benevolent truth, but a false verity: one used by those who would seek to sanctify their own dominion. The fact that it was last held by Belladonna and retrieved after a priestess of Shar was arrested suggests a polar ambiguity. Verinius might be "the greatest" precisely because it can veil truth as easily as it reveals it.

   Symbolism: It is power perfected, the apex of the trio. Its theft represents a cosmic imbalance, a potentially apocalyptic loss. The narrative gives it weight by associating it with final victories, prophetic warnings, and elite handling.


COLLECTIVE IMPLICATION

The trio functions almost like a trinity of forbidden power — a perverse inverse of holy relics:

Vortellius (Least) — "Vortex" + Latinate suffix — Corruptive object used to breed abominations — Chaos, mutation

Varros (Middle) — Crooked, warped (from Latin varus) — Surrendered, cracked, destabilised artefact — Instability, ambiguity

Verinius (Greatest) — Veritas, truth, strength — Powerful relic tied to saintly battle, now missing — Authority, loss, dread

The juxtaposition of names and function — chaos (Vortellius), compromise (Varros), and concealed truth (Verinius) — sets up a compelling triad. But note: Verinius isn't necessarily about betrayal; its disappearance suggests not corruption but denial of revelation, or a power deemed too volatile to remain contained.

The suffixes of the names are highly suggestive:
  -ellius: Diminutive or derivative. Suggests a lower tier artifact or a later addition.
  -ros: A harder, more stable structure. Suggests durability or central placement in a triptych.
  -inius: Echoes Roman imperial naming (e.g., Hadrianus, Justiniani, Severinus). This suffix connotes finality, authority, or highest dignity.

The progression from Vortellius to Verinius mirrors a crescendo of gravity, and their uses mark a thematic journey from corruption through compromise to lost sanctity.


CONCLUDING THOUGHTS

These names are not only evocative but operate as narrative ciphers, encoding the tone and mythos of each relic. Vortellius speaks of chaos made flesh, Varros of compromised power, and Verinius of a truth or force once held but now lost — or denied. The orb-names, when viewed together, chart a theological arc from fallen creation to unstable redemption, and finally to vanished salvation. This also aligns with the structure of the Historic Wing, whose guardians (each with one dominant sense) and rites suggest a ritualized confrontation with forbidden knowledge.

The orbs feel like counter-sacraments. Their very naming sets them in opposition to divine order — "tools of the Enemy," yes, but also mirrors of human frailty and the limits of containment. That Verinius is now missing should rightly chill the reader.
