THAGDUL — A PROFILE

The Epigraph Problem

Thagdul's wiki entry opens with a quote from Robert E. Howard's The Dark Man: "And about the table where stood the Dark Man, immovable as a mountain, washed the red waves of slaughter." That literary framing tells you almost everything you need to know about the character's narrative function: he is a figure of obscurity, power, and uncertain allegiance, around whom violence and conspiracy orbit. The mystery, by design, is never fully resolved.

TRUE IDENTITY: UNKNOWN

The man who operates under the name "Thagdul" is not, by his own account, actually Thagdul. He claimed to have killed the real Thagdul — a Zhentarim mage — years ago in Zhentil Keep and assumed his identity. His true name has never been revealed. He is categorized simultaneously as a Harper, a Red Herring, a Drurginz operative, and a Zhentarim agent, and is listed as "Wanted by the Wanderer" — a catch-all for figures whose accounts remain unsettled.

The chronicler Grafton Fitzdecker summarizes the problem neatly: "Company Drurginz — led by the wizard Thagdul, who claimed that he and the company were all a front for the Harpers. This has never been proven or disproved to my satisfaction."

ROLE AND OPERATIONS IN WATERDEEP (1371–1372 DR)

Thagdul first appears in the chronicle as the factor — the operational director — of the Drurginz Trading Company in Waterdeep. Drurginz was described as a "semi-legit operation with connections to the Moonsea region" that "probably ran contraband through Skullport more often than not." In the Zhentarim power structure, Company Drurginz was believed to be an arm of Manshoon, set against Company Ersgul, which served Fzoul Chembryl. The drow of Company Dalael suspected Drurginz of having "connections to one known as Zantiid" — the purple-eyed beholder.

Thagdul's method of recruitment was coercion. In early 1371, a group of petty rogues and adventurers — Garth Shanks, Ellyth, Esperel, Tomas Quoyle, and the bladesinger Gilthanas — botched a heist of cargo that turned out to belong to Drurginz (eight blades of "wicked-forging," shipped from Helmfast warehouses via the cog The Misty). Thagdul tracked them to Garth's house in Dock Ward, sent his lieutenant Teskul to neutralize Esperel on the roof, then kicked in the door with four thugs. Behind him, in the doorway, stood something that took the fight out of even these reckless souls: the purple-eyed beholder Zantiid, or an apparition of it. Ellyth saw it clearly. No one resisted.

Thagdul leveraged their debt ruthlessly. In exchange for not destroying them, the five would run "errands" for Drurginz — corporate sabotage missions against Ersgul. Gilthanas alone refused the arrangement. "No one ever proved that Gilthanas' death had anything to do with the Zhents," the chronicle records dryly, "but I'm a druid if it didn't."

The wizard Jafar — ostensibly a Calishite scholar but in truth a Moonsea man, likely from Zhentil Keep itself — also fell under Thagdul's influence, and their relationship proved to be deeper and more sinister than that of master and hired muscle. Intercepted notes reveal Jafar addressing Thagdul as "Master," running errands to locate Xulla's lair, eliminate Teskul, and acquire beholder staves on Thagdul's behalf. Jafar even expressed a desire to "meet Zantiid soon." When Jafar eventually betrayed the Company of the Red Herring, it was under Thagdul and Zantiid's influence — not Ersgul's.

THE MISSIONS

Thagdul deployed the Company of the Red Herring on increasingly dangerous operations throughout the summer of 1371:

The North Ward Inferno (10 Mirtul): Thagdul's first assignment was to burn down a building in North Ward — in fact a Dalael safehouse. He provided detonation devices and a map, and arranged a "diversion" that included setting fire to portions of Dock Ward, which infuriated Tomas, whose friends lived there. The party recovered drow writings, jade, golem components, and excerpts from the Draconomicon from the building before it was destroyed.

The Southern Star Rescue: Thagdul briefed the Red Herrings on an Ersgul ambush planned against two survivors of a disastrous Chultan expedition. He wanted six vials of "grey liquid" from their cargo. The mission was framed as humanitarian — saving men from murder — but Thagdul was candid that his motives were self-interested. "He was not doing this out of the goodness of his own heart."

The Reprobates Campaign: When the Reprobates — an Ersgul enforcement gang led by the half-orc Gul'dan — caused problems for Drurginz, Thagdul put a bounty on Gul'dan's head and sent Tomas after them.

Throughout these operations, Thagdul displayed a distinctive management style: calm, calculating, and manipulative. He used detect magic on Garth while pretending to probe his mind with a more powerful spell — a bluff that Ellyth and Jafar recognized but said nothing about. He silenced Ellyth's questioning "with a wave." He maintained thorough intelligence on all his assets through Teskul, who built dossiers on each Red Herring member. Teskul's features were noted to be "quite similar" to Thagdul's — possibly from the same region of the Moonsea.

THE HARPER REVELATION (Harvestide, 1371)

The turning point came during the Harvestide celebrations. Company Ersgul, through Yarkul, attempted to flip the Red Herrings' allegiance. The Company refused. Thagdul, "visibly heartened," then arranged a gathering of the Red Herrings with a new group of heroes — the Companions from Neverwinter, who had been pursuing the Zhentarim plot behind the murders of Harpers Zaltim, Laurel Dawn, and Ansal Bloodshoulder.

To both groups, Thagdul revealed that he was "in truth, a Harper agent." He had assumed the identity of a Zhentarim mage he killed in Zhentil Keep, run a convincing front using "bona-fide Zhents" in his organization, and used the Company of the Red Herring as unwitting instruments of a long-term strategy against the Zhentarim. He explained that his cover had to be utterly convincing, and that he had "conscripted help from virtuous heroes, with the long-view in his mind's eye."

The reaction was mixed. "Jafar seemed convinced," the chronicle notes, "but many other of the Company members, and even the companions — who had intercepted a note saying as much — continued to harbour their own private doubts." The note in question was found on the corpse of the Zhentarim wizard Gilth'zan, addressed to someone named "Belduran," and written by Ansal Bloodshoulder himself: "You should seek out our colleague Thagdul, who operates a company called Drurginz in the Dockward of the City. This comrade and one-time partner of mine has been warring with the Zhents in a most clever guise, for quite some time."

This is the strongest external corroboration of Thagdul's Harper claim — a letter from a known, murdered Harper to another Harper, naming Thagdul as a colleague. But it was written before the events of 1371, and doesn't account for the possibility that Bloodshoulder was himself deceived.

After this revelation, Thagdul "proposed that the Red Herrings be 'snipped from his apron strings' and set to their own devices." He had served his purpose with them.

DEPARTURE AND THE EASTERN HEARTLANDS

Thagdul left Waterdeep for business in the Eastern Heartlands before the Phaulkonmere Council of Greengrass 1372 — conspicuously avoiding a meeting where the last Harper in Waterdeep, Agamemnon, was present. At that council, Agamemnon revealed that "whatever the truth of the man by the same name who claimed to be a Harper in disguise, the 'original' Thagdul had been a servant of Manshoon in Waterdeep." Jafar had the audacity to present himself as Thagdul's "representative," which did nothing for the Red Herrings' opinion of either man.

Thagdul's next confirmed stomping ground was Yulash, in the Moonsea region. Yarkul — who had been installed by the Zhents as Lord of Daggerdale — was revealed to have been Thagdul's vassal, "ruling only at Thagdul's behest." Thagdul was described as "an ally of the Cyricists" operating out of Yulash.

THE BEHOLDER STAFF

Thagdul carried one of three known Beholder Staves — powerful anti-divination artifacts that could only be recharged by beholders, and which also served as conduits for long-distance communication with them. Thagdul's staff was the largest: seven feet tall with eight eyestalks. It had rendered him immune to scrying throughout the entire chronicle.

In Mirtul of 1373, Beljuril of Azuth successfully scried on Thagdul for the first time — suggesting the staff had been removed or boxed. The vision showed Thagdul "riding hard, through dusty farmland, with two companies of Fist mercenaries (about thirty in all) about him," headed northbound on the Trade Way toward Waterdeep. Beside him was "a packhorse carrying a large lead box, big enough for a Staff." When a lieutenant approached, Thagdul raised a hand to silence him — possibly sensing the scrying.

On 1 Tarsakh 1373, an intercepted letter from Thagdul to Jafar — found in Zelbross by the ranger Durnax Vorn — read simply: "I have learned a terrible secret. Our other means of communicating have been compromised. I am returning from Yulash now. Do not use the staff anymore."

What the "terrible secret" was remains one of the chronicle's great unsolved mysteries. Two theories were advanced: that Thagdul had lost his staff to a superior (perhaps Manshoon), signaling demotion; or that he had realized the staves themselves were compromised and deliberately boxed his own.

THAGDUL AND THE BEHOLDERS

The relationship between Thagdul and the beholders is the deepest layer of mystery. The drow Y'morth Dalael stated bluntly that "the Beholder Zantiid used to manipulate a mage named Thagdul." The paladin Maturin Horgath's analysis went further: "I believe that Jafar is acting as an agent for Thagdul, and thus for Zantiid — not Xulla."

Zantiid — the purple-eyed beholder — was Thagdul's apparent patron, possibly his master. It was Zantiid (or its image) that appeared behind Thagdul when he first intimidated the Red Herrings into service. Zantiid was suspected to be an ally of Manshoon, while the rival beholder Xulla was aligned with Fzoul through Ersgul.

This makes the Harper claim particularly problematic. If Thagdul was truly a Harper, he was a Harper who walked with a beholder at his back, commanded genuine Zhent operatives, ran contraband through Skullport, and maintained a relationship with one of the most dangerous aberrations in the Realms. If he was not a Harper, then Ansal Bloodshoulder — a known and murdered Harper — was deceived into endorsing him.

The warrior Maturin captured the paradox precisely: "It is clear to me that he is a Zhent... yet, it seems as though it was he who saved Sparrow from his folly in Dagger Falls."

SPARROW AND DAGGER FALLS

After the Company of the Eastern Storm liberated Dagger Falls in late 1372, Sparrow (Yarron Forrestar) assassinated Yarkul but was then overpowered by an unknown party, had his hand struck off, and was exiled from the city. The chronicle suggests it was Thagdul — or agents of his — who both spared Sparrow's life and imposed the exile. Maturin's comment that Thagdul "saved Sparrow from his folly" and the Warrior's Debate entry asking whether a known Harper list contained Thagdul's name both point to a figure operating on the margins of both sides.

THE LEGACY: GORIMDUN MINE AND TETHYAMAR (1479–1480 DR)

Thagdul's most astonishing legacy is his connection to the discovery of Gorimdun Mine — the lost front entrance to Tethyamar. In the 1370s, the Zhents under Thagdul's direction excavated and located the ancient gates, then kept the discovery secret. A century later, the Shadar-kai agent Deadnettle exploited the same mine, using it as a base for her Black Troll colony.

Administrator Alen Reks confirmed the connection directly: when pressed by Grafton Fitzdecker about who found Gorimdun, Reks replied: "yes, now that you've insisted, it was Thagdul, and he found Gorimdun, and that's where Deadnettle decided to place her monster-spawning horror." Fitzdecker's own editorial note drives it home: "the wizard Thagdul appears again. Only in name and legend, but his hand still seems to be at work in the dale after all this time."

Curator Loki Mouvranesh's letter to Deadnettle confirms that the Netherese knew about the mine and were desperate to keep it secret from the Tethyamar League: "I am sure that the Nefal'im are living in the old Zhent mine at the base of Guzzinhelm, or Gorimdun in the dwarven." The White Cloaks eventually discovered the mine, destroyed the trolls, and revealed the gates — triggering the Tethyamar League's expedition. All of it traces back to Thagdul's original excavation a century prior.

THE QUESTION OF GARTH'S THEORY

The most penetrating analysis of Thagdul's motivations comes from the player narrative "Who Stole the Heart of the Earth?" — likely written by Garth Shanks or a close associate. The argument is that Thagdul orchestrated the entire Heart of the Earth affair from the beginning: arranging through Teskul for the Red Herrings to acquire the gem, then using it to guarantee their involvement in the Hunabar staff affair, which pitted them against Ersgul's drow allies. The conclusion: "Both explanations lead me to the conclusion that Thagdul knew all along about the Heart of the Earth, and used it to guarantee the involvement of the Red Herrings in the affairs of the Hunabars."

Whether this was Harper strategy or beholder manipulation depends entirely on which version of Thagdul you believe.

CURRENT STATUS

"I'm sure he's dead," Alen Reks told Grafton Fitzdecker, "but there's no body. There so often isn't with these wizards, don't you agree?"

Thagdul has not been seen since leaving Waterdeep for the Eastern Heartlands before Greengrass of 1372. He was last scried riding hard toward Waterdeep with Fist mercenaries and a lead box in Mirtul of 1373. His intercepted letter spoke of a "terrible secret." After that: silence. A century later, his discoveries are still shaping the politics of Daggerdale.

Grafton Fitzdecker's final assessment: "We have not heard the last of Xulla, or the Dalael, maybe not even of Thagdul, though he was but a mortal man."

The chronicle leaves that "though" doing a tremendous amount of heavy lifting.
