It accounts for the death of three heroes in one fell swoop, a terrible sacrifice that was made to cripple the power of the Sons. Nothing else left to say except 'read on'

Administrator: 
I am overdue putting this report together. We have suffered great losses, and the last three days have been a blur of travel through unseasonal blizzards. I am nestled warmly again in Arwencroft with what remains of the team and will head out first thing tomorrow morning for Dagger Falls and the Close. 

Please forgive the brief nature of this report; I will be summarizing several days of fighting and exploration all while battling a hideous head-injury that I fear has quite rattled my senses. On with it 
23 Marpenoth 
The morning started with a pitched battle between Gale and me. He insisted that he knew exactly where Gideon was headed. I agreed with him but wanted to take another day to provision properly for the journey and the battles we knew we'd have with devils, orcs and sorcerers. Shea and Abigail agreed with me, and although this isn't a democracy, I was glad of their support. Artorias was ready to charge off with Gale, saying that he had everything he needed, except a devil to destroy. 

We did procure and order up the supplies and weapons we'd need, to the best of our reconning, and made arrangements to leave on the 24th, as early as possible. We made fast friends with Sir Simon Whitmore, who would accompany us along with a half-dozen riders to Fergy's Hole. I half-expected Gale and Artorias to be gone when I got back to Arwencroft, but to their credit, they waited for us. My patience as a leader will require more effort in the future. 
24 Marpenoth 
Set out in terrible weather, rain and sleet. We were aware of scouts following us on the opposite side of the Charnel River but going after them would be a distraction. From a distance, it looked like a single man or man-sized thing. We were set-upon by some orcs just on the other side of Bridgehead, and the wounds we suffered caused us to turn back to that small hamlet and rest for the night. 
25 Marpenoth 
Terrible weather again today. I think there might be some sorcery at work here, trying to keep us from our quarry, but I'm told I'm getting paranoid (though Gale says I'm not paranoid enough). We fought a terrible fiendish-ogre on a bridge today, and two of Whitmore's men were killed by thrown-spears from the creature. We destroyed it in the end, though at great cost to our resources; the thing required magic and silver to harm, and it did a great deal of damage to many of us. Artorias says that this creature likely represents a full soul-coin from Bel's Bargain. I wondered out loud how many such terrible foes we'd face in the days to come. "At least twelve" was Artorias' answer. 

By night we made it to Fergy's Hole. This is a fairly austere camp of about a hundred people, or was a hundred people. These are the families of the miners, and with half of the miners killed last year in the "collapse", and the closure of the mine, this village lacks purpose and morale. To make matters worse, they were sorely treated by Dretch several days before we arrived. Here are some details as I recall them; 
Fergy's Hole 
This is a settlement of 90 people, mostly working men, but a few families and unattached women. Architecturally, it could be described as a semilunar stone fort against the side of the mountain. 

However, it is not typical of other forts in the region. The walls are lower than forts found all around Daggerdale, and they haven't been constructed with too much care; many blocks are set about by crumbling mortar, and the odd block has tumbled to the ground. Despite this, there are still wrought- iron embellishments atop the walls, and well-crafted and decorative wrought-iron lamps line both sides of the wall. 

With Sir Simon leading the way, we were lead through the single gate of the fort without too much difficulty, but the guards all eyed us suspiciously. As we entered the courtyard, we were noticed by 

many people bustling about wrapping up their duties at the end of the workday. Most of them shot icy glares at us, and some actually headed indoors at the sight of us. 

"It's your colleague, Mr. Dretch", a good man named Sheriff Harrison explained. "He put a proper scare into all of us, 'e did. Now, Sir Simon 'ere vouches for you, but you better be on your best behaviour, ladsand ladiesyou don't have friends here, and none of you looks quite so scary as Mr. Dretch. Come with me" 

He lead us to a small building in one corner of the courtyard. It is a single-story stone building in a dreadful state of repair. The roof has many gaping holes in it, and there are no furnishings to speak of. "You can bunk 'ere for the night, 'Cloaks. But best be on your way first thing in the morning." 

"I'm dread sorry about the attitude of these men, Paros." Simon began. "We don't take to strangers much in Charity, and the mining outposts are worse than the rest of us. With the visit that Mr. Dretch paid a while ago, I don't think you can expect better treatment than this. I have some wounded men to tend to, and some supplies to deliver to these men. I'll check in with you later." 
Emma Cole 
This is a young woman of about sixteen years who visited us in our humble lodge late in the evening. She has long, curly brown hair that frames a dainty face. Her eyes are bright, and her expression firm and confident. She was wearing a dirty, kitchen-stained garb, but her hands were clean, and her clothing in good repair. "Emma Cole, Sirs. Pleased to make your 'quaintance." The girl speaks confidently and looks us in the eyes when she talks. 

"I know everyone 'round 'ere 'ates you, and is afraid. Mr. Dretch asked some very 'ard questions, and 'e actually drew on the Sheriff. But I'm not afraid of 'im, and certainly not you. Dretch was kind to me, 'cause I was honest with 'em. 'E asked about the 'cave in' up at the old Saltpetre mine, and I told him I never thought it was an accident. There's none so careful as the Saltepetre miners. I know, 'cause my da was one. He was one o' them as vanished in that mine. I went there a few months ago. I got a proper punishment for it, I did, but I seen that mine, and it didn't look caved in to me. I also thought I seen a man walking around down in the 'ole, but to be honest with you too, the man scared me proper, and I run all the way 'ome, I did. 

"I think those men are down there. I think something really evil happened, and I think you're 'ere to fix it. I don't know why Mr. Dretch never came back, though" 
26 Marpenoth 
Early in the morning, the Sheriff came to visit us. He had been given some papers to read by Whitmore, and was now properly up to date on happenings in Charity. 

"I may have been a little 'ard on you lot. I didn't realize what was going on in the rest of Charity. I still don't think there can be a proper accounting for that Dretch, but you lot can come and go as you will from my fort. 

"I would really caution you against going any further west of 'ere; there's a lot of bad road between 'ere and the old mine. And it has been standard procedure to caution all people from the mine. But Sir Simon comes with orders to help you as we can, and if you aim to head to the mine, we won't stop you, but just tell you to be careful." 

We would need more than a little care, as this is as far as Whitmore was authorized to take us. For all that, he still rode with us for a few miles beyond his charge, to make sure we were on the right track. We found ourselves missing his blades before the day was over. 

This day was the first of three very hard days, and I wish now we had turned back with Whitmore. 

With the weather as it was, we didn't make the mine until nearly evening, and found the area guarded by two cave giants. We weren't sure that these were part of the coven at the time, but surely they must have been. How they were enticed into the service of the Sons we never learned. Anyway, they broke three sets of ribs in our company, and left us in a terrible state for the next encounter. 

We did indeed enter the mines, and found no evidence at all of a cave-in. In fact, it was clear to us that many people had been coming and going from the mine for days, weeks, possibly months. In one of the top-most chambres of the mine we fought with a group of fiendish orcs, this time an orcish sorcerer was with them, and though we had girded ourselves against flame (knowing our foes and having prepared), she attacked us with lightning, sensing our strengths and weaknesses. We killed half of them and sent the other half fleeing into the depths of the mines.

It was at this point that I started to think about our brothers in Tethyamar, and the War in the Deep. It is dangerous business, and I don't envy the captain and his men their charge, though I'm ashamed to admit it. 

Rather than risk the mine, we camped for the night outside a little distance away. We could not keep a fire going, and were cold, wet and sore the next morning. 
27 Marpenoth 
This was a day of searching the mines and skirmishing with various orcs and other vile vermin. Fighting all day in the maze of tunnels was tiring work, but we prevailed each time, though it was taking its toll at this point. We were out of healing potions and other restorative magics, and we had a very heated discussion about turning back. We decided to stick it out, as at one point in the day, Artorias noticed something in the mine; a wall with Netherese carvings on it. Ardelusk and his brother had been looking for Tethyamar all those years in this region. They nearly found an old door to Netheril and more. 

Tonight we decided to brave sleeping in the mine, rather than face another night of cold and rain. We regretted it, as we were set upon all night by small skirmishes, designed to keep us tired. We would set out the next day unrested and unprepared. We should have turned back 
28 Marpenoth – Kahlmadrid and Tragedy 
We went to the Netherese wall and found that it had arcane scrawlings about it that suggested what we'd find on the other side; an ancient Netherese summoning chambre. Artorias and Abigail set about 

investigating the wall and the markings and discovered a very well-hidden message carved on the wall. With a source of light held down low to the ground (as only someone searching would notice), there was a barely-perceptible warning carved on the door; "Turn back, you fools! -AD" 

Dretch had been here and left a warning for us. Unfortunately, it wasn't enough to convince Gale and Artorias, though we argued about it for an hour. In the end, Abigail opened the door with the proper incantation, and we went into the chambres beyond, which we now know to be the ruins of Kahlmadrid, of ancient Netherese origin. We had read of this chambre in Abandon Hope, and how a renegade- branch of Netherese summoners cavorted with and summoned devils of old. 

The chambres beyond were a horror show of terrible devils, derelicts, and fiendish orcs. Tired before we began, we pressed through battle after battle, before coming to the central chambre of Kahlmadrid. There, we found Niles Gideon, a dozen soldiers all well armed and prepared, and the missing guildmasters, Keystone and Silver. With them were a couple of the lesser devils we turned back in previous chambres. 

This battle was the most difficult we faced as a team, but we pulled through, largely due to the magics of Abigail, who displayed a great deal more efficacy in dispatching groups than we'd seen before. In the end, we managed to capture Gideon and a few of his men, and slew the rest. No one and nothing escaped from us. Our greatest discovery was in Gideon's bags; the Itineris Diabolus was on his person, and Gale and Artorias, even the others, were astonished to find it. 

But our real battle was still ahead of us. In the central chambre we found a great summoning portal, upon which was carved a complicated puzzle. We could see that the cultists from Charity had been trying to open the portal but had been unsuccessful. This is when everything went wrong 

Gale recognized the puzzle as being the back of cover of the Itineris Diabolus, and looking closely, he could see that the book itself contained the 'key' to the puzzle, which was a simple order in which various symbols needed to be activated on the portal. We might have gathered up our prisoners and left, disregarding the door, the book and the key, but this is where Gideon played his final card. 

"Wouldn't you like to end all of this, heroes?" he inquired bitterly. "Do you know what's on the other side of that door? Why, it's my master, Azbael. He is waiting for us to open this portal, so that he may come here without the restrictions of the currency of souls; he would be completely free to stay, to roam, to dowhatever he wishedBut you are too frightened to open that door and face him, aren't you?" 

This turned into a heated argument among all of us. Gale wanted to open the portal and face Azbael. I think he wanted to face down Avernus, as he had been doing in his dreams since Dwimmerholme, but I won't guess at his motivations. In the end, we decided that we would camp out in the chambre and decide in the morning what to do. We had secured many of the derelicts as well as we could and had our other prisoners besides. We set watches, as the entire situation was precarious. 

It is at this point that I should own my failure of leadership. We should have marched out of that place, and into the cold fresh air of the mountains. But I was tired and my head throbbed from days of being bashed about, and fighting with my team. I relented, and allowed for camping in a stupid spot. 

On third watch I placed Gale and Shea. I knew that Shea was opposed to opening the portal and gave her instructions to wake me if Gale got up to anything. However, Gideon managed to fool us all; he put Shea to sleep with sorcery, and then convinced Gale to open the portal. I shall never hear the words 'and all hell broke loose', and not think of that moment, or what that really means. For the room became nearly too hot to breathe of a sudden, and a horrible wailing wind cut through everything, waking and terrifying us all. I saw Gale enter the portal; where once there had been a great stone, now there was a shimmering doorway to Avernus, and many devils waiting on the other side. I saw the hideous face of Azbael, as it smiled grotesquely at Gale. 

At that moment, Abigail and Shea (who had been woken by the burning, screaming wind) raced into the portal to come to Gale's aid. They began a desperate battle with Azbael and a dozen other devils, while Artorias, myself and Imogen tried to organize a defense. Already, devils were starting to approach the portal, and Artorias held them back, screaming for the rest of the team to retreat to safety. "The only safety" I heard Gale say "will come with Azbael dead!" 

While Artorias held the door, Imogen was now caught up dealing with our prisoners, who were doing their best to attempt an escape, and to free the derelicts from the next room in which we had them secured. A couple of times I tried to enter the portal, but Artorias checked my advance; "this portal will not stay open long, boss" he yelled "and we must keep it that way! Hold the door with me and pray for our friends". 

But it was hopeless; though Artorias and I held the door, beating back lesser devils of a dozen varieties, we could see our three friends through the chaos; they fought now, back-to-back on a blood-red plateau of hellish stone, and the waves of heat and the evil miasma of the place made them nearly impossible to make out clearly. And now, they were surrounded by a throng of devils that seemed to grow in numbers with each passing moment. 

At this point, Azbael himself, unengaged from our friends, strode confidently toward the opening, his 12' high hulking form a terror. My stomach turned, and I took a step back and wanted nothing more than to run. In a haze I looked over my shoulder, and saw that Imogen was now on her back fighting for her life, with the throng of derelicts atop her flailing mindlessly, their numbers too much for her. The other prisoners were gone, except for Gideon, who now stood in the doorway, poised to flee, but delaying, taking in with sick glee, the horrible and desperate scene he had created. 

I had a feeling of despair like I've never felt before or since, and I know now that it was the combination of the situation and the aura of the devil that advanced on us. But Artorias held fast, and he bolstered my resolve. Azbael was now at the door, though already it began to shrink, the 10' diameter opening shrinking at the rate of about 1' a second. Azbael had little time, and he did his best to get past Artorias; he charged headlong with his great horns, but the armored form of my friend held a divine steadfastness, and Azbael was rebuked; he bounced back fully into the portal off the great shield of my friend. It was at this point that everything slowed down in my mind; I could see it all happen in horrifying slow motion. 

Azbael, now laying on his back on the other side of the nearly-closed portal, cracked his whip and it wrapped around my throat, pulling me violently toward the shrinking portal, threatening to pull me in. Inside Hell, as I lurched toward it, I could make out the voice of Shea call out "Blessed Amaunator protect us!", as she held something over her head in both hands, and a great flash of light seared away 

the devils around them, and then all was replaced by a blinding light none could look on. Azbael roared in defiance and pain, and just as the portal was less than a foot wide, Artorias grabbed the whip, and pulled hard and fast, and the forearm of Azbael came through the portal, whip in hand, just as it closed for good and severed the arm. 

All the while, Gideon cast one last spell, dousing Imogen in acid, burning her terribly and many of the derelicts that had already beaten her into unconsciousness. She now lay in a shallow pool of acid, and as I tried to pull the whip from my throat (it still seemed to tighten with its own malevolent purpose), and Artorias rushed to rescue Imogen, Gideon fled, and that was the last I saw of him. 

In the moments that followed, Artorias dispatched all the derelicts, which was an even lower-low for us; their souls were indeed forfeit now, but we needed to protect Imogen. Imogen was revived with what little magic we had left, and rushed to the stone door, that moments ago was a portal to Hell, and beat her fists upon it. The sight of her acid-burned face screaming and crying at that door I will never forget. 

Artorias dutifully secured our things as best he could, while I pulled Imogen away. We then gathered as many of our things as we could and taking one last look at the portal which had consumed our friends, departed Kahlmadrid and tragedy. 

On the way out, taking a large supply of Gale's powder, we rigged up a blast to shut the mine- entrance. This actually worked better than planned; we heard several secondary explosions happen deep within the mine, and the sounds of rock settling lasted for hours. I believe the entrance to Kahlmadrid is thoroughly closed. 

With the noise likely to draw attention, we decided that, despite our fatigue, we'd have to keep moving. We marched all night, and found ourselves back at Fergy's Hole mid-day on the 29th.

1 Uktar 
Arrived in Charity around evening. Went to Arwencroft, and had Flora summon the authorities. Chancellor Ferguson, John Constable, Secretary Carpenter, Ambassador Oakdale and a few others all attended us promptly, and we gave our story. 

The group was dismayed at what they heard; that Niles Gideon had escaped, and worse, that the Itineris Diabolus was unaccounted for. We expect that Gideon managed to grab it in the chaos of our battle at the Gate, but we don't know for sure. The Gate Chambre was such a mess of bodies, gear, smoke and the miasma from Avernus, that our departure was disorderly and hasty. 

They were equally sorry to hear about our companions, who remained in Hell battling a ceaseless army of devils, and a prayer was organized for them. Flora and Oakdale both asked if we wanted messages sent to Dagger Falls and the Close, but I declined. I was, and remain, unsure of who our allies and who our enemies are in Charity. Gideon never returned to Charity, as far as anyone knows; there are likenesses of him on every corner of the town, and a 1,000-crown price on his head. Chancellor Ferguson congratulated us on a job well done, and said that they would have to remain vigilant, but for the time being, suspected that the 'Gideon Cult' (as he's calling it) has been supressed. I am unsure it has. I'm only sure that we found the book, found a gate to hell, lost the book, lost our friends, and caved in the mine. I'm not sure how permanent any of those certainties are. 

So, in the morning, I will be riding hard, and you'll have heard all of this first-hand from me, by the time you read this report. 
I am tired, and my head won't stop ringing, so now I must rest.
