DAGGERDALE
Unfinished Tales


Fresh Blood, 1480 DR  ·  Abandon Hope, 1481 DR


A record of play-by-post tabletop roleplaying in the Forgotten Realms, set in Daggerdale during the Era of Upheaval (4th Edition, D&D). This archive preserves the unvarnished text of the forum from which it was extracted, edited only for clarity and structure. Out-of-character material from within the narrative threads is indicated by [editorial brackets].

Editor’s Note
This archive reconstructs two connected play-by-post campaigns conducted on a private Vanilla Forums installation between January 2011 and July 2012. The setting is the Forgotten Realms, specifically the region of Daggerdale and its environs, in the year 1480–1481 DR — a period of precarious peace following the Winter War between Daggerdale and Netheril.

The forum’s categories organised the material into distinct registers: Plot Narrative & PC Actions (the primary thread of play), PC Background & Reflections (journals, origin stories, and interior documents), the Daggerdale Gazetteer (DM world-building), Table Talk and related meta-categories (out-of-character discussion), and private one-on-one DM/player conversations. This archive draws on all of these, excluding only material relating to forum administration and software.

The archive is divided into two parts corresponding to the two campaign arcs. Within each part, scenes are presented in in-world chronological order. Each scene opens with the Dungeon Master’s narrative, followed by player-authored character actions in posting order, followed where relevant by Background & Reflections material anchored to that scene. Out-of-character intrusions within action threads — mechanical queries, player parentheticals, DM rulings — are enclosed in [editorial brackets] rather than silently omitted, in the interest of preserving the texture of the medium.

Private DM/player correspondence, where it illuminates events in the main threads, is included as an appendix to each part.

Part One
Fresh Blood, 1480 DR
The first campaign arc — known among the players as Fresh Blood — begins in Flamerule of 1480 DR, with three characters converging on a roadhouse outside the city of Arabel, in northern Cormyr. Their destination is Castle Radiance in Daggerdale, and their road leads north through the Thunder Peaks and into a land still recovering from a winter of shadow-war.

Dramatis Personae
Fresh Blood, 1480 DR
Beliard Kathklan of House Emmarask
Cormyrean bard and diplomatic envoy, newly posted as secretary to Ambassador John Tunbridge at Castle Radiance. Sociable to a fault and difficult to truly know — a skilled deflector of personal inquiry. His hatred of Netheril and the Shadow is the one crack in an otherwise impenetrable composure. A voracious reader; carries a monocle on a chain. Fencer rather than soldier. Devoted follower of Selûne.
Tyrian Grey
Paladin of Ilmater, travelling under a burden of royal disfavour — exiled from Cormyr by King Florin, yet permitted passage north as an agent of the legendary Reynard the Boar of Westgate. Young, genuinely noble in character if not in standing, and still learning that the quality of Reynard’s virtue lives in slum tenements and leper-houses rather than tournament fields. Armed and armoured by the generosity of Crown Prince Irvel.
Carpathia Twist
Half-elven hunter and tracker, born Carpathia Hearthstone of Amtar Forest. Her village was destroyed over years of escalating attacks by servants of Malar, the Beastlord. She carries the skull of an ancient gryphon — Torestriwansolinath, who gave her his name and his spirit before dying — and with it a mission: to hunt Malar’s followers until she has weakened the god himself. Laconic to the point of unnerving those around her. Not travelling for company.
Eremus, Ineluki, and Cirdan
Three wood elves of Cormanthor, travelling under something between escort and exile — assigned to Ambassador Miritar’s delegation by a Marshal of their people, against their inclination. They are contemptuous of human settlements and slow to trust. Eremus leads; Ineluki and Cirdan follow. Their precise offence and the terms of their assignment are not disclosed. They walk at the edge of the caravan and keep to themselves.

The Dragon’s Gate Inn, Arabel
Flamerule, 1480 DR
DM
It is in a large road-house outside the walls on the northern side of Arabel that Beliard and Tyrian first meet. This should be a busy place; 100 years ago, this road-house, which more resembles a small fortress built outside the walls of the great Cormyrean city, would have been packed with traders, this time of year especially. These days, the world such as it is, the enormous tap-room of this inn is 90% empty. It would have been nothing for merchants to exchange thousands of gold a day in handshakes in this place, but now the roads are a dangerous place, and travel up the road and into the dales is not what it once was.

Despite that, the world is waking up again from its long and troubled nightmare. It is recovering from the Spellplague, and trade is one of the first healthy signs of that recovery. And so it is that a few merchants are gathered together in one lonely corner of this place, arguing about when they should set off. A dozen retainers, followers and other caravan add-ons wait sitting or standing at tables nearby, where the merchants — who in this age more resemble warlords — discuss their fate.

At one of these tables sit an interesting trio: Beliard Emmarask, envoy from Cormyr, on his way to Castle Radiance to replace a low-level bureaucrat there; Tyrian Grey, the paladin of Ilmater, who has likewise been tasked with going to Daggerdale, where he is to earn his knighthood; and Carpathia Twist, guide and hunter, who is leaving The King’s Forest for her own reasons, and likewise heading north.

Beliard, who is quite astute, has noticed that Tyrian has the vocabulary and mannerisms of someone who has spent much time between various polities, and is drawn to him. It isn’t long before he figures out that this is one of the Scions of the ill-fated Achres family.

The lot of them will be travelling to Tilverton Gap together with a larger caravan accompanying a man named Denelius Ormath, a merchant-warlord from Suzail bringing goods all the way to Castle Radiance. He has a contract with the Whitegates, apparently, and has been to the Castle four times in the last two years. He is bickering with two other merchants who want to wait a few days before going. Ormath wants to leave right away to fit in two more trips this year. And besides, as the discussion is focused, there is war raging between Netheril and Daggerdale, so what’s the rush?

Beliard
“Yeah, what’s the rush, Ormath?”
Unable to help overhearing the semi-private conversation happening at the next table between Denelius and his colleagues, Beliard clears his throat and begins ruminating, seemingly to himself, but projecting his voice loudly enough that he can easily be heard throughout the room.

“What’s the rush, indeed? Why help the Dalesmen with their little war when we could wait for them to succumb to the Shadow? Why, they’d be doubly grateful for our commerce. Nay — triply! What’s that you say? They wouldn’t then want these swords and shields — not even good Cormyrean steel? I’ll grant it you have a point. But you’re too focussed on the short term. Take the long view, my friends — it’s a seller’s market! So what if Netheril takes the Dales? We might sell north! To... Netheril! Ah, yes — yes, my mistake. Well, let us sell east then, to Sembia! What’s that you say? Sembia is naught but a Netherese puppet-regime? So it is! Politics — one forgets the little things. So be it. Those rascally Netherese. They seem to be just everywhere, don’t they? In every shadow you might say. Well, as the halfling harlot says, if you can’t beat ‘em, bill ‘em! Netheril is a growing market! After a war, they’ll need to resupply — and, why, seeing as they’ll have eliminated our competition, we’ll have them by the short and shadies! We can sell them anything! If they’re going to keep up this expansionist policy, they’ll need all the materiel they can get, from swords to siege engines! Once we share three borders with them, we’ll be their favourite neighbours! They couldn’t NOT buy from us. Yes, sir — we can just sit back and let the jack roll in as the Netherese do their thing. What’s the rush indeed?”

Turning to address Ormath directly, Beliard’s voice hardens. “Now, Master Ormath. Though it pains me to encourage what we can all see to be the less profitable option” [this last now dripping with sarcasm] “I am on His Majesty’s business, and intend to be at Castle Radiance by Flamerule’s first ride. Sorry scrollpusher that I am, I should prefer to ride with the strength of numbers, and though I do not command the royal purse, I can extend His Majesty’s gratitude for any haste you might be able to muster on my account.”

He pauses for a second, then his eyes brighten. Turning to gesture at a wide-eyed Tyrian, with whom he has exchanged only a few words so far, he continues: “In payment, I can offer the axe and strong arm of this fine young knight, for he tells me that he, too, is bound for Castle Radiance.” He claps Tyrian on the shoulder. “This young man, whom I know to be a fine and noble fellow, has sworn an oath of anonymity, and may not name himself until he has found Azoun’s Lost Crown!”
(Gasps from the room, now fully enthralled.)
In a stage whisper, he adds: “I can, however, tell you, good master, that this man is a favourite of the White Prince. His glorious future is assured, and the favour of your service is sure to pay off in spades!”

During this exchange, the knight in question appears to be trying to shrink under his own breastplate. Clapping his aghast companion on the back, Beliard whispers to him sotto voce: “No fear, my friend — I know exactly what I’m doing.” Then he smiles brightly at the room — all eyes upon him, now — and stands and lifts his glass.
“Ladies and Gentlemen — the King!”

Tyrian Grey
Arriving at The Dragon’s Gate Inn in Arabel, the road-house built outside the fortress city as an extension of the old landmark Pride of Arabel housed inside the city walls, Tyrian bade his friends farewell and prepared himself for the next stage in his journey.

Opening the doors of the Gate, Tyrian was stunned to discover the tap room nearly empty. In truth, this had been his first entry into this place despite the many times he had been to Arabel. In the past he had always been housed in the garrison within the walls and had had no reason to mingle with the merchants, pilgrims and assorted other travellers that congregated here. He knew this was the place where all voyages north began, and he had expected a rollicking and rowdy tavern. Even the refectory at the House of Ilmater in Westgate was more boisterous than this room.

After quickly arranging stabling for his horse and securing a private room and a bath, Tyrian retired without a glance back at the tap room, ashamedly longing for his first sleep in a real bed in almost a year.

Very early the next morning, cleansed and refreshed, Tyrian set about exercising himself and his horse, watching carefully as the sun-worshipping patrons of the New Pride went about their morning adorations. Tyrian had never quite understood Amaunator and his worshippers. He had been well schooled in theology when he had trained for the priesthood, and knew about all the faiths of Faerûn. He knew enough about Amaunator to know that he did not really trust him or his clergy. While their devotion to the fight against the world’s evil was admirable, and while he had found the pilgrims he had met on the road to be genial and affable on the whole, there was a certain element of trickery and misdirection at play in the faith’s history, and its clergy’s dealings with the people which had always given him pause.

Re-entering the roadhouse, Tyrian made his way to one of the empty tables for breakfast. Before he could get there, an impeccably dressed, middle-aged man made a discreet attempt to get his attention. Before long, Beliard Emmarask, newly appointed Cormyrean envoy to Castle Radiance, was sharing pleasantries of tales of life at the Court of the Purple Dragon, and trading names of mutual acquaintances and friends.

Beliard told Tyrian that the best way to get to Castle Radiance would be accompanying a merchant by the name of Denelius Ormath, but it was difficult to know when he would be leaving. Negotiations were in progress, and it was just as likely that they would depart tomorrow as next week or next year. A little patience was all that was needed. With that, Tyrian headed off to the garrison in Arabel, and then to replenish his supplies.

Upon his return in the evening, he found Beliard sharing a table with the most peculiar-looking woman he had ever seen. Tyrian had spent a long time in the slums of Westgate, and had seen people of just about every race and culture there is to be found. But this woman? This one was odd.

Tyrian approached the table and was about to open his mouth in greeting to Beliard and his unusual companion, when Beliard turned to the table next to him and spoke. Tyrian listened to the orator’s words enthralled. Too perplexed by the last statement about Azoun’s Lost Crown, Tyrian let it go, mesmerised by the demagogue before him. He simply sat, overwhelmed by the spectacle he had just witnessed.

“Mister Emmarask?” he eventually asked in a whisper to his tablemate. “I was not aware Azoun had lost his crown.”

Carpathia sat with the two men she had just met, and without provocation, began softly to laugh.

On the Road North from Arabel
Flamerule, 1480 DR
DM
The trader caravan set out north only a day after the three companions had met in the inn. The mocking words of Beliard had not, it would seem, fallen on deaf ears.

The first two days represent a relatively quiet journey. Tyrian and Beliard share many a tale, and have the odd relatively heated discussion on politics or treaties. They remain composed at all times, however, speaking quietly so as not to disrupt the soothing creak of the wagon axles.

Carpathia sits upon her own cart, alongside their horses. She rarely speaks, and hence it is a slight shock to hear her mutter.
“There, there, rest ancient one.”
Both Tyrian and Beliard turn to look at her. Carpathia sits gently stroking the avian skull she has sitting on her lap. She seems lost in a dream, or a memory.

Carpathia Twist
It had only been a day since Carpathia had left the side of the Gryphon.
Some thought in her mind tugged at her, and she found concentration difficult. She had decided to skirt the Snowflake Mountains rather than attempting to cross them with her horse and cart. She wrote of the incessant thrumming in her head as guilt for not having buried the wondrous creature before she left. But something told her the Gryphon was not meant to be interred in stone and earth. It was only after setting up camp, and finishing a sparse meal, that the real reason struck her. The feather.

She looked everywhere in her camp, within her belongings, all through the loose planks of her cart. But the feather was nowhere to be found. She cursed herself, and cursed Malar for bringing yet another misfortune upon her. Without delay she packed up her camp and travelled through the night. She reached the location where she had previously ascended at dawn. The sun was shedding its first rays of golden light over the peaks before her.

She searched for the narrow smooth trough which she had ascended the previous day. It was not to be found. Looking up the slope from the location she was sure the trough ended, only a ragged, cracked, age-old crevasse could be found. It was as if the mountain had aged a hundred years in a day. She stood there, looking up, perplexed and unable to move until the sun had reached half way to the pinnacle of its climb.

With a shake of her head, under the heat of a sun that seemed too close, she began to climb up through the crevasse. No geological mystery could dissuade her forever. Not from finding that which she sought. The climb that had taken only minutes the previous day took her hours. By the time she reached the plateau she sought, her hands were covered in her blood, cut ragged from clutching the jagged edges of rock. The pain had not deterred her; she kept her focus on her goal. At one point she thought of the mare Freedom below, and the disruption in focus made the pain all too real in her hands and limbs. She had to fight to remain conscious.

Upon reaching the plateau and looking across to where the Gryphon had lain, all pain was completely forgotten. Where just yesterday she had sat and held the head of the magical creature, only a skull remained.

She moved forward with some caution, unable to grasp reality. She reached out and picked up the skull. Touching it brought her mind back under control — and more. Her knowledge increased tenfold, and she knew, without question, that this skull was that of a Gryphon that had died here more than two hundred years ago.

Upon closer inspection of the partial skull, she received her second shock. A petrified feather was fused to the crown of the beak. A petrified feather she recognised. It was her feather.

In the Taproom of Quick Axes
Flamerule, 1480 DR — Dagger Falls
DM
Arriving at the taproom of this establishment late in the day during the peak of the trading season makes it difficult to get a table. There are easily thirty tables in the massive oak-beamed hall, which soars to a ceiling two floors above. Balconies run around the entire perimeter of the walls on two upper floors, and those on the north wall are deep, with more tables and chairs.

There are between two and three hundred people in Quick Axes tonight. There are dozens of locals, but there are scores upon scores of travellers. A caravan that just came in from Cormyr had well over a hundred people in it, and a few smaller ones arrived during the day before that one. Many traders appreciate the home-away-from-home feeling of this place; in Dagger Falls, this establishment alone has a license to serve unlimited alcohol, and the booze-soaked smell and the rowdy ambience that goes along with this practice is a welcome escape from the comparatively severe atmosphere of the town.

The taproom is full, and there isn’t a place to sit. There are people wearing clothing from every corner of this part of Faerûn: some merchants from the modern city of Westgate in their smart waistcoats, rugged-looking frontier types who trade in resources from the wilder parts of the dales, and everything beyond and in between. There are dozens of staff on duty tonight, serving food and drink energetically, trying to keep up with the demands of the traders, mercenaries and other travellers in this elbow-room-only establishment.
“Nice axe!”
Tyrian and Carpathia stood helplessly just inside the door of the inn, almost but not quite out of the throng and press of the evening’s custom. Beliard had tasked them with finding a seat while he attended to an errand of some sort, but there were no spaces at any of the great tables. The two had been waiting for what seemed an interminable length of time for an opening to appear, but each time a table began to clear, it was claimed almost before its previous occupiers had risen. There were easily a hundred other caravanners and Dalesmen waiting for similar opportunity. Carpathia thought of vultures attending a wounded bear that just wouldn’t lie down and die.

“What do you think we should we do?” Tyrian looked down at the slight figure of his companion, his hand running back through his slightly unkempt golden curls.

Carpathia glowered. Tyrian would not make a good vulture. She was getting antsy. Where was that fool bard? “These are your people, boy,” she hissed. “You tell me!”
Tyrian was taken aback by her sudden vehemence. What had he done now?
“What? They’re not my —” he began. “I mean, I’ve never been the —”
He broke off as a full-figured young dwarfess coalesced out of the chaos to stand before them.

“I’ll get those packs up to a room for ye’s, sir knight, milady. Ye’s’ll be in the Ironbark Dormitory — there are two bunks left, I think.” Before either of them could say a word, the young dwarf hoisted both travellers’ packs into her well-muscled arms, resting one on each shoulder, then turned to face them. “Second floor up the back stair. Ye’s’re in with a bunch o’ those other caravanners from Cormyr. How many nights, then?”

Tyrian stared distractedly at the dwarf’s downy cheeks. He’d heard that dwarf women had beards, but had never seen one in the flesh. The hair on her chin looked so soft. He wondered how old she was. Would it grow fuller?

“How many nights, then?” The dwarfess repeated her question, staring unblinkingly at Tyrian.
Tyrian blushed, and stuttered, “Um... I’m not... there’s another...”
“One night. For now.” Carpathia came to the young man’s rescue. Beliard could deal with his own room, she thought.

The dwarf nodded. “Right, then. Pay Kilbur over there at the bar.” She nodded towards a hirsute dwarf sitting atop a stool on a raised platform at one end of the bar, his odd features poring over a ledger while he argued with a customer in a wheezing, high-pitched voice. The fellow’s coarse beard, the same dull grey as the thick hair that bristled wildly from what could be seen of his head, arms, and chest, formed the crowning glory of a veritable pelt. His features were almost entirely hidden, only a pair of glittery black eyes and an extraordinarily porcine nose being free of the wiry growth. Carpathia thought of a wild boar, and smiled.

“Do s’all a favour and give him the correct coins, won’t ye’s?” The dwarfess grimaced. “By the way” — turning to Tyrian — “nice axe.” With a sly wink at the flustered knight, she took her leave.

Not long after, a melodious voice chimed from the doorway, and Tyrian and Carpathia turned to greet their erstwhile companion. As they did so, however, their eyes widened to take in the sight of Beliard Emmarask, who appeared to have shaved, bathed, and changed since he had left their company almost an hour previously. Over a finely tailored cotton shirt, the bard wore a tunic trimmed tastefully with a collar of silver fur. The blue fabric was embroidered with the golden image of a rampant stag — a device adapted from the crest of House Emmarask, Tyrian thought he recognised. Dusty trousers and travelling boots had been exchanged for breeches of burnished leather and a pair of stiff black boots collared in the Sembian fashion. Gone was the rakish beard he had tended somewhat vainly during the journey, and his oiled hair glistened in the torchlight of the inn.

Affecting confusion, the bard spread his arms questioningly: “No table yet? No drinks? Why friends, you needn’t have waited on my account. You must be parched! Now, please. Allow me.”

Tyrian and Carpathia watched as Beliard negotiated the crowded taproom like an experienced pilot navigating a well-known reef, effortlessly making his way to the raised dais in the centre of the room. Mounting the dais, the bard approached a grizzled old dwarf who seemed to be directing the inn’s staff like a general directing troops in battle. The dwarf seemed surprised when Beliard jumped up on the platform, and his face darkened, but the bard disarmed him with a deep bow and then crouched to engage Balak Quickaxe, Master of the Inn, in intense dialogue.

Though his friends were unable to hear the content of the conversation over the not-so-dull roar of the taproom, they could see the dwarf’s countenance visibly brighten as he listened. Still talking to the old dwarf, the bard pointed in their direction, then held up three fingers. The dwarf seemed to consider this, then nodded, turned, and bellowed at a surprisingly corpulent fiery-haired dwarf standing near the bar: “OTHEN! GET’CHER CHUBBY WHISKERS OVER HERE!” The old dwarf’s stentorian voice belied his venerable appearance; after the roar, the general hubbub seemed somehow quieter by comparison.

As Tyrian and Carpathia watched, Othen ambled quickly towards the pair at the centre of the room. The dwarf’s great bulk acted as the prow of a ship, and many customers were forced to suddenly dodge out of the way to avoid being run down. Despite their complaints, the surly dwarf ignored them, continuing as though they weren’t there at all. Eventually the ousted patrons retreated to the sides of the room, awkwardly clutching bowls of stew and mugs of ale in their hands. Up on the dais, the old dwarf jabbed a finger at Beliard, muttering something low and threatening. Beliard bowed again, jumped down to the newly cleared table, and waved at Tyrian and Carpathia.

“Good news, friends!” Beliard beamed at them as they finally made their way through the mob. “It turns out that the minstrel Master Balak retained for this evening’s entertainment has failed to appear. I have offered my services to assist him in his hour of need, and he has generously offered us this table for my performance.”

A matronly woman suddenly appeared with a bottle of wine, two mugs of ale and a steaming loaf of crusty bread. “Yer vittles’re on the way, songster,” she said, “but you’d better wet your whistle and start blowing. We’re getting run off our feet out here!”

With a nod, Beliard looked at Tyrian. “Keep this table free. We’ll have company, ere long.”
Taking a sip of wine, the bard leapt up onto the table.

Beliard
Ormath’s Ode
“Ladies and Gentlemen, Harlots and Cads!” The bard’s clear voice rings out across the room, and a few — though not many — patrons glance in his direction. “You are to be commended for your perspicacity in choosing to dine tonight under the Sign of the Quick Axe. Master Balak welcomes you one and all to his establishment!”
A few locals yell out the dwarf’s name approvingly as they hoist their glasses.
“My name, friends, is Beliard Emmarask, and I drink to your health!”
Raising the bottle to his lips, the bard takes a dramatic swig, though Carpathia notes that the man’s Adam’s apple does not move — the drinking is an act. Nodding at the table where the caravan master Denelius Ormath and his cronies have set themselves up, Beliard continues:
“And more importantly, to your wealth!”
Laughter and cheers, as Beliard appears to take a second, even deeper swallow of the crimson wine, which dribbles down his chin before he wipes it away with a flourish. Ormath and his friends chuckle, hoisting their own glasses in the bard’s direction.

“My friends and I have come here this day, thanks entirely to the efforts of our Fearless Patron, Denelius Ormath! Were it not for his stalwart leadership, sterling character, bold courage, and unparalleled business acumen, we would not have made it here this season. Truly, I do believe this man could lead a caravan through the Nine Hells in winter. If you’ve a cargo to ship anywhere between here and Suzail, folks, Ormath’s your man.”

With each compliment, Beliard’s voice has risen, bringing more of the room under his spell. At his table, Ormath appears to be enjoying the attention, his chest swollen with pride as he nods to the bard’s paean. Now the bard’s voice takes on a rhythmic cadence, and his hands begin tapping his hip in time with his words:
Who thunders ‘cross the Thunder Peaks? Why, Denelius Ormath!
Who knows what every merchant seeks? Why, Denelius Ormath!
A few in the crowd catch on and begin chanting the man’s name at the end of each verse.
Will he be stayed by bandit swarms? Not Denelius Ormath!
By orc marauders? Or fire or storms? Not Denelius Ormath!
Many are pounding their tables now, and Ormath is beginning to look positively regal.
Who carts the cargo and lugs the loads? It’s Denelius Ormath!
[A number of Denelius’s underpaid and overworked carters frown at this one, but are too much in the rhythm to break it.]
He’s the iron captain of the roads, is Great Denelius Ormath!
A cheer erupts from Ormath’s table at this last verse, and there is general applause from the room, especially the caravanners, despite the fact that the journey from Cormyr under Ormath had been one that few would care to repeat, in this lifetime or the next.

Nodding at Beliard, Ormath hoists his considerable bulk onto a chair, raising a hand to quieten the chants of “Ormath! Ormath!” that greet him.

As Beliard steps down to let Ormath address the crowd, he is one of the few to notice the arrival of a young, flamboyantly dressed man carrying a lute, out of breath and seemingly fuming with anger. Espying Beliard across the room, this new arrival cries out, but is immediately shushed by the crowd as their master begins to speak.

“Thank you, Sir Bard. You do me a great honour, and I thank you for it. Now as you know, I am a man of few words, so you’ll have no speech from me. However, I am a man of great appetites, and I feel powerful thirsty. Master Balak — a pitcher of ale to each table, on my account!”

After this announcement, no one is paying attention to the newcomer at all, except Beliard, who sneers at him from the centre of the room, shaking his head in a mocking expression of false sympathy. Brandishing his fist at Emmarask, the fellow with the lute slinks out into the night.

Beliard’s Ballad
“Songster!” Balak’s bellow again cuts through the raucous atmosphere that has returned to the taproom with Ormath’s largesse. “You’ve had your fun. Now give us a song!”

Amid cries of agreement, Beliard lowers his head in acquiescence and remounts the table. Facing the crowd, he assumes a lecturing stance.

“I am most happy to take up Master Balak’s request. As we all know, the dwarf is not apt, when idle, to break into singing, being not of so airy a constitution as those of more reasonable stature. Yet I have found that he will drink to his health — often, I have also found, to the number of his years — when a song is on offer. To that end, I present this tune, of my own composition.”

The crowd, now familiar with the bard, dims its murmuring somewhat as Beliard begins singing in a low voice. As the patrons of Quick Axes strain to hear the words of the song, the din lowers further still.

A stick, a stone, it’s the end of the road, it’s the rest of a stump, it’s a little alone,

It’s a sliver of glass, it is life, it’s Selûne, it is night, it is death, it’s a trap, it’s a rune.

The oak when it blooms, a fox in the brush, a knot in the wood, the song of a thrush,
The wood of the wind, a tower, a fall, a scratch, a lump, it is nothing at all.

It’s the wind blowing free, it’s the end of the slope, it’s a gleam, it’s a void, it’s a hunch, it’s a hope,

Where the River Tesh crosses the Tethyamar trail, it’s the end of a journey, it’s the start of the tale.

The foot, the ground, the flesh and the bone, the beat of the drum, a slingshot’s stone,
A fish, a flash, a silvery glow, a fight, a bet, the range of a bow.

The bed of the well, the end of the line, the dismay in the face, it’s a loss, it’s a find,
A spear, a spike, a point, a nail, a drip, a drop, the end of the tale.

A cartload of hay in the soft morning light, the howl of a wolf in the dead of the night,

A pass in the mountains, a horse and a mule, in the distance the elves, three shadows of blue.

Where the River Tesh crosses the Tethyamar trail, it’s the end of a journey, it’s the start of the tale.
A stick, a stone, the end of the road, the rest of a stump, a lonesome road,

A sliver of glass, a life, the sun, a knife, a death, the end of the run,

The Knight’s Journey — Tyrian Grey

The journey out of Westgate was as uneventful as can be expected for seafarers on the Dragonmere: no pirates, no aboleths, no monsters, no storms. Ilmater and Valkur favoured the voyage, and Umberlee took no notice. The old cog moved nimbly down the canal that had been dredged to link the old city’s harbour with the anchorage at the new shoreline, and then out, across The Neck and into The Dragonmere heading west, hugging the coast to Teziir. A quick resupply and change of cargo later, the cog turned north with a good, strong sou’easterly wind and in no time at all they were anchoring in Marsember.
Forbidden Kingdom
Cormyr proper. Tyrian had mixed feelings about this next step. He had spent his youth admiring this kingdom, and longing to pledge his sword and lance in the service of its king. Yet, that king had rejected him; banished him from the realm. It wasn’t his intention to remain here, only to cross it as quickly as possible and enter the Dales. Was he betraying King Floril’s order by stepping an uninvited foot in his kingdom?

Heading west out of Cormyr to Daerlun and from there into Sembia and up to the Dales was out of the question. It would be fall before he reached Castle Radiance if he took that route. No. Ilmater had seen to it that the only ship that could carry him across the Dragonmere would bring him here. Ilmater had not chosen to have him cross from Westgate to Daerlun. From Marsember the Amaunator pilgrims would group up and head west, then north to Daggerdale — precisely the route Tyrian was hoping to follow.

His instinct proved true, and no more than a mile outside the gates, as the sun was going down in the distance ahead of him, he came across the first group of pilgrims praying to that disappearing disc. Tyrian waited until the formalities of ritual were over, then introduced himself and asked to join them in their trek.
The White Prince
A few days later, Tyrian found himself in Suzail, the city he had called home for three years. Leaving his sun-worshipping pilgrims behind, curiosity got the better of him and he made his way to the sprawling palace complex known simply as The Court. This had been his home.

It was hard for Tyrian not to smile as he felt the wind against his bare face, galloping north on the road. And then, when he had been tricked into the journey to Westgate, he found himself on the other side of that coin: Reynard had stripped him of everything and sent him to tend to lepers and plague victims and lunatics and worse, in the most vile, dangerous slums in the Realms. From Prey into Hunter

Seated in the shelter of an outcropping of rock, Carpathia heard a soft coughing sound. Weary, but too tired to think better of it, she arose to investigate. “You must hunt, Carpathia. You must take vengeance for your village.”

With the last of its strength the Gryphon turned its head once more, to look her in the eye and gaze upon her soul. “I am a creature of life, and magic. When this body dies, my spirit lives on. I shall accompany you on your quest, that you need not be afraid. Find others to aid you in your quest, and I will do what I can to assist them as well.” With these words he pulled the last of his healthy feathers from his wing and gave it to her. “I am named Torestriwansolinath — in your tongue, Twist. Take my name, and this feather. Carry them, and my spirit shall accompany you.” With that, the last light slowly fled from his eyes.
Carpathia to this day doesn’t know how long she sat upon that mountain side.
But when she descended to her cart and the mare Freedom, she was Carpathia Hearthstone no more. She was Carpathia Twist, and there was a light in her eyes.
She had hunting to do. She had a god to kill.

Beliard Emmarask — Character Document
In the eighteen months of his posting to the Cormyrean embassy at Castle Radiance, Beliard Emmarask has made many friends, and few enemies of note. Since his arrival, he has become an invaluable assistant to the corpulent ambassador, John Tunbridge, and acts as secretary and emissary of the ambassador in all but the most important affairs.

When not in the embassy offices or on official assignment — he spent three months at Base Camp Silver as the Cormyrean Envoy to the Silver Council — he is often found in the Tethyamar Hearth Inn, where he entertains of an evening once or twice a week, and keeps an open eye and ear for strangers and news from the world beyond the Dale. He is quite sociable, but should anyone pause to reflect on his character, they realise that the man gives little of himself away. In fact, upon further reflection, Beliard seems to be very good at deflecting the focus of any conversation onto either his interlocutor, or some other topic, such as a mutual acquaintance. Rarely, if ever, can anyone claim to have found out much about the man himself, or his thoughts. Obviously, he is a loyal supporter of the Crown of Cormyr — he is its eyes and ears in this hinterland after all — but beyond what is known of his family (servants of the crown for centuries), Beliard is simply “that bard from Cormyr”. The one thing that is unmistakable, however, is his hatred of Netheril and all things Shadow-wrought. Only when such topics come up does an intense anger seem to seethe within him, giving some small window into his private self.

He is a voracious reader, and can often be found perusing tomes and correspondence in many languages through a fine monocle attached by a chain to his person.

In combat, he has acquitted himself several times with a longsword, demonstrating a finesse that comes with having learned fencing as a sport rather than through the necessity of combat. He is more agile than powerful, and maintains a steady banter of mockery during his encounters, enraging his victims to the extent that they lose control. He is not a trained warrior, however, and violence is always a last resort.
