Narrative read by the DM about the site of the Dwimmerholme families abductions

600px|right

The abduction site is actually two fields all close together; a winter corn field, and a pumpkin patch.  Oakdale brings you out to the place cautiously, constantly looking to the men that are travelling with you.  The local Sun Blades look a little uneasy, especially the younger ones.

“Everyone is nervous about this place now” he explains as you get nearer.  “Some of the children say it is haunted.  The families aren’t coming out to finish the harvest, a fearful and superstitious behaviour that we would not be able to abide in leaner years.  The Cultivar still comes out every afternoon, and lights all of these...” and with this, he motions to a few scarecrows that you can see watching over the 3- or 4- acre pumpkin patch.  The eyes of the frightful jack-o-lantern heads still burn with the light of the candles behind them, and with the day slightly overcast as it has become, they are most conspicuous.  “She thinks...I don’t know what she thinks.  If there’s ghosts out here, they aren’t afraid of carved pumpkins!”

Your group is now upon the pumpkin patch, and you can see that the ground has been turned up in terrible and tell-tale fashion.  Despite days of rain that fell between that day and this, there are still some obvious signs of the abductions, not the least of which are many smashed pumpkins.  A couple clearly have large heavy boot prints in their mushy guts.

Just to the east of the pumpkin patch is a small oasis of winter corn; it stands alone, this 5 acre field, as all of the surrounding fields have been cleared.  There are zig-zagging paths through the corn that also tell a horribly tale; one can easily imagine the flight of frightened children through the corn, and the terrible outcome.  To show you that he is not afraid of the place, Oakdale leads you into the maze, and continues on.

“When the tracks were fresh, we could see that a group of bandits actually crept up to the southern and eastern edges of this stand of corn.  Another group came from the west, and flushed everyone; the families in the pumpkin patch and those here, in this maze, into the waiting hands of the ambushers.  The tracks were really clear; we could see exactly where everyone went.  They didn’t have a chance.”  He turns around a bit, and the lot of you realize that you are already lost in this maze.  “This way” he says, and leads you out.  

You emerge on the northern edge of the corn field.  Between this small stand and the forest ahead there is only perhaps 50 yards of fallow to cross.  At the edge of the forest, you can clearly see a black opening in the golden and orange canopy, where a good trail begins.  He points to it.  

“That’s where they took them.  All 16 of them.  We followed, of course, but that is where things got...frustrating.”  He actually brings his gloved hands to his face to rub at the guilt he feels.  “We couldn’t go ten yards without the very woods themselves hindering us.  We were tripped up, turned around, confounded.  We couldn’t make any distance on those bloody kidnappers.  We failed.”
