A dossier compiled by Sandoval Luchra, Rank Three Watch Wizard of Waterdeep, 1457 DR

On the Subject of the Warders of Waterdeep

I will begin this particular chapter of my research the same way I have begun everything I have written thus far regarding the individual known as Steele: with the admission that I am not entirely certain I wish to continue.

Nevertheless.

I was informed, when this task was first set before me, that I would receive one platinum piece for every page of verified information I could compile. At the time I took this for a reasonable arrangement, perhaps even a generous one. A score of days into the work, I am no longer certain that any rate of compensation could be called adequate. I am also no longer certain that the individual who set this rate did not know precisely what they were asking, and find the whole matter quietly amusing at my expense.

The group known as the Warders of Waterdeep came into being sometime in the middle of the previous century, drawn together around the wood elf Leirran Grennirath — an individual who went, among his companions, by no name other than Steele. I have found no record of who suggested the name for the group, and I suspect that if I were to find such a record, it would only lead to more questions, as everything associated with this particular elf tends to do.

What can be established is the following.

In his seventy-ninth year, Steele came out of a forest somewhere to the north of Waterdeep — the exact location has been speculated upon by a number of individuals but confirmed by none — and found himself in the company of the following persons: Kennyth Thames, a Priest of Oghma; Gaebril Tremalkin, an archer of some skill who seemed at the time to aspire to the paladin's mantle under Selûne; Kyahs Agbar, a Priest of Torm; Phineous Reoul, a wild mage of Mystra who was, by all accounts, as dangerous to his companions as he was to his enemies and who had a documented tendency to accidentally transform himself into a winged cat; one Gable, a thief; and Tallanvor Durinbold, a warrior. Rychel Voordan, a ranger whose later exploits I have occasion to mention elsewhere in this document, joined them not long after the group's formation. Upon the death of Tallanvor, his brother Bram took his place. There was also Edgar Grimbold, a swashbuckler and monk who went by the alias Stuart Bold, and I confess I have not yet been able to establish why.

This collection of individuals called themselves the Warders of Waterdeep. I have spent some time wondering what, precisely, they believed themselves to be warders of, and I have concluded that the answer is simply: everything. Which is the sort of answer that tends to cause a great deal of difficulty for everyone involved.

The first significant episode I have been able to document with any confidence is their collision with an organization called Company Dalael in the summer of 1368. The Dalael were drow — worshippers of Vhaeraun — who had come to the surface and established themselves, with considerable success, within the noble families of this very city. By 1368, they had fingers in the Anteos, Dezlentyr, Eltorchul, Margaster, Umbrusk, and Wavesilver houses. The heads of the company had gone so far as to marry into or father children by women of the Anteos, Dezlentyr, and Eltorchul families. They had also, with characteristic elegance, cultivated the hatred that one Breton Durinbold bore for his brother Tallanvor — who happened to be a Warder — as a tool of influence over the Durinbold house.

I note this last detail because it is, in miniature, the entire story of what it meant to fight the Dalael.

Both the Warders and the Dalael were, that summer, searching for the same location: an island southwest of Neverwinter, somewhere in the Sea of Swords. The Warders sought a keep called Blethmark. The Dalael sought a drow fortress they named Kulgen Sussun — which is to say, in the drow tongue, the Fortress of Light. These proved to be the same place, Blethmark having been built atop a structure of considerably greater antiquity. In the sub-levels of that fortress, the Warders encountered a dragon by the name of Bliz'Anioch. The dragon, by all accounts, came very close to ending the matter then and there. Tallanvor managed to relieve it of its tail. The encounter ended in a standoff. The dragon negotiated its escape and was last seen flying east. I am given to understand that these things happen more often in Waterdeep's history than the broadsheets would lead one to believe.

Back in the city, the contest with the Dalael continued in earnest. The Warders were able to raid the Dalael stronghold in North Ward and foil enough of the company's operations that, ultimately, the Dalael withdrew from Waterdeep in something resembling defeat. The only member of their number to remain was Y'morth Dalael, known on the surface as the elf Dally, who had no great involvement in his family's enterprises and was accepted — with what I can only imagine was a degree of institutional optimism — into the Watchful Order of Magists and Protectors.

What came next is harder to document, because the records are thinner and the events are stranger.

The Warders became entangled in something larger than the Dalael. A drow conspiracy of wider scope, involving noble houses, a hidden drow city far to the north, and the possible destruction of an ancient blue dragon. They also came repeatedly into conflict with a warlock by the name of Hagan, a conjuror of considerable power who favoured invisibility and was rumoured to travel with a familiar — a giant spider that could likewise turn invisible. I mention this because I have come to believe that Hagan, perhaps more than any other single enemy, was responsible for the particular shape that Steele's madness eventually took. When your enemies are both drow and an invisible warlock, and both are masters of misdirection, manipulation, and literal concealment, it would seem to produce, in a sufficiently determined individual, a rather extreme reaction. Those who knew Steele in later years described an individual who attacked enemies as quickly, as violently, and as publicly as possible — as if to burn the very concept of subterfuge out of every encounter before it could take root.

I find that I understand him, which disturbs me.

At some point the Warders descended into the Underdark. I do not have a full account of what occurred there, and I am not certain that I want one. What I know is that when Steele, Kennyth Thames, and Gaebril Tremalkin were eventually recovered, all three were changed. Steele had the worst of it. Something he had consumed underground — a mushroom, a grub, the accounts differ — had done considerable damage to his mind. A fungus had scarred his face. He had to be carried out. It fell to Kennyth, through his connections at the Font of Knowledge, and to Gaebril, through the temple of Selûne, to bring in healers sufficient to restore Steele's functioning. Whether the restoration was as complete as it was reported to be, I cannot say. What I can say is that the elf who emerged from that process was one whom witnesses described, in the years that followed, as someone who muttered to himself and wrung his hands as though perpetually distressed. I will leave the reader to draw their own conclusions.

The Warders came apart, in the end, not because of drow, or warlocks, or dragons, or the Underdark.

They came apart because of Breton Durinbold.

The same Breton Durinbold whom the Dalael had cultivated for years as a source of leverage over the Durinbold family; the same man whose hatred for his brother Tallanvor had been nurtured and shaped as a tool — he was, in the end, the instrument of the group's destruction. The Warders formally dissolved following the assassination of Tallanvor Durinbold, his brother Bram, and the Priest of Torm, Kyahs Agbar, in an assault upon the Durinbold Family Villa. The records name Breton as Kyahs' killer. Whether the Dalael, by then driven from the city, had some hand in orchestrating this, I cannot confirm. I suspect they would have found it gratifying.

What followed the killings was the usual spectacle. Magistrate Richter — a man I have found to have been on the Dalael payroll since at least 1368 — moved to prosecute the surviving Warders. They were saved from that particular fate only through the intervention of a paladin named Harkas Kormallis, whose good offices apparently extended to keeping Watch Magistrates from pursuing vendettas on behalf of drow companies.

By 1371 and 1372, what remained of the Warders was scattered. Kennyth Thames had become Loremaster at the Font of Knowledge, which strikes me as the most sensible outcome for any member of this group. Rychel Voordan had nominally retired from adventuring, which I interpret loosely given what I know of his subsequent activities. Gable had retired entirely, whereabouts unknown. Phineous Reoul had likewise vanished, whereabouts similarly unknown, which, given his tendency to accidentally turn people to stone, I choose to regard as a mercy. Edgar Grimbold had spent time in the Eastern Realms and had only recently returned to Waterdeep, where he had fallen in with a community of Chessentan immigrants in the Little Chessenta district.

And Gaebril Tremalkin — who had once harboured aspirations toward the paladin's path under the light of Selûne — was, by 1372, a paladin of Shar.

I have thought about this at some length. I find that I cannot adequately account for it, except to observe that the Underdark changes people, that long exposure to darkness tends to leave marks, and that Waterdeep is, at bottom, a city that rewards adaptability above almost every other quality.

As for Steele himself — that particular account, I have reason to believe, is far from over.

I find I am less eager to press forward with it than I was when I first began.

One platinum piece per page.

I am beginning to think the rate was set by someone who knew exactly what they were asking for, and found the prospect entertaining.
