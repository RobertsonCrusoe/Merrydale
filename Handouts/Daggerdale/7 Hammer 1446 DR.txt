Dearest Sister :

We followed the coastline in a small but fast one-masted galley.  We could stay quite close you see, avoiding the largest of the pirates that plague the coasts here.  We rarely stopped until we came to Chessenta, and here we lingered for nearly a month.  There is a great deal of political conflict between the Chessentans and the nation of our destination. 
I should say that despite the language barrier (they speak no Chondathan, but the trade-tongue works well enough here), the foreign and almost drow-like look of these people, their strange gods (though I think their dog-headed Ho-Ka-Tep is the same as our Amaunator), their strange customs (I was nearly jailed for kicking a stray cat out of my way), and bizarre architecture, I rather like these people.  For starters, they keep elves as slaves, and I never heard that sort of thing was possible.  I've had many a long night of drinking their spiced wines with owners of these slaves, and I still can't understand how they manage it. 
The city I was in, Luthcheq, was beautiful in its own way, though I'm told it was much finer 100 years ago (what wasn't?)  They don't tolerate wizards here, which is sort of what I had expected.  The factory here was the finest of the lot, and the factor had met the old patriarch a few times, and was fond of him, and treated us well.  I think he knew more than he let on.  Anyway, we were comfortable there, and really didn't want to leave, and now I wish we hadn't.  I could have spent the rest of my days there happy, but its an expensive place to live, and our money wouldn't last forever. 
The final leg of the journey was perilous, and now we were outside the reach of the old patriarch; these were lands he told us he had no friends in, and that if they got the slightest idea that we were friendly with the Chessentans, there'd be trouble. But this is the agreement we made, and knowing that the man has reach this far, I'm doubly determined to keep up my end of the bargain, and I know the others are too. 
From Luthcheq, we marched and marched and marched, and the Chessentans (whom I consider mostly 'our people', if you catch my meaning) gave way to the mean-faced Imaskari, who are kinder, I would later learn, than the frightening people that have become my neighbours.  I write this now from a fine apartment in our little villa, which our money bought cheaply here (the old patriarch was right about that, and his factor made the arrangements for us to our benefit).  We have a couple of slaves, which is new for me.  It isn't at all what you might expect, from the stories.  They have their own 'guild' of sorts, and standards of service, and I have to sign all sorts of things to buy one, promising not to do anything terrible, at the top of the list is the strict forbiddance of introducing them to our gods and a few of their own (more of the dog-heads and such here).  Though I like many places I passed through much better than this, we are comfortable, the money will go far, and indeed, I will be able to do something I thought impossible; send some back home to my sister, in the hopes that I won't be the only Alavandor to climb out of poverty.  A bag of precious stones accompanies this message, and I hope you'll put it to work.  I never made a copper as an artist, and the prospects of your career as a courtesan have likely dimmed (no offense, mind you, my lovely).   
Yes, I expect the best thing to do here is to continue mimic the old patriarch (there are no conditions to the contrary after all), and rather than making a show of being a merchant, actually become a merchant.  I sincerely hope you'll make a go of it, and that some day you will come and visit your brother and his kin, now adorned in our simple linen wraps like the locals, whom I've drawn you a picture of below.

The Gargoyles and the Worms 
I won't dwell too much on the specific battle that took place on the morning of the 29th, as many others have already done so, and probably with more authority than me.  For I ran as soon as the rocks started falling from the sky and didn't look back when I heard the ground erupt, and the purple worms emerge to cause so much mayhem.  Suffice it to say, this was a dastardly attack, and one that proved fatal to seventeen people in attendance that day.  Though I would dare guess that Melegaunt wished for more.  This was a costly attack for him to launch against the dale; it required a great cost in magical resources to command the worms.  The gargoyles, Brother Darred later informed us, are from the Grilmholst Clan, and could be considered very expensive mercenaries bought and paid for by Melegaunt.  They won't be happy that they lost ten of their number in this attack.  Finally, Melegaunt tipped his hand completely; this was his declaration of war, and his naked attempt to interfere with the retaking of Thistle Ridge. 
If not for the loss of so many souls, I should say that we emerged the victor; many heroes were assembled, and I wish I would have been brave enough to stand my ground, if only to watch as Sun Blade, White Cloak, Chrysalis, and other heroes fought off enemies from both beneath the ground and above the clouds.  I am told that the final charge to the centre of the arena by all of those mentioned, as well as common men who charged with rocks, broken bits of wood and discarded weapons was incredible to behold.  Blade and bow, spell and oath were brought quickly to bear on the large menace, and in less than thirty seconds, it slumped down dead, and a great roar of triumph could be heard by all within the walls of town. 
The effort to help the wounded and see to the dead was put together quickly and flawlessly, and in under twenty minutes, the area was secure, the chaos over, and everything brought to good order. 
The attack that morning did put an end to the contest, but not as Melegaunt intended.  It is clear that the gargoyles were focused on the contestant groups; all four in attendance were singled out by these monsters as they descended, and indeed, they took the lives of both the Captain of the White Cloaks, Tara Hilltop, and the forester from the Standing Stone League, Madelaine Meroviskyr, late of Harrowdale.   
The attack, having disrupted the final match of the contest, created an unanticipated conclusion, which we would discover the following day. 
In the immediate aftermath of the attack, Jeska Monarch and her team quickly directed nearby Magistrates to set up a casualty collection point, and by less than an hour later, all wounded were tended too, and all souls accounted for properly. Once the chaos was over, and wounds seen to, she was heard to address the crowd with the following words; 
I am Jeska Monarch, assumed ambassador to the embassy here in your town. I am not that. I am in my culture just a young elf, a mere 270 years of age sent here by my own superiors for reasons I do not understand. 

I have formed a team, "Chrysalis" as you are aware... and we have won the right through competition to cleanse Thistle Ridge of that which that resides there. 
However, this competition has brought evil and grief to this town. 
I do not know you, but I know this was an attack by our common enemies. 
We face a dire foe, and only as a community can we prevail.  
Shall we forge onward?  
Yes. 
We will burn out the corpse of the worm! We will fill the hole with rock, sand and mortar. We will plant a garden! 
There was a bit more, but what most people heard was a commitment by the elf, whose fame has grown greatly every day, to 'burn the corpse of the worm' and to 'plant a garden'.  These actions prudently calling to the deific natures of the prominent faiths in town.  This was the first mortar in the great work she would undertake this week to mollify the relationship between Chrysalis, the Amaunatori, the Chaunteans and the rest of the dale. 
The toil that followed I won't recount, but I can see from my window in the Yellow Abbeye that there will indeed be a beautiful garden where once the arena stood.  It has all been removed, and as I write this some days later, I can see the Chaunteans planting a great many flowers and fruit trees at that site, while Amaunatori Magistrates are measuring the grounds to erect a monument to the heroes and unity that won the day. 
Nemia Ul-Nishaadrhi – The Ghost Hunter Meets with The Hedge Wizard 

The afternoon following the attack in the arena is something of a blur now, trying to remember it a few days later.  There was so much going on, and Chrysalis was flung to the corners of the Falls, carrying out myriad errands and discussions, most of which I missed.  However, just before dinner, Jasper and I returned to the Embassy.  He had finally taken note of the state of his person (he had not washed since the attack, and was a dreadful sight), and thought to put on a clean shirt and face.  Waiting for him was the Vaasan Ghost Hunter, Nemia.   
I am not sure if she would be considered a great beauty in her homeland, but I can tell you that she turns heads here in the Dales.  She is pale, and her short white hair is unusual.  She is much skinnier than the girls of the dales, especially the Chaunteans, and could be mistaken for a lean teenage boy in poor lighting.  However, her face is one of the prettiest I've known, with proud cheek bones, perfectly formed lips and piercing blue eyes.  And her gravelly foreign voice so filled with mystery and confidence is spellbinding.  Surely, she's caught the eye of my good friend, though his interest in her is surely as academic as it is anything else. 
Ms. Ul-Nishaadrhi was waiting for Jasper in his room, and we both veritably stumbled into her as we rolled across the threshold, too caught up in a discussion about the celestial nature and significance of twins to notice our guest.  She was sat cross-legged upon Jasper's bed in his incredibly messy room, and had the four dolls recovered from the Clarandal Farm and also from Jaspers things in front of her.  She smiled to see Jasper, and nodded respectfully to me.  We had come to know each other through much shared time in the library, and though I find her mannerisms unusual and often unsettling, she has always been polite with me. 
"Krasivyye mal'chiki" she started, in her thick mother tongue.  "I am pleased to meet you both here.  Come, sit with me" and she patted the rug beside the bed with one outstretched leg, and nodded towards it.  "Da, da, sit sit my krasivyyei.  We talk of ghosts, no" and she held up three of the dolls in her hands towards us, as Jasper and I both took a spot on the floor like a pair of grammar school freshmen, looking up at her bashfully. 
"First, I offer gift" she continued.  At this, she reached into her cloak, where dozens of pouches, bags and fetishes hang, and produced a sheaf of papers, neatly folded and bound in a blue silk ribbon.  "Tseremoniya govoryashchikh mertvetsov" she said, and then stared hard at Jasper with a wry smile on her face. 
"UmYour ritual for talking with the dead!" Jasper whispered, with more confidence than I usually see him imbued with.   
"Da, very good umnyy mal'chik.  My clever, sweet boy.  I have made copy for you.  You study, and maybe find materials for call dead someday.  We practice one last time together, da?"  Jasper nodded and smiled.  They would indeed carry out the ritual a couple of nights later, but let me not get ahead of myself. 
"But now, Nemia will tell you what she knows that you do not."  And with that, she picked up the single doll that represents as far as we can tell the person of Alaventa Alavaxi.  "Bednaya devochka.  Sweet, poor girl.  She is dead. Her soul is at rest.  Her story is important, but her role in all this is how you saytool, or path.  Seek her not, but know her."  Jasper and I both drew deep breaths at this revelation, and before we could inquire further, she held a finger to her lips to quiet us, and continued. 
"Now, I tell you something else you don't know, khozyain mertvykh" and these last words she said with great emphasis which I took to be sarcasm towards Jasper.  She smiled while we looked at each other.  I had figured it out just before him. 
"UmMaster of theumDead?" he asked in a hushed tone. 
Nemia has a way of laughing with her lips pursed together, without any movement from her chest or shoulders which normally accompanies this exclamation in others, and I find it unsettling.  She did just that by way of answering Jasper. 
"Umnyy mal'chik.  You will catch up, hopefully to yourself and your own fate.  It may otherwise catch you if you are foolish, or slow, or choose the wrong friends.  But now, let me talk plainly, for I do not wish to see so pretty a boy come to bad end.   
"You were not sent here by your friend, Lady Sulwood."  Jasper again took in a deep breath and made a confused sound, which appeared to delight the Vaasan. "Hmm, no.  You were summoned here, by a weaver of fate.  Which one, Nemia cannot say.  But you will face your fate, on river and ridge, in forest and bog, in ruin and cave.  You will face the dead and those that try to master and profane them.  The dead are waiting for you.  Be always prepared, milyy."  With this, she rose up gracefully and took one step from the bed to stand between our two seated persons on the floor, facing away from us.  She playfully pulled our heads to her hips and tussled our hair.  I thought I heard her give just a slight moan and take a deep breath before laughing in her way again. 
"You have more questions, but Nemia is out of time.  I have other inquiries to make, mertvyye i zhivyye dushi.  Nemia has much to discover.  Though not so much as you, Krasivyye mal'chiki.  We shall all know more together some day, I am sure.  Fate demands it." 
And with that she was gone, and Jasper and I were completely exasperated by the encounter.  My heart raced for ten minutes after she left, and Jas couldn't stop pacing and talking to himself.   
Nemia Ul-Nishaadrhi captivates and confounds. 
A Much-Needed Rest 
I imagine that anyone reading this chronicle might be surprised to learn what happened next.  There were so many loose ends to take care of; the conclusion of the contest, questions to ask of a dozen people around the Falls, an expedition to plan, soldiers to muster, companies to form, and more besides.  But as Jasper and I came downstairs from our encounter with Nemia, we found Master Burgundy politely shooing well-wishers and other visitors out of the common area of the Embassy, assisted by a half dozen women from the Halfling Auxilliary.  The little matrons helped him by pleasantly inviting the guests to their rooms or the door, and then exiting themselves after bowing and exchanging some small gifts (something the little folk are fond of, and the rest of us would do well to emulate).  The noise of the boots and shoes and well-wishes and farewells gave way to a tremendous snoring, issuing from the person of Ser Rowan, who was presently asleep in a chair by the fire, with a smile to match his girth. 

Master Burgundy walked purposefully towards me and Jas, a smile on his face and his hands clasped in front of him.  "There's a good man" he said, looking at me.  "Can I help you find your cloak, sir? I'm at your service".  I turned to Jas and noticed that he could barely stand straight or keep his eyes open. 
I took a quick inventory in my mind then, not of what was to be done, but what had been done in only ten days.  It fills some dozens of pages in the chronicle, and the briefest possible summary includes the following great deeds, to say none of the countless lesser ones that Chrysalis performed at this time; 
 Complete strangers forged a new company together, taking oaths and vows, and undertaking a great contest against renowned heroes and infamous scoundrels 

 A legendary expedition to the hinterland of Thistle Ridge, which included battles with a great swarm of giant wasps, strange fey fungus-creatures, and a protracted raid-turned-siege against the Yellow Tooth warband of Gindlebad in which scores of goblins and their monstrous pets were fought and slain before a desperate escape back into Cormanthor and civilization  The storied outcome to the treacherous declaration of war by Netheril, delivered by the gargoyle mercenaries and magically-controlled purple worms of the villainous Melegaunt and his henchwoman, Loki Mouvranesh (who has surfaced again, just in time to plague our heroes) 

That and a hundred other things I have forgotten or never knew.  The point of this list is to make our reader understand one thing; that by dinner time of the 29th day of Ches in the year 1483 DR, Chrysalis was spent.  They had done a years' battle in ten days, and retired to a well-earned rest. 
Master Burgundy brought me to my cloak, and placed in my hands a small basket.  "You're much too skinny, Master Vessuvian.  Academia is hard work, and the brain needs as much fuel as a black smith's forearm.  Especially when that work takes you to so many places outside your library.  There's a good man."  And with this I was bowing awkwardly at the proper little gentleman, and found myself on the front steps of the Embassy, headed back to the Yellow Abbeye. 
As I write this, I am still enjoying the trappings of that basket; the jar of candied cherries, the sour dough bread dripping with butter and honey, and the flask of apple cider that was in the basket.  I wish I had a quarter of Master Burgundy's hospitality.

The 30th of Ches would prove to be as busy a day as any that Chrysalis had endured the tenday previous.  Thankfully, there were no battles with swords and spells this day, but many of wits and cunning, politics and business.

There was more, but I think it most relevant to illuminate the triple-front threat created by Netheril and her agents.  I will do my best to support Jeska and my friends in Chrysalis, and will remain vigilant against the Enemy Within.

Journal of Tergus Clarandal Ches 7, 1431 
From the Chapel Hospital of Castle Radiance, I write this entry with a hand unsteady from the wounds of battle and the haze of healing herbs. Carried in on a litter, my body bears the marks of our most recent skirmish, but my spirit remains unbroken. The Chapel Hospital, with its sanctified halls and diligent clerics of Amaunator, provides solace and care to those of us who have faced the brutalities of war. 
As I lay here, memories from my youth in Thistle Ridge come unbidden to my mind. It was the year 1416, a time of innocence and confusion. I was but eight years old when the Alavaxi arrived in our village. Their appearance stirred a great commotion among the elders, my father included. I recall the heated discussions, the worry etched on their faces as they debated the fate of these mysterious newcomers. 
The Alavaxi, with their strange customs and distant origins, were met with suspicion. There was fear that the Church of Amaunator would condemn them as witches, for such was the fate of many who bore the mark of the unknown. I remember the older Alavaxi, afflicted by a peculiar skin condition, being sent to Castle Radiance. They were to be treated in the Chapel Hospital, or so we were told. 
Today, as I lay in this very place, I found myself compelled to inquire about those long-lost souls. The cleric I approached responded with a stern gaze and a terse command to mind my business. The Alavaxi, it seems, have vanished from memory and record alike. There are no traces of them within these hallowed walls, and I am left to wonder what fate befell them. 
Were they truly treated and cured, or did the fear and suspicion of the unknown lead to a darker outcome? My mind wrestles with these questions as I grapple with the pain of my injuries and the duty that lies ahead. The path of the Sun Blades is one of austerity, discipline, and unwavering focus, yet these memories remind me that not all battles are fought with sword and shield. 
As a sergeant of the 2nd Company, I have seen much and learned more. The world is filled with mysteries and shadows that our training cannot always pierce. Perhaps one day, the truth of the Alavaxi will come to light. Until then, I shall continue to serve with honor and strive to uphold the principles that guide us. 
For now, I must rest and recover, trusting in the care of the clerics and the strength of Amaunator's light to heal my wounds. The journey ahead is long, and the battles unending, but I remain steadfast in my resolve. 
Journal of Tergus Clarandal Tarsakh 21, 1434 
The quiet of the night finds me once again in the solitude of my quarters at Castle Radiance. Earlier today, while standing watch on the western battlement, I found myself in conversation with my captain. We were enjoying the sweet, crisp taste of Dale Burgundy apples, a cherished reminder of my home in Thistle Ridge. The captain remarked on the unrivaled quality of these apples, attributing their excellence to the fertile soil of the Tesh Valley. 
His comment, laced with a touch of humor, extended to the fertility of my dear wife, Senthia. It is true that despite my infrequent visits, she has blessed me with a child each year since our marriage in 1427. We shared a laugh over my apparent virility, but the captain's words lingered in my mind long after our watch had ended. 
As the night deepens, I find myself contemplating the peculiar abundance of life in Thistle Ridge. My father, who had two wives before my mother, was childless until he came to this valley. With my mother, his third wife, he fathered me within the first year of their union in Thistle Ridge. This pattern of fertility, repeated now in my own marriage, seems more than mere coincidence. 
Could there be something unique about Thistle Ridge, something that enhances the fruitfulness of those who dwell there? The thought stirs a mix of curiosity and reverence within me. The people of Thistle Ridge, with their ancient ties to the land and their worship of the Old Faith, might hold some ancient knowledge or blessing that influences this phenomenon. 
Such musings lead me to ponder the broader implications of our connection to the land. We, the Sun Blades, pride ourselves on our discipline and martial prowess, but perhaps there are forces at work beyond our understanding. Forces that shape our lives in ways we cannot readily see or measure.
