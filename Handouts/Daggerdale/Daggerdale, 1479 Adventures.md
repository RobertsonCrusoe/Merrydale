# Daggerdale, 1479 Adventures
# Source: The Sieges Perilous wiki (backup dated 2010-02-21)

The Old Skull Inn

“Just plain un-dales-like, I says” is what you must have heard a dozen times in the tap room at the Old Skull Inn after you announced that you were on your way to Daggerdale.  Sitting at a table with three local septuagenarians, you are given a colourful accounting of the history and character of Daggerdale. 

“Those folk up there just aren’t right,” one old codger is remarking, when he is interrupted by one of his two cronies.

“Never damned have been, what’s more!  It’s all ‘Sun God keep you’ and ‘Sun God damn you’ just now, but it wasn’t long before that but half of ‘em worshipped the dog-humping shadow bitch herself!  They are just damned…I dunno…”

Another of the old men around the table at which you are sitting chimes in, filling in for his stammering comrade “…extreme…” he says slowly and with a pointing finger and squinting eyes.  

The second man nods assent, and continues his line of thought.  “Extreme.  That’s what I was saying.  Don’t know how to take just a bit of this, and a bit of that.  Always have to jump in head damned first, whether it’s damned good or damned evil, damned right or damned wrong.  Where else could you find the sodding Zhents dug in for so many decades…”

“Or vampires!  That’s what started it all, see” the first old man exclaims.  “In my day, they called it Sorrow Dale, for all the misery that was clutching the place…” the first man is cut off again by the second old man.

“Oh dammit, Aubus!  They never damned called it damned Sorrow Dale or damned Misery Dale or goddamned Damned Dale, and anything else you says they did, not in your damned day!  It’s been Dagger damned dale the whole damned time.  But you’re right about the vampires.  That was in my great, great, great grand daddy’s day, and he used to say the damned blood suckers sucked the, sucked the…I dunno…”

The third man squints his eyes and wags an accusing finger at to the first old man, Aubus, and slowly says “…soul…sucked the soul outa the valley herself.”  He nods slowly with a look of conviction as though he has announced something as indisputable as the time of day.  

Aubus nods at his friend, and then turns to you.  “When Zeb’s right, Zeb’s right.  Never been the same since, and that was hundreds of years ago.  They lived in uncertain times up there before…Uncertain Times.  That’s why they called it ‘Dagger Dale’; because you used to have to keep a dagger in each boot, because you never knew which side you was going to get it from.”

The second man interrupts Aubus again.  “They called it Dagger damned dale because of the magic pig-sticker they used to kill the damned vampires, professor Aubus.  Anyhow, that ain’t neither here nor goddamned there now any goddamned way.  They may as well call it ‘Sunny damned dale’ or ‘Amaunator’s Arse Farts Sunshine Dale’ for all the ranting and preaching they do up there.  They go from vampires to damned Sun Gods, from Zhents to damned Harpers…they just seem to go…from, well…”

“Extremes, Diff.”  Zeb finishes his friend’s thought.  “They go from one extreme to the other.”

“Zeb’s right again, friend.  You need a fill-up on that tankard?”  Aubus calls for more beer for the table, but you can see that he and his two cronies are just pretending to sip at theirs.  “Where was we?  Oh yeah; here’s the thing about Daggerdale you have to get your head around.  They never knew peace up there.  Probably never will.  When the rest of us was ploughing our fields and our wives in peace and comfort, the folk up in the Tesh Valley were still fighting with goblins and ogres and giants and that sort of thing, and when that wasn’t enough, they had evil wizards, evil Zhents, evil vampires, evil beholders, evil paladins, and anything else evil you can think of to chew on’em from the inside-out half the time, and that hardened the lot of them.”  Diff and Zeb just look down into the tankards they are clutching on the table, and keep listening.

“Yup.  It hardened ‘em” Aubus continues.  “When the Hard Times came, well, they should’a just moved on.  No one would have said nuthin’, or talked ‘em down.  But they stuck it out; through dead gods, armies of orcs and goblins, plagues, the Netherese, all of it.  They stuck it out.  Now they got Amaunator up there, and things run like a gnomish clock, or so I’m told, but you’ll hear a lot of us say they aren’t like regular dales folk.  But the truth is; they are the most dales-like among us.  There’s nothin’ more defines a man of the dales than stickin’ it out.”

The Old Tethyamar Trail

There are many things about Daggerdale that make it stand out from the other dales, not the least of which is the Old Tethyamar trail.  This is one of the last true roads left in the Dalelands, its maintenance possible only by the economic and military surpluses of Daggerdale.  While Cormanthor has closed in tight about every other road in the area, and choked out many completely, The Old Tethyamar Trail continues to serve as a viable conduit for people and goods to travel.  The vast wealth of the Whitegate Mine not only keeps the road open, but requires it for the continuance of trade that the mine has fostered.

Cormanthor is constantly trying to grow over the road and devour it, but the Sun Blades are constantly cutting back the voracious oaks while they patrol the road.  They have managed to keep the road about 20’ wide in most places.

Not only has the forest been kept at bay, but many parts of the road that go through soft or wet ground have been paved with large stones, most of this work having been done in the last 20 years.  There are small stone bridges in excellent condition, and one large one over the Tethyamar River which is a point of pride for many of the nearby residents.

Travel on this well-kept road is not free of its dangers, though.

As you top a hill about a mile away from the bridge, you can see small tendrils of smoke rising up from the forest ahead.  The caravan-master that hired you on for the journey to Dagger Falls decides to send you and a couple other scouts up ahead to check things out, before the wagon train approaches.

Approaching the bridge cautiously, your small scouting party gets first a smell of the situation.  From a couple hundred yards away, with a light breeze in your face, you can smell burning flesh.  This slows your careful approach to a slow and measured creep, and before long, rounding a bend in the road obscured by a thick copse of oaks and underbrush, you can see a single smouldering body partially upright, partially hanging from a steel crucifix, its limbs blackened, and it’s head burnt to a charcoal-like lump, with the skin blackened and receding from its death-maw, to reveal angry white teeth left in a perpetual snarl.

Nearby the smouldering husk, three other men are hanging from an incredibly thick bough of an oak, their arms dangling bloodless and handless at their sides.  Edging up to the scene of the carnage, you can see that the three men have had their hands cut off, and that nearby is a pile of hands.  One of the scouts with you says “Sun Blades were just here.  These were bandits.  The Sun Blades cut off the hands of all the bandits they catch; they burn one, hang half, and let the other half go.”  He pokes around in the pile of hands, and nods.  “Must be about 12 or 14 hands in this pile.  We should have a safe trip for the next day, anyway.  That smoke is as much a signal for any other would-be highwaymen than anything else.”

Soon the caravan is passing by the grisly scene, and over the bridge and into the dale.  Though the day is free of any violence, every mile or so you pass by an old site similar to the one you left behind.  Here and there are found burnt stakes, some with bits of charred bone at their base.  These are often accompanied by ropes in trees, some of these with bits of bone and sun-dried flesh still dangling from them, and by little scattered piles of finger bones that have been picked at by small creatures.  At one point, you pass by a similar site, but adjacent to this one, partially obscured by the growing forest, is a little grave yard; about a dozen stone-covered graves mark the resting place of nameless travelers who did not fare so well against the bandits and monsters of the wood.

By the end of your first day of travel up the road, you come to Crossed Blades Farm.

Farmers in the Dale

As your caravan approaches the walled settlement up ahead, you can see that there are a number of trails of smoke rising from a number of chimneys, and this being late spring, you can only surmise that there is a bit of production here.  A smithy, a baker or two, a brewer.  Who knows?

At any rate, once you get within a few hundred metres of the farm up ahead, the road becomes firmly paved with smooth, even stones, and it opens into a large staging area in front of the main gates to the walled settlement.  From here, the paved road goes all the way around the farm on its east side, and then curves back to find another main gate on the north side, where the road then continues north towards Dagger Falls.  In the paved staging area, which is about 150’ wide x 100’ deep, there is another road which heads due-east, with an old wooden sign in a contrasting state of disrepair that says “Castle Daggerdale”.  That road is not paved, and looks to be largely unused.

Ahead of you, there is a 15’ high wall that stretches across the southern face of the settlement.  It is several hundred feet long, and has a pair of stout stone towers at both ends, and a fortified gatehouse in the middle, which you are approaching.  Having been spied a long way off, your caravan is met by walls and towers stationed by many men, perhaps 20 or more, all standing at alert and watching the incoming visitors.  One of the caravaners is heard to say “This is a farm?” during your approach.

Outside the gates there is a small group of armoured men with yellow tabards with the crest of the Sun Blades prominently displayed.  The master caravanner gets off his horse and approaches the men, the leader of which seems to recognize the merchant.  They exchange some words, gesticulating in a friendly manner, and the merchant waves up the rest of the caravan, while a couple of the guards outside the gates head into the settlement.

Marketplace

The caravan you are with has been here before, obviously.  And why not?  The road is relatively safe and business is good.  Before many orders are passed around, you can see some of the more seasoned traders among the caravan starting in immediately to a number of tasks; there are tents being set up at the edge of the paved area, horses and other animals with the caravan are being herded to the wooden pens found on the west side of the paved area, where there is water and hay waiting for them, and some tables are produced from some of the larger wagons, and some of the smaller ones are turned into make-shift tables.  Before an hour has passed, a small market has been set up, and it is clear that the merchants intend to do as much business here as possible.

It is at least an hour before anyone passes in or out of the gates except for guards, however.  Most of the hired muscle gets out of the way and looms about the gates, some trading news and stories, waiting for admittance into the compound.  On the other side of the portcullis, there is a growing number of villagers gathering, taking in the spectacle of the market that is setting up in the staging area.

Before the portcullis is opened, you can see off in the distance, across the ploughed fields, tracking a course along the forest’s edge, two groups of mounted riders that have, one would surmise, come out of the north gate of the farm, split into two groups, and reconnoitred the forest and fields nearby, to make sure that there are no uninvited visitors hiding and waiting.  When they meet again behind you, at the opening in the forest which the road and the caravan has emerged from, one waves a yellow flag, and the Sun Blade Lieutenant motions for the portcullis to be raised.  As it goes up, there is a gentle rush of people in both directions, and both groups exchange pleasant salutations and pardons for getting in each other’s way.

You watch as dozens of villagers pour out to examine the wares that the merchants are displaying in the make-shift market.  There is much coin and goods exchanging hands before long, and you notice that the dalesmen don’t generally haggle, instead paying the asked price with smiles and thanks.  The locals here have also brought out goods for sale, and some do change hands, though the merchants aren’t interested in most of it.

Inside the Farm

You make your way into the walled compound after a while, and look out upon the most orderly village you have ever seen.  The area inside the walls is several hundred feet on each side, with a small river bisecting it (and disappearing under the east and west walls).  On the near side of the river is the village; most of the buildings are crammed in tight like you might expect in a city, which leaves about 90% of the space inside the walls open ground.  These grounds are covered in immaculate gardens and some animal pens, and barns of every size are found at the periphery of the settlement, some hard-upon the walls.  In one corner of the grounds is a small apple orchard which grows the brightest apples you have ever seen, and one corner has what appears to be a small vineyard, though how grapes could grow in this climate, you are unsure.

The village itself is clean and orderly.  Most of the buildings are very new, and almost all are stone.  Some two-story buildings have wooden upper floors, and there is an occasional 3 story building.  The village is arrayed about the large stone cathedral, which sits hard upon the eastern wall.  The streets of the village radiate out from in front of the square in front of the cathedral, so that from above it might resemble a half-circle, divided like a pie.  The streets are all smartly paved.

You can’t help but think that this place looks more like a village from a story rather than a real place.

The Monastery

The monastery which forms the centre piece of the village is the largest building there.  It has a 150’ x 75’ footprint, and a large, 60’ tower at each corner which have clearly been designed for defence.  The place looks to be more fortress than monastery, but it is decorated with stained glass on the upper levels, and the grand entry with its deep-set ribbed arches and high marble stair case in front gives it something of an elegant feel.  The priests and monks of the monastery are mostly in the large common square in front of it, carrying on their daily duties, which today include cleaning a barrel of fish, and shearing a small herd of sheep.

As you take a moment to appreciate the stout fortress cathedral, you notice that the roads which radiate out from the place all line up with the various towers, so that a command of every approach to the place is achieved.  Indeed, one of the first things that caught your eye in the monastery was the glint of a metal helmet from an archer in one of the towers that could have taken shots at you from over a hundred yards away, down the straight-run of road.

The Castle Gate (Inn)

There is only one Inn in the village, and that is the Castle Gate.  This is a large building, and seemingly an original structure; the stones of this prodigious place are rough-hewn compared to the dressed stone of the rest of the town (the monastery especially), and they are darkened and covered in ivy.  This building has smaller windows with glass that is nearly opaque with age and grime, and the ceilings are only a hair over 6’ high.  Despite the odd, antiquated proportions of the place, it is a large structure, with three floors, and a large, well-appointed tap room.

This place has none of the disposable or easy-to-repair wooden chairs and tables of the rougher sort of places that one finds at many inns and taverns.  Rather, the furnishings are mostly leather chairs which have been worked in to a perfection of comfort from decades of use.  The tables are made from dark oak that has decades of patina to make them appear rich and deep, and most are carved with an assortment of incongruous themes and decorations.

And there is alcohol.  Of all the things you were told to expect in Daggerdale, booze was not one of them.  You know that the Church of Amaunator frowns on consumption of alcohol, and the straight-laced and pristine looking streets, buildings and guards of the village made you doubt you’d find a drop of beer here, but in the dim and smoky tap room, you can see a dozen men in a couple different groups enjoying beer and wine, and a couple of the other caravan guards are already up at the bar getting pitchers.  

A pair of beautiful young women wearing emerald-green dresses over their slim bodies bring drinks to the guests, and you can see that both prominently wear holy symbols of Chauntea.  Their hair is worn in curly trestles like you might expect for a spring-bride, and they seem to have a limitless well of mirth and joy.  Before you are able to take the whole place in, one of them is at your side, taking your jacket (or your sword) and seating your nearby.  “Wine or beer, my good man?” she asks with a smile that reminds you of the last rays of sun dancing off the gentle waves of a lake.  After she brings the first drink, you reach for your money, but she stays your hand with a gentle touch, and says “Let us wash the road from your throat, my lord.  We are happy to serve you.”

“What is this place?” you find yourself thinking as you eye the drink suspiciously, and the slim beauty that is making her way to the next guest who has just walked in even more suspiciously.  “This drink must be drugged or poison?  Or perhaps…I don’t know what…”  Eventually, you decide that nothing terribly malicious could happen in such a place, and down the finest beer you have ever tasted.  But only the first one was free.

Sun Blades

You never see a single Sun Blade in the taproom of the Inn, but they maintain a constant presence everywhere else.  They are friendly but aloof with strangers, but you can see that most of them get on with most of the villagers well.  They don’t drink but you notice that they seem to have no rules about taking a sliver of cheese or a loaf of bread from whatever charitable villager crosses their path.  While you were in the taproom, you learned that an entire platoon of ‘Blades is always garrisoned here, and that a second came riding into town a few hours before your arrival yesterday.  The additional platoon, 37 strong, is a specialized cavalry platoon that patrols the roads, and you are happy to learn that they will be escorting you north tomorrow.  

The men drinking in the taproom mostly say nothing about the Sun Blades, beyond agreeing that they are useful in times such as these, but there is the odd grumble that you might expect from elderly men who no longer swing a blade.  “Couldn’t hurt’em to pick up a plough once in a while” or “That tall dusky one; whatsis name?  Nevermind, it don’t matter, except he might become your son-in-law if you aren’t careful.”

While you are out looking at the wares in the black smiths shop, a lone Sun Blade approaches you, and identifies himself as Lt. Eversmoore.  He’s a young man, perhaps 24, but he’s big and has a square jaw.  Instead of chain mail, he wears a plate chest piece, and instead of a sword he carries a large axe and a pair of wicked looking daggers on his thighs.  He has a friendly and unassuming way of asking you where you have been, what you have seen and where you are going, and you find it hard to resist answering the man.  When you give him a little white lie, he frowns a bit like he has just learned it will rain tomorrow, and you find yourself correcting your answer, and wondering “why the hell did I just do that?!”.  However, it pays off.  This man is the first that mentions ‘Castle Radiance’ to you.

“We could sure use a man like you up at the Castle.  Castle Radiance, that is.  There is work for men like you, and it pays good.  Go find the Lt. of the dragoons that are in town, and tell him you are thinking of going all the way up to the Castle, and he’ll make good and sure you get as far as town safely.”

It isn’t long before you are talking to another young lieutenant in front of the monastery, who asks you the same line of questions, but not as convincingly as Eversmoore.  You surmise there is a sharp difference in the skills of the garrison officers and the road officers.  The young lt. of the cavalry is gruff and demanding, and you can see that he has a good many more scars than his colleague in the garrison.  At length he informs you that his platoon will be going to town with the caravan, regardless of your interests, and says dismissively “Castle Radiance is to the West of town.  Good luck, adventurer.”
