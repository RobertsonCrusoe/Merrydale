29 Tarsakh, 1483 DR  My Surprise Visit to Greenwood Farm & Ovetchkins Warnings 
I was reluctant to make the trip to Greenwood farm.  It is very busy in my shop before the Greengrass celebrations, and for the most part, I prefer the sights, sounds and safety of the town, but it had been a long time since I was out of doors, and the small caravan resupplying the Sunblades was happy to have a wizard in company, given all the dreadful things that have happened in the Dale the last couple of months. 

But Im happy I went.  The weather was fair, with cool spring air, a rejuvenating sunlight and the sounds of birds all round, and the company was just as good. Under contract to the Sunblades to resupply the platoon helping my friends, Chrysalis, The Standing Stone League sent its most reliable caravaner, Captain Hardrada, to deliver the next months supplies to Greenwood. Hardrada is a good man with many rare tales, and makes excellent company on the road. Knowing my lack of experience in the wilderness, he is a good mentor and aid, pointing out this important geographical feature and that, and making sure I dont put a foot anywhere I shouldnt. 

Ovee was happy as you can imagine, even though he prefers to fly about at night. He flew overhead keeping an eye on the road ahead when he wasnt distracted by a field mouse or garter snake. Indeed, his belly was full by the time we made Gribbons, and he sat a while on one of the wagons while we turned north, heading through the soon-to-be-golden fields of that fine farm. 

I think if not for the nature of my familiar, I would have found the forest daunting. Even with most of the trees still abud, and mostly naked, it is dark and unwelcoming. For the most part. On the other hand, we passed by a camp of men, a mix of woodsmen and soldiers, who were busy clearing and ditching a damp stretch of the trail. They were friendly and glad to see us, though not so glad as we were to see them; we have enjoyed the fruits of their labour for a couple of miles now, with a good trail that even the heavy-laden wagons traverse with ease.  For Ovee, Cormanthor is heavenly. The tall dark oaks allow him to fly with nearly-perfect cover, and friends and prey are plentiful. 

By 6 bells of the evening, we made Greenwood Farm. The state of the settlement surprised me; I understood this 

to be a brush-choked ruin just a few short weeks ago, but today, there is a fine clearing, a tall stone house with a roof, a sawmill, running water, and the start of a monastery complete with a bread oven. There must be about 100 people here already, and the place is a hive of activity. 
An owl flying in the moonlight

It wasnt hard for me to find my friends; they had returned only a little while earlier, having been on some terrible mission that I wouldnt press them about; Ser Rowans new armour had many nasty gashes, and the garb of nearly all the company showed evidence of terrible wounds restored by powerful magics. I know that they were going to the newly-discovered Temple of Jannath, but I wasnt interested in that, for now.  I was simply here to show my support in person, which I think says a great deal more than a letter (the likes of which Ovetchkin can deliver for me anytime). The commitment to come in the flesh was also good for me; I got to see first-hand what life is like on the frontier, and get some appreciation for the lack of security out here; for despite the fact that a stout 

company of FLD soldiers lead by a storied leader is here, and Chrysalis themselves, there are no walls, or even watch towers. The settlement sits on a naked hill that has, I have learned, already had its share of unwelcome visitors. 

But the view from the small hilltop is extraordinary, and no doubt one of the main reasons it was chosen for settlement in whatever decade that originally happened. From here, one can look to the west and see the majestic Desertsmouth Mountains, and even the smoke from Dagger Falls and some of the other farms besides.  To the east, the valley yawns a promise of adventure and opportunity through a pristine landscape. But looks can be deceiving, as Ill get to in a 

moment. What can also be seen from here, only a few miles away, is a great plume of black smoke from Thistle Ridge Tower, which I understand is now in control of Chrysalis by way of their platoon of Sunblades. And a few miles east of that, small pillars of smoke bely the reason we are all our there today; the occupation of Thistle Ridge by the Yellow Tooth Clan. 

Working my way through the camp to the main building, where I found my friends, and met many friendly settlers and workers, and was astonished to find so many Cormyrean settlers. It is the Cormyreans that are building the monastery at the eastern edge of the farm, and they are an industrious and friendly people, with many tools and no weapons (aside from Faith). 

I found my friends in the large and open central room of the new town hall. They were with Ser Reginald and a few other officers from the FLD. They were going over correspondence from the Aide de Camp in Thistle Ridge Tower, and scrutinizing their maps carefully.  I think they were happy to see me, though they were distracted at that moment by one of their chief concerns in their Grand Venture; What shall we do next? 

I am honoured to say that they shared a little intelligence with me, though Boxfray was guarded. I think without Jasper there, I would have been taken for nothing more than a meddling wizard, which might be very near the mark I should confess. But I had one bit of help to offer; they were at pains to guess when the hammer would fall on Thistle Ridge. Birchgrove had warned the day before about Yellow Tooth reinforcements coming from the Sorrow Bog, and it would be helpful for them to know exactly when they might arrive. 

Perhaps I can help I suggested. I think they were surprised to hear that a book-binder from Town could augur the arrival of the enemy, until I offered Well, not me exactly, but Ovee. Hed be delighted to have a look and report back, and I think hes just finished his nap, and will be ready to fly all night. 

They took the help offered, and by morning (after a night of catching up with my friends, and sharing some professional advice with Jasper), Ovetchkin came back with all the intelligence needed, or so I hope.  He informed me of the following troop movements;

"ATheoryofthe Hauntings ofThistle Ridge,byMagistrate Zoderick Winters, 1445 DR" 
By now, every child in the dale has heard of the Ghosts of Thistle Ridge, and more than a few men and women claim to have seen or been close to them in the few years since the Thistle Ridge Tragedy. I was given the task as the resident expert on these matters to investigate the ruins and see what truth if any there is to the rumours. My expertise comes entirely from the study of the small collection of books about the undead that is permitted to be shelved in the Athenaeum, most of which I think could be labelled as fairy tales. A couple have merit, but I have not had access to serious literature on the matter, and for that I am grateful and indebted to the Chaplain of the Athenaeum, whos vigil keeps our minds as clear as the morning sun. 

Below, I will give a brief description and chronology of my likewise brief expedition to the ruins, and follow with an analysis and theory about what I found. I hope greater men than I will be able to use this information for the betterment of the dale someday. 
The Expedition 
This might be a lofty title for four-days journey from my comfortable bed at the Abbeye, but on the morning of 21 Eleint, 1445 DR, exactly four years to the day after the Tragedy, assembling at the East Gate with a ride of Sunblades and receiving rites and blessings from the Chaplains and the Bishop, I had the feeling I was taking part in something greater than the sum of its parts. The air was cool and crisp on this Autumnal Equinox, and all the Signs and Portents suggested a safe return. As usual, they were mostly right, and remind us that mortals should have the humility to accept the judgement of the Gods, even when our limited intellect perceives them (incorrectly) as mercurial and capricious. 

I was attached to the 18th Company 1st Platoon, Task Masters.  This is the Command Platoon of the reserve company, and we were led by the company captain himself, Dexter Broad (I have included a sketch of him to the side), a stout man from the eastern dales somewhere.  His sergeant was a woman named Bella Birtch from Lostead, a capable archer, forester and leader.  Their company chaplain also accompanied us. This was a younger woman named Hannah Hillborn of Scardale, who had answered a Divine Call to come to come and study at the Abbeye. Being some ten years my junior, I didnt know her well, and I regret that I didnt get to know her in the brief time we were allotted together. 

With twenty-four Sunblades, plus the command element and attached retinue, we were thirty in total, with half that many horse and a small train with provisions and tents. 

We mustered at the East Gate for Prime, this early hour granting few witnesses to our departure, aside from the clergy and some wives and children seeing us off. The Bishop declared our commission and blessings complete, with Good Auspex. However, as the captain mounted his great warhorse, his son broke from his mothers side and ran to his father, tears in his eyes. He cried about never seeing his dad again, and how wed all be food for crows. Though there are some in the dale that put too much stock in the augury of children, occasionally they merit our attention and consideration.  We didnt think today was such a day, given our Auspex, and off we marched. 

We made Gribbons by Nones and enjoyed their hospitality for the remainder of the day and that night. A great work is underway here, the people of this farm still grieving the loss of their neighbours and fearful that the hobgoblin horde that obliterated them without warning and vanished just as quickly might repeat this treachery outside the walls of this very settlement.  They have two dwarven engineers hired, and the terraced redoubt of the central farm is shaping up to be one of the finest strongpoints in the hinterland of our fair dale.  The mayor here has chosen to make a smaller, taller, and sturdier fortress surrounded by unwalled farms, rather than a longer and lower walls which we see in the settlements to the West and South of the Falls.  I hope they are never tested, but I dont doubt the strength of that dwarf-laid stone, or the resilience of the tunnels and stores beneath. 

I took the opportunity to break bread with the local Magistrate, a good man Ive known for some fifteen years, Invictus. Invictus is a Cormyrean, and answered the Call to come to the Abbeye when he was only ten years old. He convinced his family to send him on his own to live and study here, and we enrolled at the same time, though he was some eight years my junior.  Already lettered in the Cormyrean fashion, he was a lightning-fast study of the Faith and wrote his first Treatise (on the dual nature of pain and the harvesting of the Light it can produce, if memory serves me) by the time he was fourteen years old. 

Invictus caught me up on everything he knew about the Tragedy and the condition and status of the Ruin presently.  Indeed, he had been assigned to tend the flock in Downriver Chapel the very spring before the Tragedy, and his memory of the days that followed it was keen.  Ive tried to capture his memories to the fullest of my humble abilities below.
