Forward to the Chondathan Edition 
I found this book in a less-than-reputable shop on Candle Row in Waterdeep when I was a younger, more adventurous man. I had at best a tenuous grasp on Damaran at the time, but even then I could see that this was an important work. I would like to say that I made a pointedly academic, exacting translation, but that is not really the case. I was pressed for time, when I finally got around to translating this tome, and this was a secret labour; the Yellow Abbey had just been completed, and what I knew was going to be an age of stifled ideas had begun. 

At any rate, this is what I put together. I pray that we never need to study the contents of this tome, but the world is a dangerous place, and when the veil is thinnest, we must prepare our minds as well as our bodies. 
-Phineas Hoetmer, Clerk of the Yellow Abbey, Marpenoth 29, 1454 DR 
Forward to the Second Edition 
The purview of this tome is exclusively the denizens of Avernus, the first, outer-most layer of Hell, and their known schemes in our Realms, especially those that have been played out in Narfell, Damara, Thay and Rashemen. I have had the misfortune of spending a lifetime chasing the devils of the First, and more usually, their henchmen, and I have what some consider an encyclopaedic knowledge of these cursed villains. I am told by my commander that I am now too old to fight with the Sword, and so I now fight with the Pen. 

The title of this book has been kept deliberately foreboding. This is not a tome for the weak-of-heart, or the soft-of-mind. Of course, I make no endorsement of any of the practices found within these pages, but the misguided, if so armed with my knowledge, could easily become a threat. The title is also taken from the infernal script above the Gates of Malsperanze. I was criticized after the first edition was released, by some who felt that the title was an indication that this book, like the Gates of Malsperanze themselves, is a gateway to deeper and greater evils. I would argue that ignorance is the true gateway to evil, and I have kept the title for the second edition. 
-Robert Tuugaarth, Mirtul, 1097 
Primer for Avernus, the First Hell – Account of Tuugaarth 
The first layer of the Nine Hells is Avernus. It is the surface of Baator's ruddy orb. It is a desert of stone, pumice, and ash broken by the occasional range of low, jagged mountains or a flowing river of lava. The fortresses of devils dot its landscape surrounded by the debris of countless battles. During the fiercest outbreaks of the Blood War, hordes of demons often invaded Avernus. Consequently, it is defended by legions of infernal warriors and daunting strongholds. 

Unlike the deeper hells, Avernus has a sky. In the sky, sullen, roiling clouds of red and black constantly flicker and burn with gouts of orange flame. Streaking fireballs pelt the barren landscape below at odd intervals. The air is hot, dry, and acrid, fouled by volcanic fumaroles. 

The mighty pit fiend ruler of Avernus is Bel, chosen by Asmodeus for his loyalty and his military genius. Bel is a cool, calculating creature that carefully avoids the intrigues of the other lords. A circle of pit fiends known as the Dark Eight serve as Bel's vassals and councillors. Bel governs only at their pleasure, and he must constantly consider whether his actions will meet with the approval of the Dark Eight. 

Avernus is the largest and most desolate of the hells, so many outcast devils or those few damned who escape their torments hide in its barren mountains. 

The Bronze Citadel: The Bronze Citadel is the seat of Bel's power. It is a mighty fortress of fourteen concentric rings, each studded with forbidding towers and infernal war machines. Thousands of spined devils, bearded devils, and legion devils are housed in its vast barracks. 

Gates of Malsperanze: Many caves in Avernus lead down to other hells below, but the most notorious is a great archway guarded by towering gates of iron. Molten runes above the gates read, "Abandon hope, all ye who enter here." The gates stand open, and beyond them a road descends steeply to the iron city of Dis, second of the Nine Hells. 

Lake of Despond: This large, bitter lake marks the birthplace of the River Styx. Souls newly arrived in the Nine Hells awake on its rocky shores where they are quickly snatched up by devils assigned the task of collecting the damned. 
Currency Of Souls – Account of Tuugaarth 
The currency of Hell is that of souls. This is well known. What is not as well known is that this currency actually takes physical form in our world; that of Soul Keys. These evil, reddish stone tokens contain the soul of a victim from our world, and they can literally be traded like gold, among others who deal in such currency. Many of those able to acquire this currency use it to purchase favours from their 

masters. The simplest and most common exchange is for devilish servants. A soul can be exchanged for 

the service of the least of devils (see 'Taxonomies', below) for the duration of 111 days, which is 1/6th of a Baator year. Some souls carry greater value, however, and might demand something more. 

I have known the most diabolical of Bel's sycophants to exchange dozens of these souls for the aid of Hamatulas and even greater servants. It should be noted that the term 'servant' does not always accurately describe the bargain that is struck for this currency. One usual arrangement is for the exchange of souls, in conjunction with the requisite summoning rituals, for the presence of one's patron. In all cases I know of, this has resulted in the eventual demise of the foolhardy who think they can command their masters. 

What is most important to note, and something that I regrettably found out too late in my career, is that these souls can be returned to their derelict bodies. I have personally witnessed this ritual, though I am unable to perform it. The details of this ritual can be found in the Codex of Prayer.

The Abacus of Avernus – Account of Zullmared the Mad 
To begin, we must understand the value of a soul; let us assign a value of '1' to most souls, for that is what most are worth, and none are worth fewer. 

However, let us then understand that some souls carry greater value, and this value is derived from the actual 'value' of the person whose soul is being evaluated, and also by the means of realizing that value. Much like stones from the earth; some are more treasured for their beauty, some for their rarity, and those most of all that are both rare and beautiful. 

Let us also consider race. Above all else, the souls of Humans are the most abundant currency for the bankers of Avernus. Do we not use silver, but value more the sapphires of the Blistering Wastes? Silver is more abundant, but still more precious. What is the value of the soul of the High Fey? I couldn't say. 

Next, let us consider the lifespan of this currency. Like precious metal, it does not vanish, and rarely changes its substance, though some metals are mutable to Zullmared, aha aha! But no; once stamped, the coin of the soul cannot be un-stamped. Except through the works of those in this Realm. In Avernus, that currency is fixed, and this is the basis of the entire oeconomy of the bankers therein. 

Let us consider next the marketplace for the soul. Marketplaces, rather, as there are many, but two that most men of Faerun are concerned with, though Zullmared has accounts in four, does he not?! Who but 

Zullmared has exchanged the Pineal Gland of a Captain of the Far Realm, for that which I exchanged it for?! But the marketplaces are mostly two; here and there. 
Here, a soul can be traded for some of the following; 
Of Pawns, such as the Lemure, 1 for 666 days, or 6 for 111 days, or any other balanced exchange, and this always with the owner of the host. 

Of Assistants, such as the Imp, 1 for 333 days, and this is the only contract between such creatures and the trader. 

Of Soldiers, such as the Barbazu, 1 for 66 days, or any other balanced exchange, sometimes with the creature, sometimes with his captain if he has one. 

Of Sergeants, such as the Eryines or the Osyluth, 1 for 33 days, and this is the only contract between such creatures and the trader. 

Of Lieutenants, such as the Hamatula or the Gelugon, 3 souls are needed for 33 days. Of Captains, such as the Cornugon, 1 soul is needed for 1 day. 

Of Generals, such as the Pit Fiend, 3 souls are needed for 1 day. Of greater creatures, I know not the accounting. 

Of power, those creatures above will lend their strength at the same rate of exchange to mortals. Zullmared would never make such an exchange, however; why borrow strength and risk your own skin, when you can borrow the skin too? Such is the false-oeconomy of the Sorcerer. Surely these devils laugh at the desire of moral men for power, rather than the results of power. 

What I do know is that the accountants of Hell are the finest and most diligent in any Realm I have known. Their abaci are true, and legions march, or not, based on the beads found thereupon. 

The marketplace thereI shall give an accounting of at a later date with any luck oho! 
The Preponderance of Threes – Account of Tuugaarth 
This chapter is forged of my own experiences. 
I was challenged by a certain Azbiel (found later as Azbael, and again as Azbial, and yet again as Ahzbeal, but these are likely the same), hard by Val'Murthag when it was called Val'Korthir, and was not as it is today. 

Azbiel, a quaternary of Bel himself, waiting in the wings while his minions did the necessary work to bring him to our world, could not but direct his logisticians to construe all arrangements by threes. He had 3 lieutenants, each with 3 sergeants, each with 33 soldiers. 

Later the same decade, I struggled against the Melphanigan, hard by Bildoobaris. The Melphanigan did not trace lineage directly to Bel, but was still compelled to arrange his organization into threes. Three cells, with three spies each, with three agents/traitors each. 

Again, at the close of that century, attempting to root out the wickedness that nearly consumed the Duchy of Soravia, I found the Belial Cult to have 3 distinct Belial, each with 3 Magi. Only the number of Supplicants varied thereafter, but often was found to be 11 or 33. 

I surmise that the Administration of Avernus is under compulsion to arrange things by threes, and that they venerate this number for some reason that I have never discovered. Certainly, their main iconography, the pentagram, lacks this number. There is some deeper meaning here, some weakness which we may ultimately be able to exploit. Until I or those that follow after me figure out the meaning of this number, we will use it to inventory our foes, and to account for that which we don't see. 
Bel's Bargain – Account of Tuugaarth 
This is one of the most terrible rituals I have discovered. This is the primary means through which Soul Keys are created. I should add that I have known other devil-binders who employed (or were employed by) denizens of Stygia and Minauros to have possession of this currency of souls, which leads me to believe that just as we have dozens of minters in our world creating coins, so too are there many ways to mint this terrible currency. 

Bel's Bargain is as eloquent as it is horrible. The victim is pressured into making a contract for his 

soul. It is interesting to note that this ritual is rarely successful where one victim is involved; a lone man with even the slightest of convictions will endure the tortures and torments put upon him, to save his mortal soul. However, when faced with the torment of his loved ones, he often trades his soul for their safety. It is common for a torturer to flay a family in front of the man in order to have him put up his soul, and when the bargain is struck, the torturers might just kill the family, or do something equally as terrible, so long as their actions are not interdicted by the terms of the bargain. While this seems to support the common belief that devils like to make clever pacts with loopholes and all manner of legal ensnarements, I hardly think that torturing a mortal or his loved ones in order to force a deal can be considered a legitimate venue for negotiation. But here we are dealing with the Lawyers of Hell, and their codes extend, regrettably, to our world.
