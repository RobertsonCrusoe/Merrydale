[2011-01-26 10:05:09] DM (OP)
Arriving at the tap room of this establishment late in the day during the peak of the trading season makes it difficult to get a table. There are easily thirty tables in the massive oak-beamed hall, which soars to a ceiling two floors above. Balconies run around the entire perimeter of the walls on two upper floors, and those on the north wall are deep, with more tables and chairs.

There are between 200-300 people in Quick Axes tonight. There are dozens of locals, but there are scores upon scores of travelers. A caravan that just came in from Cormyr had well over 100 people in it, and a few smaller ones arrived during the day before that one. Many traders appreciate the home-away-from-home feeling of this place; in Dagger Falls, this establishment alone has a license to serve unlimited alcohol, and the booze-soaked smell and the rowdy ambience that goes along with this practice is a welcome escape from the comparatively severe atmosphere of the town.

The taproom is full, and there isn’t a place to sit. There are people wearing clothing from the corners of this part of Faerûn: some merchants from the modern city of Westgate in their smart waistcoats, rugged looking frontier-types who trade in resources from the wilder parts of the dales, and everything beyond and in between. There are dozens of staff on duty tonight, serving food and drink energetically, trying to keep up to the demands of the traders, mercenaries and other travellers in this elbow-room only establishment.

“Nice axe!”

Tyrian and Carpathia stood helplessly just inside the door of the inn, almost but not quite out of the throng and press of the evening’s of custom. Beliard had tasked them with finding a seat while he attended to an errand of some sort, but there were no spaces at any of the great tables. The two had been waiting for what seemed an interminable length of time for an opening to appear, but each time a table began to clear, it was claimed almost before its previous occupiers had risen. There were easily a hundred other caravanners and Dalesmen waiting for similar opportunity. Carpathia thought of vultures attending a wounded bear that just wouldn’t lie down and die.

“What do you think we should we do?” Tyrian looked down at the sleight figure of his companion, his hand running back through his slightly unkempt golden curls.

Carpathia glowered. Tyrian would not make a good vulture. She was getting antsy. Where was that fool bard? “These are your people, boy,” she hissed. “You tell me!”

Tyrian was taken aback by her sudden vehemence. What had he done now?

“What? They’re not my –” he began. “I mean, I’ve never been the –”

He broke off as a full-figured young dwarfess coalesced out of the chaos to stand before them.

“I’ll get those packs up to a room for ye’s, sir knight, milady. Ye’s’ll be in the Ironbark Dormitory – there are two bunks left, I think.” Before either of them could say a word, the young dwarf hoisted both travellers’ packs into her well-muscled arms, resting one on each shoulder, then turned to face them. “Second floor up the back stair. Ye’s’re in with a bunch o’ those other caravanners from Cormyr. How many nights, then?”

Tyrian stared distractedly at the dwarf’s downy cheeks. He’d heard that dwarf women had beards, but had never seen one in the flesh. The hair on her chin looked so soft. He wondered how old she was. Would it grow fuller?

“How many nights, then?” The dwarfess repeated her question, staring unblinkingly at Tyrian.

Tyrian blushed, and stuttered, “Um... I’m not... there’s another....”

“One night. For now.” Carpathia came to the young man's rescue. Beliard could deal with his own room, she thought.

The dwarf nodded. “Right, then. Pay Kilbur over there at the bar,” she nodded towards a hirsute dwarf sitting atop a stool on a raised platform at one end of the bar, his odd features poring over a ledger while he argued with a customer in a wheezing, high-pitched voice. The fellow’s coarse beard, the same dull grey as the thick hair that bristled wildly from what could be seen of his head, arms, and chest, formed the crowning glory of a veritable pelt. It hid his features were almost entirely, only a pair glittery black eyes and an extraordinarily porcine nose being free of the wiry growth. Carpathia thought of a wild boar, and smiled.

“Do s’all a favour and give him the correct coins, won’t ye’s?” The dwarfess grimaced. “By the way,” she said, turning to Tyrian. “Nice axe.” With a sly wink at the flustered knight, she took her leave.

Return of the Bard

“What’s a noble knight without a maiden on the make, eh?”

A melodious voice chimed from doorway, and Tyrian and Carpathia turned to greet their erstwhile companion. As they did so, however, their eyes widened to take in the sight of Beliard Emmarask, who appeared to have shaved, bathed, and changed since he had left their company almost an hour previously. Over a finely tailored cotton shirt, the bard wore a tunic, trimmed tastefully with a collar of silver fur. The blue fabric of the tunic was embroidered with the golden image a rampant stag - a device adapted from the crest of House Emmarask, Tyrian thought he recognized. Dusty trousers and travelling boots had been exchanged for breeches of burnished leather, and a pair of stiff black boots that were collared in the Sembian fashion. Gone was the rakish beard that he had tended somewhat vainly during journey, and his oiled hair glistened in the torchlight of the inn. Affecting confusion, the bard spread his arms questioningly.

“No table yet? No drinks? Why friends, you needn’t have waited on my account. You must be parched! Now, please. Allow me.”

Tyrian and Carpathia watched as Beliard negotiated the crowded taproom like an experienced pilot might navigate a well-known reef, effortlessly making his way to the raised dais in the centre of the room, where in the winter months a great fire would be blazing. Mounting the dais, the bard approached a grizzled old dwarf who seemed to be directing the inn's staff like a general might direct troops in battle. The dwarf seemed surprised when Beliard jumped up on the platform, and his face darkened and he looked fit to explode, but the bard disarmed him with a deep bow and then crouched to engage Balak Quickaxe, Master of the Inn, in intense dialogue. Though his friends were unable to hear the content of the conversation over the not-so-dull roar of the taproom, they could see the dwarf’s countenance visibly brighten as he listened. Still talking to the old dwarf, the bard pointed in their direction, then held up three fingers. The dwarf seemed to consider this, then nodded, turned, and bellowed at a surprisingly corpulent fiery-haired dwarf standing near the bar. “OTHEN! GET’CHER CHUBBY WHISKERS OVER HERE!” The old dwarf’s stentorian voice was belied by his venerable appearance; after the roar, the general hubbub seemed somehow quieter by comparison.

As Tyrian and Carpathia watched, Othen ambled quickly towards the pair at the centre of the room. The dwarf’s great bulk acted as the prow of a ship, and many customers were forced to suddenly dodge out of the way to avoid being run down. After a quick word from his master, the dwarf began clearing a table near the edge of the dais, despite the fact that its occupiers were as yet in mid-meal. Despite their complaints, however, the surly dwarf ignored them, continuing as though they weren’t there at all. Eventually the ousted patrons retreated to the sides of the room, awkwardly clutching bowls of stew and mugs of ale in their hands. Up on the dais, the old dwarf jabbed a finger at Beliard, muttering something low and threatening. Beliard bowed again, jumped down to the newly cleared table, and waved at Tyrian and Carpathia, gesturing for them to come over.

“Good news, friends!” Beliard beamed at them as they finally made their way through the mob. “It turns out that the minstrel Master Balak retained for this evening’s entertainment has failed to appear. I have offered my services to assist him in his hour of need, and he has generously offered us this table for my performance.”

A matronly woman suddenly appeared with a bottle of wine, two mugs of ale and a steaming loaf of crusty bread which she deposited on the table. “Yer vittles’re on the way, songster,” she said, “But you’d better wet your whistle and start blowing. We’re getting run off our feet out here!”

With a nod, Beliard looked at Tyrian. “Keep this table free, Tyrian. We’ll have company, ‘ere long.”

Taking a sip of wine, the bard leapt up onto the table.

Ormath's Ode

“Ladies and Gentlemen, Harlots and Cads!” The bard’s clear voice rings out across the room, and a few, though not many patrons glance in his direction. “You are to be commended for your perspicacity in choosing to dine tonight under the Sign of the Quick Axe. Master Balak welcomes you one and all to his establishment!”

A few locals yell out the dwarf’s name approvingly as they hoist their glasses.

“My name, friends, is Beliard Emmarask, and I drink to your health!”

Raising the bottle to his lips, the bard takes a dramatic swig, though Carpathia notes that the man’s Adam’s apple does not move - the drinking is an act. Nodding at the table where the caravan master Denelius Ormath and his cronies have set themselves up, Beliard continues:

“And more importantly, to your wealth!”

Laughter and cheers, as Beliard appears to take a second, even deeper swallow of the crimson wine, which dribbles down his chin, before he wipes it away with a flourish. Ormath and his friends chuckle, hoisting their own glasses in the bard’s direction.

“My friends and I have come here this day, thanks entirely to the efforts of our Fearless Patron, Denelius Ormath! Were it not for his stalwart leadership, sterling character, bold courage, and unparalleled business acumen, we would not have made it here this season. Truly, I do believe this man could lead a caravan through the Nine Hells in winter. If you’ve a cargo to ship anywhere between here and Suzail, folks, Ormath’s your man.”

With each compliment, Beliard’s voice has risen, bringing more of the room under his spell. At his table, Ormath appears to be enjoying the attention, his chest swollen with pride as he nods to the bard’s pæan. Now his voice takes on a rhythmic cadence, and his hands begin tapping his hip in time with his words.

    Who thunders ‘cross the Thunder Peaks? Why, Denelius Ormath!
    Who knows what every merchant seeks? Why, Denelius Ormath!

A few in the crowd catch on and begin chanting the man’s name at the end of each verse.

    Will he be stayed by bandit swarms? Not Denelius Ormath!
    By orc marauders? Or fire or storms? Not Denelius Ormath!

Many are pounding their tables now, and Ormath is beginning to look positively regal.

    Who carts the cargo and lugs the loads? It’s Denelius Ormath!

(A number of Denelius’s underpaid and overworked carters frown at this one, but are too much in the rhythm to break it.)

    He’s the iron captain of the roads, is Great Denelius Ormath!

A cheer erupts from Ormath’s table at this last verse, and there is general applause from the room, especially the caravanners, despite the fact that the journey from Cormyr under Ormath had been one that few would care to repeat, in this lifetime or the next.

Nodding at Beliard, Ormath hoists his considerable bulk onto a chair, raising a hand to quieten the chants of “Ormath! Ormath!” that greet him.

As Beliard steps down to let Ormath address the crowd, he is one of the few to notice the arrival of a young, flamboyantly dressed man carrying a lute, out of breath and seemingly fuming with anger. Espying Beliard across the room, this new arrival cries out, but is immediately shushed by the crowd, especially by two of Ormath’s hired guardsmen, who glower at him menacingly as their master begins to speak.

“Thank you, Sir Bard. You do me a great honour, and I thank you for it. Now as you know, I am a man of few words, so you’ll have no speech from me. However, I am a man of great appetites, and I feel powerful thirsty. Master Balak – a pitcher of ale to each table, on my account!”

After this announcement, no one is paying attention to the newcomer at all, except Beliard, who sneers at the newcomer from the centre of the room, shaking his head in a mocking expression of false sympathy. Brandishing his fist at Emmarask, the fellow with the lute slinks out into the night.

Beliard's Ballad

“Songster!” Balak’s bellow again cuts through the raucous atmosphere that has returned to the taproom with Ormath's largesse. “You’ve had your fun. Now give us a song!”

Amid cries of agreement, Beliard lowers his head in acquiescence, and remounts the table. Facing the crowd, he assumes a lecturing stance.

“I am most happy to take up Master Balak’s request. As we all know, the dwarf is not apt, when idle, to break into singing, being not of so airy a constitution as those of more reasonable stature. Yet I have found that he will drink to his health – often, I have also found, to the number of his years – when a song is on offer. To that end, I present this tune, of my own composition.”

The crowd, now familiar with the bard, dims its murmuring somewhat as Beliard begins singing in a low voice. As the patrons of Quick Axes strain to hear the words of the song, the din lowers further still.

    A stick, a stone, it's the end of the road, it's the rest of a stump, it's a little alone,

    It's a sliver of glass, it is life, it's Selûne, it is night, it is death, it's a trap, it's a rune.

    The oak when it blooms, a fox in the brush, a knot in the wood, the song of a thrush,

    The wood of the wind, a tower, a fall, a scratch, a lump, it is nothing at all.

    It's the wind blowing free, it's the end of the slope, it's a gleam, it's a void, it's a hunch, it's a hope,

    Where the River Tesh crosses the Tethyamar trail, it's the end of a journey, it’s the start of the tale.

From the chaos of a half-hour previous, the inn has been transformed. Only a few low whispers are to be heard, as Beliard continues his ballad.

    The foot, the ground, the flesh and the bone, the beat of the drum, a slingshot's stone,
    A fish, a flash, a silvery glow, a fight, a bet, the range of a bow.

    The bed of the well, the end of the line, the dismay in the face, it's a loss, it's a find,
    A spear, a spike, a point, a nail, a drip, a drop, the end of the tale.

    A cartload of hay in the soft morning light, the howl of a wolf in the dead of the night,

    A mile, a must, a thrust, a blow, it's a girl, it's a rhyme, it's a troll, it's the snow.

    The plan of the castle, the body in bed, and the cart that got stuck, it's the mud, it's the mud,
    Afloat, adrift, a flight, a wing, a hawk, a quail, the promise of spring.

    Where the River Tesh crosses the Tethyamar trail, it's the end of a journey, it’s the start of the tale.

    A stick, a stone, it's the end of the road, it's the rest of a stump, it's a little alone.

A crash from the kitchen punctuates the silence, as one of the halfling cooks tries to divide his attention between his pots and the bard. Fat Othen ambles quickly out of the taproom to investigate. Across the room, the door of the inn opens and three elves walk into the firelight, their keen eyes taking in the scene.

    A shadow, a snake, it is Shar, it is fear, it's a thorn in your hand and a lie in your ear,
    A point, a blade, a bee, a bite, a blink, a buzzard, a thief in the night.

    A pin, a needle, a sting, a pain, a snail, a riddle, a wasp, a stain,
    A pass in the mountains, a horse and a mule, in the distance the elves, three shadows of blue.

    Where the River Tesh crosses the Tethyamar trail, it's the end of a journey, it’s the start of the tale.
    A stick, a stone, the end of the road, the rest of a stump, a lonesome road,

    A sliver of glass, a life, the sun, a knife, a death, the end of the run,
    Where the River Tesh crosses the Tethyamar trail, it's the end of a journey, it’s the start of the tale.

His rich voice tinged with a sombre melancholy and nostalgia, Beliard winds down his performance, glancing at the trio of elves that has entered. Before the crowd can rouse itself from the bard’s spell, his face splits wide with a welcoming grin, and he calls out across the room. “Well met, travellers!” Suddenly the room is silent, and close to three hundred pairs of eyes are trained upon the elves. After the briefest pause, during which not a soul speaks, Beliard calls out once again. “Come, join our table, friends, and bring us the news of your people!”

Disgusting.
 
An edifice of ruined trees, standing tall in the cloud of stink that poured out from it's shabby cracks and seams. Eremus ran his eyes over the tavern, gaze lingering on the axe-painted sign hanging from the front eaves. The evening breeze prodded at it with a weak effort. The nauseating stench that polluted the air around the establishment seemed to suck the life out of everything it touched - it was as though even the wind itself were susceptible to its taint.
 
"Quick Axes," he murmured aloud.
 
His companions did not speak. Eremus glanced to his left, and saw his brothers' jaws clenched tight with what he could only assume was steady determination. The avenger felt his own cheek twitch slightly as he unclenched his mouth, unaware he had been doing so. Ineluki and Cirdan turned to look at him. As others would pass words, the trio of elves merely exchanged a set of knowing stares, then nodded in unison. No doubt they were as... offended by this place as he was. His gut felt as though someone had dropped hot coals into it then twisted it over and over until its semblance was nothing more than knotted cord. They did not belong here. The cobbled stone beneath Eremus's field was hard and unyielding. Buildings arched overhead, stark and oppressive as they carved up the sky piecemeal. He felt both trapped and exposed all at once. So be it.
 
Without a word, Eremus strode towards the door, companions at his heels. He paused a half second with his hand against the door, open palm pressed against the smooth surface that so many filthy creatures had no doubt pawed at. What a sad, demeaning purpose to be reduced to, the mighty oak brought so low as to keep out the cold for drunkards and whores.
 
"So it is." The avenger spoke softly, pushing the door inwards. Eremus strode into the firelight amongst his brothers, bracing himself against the thick stench that washed over them. Not just the old hay mouldering on the ground beneath his hide boots, but an inexplicable combination of several other odours and liquids assaulted every sense Eremus possessed. He ran his tongue over his teeth as he gazed across the long, broad taproom, resisting the urge to spit out the awful taste of the place that had already settled in his mouth and throat.
 
A voice warbled in the distance, loud and deeply off-putting to the elf's ears. Such was not the opinion of the tavern's other inhabitants, however. Grubby eyes and ears across the room were focused on a well-dressed as he serenaded the crowd with what they no doubt considered to be good song. Eremus glanced at Ineluki, a short-lived smirk on his face. The expression on his cousin's face displayed a similar course of opinion to his own. Eremus placed a hand on his shoulder and leaned in close, attention lingering a moment on the grandstanding individual atop the far table.
 
"Come, let us find our board..." Eremus said, eyes now shifting between the rough individuals populating the tables around them. "...while their attention is elsewhere. We are not common fare in these parts, and I would not have us stand out any more than we already do."
 
Eremus made to sidestep the unwashed mass of humans, but before he could move the air fell silent. The well-dressed man across the room, voice suddenly absent from the ale-soaked room, was grinning hard in the trio's general direction.
 
"Well met, travellers!" He called, brandishing his bottle in salutation.
 
Over a hundred thick necks turned in near-perfect unison, and the elves found themselves under heavy scrutiny. The tavern was quiet once more, and a vast array of expressions aimed themselves at Eremus and his kin, none of which made the elf feel particularly welcome. The bard spoke up a second time, saving them from the silence.
 
"Come, join our table, friends, and bring us news of your people!"
 
Eremus shifted in place, the tavern's attention focused solely upon themselves once again. The avenger turned aside to his brothers and affected a disingenuous smile. Such an invitation was the last thing he had expected to receive, and although he had no intent nor desire to mingle with these humans, offending them would most definitely be a step in the wrong direction.
 
These elves were no fools, not while they stood this deep in the belly of the beast.
 
"Shall we join them, brothers?" Eremus inquired, a bit over-loud, a bit forced, nearly toneless as he attempted to restrain the displeasure in his voice.

He agreed with Cirdan in the way humans acted.  They seemed to be a constant assault upon the senses.  He'd yet to meet one that could appreciate the value of silence...of listening with all of one's senses; of seeing with all of one's senses.  However, if his lifetime was comparable to that of a fluttering candle flame; short-lived and vulnerable from one moment to the next...well...it was difficult to imagine such things.

Dealing with humans was not going to be an issue for him.  Missing his home.  Missing the feel of the forest beneath him and around him might very well drive him mad.  He had little respect for the Tel'Quess that now claimed Cormanthor as their birthrite and even less respect for the sentencing that Marshall Medeila had put upon him and his two companions.
