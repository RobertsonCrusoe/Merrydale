27 Tarsakh, 1483 DR  Notes from the Memoire of Ser Reginald Boxfray 
Then came the morning of the 27th of Tarsakh; a very light but very late snowfall gave a nice contrast to the budding trees and forest floor, but most of all to the smell of fresh bread. Yes, indeed! The bread oven in what is now Ambershields Monastery was fired up for the first time this morning, and the warm smell filled our lungs and hearts with joy and optimism.  For here we are, at the wild edge of civilization, trying to carve an existence out of a forest that seems determined to repel us. And this morning we ate fresh bread! 

I no longer use the term camp for our settlement.  The carpenters and masons stood up the walls of the central farmhouse quickly, and there is already a shingled roof over it all.  With 2 floors, we have more than enough room to move in all the soldiers and supplies, and are grateful to be out of the elements, fine though this spring has been. We are now calling this New Town Hall. 

The settlers that came last week have proven incredibly industrious, especially compared to my soldiers. Without the need to keep one eye on the treeline for trouble, trusting that the Free Defense League has our affairs in order, the five families that have settled here have made astonishing progress; they all banded together to clear the rubble out of the old foundations of a collection of buildings, and with direction from Billy Mason and John Carpenter, they have stood up stone walls on the first floor, wooden walls complete with exterior plaster on the second, and a finished roof of slate shingles.  They have, as I have already bragged, lit the old bread oven, and are now clearing the old foundations of several other buildings around the farm. 

It is worth noting our numbers on this late Tarsakh morning.  We are the following; 
 
Free Defense League 14th Company 1st Platoon Dawnshields (20 men and myself) 

 
Ridge and River Reclamation Company (5 staff including Chief Engineer Swelter, 3 journeymen and one apprentice of various trades) 

 
Three families of Amaunatori pilgrim settlers from Cormyr (33 people in total, with a mix of ages as youd expect, all lead by Abbot Ambershields) 

 
Two families from the dales; the Mistlewoods from Mistledale (12 people) and the Fletchers of Dwimmerholme (6 people) 

 
Andi Autumnhill  halfling poulterer 


We are 58 souls and counting. 
Mayor Winters and the elders from Gryphons Farm allocated a squad of Sunblades and a crew of men to clear the old road as far as Greenwood, and we now have regular visits daily from that settlement. Mostly soldiers, but just yesterday a group of over 20 women, young and old, Chaunteans all, came to bless our farm.  They brought a bounty with them, including the flour needed to make the bread which I have just watched my men enjoy this morning.  It has taken less than a tenday to establish this route, and the small buildings we have here.  I am eager to see what a month or a season brings! 

Of the enemy we have seen very little. We scout only a half-mile from the farm, and there are some thickets that I know the men avoid. We have chased off some very small scouting parties of goblins, but havent seen a single one in a few days. We have, however, run into our Fey allies who seem to be watching our border to the north. We suspect they have been playing mischief with the goblins, confounding and harrying them, if not outright attacking them, in their way.  We are grateful for the ties that the Captain made with the Limestone Court. The cost of a quiet northern border has been a few jars of honey and jam up until today.  As I write this, a few of the sprites have turned up, attracted by the smell of the bakery, and just now they are chasing a few of the children (yes, children!) around the monastery, who wave buttered rolls teasingly at the small fey.  It is a distraction from our work, but it is also the experience of living that we have all come here to build.  Even those of us that have been paid gold to fight and deliver it.
