Mourn "Raven" Raventree II is the chronicle's doomed idealist — the nobleman who should have been safe, who should have lived out his days in the Sea Ward villa of a powerful Waterdhavian family, and who instead walked into the underworld of the City of Splendours to save his sister, and then kept walking until the road killed him. He is one of the few characters in the chronicle who is exactly what he appears to be, and this is both his greatest virtue and the thing that gets him killed.

The Noble Ranger

Raven was the second of his name, heir to House Raventree — a noble Waterdhavian family with deep roots, interconnected by marriage to the Lathkule and Helmfast houses. His coming-of-age portrait was commissioned in 1370. He is described as "a large, muscular man with a patch over his left eye," and elsewhere as "clothed in expensive but functional leathers" with "an air of nobility" offset by "a wildness about him that marked him apart from the regular human denizens of Waterdeep." He was a devotee of Mielikki, the Lady of the Forests, and a ranger — not a courtier, not a merchant, not a schemer. In a city defined by its factions and intrigues, Raven was someone who belonged in the woods.

His will tells us the essentials of his inner life in remarkably few lines. His property goes to his father Surakh, or failing him, to his younger brother Jameen. His magical sword Ratbiter returns to Morgunn Lathkule, with the provision that it be "put to good use fulfilling its purpose." His body is to be buried in the Raventree family crypt with a service performed by a priest of Mielikki and support from the Church of Sune under the direction of his cousin Naiomee Helmfast. All money goes to the Church of Mielikki. This is a man whose final document is about returning things to where they belong — family property to family, a weapon to the family that lent it, his body to his ancestors, his coin to his goddess. There is no vanity in it, no posturing, no settling of scores. It reads like the will of someone who expected to die and wanted to make sure the loose ends were tidy.

The Sister

Raven entered the story because of his sister Jameen. She had been abducted — Raven had reason to suspect she'd been taken for sale in the slave markets of Skullport. He came to the Red Herring headquarters looking for allies, having heard of their exploits in the sewers (likely from Tomas's boasting at the Dripping Dagger). Esperel received him with cold suspicion and told him to come back later. Raven, confused but not offended, shrugged and took his leave.

It was Tomas he connected with. Walking away from Esperel's rebuff, Raven spotted the young fisherman on the street, recognized him from descriptions, and mimed hoisting a jar towards the Copper Cup. Tomas grinned and gestured for Raven to lead the way. "For the next several hours, Tomas listened attentively, and increasingly enthusiastically, as Raven told the story of his life and his sister's kidnapping." This is the essential Raven: a man who tells his story honestly to someone he's just met, over drinks, and trusts that the telling will be enough to win an ally. He was right — the rescue mission for Jameen was mounted, and it succeeded.

But the chronicle adds a grim footnote: "for unhappy reasons that will become clear in time, this happy outcome brought but little respite to the ill-fated House of Raventree." Jameen Raventree would later die through life-drain at the hands of Ravensblight — the alias of the serial killer mage Hagan, who bore a curse against the Raventree, Lathkule, and Kormallis families. Raven had saved his sister from Skullport only for her to be killed by a different horror entirely. The rescue mission was a success that changed nothing.

The Drunken Punch

One incident captures Raven's entire personality in miniature. During Harvestide of 1371, Raven and Tomas parted ways after drinking at the Dripping Dagger — Tomas heading to Dock Ward, Raven to his family's villa in Sea Ward. "Unfortunately, in his misplaced eagerness to match the young fisherman's prodigious intake, Raven found himself slightly the worse for drink." Pausing to relieve himself in an alley, he came face to face with Dragon — Yarkul's half-elf daughter, a powerful lieutenant in the Ersgul hierarchy — flanked by a half-dozen drow.

What followed was described as Raven's "finest (and bleakest) hour." Dragon began to speak. Before she could finish her opening sentence, Raven punched her in the face, rolling a natural 20, maximum damage, knocking her unconscious and breaking her nose permanently. The chronicle's player notes describe it with gleeful precision: "Her introduction to the campaign lasted all of 3 seconds before she was out cold."

Then the drow swarmed him and he went down to sleep darts. He was taken to the Wavesilver Villa, forced to fight in a gladiatorial pit filled with broken glass, compelled to kill unarmed civilians and a child while alien eyes watched. He briefly lost his rangerhood for what he was made to do there.

The episode is Raven in concentrate. He doesn't calculate. He doesn't assess the odds. A villain presents herself and he swings. The swing is glorious — one of the most spectacularly successful single punches in the entire chronicle. And then reality reasserts itself, as it always does with Raven, and the consequences are catastrophic.

The Captivity

The Harvestide notes reveal what happened during Raven's imprisonment in fragmentary, nightmarish detail, recovered through Beljuril's memory-reading spell because Raven's own memory was blocked. He was in a pit filled with broken glass, naked except for a loincloth. Three men were with him, armed with rusty saws, jagged femurs, and broken bottles. A boy was thrown in with a bottle. Raven was compelled to pick up a shard of glass and stab the boy. Many different coloured sets of eyes watched from above — suggesting the involvement of both drow and beholders. A slime consumed the bodies of the fallen. A young woman with a whip entered the pit, and Raven choked her, killed her, and killed again.

This is the darkest material associated with any hero in the chronicle. Raven — the nobleman, the ranger, the devotee of Mielikki who wanted all his worldly goods given to the forest goddess — was reduced to a naked animal in a glass-filled pit, killing children and women while monsters watched. That he briefly lost his divine connection to Mielikki for these compelled acts tells you that the cosmos itself held him at least partly accountable, even as any mortal observer would recognize that he had no choice. The theological cruelty of that is staggering.

And yet there is no record of Raven wallowing in it afterwards. He was traded back to his companions in exchange for the captured Yarkul. He was revived, he told what he could remember, Beljuril filled in the rest. And then, within months, he was leading the Daggerdale Expedition.

The Harper

By 1372, Raven had become a Harper — one of only two company members heading to Daggerdale (the other being Boregar). His mission was to pursue Yarkul and the Zhent threat in Dagger Falls, leading a mixed band that included Maturin Horgath, Boregar the Wanderer, Hanibal Felthian, Sparrow, and a gnome named Corrina. The traitor Jafar's dossier on the expedition is revealing: "Raven is the leader (and only member of the Company) of the group heading to Daggerdale in search of Yarkul. He is a hard-headed and determined devotee of all that is good and just. His skill as a tracker is impressive in the wild, as is his skill with the blade. However, his greatest weakness is his lack of self-defense."

Jafar — a man who built his betrayals on precise observation — had identified the fundamental vulnerability: Raven could hurt you, but he couldn't protect himself. "While he can cleave many opponents with but a single blow from his sword, he lacks armour and protection that would otherwise save him from a stout blade to the back." This is the assessment of a traitor, but it is accurate, and it reads almost as prophecy.

Rufus, keeping watch with Raven at Roarke's Tavern the night before the expeditions departed, offered the most intimate portrait of the ranger in the entire chronicle: "Not a sound or movement escapes him, and I freely admit that my own attention wanders more than his, though my eyes stay open! I shall miss his companionship on this return to Neverwinter. Strange as it is that we, of all people, should become friends, I count none other closer than Mourn Raventree." The Selûnite priest — a lycanthrope, a man with his own complicated relationship to monstrous compulsion — found in Raven the closest friend he had.

The Death

The Daggerdale Expedition was dogged by Grimwald's Rangers almost from the start. These bounty hunters had been hired to stop the Harpers, and a wanted poster was circulated branding Raven a murderer and Boregar a rapist. The poster was crude propaganda — fabricated charges designed to turn settlements against them — but it served its purpose.

On the road between Bargewright Inn and Secomber, the Rangers finally caught up with the expedition. The battle "went the heroes' way for the most part, but the Rangers proved to be deadly adversaries." Most of the Rangers were killed. But Sheriff Grimwald, cornered and bested by Maturin in their final duel, put Raven down with a starwheel Gond Pistola — a firearm, a coward's weapon from behind cover — as the noble Harper "heroically charged the villain."

He charged. Of course he charged. That's what Raven does. He punched Dragon in the face. He volunteered to rescue his sister from Skullport. He led the most dangerous of the three expedition teams despite having no armour worthy of the name. When the villain was cornered, Raven closed the distance, and the villain shot him dead.

The Psychological Portrait

Raven is the most straightforward character in a chronicle full of schemers, spymasters, and morally compromised survivors. Garth processes the world through control. Rychel through competence. Falcon through commerce and style. Raven processes the world through directness. When he wants allies, he walks up to the first person he recognizes and mimes hoisting a jar. When a villain speaks to him, he punches her before she finishes her sentence. When a friend needs rescuing, he mounts a rescue. When the Harpers need someone to lead the most dangerous mission — across Anauroch to Daggerdale, where everyone who opposes you knows your name and description — he leads it.

This directness is not stupidity. Jafar's dossier calls him "hard-headed and determined," not foolish. He tracked a spider-creature to its lair, compiled intelligence on the serial killer Hagan that the Rooken Network later inherited, and managed a mixed-temperament expedition through hostile territory with sufficient skill that Rufus trusted him absolutely. He was a ranger — a man whose professional skills are observation, tracking, and living off the land. He was good at what he did.

But there is something in Raven that refuses to accommodate the complexity of the world he inhabits. Waterdeep is a city of masks — Garth has five aliases, Esperel plays "Bladetail," Nero goes by "Paris" and "Jacob Fink," even Rufus hides his lycanthropy. Raven has no aliases. He shows up at the Red Herring headquarters as himself, gives his real name, explains his real problem.

The patch over his left eye is never explained in the sources, but it is symbolically perfect. Raven sees the world with one eye. He sees it clearly, but he sees it without depth perception — without the parallax that allows you to judge how far away the danger really is. He sees the villain and he charges. He sees his sister in captivity and he mounts a rescue. He sees the road to Daggerdale and he takes it. What he doesn't see — what he perhaps can't see — is the distance between himself and the thing that will kill him. The bullet from the Gond Pistola came from behind cover, from a man who'd already been beaten, from a weapon that didn't require the courage to close the gap. Raven died because he charged a man who had learned not to fight fair.

The chronicle buries him quickly and moves on. His corpse is sent back to Waterdeep on a bier while the surviving expedition members bid farewell and continue east. There is no extended mourning, no journal entry devoted to the loss. Maturin grieves; the party recruits a dwarf named Dalak. The world turns. But the way Rufus wrote about him at Roarke's — "I count none other closer than Mourn Raventree" — and the way Esperel's cold stare couldn't deter him, and the way Tomas grinned and let him lead the way to the pub — all of this tells you that the chronicle lost something when Raven fell. Not its most powerful character, or its most cunning, or its most entertaining. Something simpler and rarer. It lost the man who did the right thing without having to think about it first.
