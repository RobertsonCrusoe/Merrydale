Profile: Aisling Maeliticus (circa 1390 - 1439 DR)

This profile synthesizes known facts and reasoned speculation regarding Aisling Maeliticus, a key figure in the early history of the Sembian settlement at Thistle Ridge.

1. Origins and Arrival (Pre-1408 - 1408 DR):

- Birth: Recorded as born in 1390 DR.
- Family Name: Maeliticus. This name is noted as potentially significant, with Netherese roots possibly meaning "Evil" or "Malevolent," while its Thorass (Old Common) roots could relate to "Milestones" or "Legacy." The family itself is obscure, with no available records. (Further confirmation indicates the surname Maeliticus is specifically native to Daerlun, according to Brother Vessuvian's research.)
- Marriage: Aisling was the third wife of Alvund Alavandor (the man who settled Thistle Ridge under that name, potentially an impostor). Alvund's previous two wives, Senthia and Abrecia Maeliticus, bore the same surname as Aisling, suggesting they were her sisters or close relatives. Neither of these prior unions produced known offspring.
- Arrival in Thistle Ridge: Aisling arrived circa 1408 DR with her husband Alvund and the Clarandal family as part of the Sembian group settling the area. They likely came from Daerlun, under circumstances suggesting exile or a hidden agenda orchestrated by Netheril (potentially involving an entity known as the "Old Patriarch" who dealt with other Alavandors). It is strongly suspected the settlers were afflicted by a sterility curse imposed and/or exploited by Netheril/Shadovar.

2. Life in Thistle Ridge (1408 - 1439 DR):

- The Phylactery: Soon after arrival, Aisling established an apothecary and possibly a school known as "The Phylactery." This establishment gained a significant reputation.
  - Fertility Medicines: The Phylactery became particularly renowned for producing highly effective fertility medicines. The Yellow Abbey's 1440 survey noted their success bordered on heresy, though no overt occultism was proven by official inquiries.
  - Name Significance: The name "Phylactery" itself is noteworthy. While rooted in Old Chondathan meaning "safeguard" or "protection," Brother Vessuvian noted it later acquired darker connotations associated with unnatural preservation and holding onto power. Aisling's choice of this name raises questions about the true nature of her practices – were they purely herbal, or did they involve more arcane or potentially darker methods?

- Motherhood & Fertility Success: Aisling bore Alvund three children in relatively quick succession after arriving in Thistle Ridge: Andarl (b. 1408), Senthia (b. 1409), and Abrecia (b. 1410). This, combined with Saralla Sakurth bearing Claudius Clarandal's children only after arrival, strongly suggests Aisling's expertise at the Phylactery, potentially combined with Thistle Ridge's inherent vitality, was instrumental in overcoming the settlers' prior infertility/curse.

- Potential Interaction with Palus/Jannath (Speculation): Given the later revelations about Palus operating disguised as Jannath/"The Ancient Mother" and potentially influencing the settlers via the hidden temple, it's highly plausible Aisling interacted with this entity. Did Palus, under guise, provide Aisling with knowledge, ingredients, or methods for her fertility cures? Was Aisling an unwitting pawn, using techniques supplied by Palus without understanding their true source or implications? The success of her Phylactery might stem directly from this hidden, manipulative influence.

3. The Tragic Year (1439 DR):

- Family Loss: In 1439 DR, Aisling suffered immense personal tragedy. Her daughter Abrecia (now Alavaxi), her son Andarl (Mayor), and her grandson Dorian Alavandor were all killed during a "hunting trip." This event was later revealed (via seance) to be a sacrifice orchestrated through trickery by "The Ancient Mother" (likely Palus).
- Suicide: Later that same year (1439 DR), Aisling Maeliticus died by her own hand.

4. Motivation for Suicide (Hypothesized):

- Discovery of Truth: The timing strongly suggests Aisling's suicide was directly linked to the deaths of her children and grandson. It is hypothesized that she discovered the true nature of their deaths – that they were murdered/sacrificed through the machinations of "The Mother of the Ancient Faith."
- Realization of Complicity?: Furthermore, she may have realized that "The Ancient Mother" was the same entity (Palus/Jannath) who had potentially aided her at the Phylactery. This horrifying revelation – that her 'benefactor' had murdered her family, and that the fertility 'cure' she provided might have been tainted or part of a darker bargain – would create an unbearable conflict. Had her work, her success, been built on a foundation of deceit and evil, ultimately leading to her family's demise? This profound sense of guilt, betrayal, and perhaps contamination of her entire life's work in Thistle Ridge offers a compelling explanation for her suicide.
- Connection to Alaventa's Change: It is highly unlikely to be coincidental that Tergus Clarandal observed the unsettling change ("faint shadow") in young Alaventa Alavaxi at the Winter Solstice later in the same year as both the 1439 sacrifice and Aisling's subsequent suicide. Both Aisling's despair and the change in Alaventa likely stem from the same root cause: the profound trauma and encroaching dark influence unleashed by the sacrifice orchestrated by Palus/"The Ancient Mother." Aisling's death reflects the consequence of discovering the truth, while Alaventa's change may reflect the impact of that ambient darkness on a potentially sensitive individual.

5. Summary:

Aisling Maeliticus was a central figure in the early Thistle Ridge settlement. Hailing from a family associated with Daerlun (Maeliticus), she arrived with her husband Alvund under mysterious circumstances possibly linked to a Netherese-imposed curse. She established the renowned Phylactery, whose success (especially with fertility) likely stemmed from a combination of her own skill, Thistle Ridge's inherent vitality, and potentially unwitting collaboration with Palus disguised as Jannath. The tragic, sacrificial deaths of her children and grandson in 1439 likely led her to uncover the horrifying truth of Palus's manipulation, revealing the dark bargain underpinning their presence and success in Thistle Ridge. Unable to reconcile this devastating knowledge, she took her own life later that year, becoming one of the key tragic figures whose story foreshadowed the final catastrophe of 1441. The darkness surrounding these events also likely manifested in the change observed in Alaventa Alavaxi around the same time.
