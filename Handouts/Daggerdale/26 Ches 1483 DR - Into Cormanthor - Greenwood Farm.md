26 Ches – Into Cormanthor!
By 10 AM you set out from the village of Gribbons’ Farm, escorted by Lt. Maddy Riverstone,
and 12 of her Sun Blades.
Being in the closing days of Ches, and enjoying an early spring, the cold weather is behind

you, and everything is starting to bud. The air is cool with a pleasing gentle breeze that

delivers the natural fragrances of the season. “Oh! We’ll see our first Hyacinths on the

other side of this hill!” Mari exclaims as one particular scent drifts by, and as you top the

small hill, you see a field of blue & purple flowers abuzz with the earliest bees.

It only takes about half an hour to reach the base of the escarpment from the village, and

the switch-back road that has been cut out of it. At the base of the escarpment is a little

stone cottage, outside of which a family is tending their gardens. They exchange friendly

salutations with the Sun Blades, and the father of the family escorts the lot of you up the

road, talking the whole time with Maddy. It is clear that he maintains the road and points
out the work he’s done this spring to keep it in good order.
The road goes up over 100’, and at the top the view is spectacular. From here you can

admire the expanse of the Tesh Valley; the river stretches out before you, winding lazily

through the valley, following the limestone escarpment it has been carving for millennia.

To the west, you can see the imposing Desertsmouth Mountains, and the foothills before

them. You can clearly see the walls of Dagger Falls, and the drifting smoke from dozens of

chimneys. Laid out below you are the many hamlets and fortifications collectively known

as Gribbons’ Farm, and you can make out groups of farmers with draft animals working a

dozen fields. Across the river and the valley you can see the menacing Dagger Hills, where

many outcroppings of jagged limestone mix with the forest to make what should be a

beautiful vista, if not for the knowledge of the danger of the place. You can clearly see the

large round wall and grove of Mystral Farm at the base of those hills and realize just how
close that farm is to danger.
Behind you, atop the escarpment, is Cormanthor. This forest is dominated by the
Cormanthor Oak, a fast-growing tree with some connection to the First World. The trees

grow to over 100’ in height, with great thick trunks and broad canopies made by strong

limbs. The trees are just starting to produce leaves, so there is ample sunlight today, but

you can see that in a couple of weeks from now, the forest floor will be deprived of light.

For the most part, the forest is pleasant to navigate; huge glades of mature oaks have

choked out most life on the forest floor, save for the mosses and lichens that favour the

limestone-based soils here. A horse can easily navigate the open spaces between the
large trees, and one can often see a long distance in these glades.
The glades give way every few hundred yards to thicker copses of trees and bushes that

provide more ground cover. Here, the aspen, poplar and dogwood trees provide cover for

juniper, buckthorn and honeysuckle bushes, with occasional patches of wild strawberries,

blueberries and blackberries (not yet in season). The forest has a thick musk of rotting

leaves, with the gentle breeze often delivering the refreshing scent of the bloom. The air

today feels more like drinking than breathing, the quality of it appreciated by the most
urbane to the most rural of you.
Many small animals mark your passing; large black squirrels are common, and their

smaller cousin, the brown chipmunk, can be found on nearly every tree, scurrying thither

and hither with acorns. Indeed, Mortimer’s time is full of meeting so many new friends as

to require reminding by Jasper to keep up and stay close. Birds are likewise plentiful in this
part of Cormanthor. Red-headed woodpeckers favour the older oaks and crack open
acorns on the forest floor, while flocks of small-bodied thrushes race through the branches

overhead, often following your party. A couple of the Sun Blades open crumb-filled palms
for the birds, which confidently perch on a finger to enjoy the bounty.
Of larger game, you see much sign; droppings from large deer are found throughout the

day, and you realize that Maddy is following an old road that has maintained itself as a

game trail to Greenwood Farm. You also see signs of black bears, foxes and coyotes as the
day goes on.
At one point shortly before noon, a great stag is seen off to your left. It is large and mature

and appears to have a scar over the left side of its face which has taken an eye. It doesn’t

seem troubled by your passing, taking you in from about 50 yards, casually nipping at

honeysuckle the while. Lucan begins to pull out his bow but finds a hand from a nearby

rider checking him. The hand belongs to Sephany, a forester about 20 years old. “We’re
not hunting, Sembian” she says quietly,
watching him closely as he stows his
bow.
“But, it’s just looking at us! Surely we
can make a meal of it!” retorts the young
investigator.
“We’re not hunting. Killing things in
these woods is dangerous. There are
always other things that will smell or
hear or feel the kill and come to
investigate. Then we’ll be hunted. Keep
your bow on your back.” Sephany’s kind
round face gives Lucan an approving
smile as he follows her orders, followed

brown hair to dance just a little on her
shoulders. With years of physical activity and the outdoors to lend an athletic and durable

tone to her otherwise plain womanly figure, Sephany would not stand out at court, but in
her native environment, her calm and confident demeanour is alluring somehow.
“Anyway” she continues, “That is Graff. We don’t shoot Graff or others like him. He’s good
stock.”
“Why has it a name?! Is that some druid friend of yours?” asks the Sembian?
” No, I’ve just told you. That is Graff. He’s a stag. Is that not plain to see? Maddy was

right, you do need a babysitter…” and for the rest of the day, Sephany is never far from

Lucan, correcting his actions as your party picks it’s way on horseback through the great
forest.
Throughout the day, you get the chance to observe how this half-platoon operates. The

core of the platoon are the 8 men with spears and shields. The ride in single file, and you

can see that each has a sector they are responsible for watching. The 4 women are called

‘foresters’. Two of them ride ahead scouting, while one tails the party at a significant

distance. The fourth, Sephany, stays with the company, usually nearby Lucan. At the head

of the column of men is Lt. Maddy Riverstone, whom the foresters periodically return to

report to. Though most of this platoon are very young, the average age likely being 23 at the

most, you can see that they are well trained and disciplined. And the young men are

friendly, exchanging stories with any of you willing to engage with them. They know quite a

bit about your exploits in the arena, and seem to have a healthy respect for you.

The journey through Cormanthor should be pleasant; the weather is perfect, every sense is

rewarded with sylvan pleasures, and the company is good. But for all that, some of you

(though not Mari or Jeska) have knots in your stomachs and feel as though you don’t
belong. Seeing the concern on Ser Rowan’s face, Maddy addresses him.
“It’s beautiful here, isn’t it” she asks of the large man.
“It should be, Lieutenant.” He responds “The air, the flowers, the music of the forest. All

that is perfect. Even the trail is good, and I didn’t expect that. And yet, something doesn’t
sit well with me.”
“Cormanthor doesn’t want us here. People that is. Humans. It’s been angry about our

presence here since we erected the Standing Stone and began to carve it back. Our

forebears didn’t realize it. It’s only in the last hundred years or so that you can feel it. You

can even see it, how it tries to choke our settlements, and spoil our roads. It doesn’t do

that to halfling settlements, or the elves. Just us. It’s why there was so much trouble at

Thistle Ridge all those years ago.” With the mention of Thistle Ridge, Ser Rowan sits up
straight in his saddle and takes a deep breath.
“I know you’re aiming to go and sort that place out, but you have your work cut out for you.

If goblins and wolves and bears aren’t enough, you’ll have the forest itself as an adversary.

But we have a while together today and at camp tonight. I’ll share with you everything I

know so you’ll have some chance to come out the other end of this thing.” And with this,

Ser Rowan and Lt. Riverstone begin a cordial relationship of colleagues-in-arms, sharing

stories, tactics, strategies and hopes. This does indeed brighten the journey for the young

men of your company, who listen as the young Lt. pours out knowledge from what seems
like several lifetimes of experience in the region.

haste as the sun starts to set. You get the impression that the Lt. didn’t get as far as she

planned by some of the looks on the faces of the men with her. But after less than an hour

of pressing on, your host comes to a thick copse of deciduous trees and brush, and you

can see the bony remains of some old cottages peeking through the foliage. You have
made Greenwood Farm.
The farm itself was once 6 buildings, but 5 now are little more than mossy piles of

mouldering timber. The last was once a grand farmhouse by the look of it, though now it is

little more than the crumbling stone walls of the ground floor of the large building. The Sun

Blades get to work quickly, securing horses and preparing camp. They bring all their

supplies inside the stone wall remains, and many of them get to work with machetes and

axes, clearing away brush to make camp. In doing so, they uncover a few rings of stones
from bygone campfires.
Before long, your entire company is nestled neatly into a tidy camp, and the second most

senior officer, a rugged young man named Sgt. Brendle Vale, declares “the walls are still

good, L.T., and the bush is thick about them. I think we can risk a fire. It might be last our

friends enjoy for a little while…” and with this, Maddy nods, and soon a small fire is going in

a tidy pile of stones. While a couple of the Sun Blade men are getting it going, Lucan goes

to toss in a green branch that has been cleared, but Sephany grabs it from him gently and,
gives him a kind and patient smile.
“This will make smoke, Sembian. Let me show you what to burn and how.” With
Sephany’s words, a quip comes quickly to Lucan’s mind, but before he can give it voice,
she puts a finger on his lips.
“There is no need for you to wag your tongue and say charming but inappropriate things to

me. You’re as cute as I’ve heard, and your helplessness out here is somehow charming.

But I’ve got my orders to give you enough knowledge to live at least for an afternoon on your

own in Cormanthor, and that leaves us little time for diversions. Save your fancy words

and overtures for town-girls, Sembian. If you listen to me, maybe you’ll live long enough to
bed another dozen of them.”
And over the next couple of hours, Sephany checks in regularly with Lucan, showing him

how to adjust his pack so it won’t get caught on trees, how to properly oil his weapons and

gear so everything is protected and supple without the tell-tale odour of oil to give away his

position, how to pack things to make the least amount of noise during travel, and dozens of

other tips that would be impossible to bear from anyone without her curly brown hair,
rugged-but-womanly figure, and
compassionate demeanour.

Between the 8 men, 2 flasks of mead are shared, which they readily share with your group.

“You’ll recognize the flavour, Mum” Maddy says to Mari. “This is made with your honey. It

As the camp comes together, bellies are filled, gear is squared away for the morrow, and

everything is in order, the watches begin, while the rest of the camp gathers around the

fire. A couple of the men are already sleeping, but most of you gather in close to share

quiet stories, jokes, recent news from town (the soldiers are rapt when you discuss recent

affairs), news from afar (they even more rapt by the words of the Sembian and the Elf, who
are both exotic to these men).
