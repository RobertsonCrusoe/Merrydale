Significant Entries from the Journal of Brother Stephan Dunmorrow
Envoy of Prelate Calvin Van Miserly of St. Ignatius
October 6, 1746
When I first woke today, I was happy to see that my long journey from the Seminary was coming to an end.  Across a broad valley, I espied the Hedge, of which I was warned.  My good man, Janvermin, sensed some comfort in me, and was quick to dispense with it.
The Hedge of the Valdenwail
He said grimly, pointing as if I was as nearsighted as my thick spectacles make me seem.

It marks a new phase of our journey.  And a phase you shall enjoy as much as the previous
The man is cheerful as a gallows keeper.  He was right though.
We quickly packed up our austere camp, and headed through the valley and towards the hedge on the other side.  This distance I greatly misjudged.  It seemed to me a distance of perhaps a half mile.  But my judgement was misguided by the size of the Hedge.  Thinking it perhaps 20

high, I measured the valley thusly.  And so it was that we had to cross a broad valley of several miles before our steep assent up the decrepit road to the edge of that wall of brambles.
The hedge itself must be well over 100
high.  Like any natural forest growth, it is thinnest and shortest at the edges.  The road, such as it is, is overgrown by the encroaching Hedge at many places, and we were forced to veer off our course several times, and Janvermin had to find our way back.  He is good at what he does, though, and we were never waylaid long before he had us marching over top of the ancient stones that form this once-proud road.

After a couple hours of this winding and trail blazing, we came to the densest part of the hedge.  By this time, the road was a little better, and as we progressed, the Hedge around us became thicker and more sinister looking; large and wickedly curved thorns decorate the cheerless Hedge trees.  One cannot pass anything larger than a hand into the hedge at this point, without sustaining many lacerations.

This thickness and impassability of the hedge is, at this point, marked by its vast height as well.  The hedge is certainly over 100
high, but only at some points is it possible to take a sounding of it
s height, for it is mostly like a tunnel; the grim coppice forming a vaulted tunnel, sometimes just barely over our heads, sometimes many scores of feet above us.  Most times, the light of day is so thoroughly obstructed and diffused that one would think it the twilight hour.  At some points it is pitch darkness.  Still, at times, the canopy opens up, and sunlight falls, and it is much like taking a deep draught of air after a long submersion in a cold and inky lake.

I should say that the path is by no means straight.  I doubt very much that we followed one tract of that gloaming grotto that stayed its course for more than 100 yards before an obtuse change in course presented itself.  At many times, the conduit of the Hedge diverged from the ancient stones, only to find the venerable path again after several twists and turns.

Though we did not uncover any forks in the road, we did espy on two separate occasions, something which made us think we were nearing our destination.  The first time, to the left of us, we saw some light struggling to penetrate the sable undercroft, and after a protracted bout of straining scrutiny, both I and Janvermin spotted a grey stony structure through the Hedge.  We thought we would soon be coming to the Wall that I had been told of, which marks the halfway point of the veiled journey through that cheerless furrow.  However, the meandrous thoroughfare went soon to the right, and it was long before our next sighting.

Several hours later, we again noticed, through and uncommonly thinned, yet still inexplicably obstructive, portion of the Hedge, a portion of grey stony wall.  This time, we were both able to make out an archway in what was clearly a man-made, though ancient, wall.  It was perhaps 50
into the Hedge.  I asked my man;
Perhaps we are meant to carve our way to that bit of stone there, Vermin?
His look was blank.  He looked about briefly, and shrugged his shoulders - an uncommon response from him, to be sure.
What do you think, man?
I demanded of him.
I am ashamed to own, Sir,
he replied
I am quite out of my sounding.  I have long ago lost track of our direction, and I am no longer even certain of the time of day.
Finding the time, at least, takes less skill than a leathery wanderer
I began.  I took my timepiece out of my shirt pocket, and to my horror, I noticed that it had become unwound.  It should have been good for a day or two, at least, before it needed winding again.  Perhaps this was a coincidence, but I begin to think, now especially, that it was not.
Well, then!
I commanded myself.
We continue on.  Though I have read almost all there is to read of this Hedge and the Valdenwail itself, I have read no tales of forestry efforts or the like.  We continue down this crepuscule path!

Brave words then.  Thinking back on those Hedge-hidden blocks of stone has certainly fueled my imagination.  Whatever could those buildings be used for?  By whom?  How does one arrive at them?  How long is it since they have seen the sight of Men?

After those mysterious structures, the path became more serpentine and increased in its opacity for the next several hours, until at last, we were sure that darkness had fallen on the Natural World outside of this confounding Hedge.
We have now made what camp we can.  I am thankful, just now, for Janvermin
s expertise and circumspection.  He had filled all of our skins with fresh water in the nape of the valley before the aperture of the Hedge.  I was too excited to tend to such minutiae, but after such a journey, we are both in need of replenishment.

If I never saw pitch blackness before, I have tonight.  Our camp is of such a stygian nature, as to make me suspect that cheer and light were but tricks of the mind; falsely remembered and unconceived in fact.
October 7
Today brought more hope with it, but ended with less.
When we both agreed that it must be somewhere around daybreak, we packed up and made our way.  We were not convinced of our reckoning for some time; the vault of the Hedge was close in about us for a couple hours (if time exists in such a piceous void as this portion of the Hedge), and we saw no hint of light all that time.  Finally, though, the way became more cavernous, and through the brambles above, we again saw light.  Such was my joy at the sight of those wan cobbles ahead of us that it was long before I noticed the condition of my guide.

My good man, Janvermin, had been using what little light we had with us (some candles and one anemic lantern which burns more oil than it casts light) to find our way.  But he had been using his hands to find our way in some of the more nigrous and sigmoid portions of the journey, and he was covered in wicked lacerations too numerous to tabulate.  I implored him to stop so that I could dress his wounds.

Upon cleaning the wounds with what little water we could spare, I noticed that they were grossly inflamed as well.  I am no Doctor of Medicine, but I know a dangerous wound when I see one, and he has scores.  Already, he has developed a cough and a pallor that worries me.

At any rate, we continued on.  The day was full of the same findings as yesterday; the meandering conduit within the Hedge, sometimes following the ancient and worn cobbles, sometimes not, rare sightings of structures, perhaps forgotten by all but us, through this swart nettle.

Finally, though, the road and the path widened, and we followed a straight path up a hill for several hundred yards.  The road here, though grown over considerably, revealed some particularly large stones that had engravings of all manners of things upon them.  At a few spots along the way, the broken remnants of old statues of many sizes, now covered in the same bracken as the rest of the land, evoked disconsolate images of a great Empire, erstwhile and unremembered.  I wanted to examine some of the carvings and statues, but Janvermin would abide none of my distractions.  Today, I did not argue with him.  He is now a sick man.

That steep approach is bathed in a wan light, as the gorse above is presumably not so thick.  Towards the top of the hill, we were able to see quite clearly, a stone wall which obstructed our path, and a great archway in that wall through which we might pass.  As before, I misjudged the size of that which I observed.  The wall and the archway seemed to get no nearer with my first many steps, until it seemed like we were marching up a most unnatural hill, which checked our progress.  But it was long after apparent that the hill was just longer and higher than I thought, as was the wall, and the width of the very road leading to it.  Though this misconception is partly due to clever engineering and tricks of scale and perspective, it is also largely due, I am sure, to the unnatural and oppressive nature of the Hedge, and it
s adeptness for inspiring confusion and doubt.
At length, we came to the top of the hill, and found ourselves before a great wall and a great archway.  I should think that it may have been a veritable splendour of the ingenuity, industry and will of Men, if the wall and the aperture could be seen in the unobstructed light of day.  But, though some little light did come through the furze above, it was still no brighter than a cloudy twilight.  Details and convolutions upon the wall and the archway there were aplenty.  Too many, in fact, to get a focus on one, for the first time observer of this magnificent and cyclopean structure.  I should have liked to stay a while and recorded some of them.  There is an entire encyclopedia of information and history in that archway, I am sure.  But, it was not to be.  For starters, my guide would not have permitted a lingering.  But it was our new company that hastened things along.

For, we were not alone under that prodigious structure.  Under the archway stood a trio of sentinels, as quiet as they were dark.  We were both quite frightened when we saw them, and no one stirred a fig for quite some time.  I confess that I went through a series of thoughts and emotions.  The figures, under that enormous archway, looked like toy soldiers beneath a more commonly sized archway.  They provoked fear at first, but then doubt; I thought that perhaps they were statues, so still they stood.  But then I knew that they were living things, of a stamp.  They did not move or make noise or give any other indication to the observer of their prescience, but they did inspire one to realization and further fear.

Finally, after many exchanged glances and whispers between us, Janvermin and I approached the trio of sentinels.  That they are of a human proportion and configuration is a good place to begin my description of them.  They are adorned with the trappings of men, ancient though they are.  There, their likeness to living, breathing men stops.

These sentinels stand about as large as the largest of men.  They are clad in bronze armour so old and thick with grime of ages as to appear black in all but the most elevated of reliefs.  Their armour is the plate variety that one can now find in museums and the halls of the warlike Saints and Nobles; it covers every possible bit of flesh, and is so bulky and heavy that it was, in its day, more of a liability than an asset, and has thus been replaced by lighter armours in our times.  The begrimed armour is covered, on all surfaces, by very intricate relief.  I have not had the opportunity, as one could well imagine, to clean and inspect one of these suits properly, but even though the grit of a dozen centuries chokes those friezes, I could plainly see that the armour was beautiful, and that each suit must have a great story to tell.

But that is neither here nor there.  The issue at hand was salutations, and those were of the most unusual variety I have ever experienced, and hope never to experience again.  I remember a time, during my Investiture at the Seminary, that some of my fellow students would sing the most salacious song.  It was entitled Lonely Sister Rebeccah Tuck, and it was full of all the obvious limericks you might expect from such a title.  Anyway, there were verses that would stick in my head for days at a time, and it felt like a veritable intrusion into my own mind.  Meeting the sentinels was much the same.  That they spoke directly inside my head, I don

t doubt, though it was not the same as hearing one speak with a corporeal tongue.  I was given the distinct impression by the sentinels that I would not be able to pass without some explanation as to my business in the Valdenwail.
I croaked out an explanation.
Sirs, my apologies for intruding upon your most serene and amicable home.  I am Brother Stephan, Envoy from the Seminary of St. Ignatius, and I come bearing an marqued letter from His Most Enlightened Prelate Van Miserly, to be delivered to the hands of the Scion of St. Avitus, and none other.

I remember looking at my toes the whole time I delivered this speech, and the shiny creases on the folds of the worn leather of my shoes.

I was then aware that I was free to pass, but that Janvermin was not.  I stammered a plea for him to accompany me, but it was met with a wall of obstinate will, and I knew that he could not pass.  He knew it too.
I will go down the hill, and await your return
Janvermin said to me through his weak coughs.
I do not wish to wait under the gaze of these things, and under the maw of this wall.  I wish you well.  Though this place is foreboding, I feel you have safe leave.  If they wished you harm, it would have been done to you already.  Fare you well, Envoy

I nodded a weak nod, and approached the sentinels.  Finally they moved; they pivoted on one heel, and pointed into the archway, to the gloom beyond.  And then, one by one, they turned back to their previous resolute stance.

I journeyed past the sentinels, leaving Janvermin behind.  He has been in my mind all the rest of this dreary and trying day.  For the Gate is but the halfway point in the Hedge, if what I have read is true, and I have at least 2 days to spend, clawing through this caliginous bramble, before I am delivered from it.
October 12
Today, or rather, tonight, I have emerged from the Hedge, not 2 days, but several, since the Gate.  I scarcely noticed that I had emerged, as it is a moonless and overcast night, and light is but a memory to me again.  I have a few scratches from the Hedge, and my mind is on fire half the time, and dreamy the other half.  Now, with a hint of breeze in my hair, I again feel like I have command of my senses, though I have not these past several days.  I feel like I have just woken up from a very long bout of sleep walking through a nightmare.
October 13
It is first thing in the morning, and though it is raining and dismal, the grey sky has a hint of lithium where the sun is hidden, and I know that I am free of the Hedge.  Before me is a rolling moor, with a lonely road of ancient cobblestones tracking a path through the downcast countryside.  The moor is very boggy at this point, but there are some patches of bright green growth at the peaks of some small hills, and their colour is the only hope I have seen in this entire Ecclesiarchy.  The wind is strong, but refreshing.  It has the smell of the ocean that I was told to expect, though I had never smelled myself.
Later on
I spent all day in my solitary travel down the old road, and have seen not a soul.  The road is broad, and many of the stones, like some of those before the Gate, have been carved with friezes and words.  The language is clearly Old Imperial, though much of it is interspersed with older characters that I cannot discern.  I have made drawings of some of them, but the weather is too foul for such business, and I must make haste.

Tonight, I make my lonesome camp under a large rock on top of a hill.  I found a little stream, and have filled my flasks and drunk, and feel coherent and well for the first time in days.

Though I feel well, I wonder how poor Janvermin is faring.  Already, I am far behind schedule.  We estimated 2 more days in the hedge, 2 days to the Valdenwail, a day there, and 4 back to the Gate.  I have been 5 days emerging from that damnable Hedge.  I hope that Janvermin has both patience and health to hold.
October 14
Today has been drizzly all day.  The soggy skies seem satisfied to deliver as little moisture as possible, to protract it

s irrigous business as long as possible.  The wind blows right into my face as I continue in what I now think is a south-easterly direction.  It has been increasing it
s fierceness all day, and the organic smell of the ocean is growing stronger.
I saw people for the first time today.  They were very distant, perhaps the better part of a mile away, and several hills removed from the road.  They seemed to have a small flock of sheep or some other animal with them.  We looked at each other for a long time across a hilly lowland, and at length I decided to approach them.  I was not certain what I would have to say to a bunch of rustic shepherds (in any part of the world, let alone this bygone country), but I was in need of any human interaction.  I think I would have settled for a beating from them, if they would have it.
They wouldn
t.  By the time I got to their previously espied location, they were gone.  I must confess that I doubted my sanity at that point.  I worried that I was imagining people, and shepherds at that!  But no; I have spent enough time on the road with Janvermin to know to look at it once in a while, and sure enough, there were plenty of tracks of both men and beasts, and I was satisfied that I was not insane, but rather unsavoury company for these folks, and set back to my trail.
October 15
I am camped at the gates of the Valdenwail, and won

The day began with a continuation of the doleful weather for which I have now come to associate with this dispiriting Ecclesiarchy.  A deliberate march through the rolling hills brought me alternatively from the boggy swales, to the wind-blasted heaths of this woebegone moor.  As the march progressed, I became aware that I was climbing higher and higher, and the nadirs of the rolling moor became tolerably moist the further I went, though the bitter wind became fiercer upon those unwelcoming zeniths.

I should say a thing about the road at this point.  The road follows a very irregular course, and there are at many points, ancient junctions that lead to Sodor

s Door for all I know.  It is only by the lasting and antediluvian virtue of the map which I copied from the Magnus Corpus Avitus that I was able to make my way to my final destination.  The road is confounding, to be sure, though, and I had to
back track

The road leading to the Valdenwail itself is unmistakable though; all of the secondary arteries which I found myself walking down soon proved to be of an even more senescent and disused nature, if a magnitude of age and neglect greater than that of the primary thoroughfare can be imagined.  I confess that it makes my head swim to think of such ancient roads, once so large and well constructed, becoming so old and timeworn as to be all but forgotten.  What once happened upon those Quondam Arteries of Empire?

One veritably stumbles into the Valdenwail, if it can be imagined.  In the last league before the ancient acropolis, the approach becomes very difficult.  The land becomes very stony and mountainous, with very little vegetation of any variety beyond nasty fungi, and the road at this portion of the journey has been carved out of the rock itself; it is much like a trough in the stone, and at many points, crumbling bulwarks rise above the edge of the trough, and stare down menacingly, like an old man with no teeth, at the weary traveler on that compromised road.  This rocky flume has certainly been carved out of the now-mountainous country for a dual purpose; it allows some semblance of ease of navigation through what would now otherwise require the aide of a seasoned mountaineer, but it also provides a very compromised position for any invader that might come up this road. The flag stones themselves are a rich tapestry of history, and the walls are lined with statues of men and women and creatures that are forgotten by all save the most privileged and lore-stepped inhabitants of the Valdenwail.

When I got there, it was nearly eventide, though, and not one black window framed a hint of light.  The sight of such a magnificent structure, so cleverly conceived, artfully constructed, graceful to behold, and seemingly impregnable, the sight of it in such a state of neglect and disuse made me so saturn that I nearly wept.

However, I knew that I was getting closer.  The towers and battlements that rise above the carved-out trough became more frequent (though in no greater condition of repair or use) and much more impressive.  The journey became much steeper as I went along as well, and I had to scramble over some debris at many points, presumably from depreciating battlements above me; by this time, it was quite dark and the rain had become quite fierce.  A great howling was overhead as well, and occasionally a thunderous whistling (one really must hear it to agree that this description, while seemingly oxymoronic, is actually accurate) came down the Channel accompanied by stinging blasts of the salty, acrimonious gale.

But it was not this detail of the ancient architecture which caught my focus, for before me is the Valdenwail.

The assent to this peak is so high and steep that you cannot have any notion that such an amazing acropolis is just on the other side, waiting to surprise you like a bandit hiding behind a great fallen log.  As you climb this last bit of this unhappy road, you are compelled to take in the twin towers, which I believe are named Gromhaufel and Zuirenhahst, so that the vista beyond the zenith is sprung like a trap.  Add to this contrivance of the clever and ancient architects, the merciless winds which come straight off the ocean and up to this peak, racing for your exposed and tender flesh like coursing hounds for the hare, which force you to recoil and make your redoubt before you can again look out, and you find yourself at a loss of initiative, to be sure.

There is a small aperture in that natural wall, perhaps one hundred yards high (though it is tough to tell at this distance, and I have proven myself a most terrible judge of measurement), opposite the Valdenwail, through which a couple large ships might sail.  Even from this distance, I can see that a great citadel sits atop this natural arch, so that no ship could pass unwelcome into that Harbour.

Looking down over the substantial battlements, I could take in the serenity of the Harbour below.  Though the ocean raged beyond the wall, the Harbour was calm.  It must be horribly and unfathomably deep, so black are those still waters.  The natural precipice upon which the Valdenwail sits rises at least one hundred yards from the face of the water, and nearly straight up, until the huge blocks of the outer wall are found.  At the base of this escarpment, growing out of the sea like hundreds of gnarly and broken teeth, are smaller stone protrusions; the remnants of a slowly receding cliff.  They are worn to every shape thinkable, and make old things look new, so moss-covered and weather-eaten are they.  And yet, upon most of them are small buildings.  Small only because of my great distance from them, and their relative scale beside the vast walls of the Valdenwail.  Surely, those are small fortresses upon those stalagmites of the Harbour, and they are as mossy as the geologically uncertain protrusions upon which they sit.  Many have vast and nearly impossible arches spanning the distance between them, so that many comprise small networks between themselves with these graceful bridges.  And still many others have only partial bridges which lead out over open water.  Time does pass, even here.

I have espied at least three arches that lead from some of the closer protrusions, upon which sit larger and taller fortresses; arches that lead to the cliff face upon which the Valdenwail sits.  There are bridges upon them that lead to the belly of this behemolithic acropolis, and, presumably, tunnels commencing therefrom, which worm their way up, unseen and unremembered.

I was shaking my head, trying to take it all in.  I was again lost in the complexity and the vastness of the Valdenwail, and was again feeling the sorrow of such a monument of Man

s Industry remaining all but vacant for centuries unnumbered.  I had already taken for granted the feeling of the open air, and the quality of the shrouded light of the sun, when it was all sundered again. This armour was quite green with moss, though, and the legs the more so.  It carried a great shield and a great maul, also bronze, also detailed, mossy and green.  And it wielded the same oppressive will of his fellows.

I came to a pitch black intersection, the pale light now distant behind me.  I could hear the unremitting pace of the Sentinel as it lumbered along. I walked a few blind paces into the darkness, clenching my teeth in my bloody mouth half in pain, half in anticipation of another fall and more pain.  I was lucky.  I found myself on some flat ground, on one of the wider and more worn of the thoroughfares of the Valdenwail.  I had no candle at this point, and was entering the heart of obsidian blackness, my hands outstretched, my left leg limping along behind me, blood trickling down the back of my throat.  My eyes must have been as wide as saucers, so wanting for light was I.

And I found it.  As I hobbled along, I did finally notice a light off to my left.  It was very distant, and I headed towards it gingerly.  I did in fact find one of my candles, and though the Sentinel had made ground on me whilst I clamoured in the darkness, I was easily able to outpace the thing back to my appointed accommodations.  I was then met with my next challenge.

Though I am not known as a man who does not think things through (in fact, I have been accused of quite the opposite in my time), I did leave a large, and insurmountable problem for myself in the poor laying of this plan for adventure.  As I approached the last candle, now burnt nearly to the stone of the street outside of Dunvegan House (a place, by all that is madness, I was elated to see again), I noticed the dangling rope of sheets hanging by the light of the candle, right where I left it.  Now, I am a lean man, and have had many long days toiling at the more bodily tasks that the Seminary demanded of me during my Investiture.  But I am by no means strong under the best of circumstances.  Whether I could have climbed that rope in perfect health, I will never know.  I can say with certainty that I was unable to climb it after my starving incarceration, and with a sprained ankle and broken wrist, though Ignatius knows that I tried.  I tried, in fact, until I was in tears with pain and desperation.  I think I actually tried to use my teeth to replace my lame hand at one point, for the last thing I saw by the light of the dying candle outside the prison I was so desperately trying to enter again, was a very bloody bedsheet.

I was spared only by my ears.  I took a moment to pause, and catch my breath, and I heard a resounding clicking and whooping coming towards me from somewhere within the reticent blackness of the Valdenwail.  Numble.  I blew out the candle, and waited and listened.

Numble it was, sure enough.  I heard him get nearer and nearer, and before long I saw the light of the small candle that he bore before him.  He passed like a ghost, albeit a rambling and noisy ghost that looks capable of dying a second death at any moment.  At the end of the street upon which I hid, and I heard him fumble with his mess of keys.  He made entry into the House.  I paused, then followed in behind him, doing the best I could to be quiet, though I hobbled like a war veteran.  I managed to find a chair in the corner of the parlour that was somewhat obfuscated, and pretended to sleep.  He passed back out of the House, seemingly none-the-wiser.

Just as I thought I was clear of danger, I heard the diligent lumbering of the Sentinel as it walked up to the entrance of Dunvegan House, just as Numble was leaving.  Surely the two saw each other, and indeed, Numble stayed for quite some time on that porch, undoubtedly having an unnatural conversation in the voiceless manner of those terrible Sentinels.  Numble finally let out a gurgling laugh, and clicked off into the lightless labyrinth.

The Sentinel is still standing on that porch as I write this, and has not moved a finger.

I am having a lucid moment just now, and feel like writing.  It is madness to be kept here in the darkness, eating one soft apple a day, and drinking some wine which once tasted so sweet.  I would not be surprised if they have switched the brand, finding that it is the only thing that I consume regularly, and I would not be surprised if I am just plain sick of drinking it day after day, but it is now a noxious swill to me. January 10.
I write this from outside the Gate of the Valdenwail.  The diminutive door in the great double arch of the Gates has only recently slammed shut behind me, and the trio of Sentinels have reeled back their oppressive wills from within my head.  I am over the crest, beneath the great fork-towers and arch under which I camped for days.  I shall only camp one night here this time.  I am on my way home, finally.

The day started as usual.  I was wandering around in the candle light of Dunvegan House, though with a little more excitement than usual.  Numble came in with his usual pomp, which is to say, none.  I confronted him eagerly.

Numble was not carrying his usual sack of rotten food and faggot of soggy wood.  He wore a pair of grey and hoary gloves which I have not seen him wearing before.  He saw me, coughed and choked and roiled and creaked and groaned for some time, and then addressed me.

Numble lead me through miles of veiled street, through avenues even broader than before, and through great gates in thick walls.  Back through time we seemed to journey; the architecture became more simple and prodigious as we journeyed, and the choking dustiness seemed to hit a peak somewhere partway through that twilight journey.  At a couple points, we passed by streets down which I could see daylight shining, and at one point, we even walked through an open-air garden.
