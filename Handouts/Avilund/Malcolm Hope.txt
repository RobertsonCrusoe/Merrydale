A decade ago, '''Malcolm Hope''', architect, mathematician, and one of the most influential Fellows of the [[Imperial Geographic Society]] - perhaps the best regarded and most famous architect in the city - was contracted to rebuild the historic [[Chapel of Saint Caspar-on-the-Hill]], after a fire destroyed the ancient structure. Ten years later, Hope unveiled the interior of the rebuilt chapel shocking almost all who saw it. 

The new frescos, sculptures, mosaics and glass told the well-known story of the [[Creation]] of the world and the rise of the Saints. However, the imagery Hope used has been deemed blasphemous in the extreme. In his frescos, he suggests that Man invented the Saints for his own purposes. He depicts the early Saints as [[Ferals]], and clearly maps out a relationship between ancestor worship and the [[Saintly Faith]]. Every single scene is a rewriting of accepted history removing the layers of religious dogma, and leaving only the raw interpretation of the [[Humanist]] historical truth.

Although he remains a powerful and influential figure in the City, and continues to be a key Fellow within the Society, Hope has recently been imprisoned “for his own safety” while the [[Church]] investigates the numerous complaints regarding Saint Caspar’s Chapel. It is rumoured that he is to be charged with defrauding his patrons and breaching his contract (as those who donated the money for the rebuilding of the chapel are not at all happy with the finished results, and have testified that what Hope delivered was not what they had agreed upon in the contract) rather than with heresy or blasphemy.

It is believed that to try Hope in the [[Ecumenical Courts]] would result in an enormously sensationalist trial, given his fame and notoriety, that would only bring the ideas of the [[Humanist Movement]] further out into the open for the general public to hear. A trial of such nature in the Ecumenical Courts would necessarily result in putting the Humanist Movement on trial for all to see. It is thought that neither the government nor the church want to see that happen yet. Therefore, trying Hope in the [[Civil Court]] over a breach of contract would deflect much of the attention and minimise the potential damage.
