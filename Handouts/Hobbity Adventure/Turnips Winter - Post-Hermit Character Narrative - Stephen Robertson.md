That winter, Turnip avoided the woods entirely, except for short forays at dawn when he believed shadows would be at their weakest. He brought a walking stick on these excursions, though he never leaned on it, preferring to hold it like a cudgel. And he still kept the haft of the hatchet that he had shattered on the Hermit’s forearm when it had grabbed at Boffo. More than once, he beat tree stumps just to see if they rang hollow. Sometimes they did. This confirmed nothing but made him feel justified. 

His nights grew restless. He kept the lantern lit and worried about his stock of oil, but not enough to conserve it. He checked the windows three times before sleep. He left his slippers by the door pointing outward, in case “something tries to come in wearing them,” a precaution he could not fully explain except to say it seemed sensible. He salted the threshold once a week. He salted it again after snowfall, because you never know what gets tracked in on the drifts.

He took to writing. He began cataloguing oddities around Thistledown that no one else thought odd: that the foxes were quieter, that the owls were flying wider circles, that the Hedgebrooks had a baby who never cried, that a scarecrow two fields over seemed to have changed hats despite no one touching it. He wrote these things down in a small notebook which he kept in his vest. The notebook grew thick with his observations and before long it had become a diary of sorts.

The thing that threw him off the most was that magic was real. That changed everything. 


I confess I have been thinking far too much, which Mum always said was dangerous for a hobbit, especially in winter when the cold gets into your bones and your nerves start hopping about like field mice, so I decided to put my thoughts down here, though I never meant to keep a diary because diaries feel like invitations to one’s future self to look back and say, “What on earth were you doing, Turnip, you ninny,” but the trouble is that ever since the business with the Hermit I keep remembering things at odd angles, as though the memories themselves are leaning, and if I don’t pin them down somewhere they will keep rolling around in my head like apples escaping a basket on a sloped floor and getting bruised and then all you can do is bake them into pie, but then you can’t separate one thing from another and just have to savour the lot of them as a whole, which is tasty but doesn’t help the old cogitations.

Anyway, the thing is I had another dream, and I know it was a dream because I woke up, but part of me wonders if that is the only difference, because it felt real in the way a memory does when you’re suddenly reminded of it by a smell or a sound you didn’t expect, and the details were sharper than dreams usually allow. I stood in front of the Hermit’s home again—except writing “home” feels wrong, as if I’m giving the place too much credit. It was a hobbit-hole, like I saw in the woods, a tall hobbit-hole that truth be told had no business being so tall, because it was built into a hill but then the hill rose up too far and the round green door had a second row of windows perched above it like eyebrows drawn by someone who had only ever heard rumours of face shapes but had never actually seen a face. It reminded me of the Alewarden’s burrow after he tried to expand upward without asking permission from the Thistledown Planning Committee, except his only sagged and this one… leaned forward, just a little, like it was interested in me, which was more unsettling than if it were disinterested, which although its impolite is a more natural attitude for a hobbit hole, architecturally speaking.

Now I know Wedge says he saw an enormous skull and Boffo says he saw a drafty old cabin, but that’s not what I saw, and not what I would have wanted to see, if it comes to that, but then the world doesn’t always show itself to us in the shape we’d rather see, and even when it does you’d better be doubly careful. Wedge told me the other day that if a house can choose how to appear, it can also choose what to hide, which is a thought that makes me want to crawl under the covers and never come out. Still, like Boffo says, “You can worry all morning, or you can have breakfast. Breakfast works better.” I had three breakfasts this morning, and I have to say he’s right. He also says, “Things look less scary after breakfast,” which is probably true, though I’ve found it depends very much on what was in the breakfast and whether there’s sufficient jam. Boffo always says things that are profound.

Anyway, I was standing there in front of the too-tall hill, and he stepped out—the Hermit that is—with his beard blowing in the breeze (which there wasn’t one), and his face stretched wrong like a sheet of pastry rolled too thin, and his eyes glowing with that hungry pumpkin-orange light. He asked if I’d brought pie. I had not. He looked disappointed. Deeply.

I woke up with my mouth dry and my feet cold and ate half a loaf of seedcake without even slicing it, just chewing and trying not to think about how wide his jaw opened.
