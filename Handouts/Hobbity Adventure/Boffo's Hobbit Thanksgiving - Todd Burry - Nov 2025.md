Early morning, in the hamlet of Thistledown. It’s crisp, the smell of last night’s rainstorm still lingers in the air. You’re checking the straps on Binny, your uncle’s old pony, one last time, making sure your bag is secure for the journey ahead. It’s not a long journey, just two days’ travel with a night's camp in between. Your mood darkens slightly at the thought of having to spend the night on the hard ground and not on a soft bed at the The Right Way Inn as you had planned. You were meant to have left yesterday, but the storm forced you to delay your departure.  If you’re going to make it to Gomwick by tomorrow evening, you’re going to have to forego taking the North Road, and instead cut across the woods on the Old Road. You know the forest route well enough, and there are good places to stop and camp along the way. But you will miss the call the comforts of the Right Way tonight, as you lie on your bedroll hoping it doesn’t rain again.

So the plan’s made. Leave this morning, heading out of town, and nor’west along the Old Road, through the forest till dark. Tomorrow, keep at it, and enter Gomwick with plenty of time for the supper feast to kick off the Harvest Festival! Then sell the goods the family’s loaded up on Binny, and settle in for a few days of drinking, eating, games and more. Home by the end of the week!

----------------

Hopefully you guys are happy and willing to play this little adventure.

I’m making this using the Dungeons & Dragons Basic/Expert rules, which are the exact same as OSE. I have the physical boxes and booklets of D&D, so that’s the only reason why I’m using that instead of the pdf’s of OSE.  I want to run this with as much actual physical dice rolling, pencil+paper, real books, etc as I can. I realize we have to use the VTT, but there won't be too many maps and other VTT things, if I can help it.


Some minor items for your consideration / discussion, that I am implementing for this light-hearted jaunt:

1.  You are all Hobbits, and you start at 0th Level.

This is just because.  Why not?

You’ll roll 3d6 for each attribute, then roll a d6 for hit points, and away we go.  All you are is potential.


2. No traditional character classes.

When you reach 1st level your character class is going to be 'Adventurer’.  You’ll essentially be a Fighter/Mage/Thief.  This will make sense when we get there.  Just think about it as being a generalist able to swing a sword, wear armour, disarm traps, cast a spell or two, etc. 

You can have a look at page 15 of the OSE Advanced Book to see how it works, but the XP advancement is going to be a bit different, since you won’t have to advance each class individually. Your class is going to be ‘Adventurer’, and not Fighter/Mage/Thief, so you’ll only have a single XP table.



That’s about it.


Some hobbits off to have a good time at the harvest festival! What could possibly go wrong?

On magic:

Magic is real in the world, but your experience of it in the Shires is of it being very subtle and benign.  Certainly nothing like what you’ve just experienced (Hungry Hank coming to life to rampage through the fair grounds, or the feeling of being ’Seen’ straight to the very marrow of your bones, or even the strange mist in the woods, etc) in the last few days.

As examples, you each carry a magical item that you picked up from Farmer Fallow’s Knick-Knack and Produce Road-Side Stall on your way to Gomwick.

Wedge picked up a whistle that seems to call an animal to him. The precise nature of this magic is yet to be fully explored.

Boffo pickled up three feathers that, when worn, seemingly give him a boost to his speed.

Turnip picked up some strange stockings, the nature of which is as yet unknown.

Is any of that magic? Could the whistle bring an animal to Wedge because it is curious of the sound? Could Boffo’s speed be boosted simply because of the confidence he has from wearing his spiffy new hat, and his hard-won ribbons and trophy? And could Turnip’s stockings give him… well, we’ll see what they give him if he ever decides to swallow his Hobbit pride and put them on.

You’ve also heard of other subtle magic, such as Otto Tilly’s grandchildren singing to his apple trees, netting him first prize for best apples 7 years running. Everyone practices some version of folk magic to keep the nasty elves away, and perhaps believe that the items the dwarves craft possess some enchantment or other.

But in practical game terms, there is definitely magic in the world and once you get to Level 1 (when you make the commitment to abandon this parochial life in favour of a life of adventure) you’ll get a chance to explore that first hand.

Now...

In the aftermath of Hungry Hank’s rampage:

Once the dust has settled a bit, a few narratives begin to take hold amongst the crowd. First, you see Gammy go up on stage imploring everyone to calm down and try to convince people that Hungry Hank simply caught fire early, and then that fire caused it to come loose from the pole to which he was tied. He points out that it didn’t move far at all, and what is being claimed to have been 'Hank’s Rampage’ in reality was just Hank falling over very slowly.

And indeed, before long this version of the story takes root since the vast majority of people were not close by to Hank and just either saw the whole thing from a distance, or heard about it second or third hand. Within the hour, the story is that people are being ridiculous to suggest that Hank could’ve come alive at all, and they’ve just had too much cider and beer for their own good sense.

However, within that narrative, another thread is being spun about Boffo specifically. People are talking about how he selflessly threw himself off the stage to protect the children in Hank’s path as best he could, and then heroically stopped Hank’s dangerous advance by fanning the flames and bringing the scarecrow low with the use of a torch, in complete disregard for his own safety. (All the while, his two so-called friends froze stiff in abject terror in this moment of need! It is  only Boffo that has the courage and heart of a True Hero!)

And so, as the rumours and stories spread through the crowd, more and more hobbits clasp Boffo’s hand, shaking it vigorously, and congratulating him on being such a "Fine Fellow, Indeed!” and placing a mug of beer or a turkey drumstick or candied apple in his hand.  (And a few people give Wedge and Turnip a bit of side-eye and the occasional “tut-tut” of disapproval.)

About an hour afterwards, the whole incident has been dismissed as having been just a "queer sort of accident, dont'cha know?” And the music starts up again, and everyone resumes their festive mood, joking about how now that Hank was burned early, it means everyone’s wishes will come true quicker, and wondering if Brave Boffo the Pie-Champion might have it in him to win again next year, and so on.

But within that aftermath that swirls around you, you seek out Buford Niss, finding him in a bit of contemplative thought, his jovial mood somewhat diminished. You ask him if he has ever seen anything like this, and he insists that this is not the place to be talking of such things, and invites you all to come stay at his house this evening, where you can talk in private.

“Gather your things, and meet me at the ’Toppo Hill House.” He walks a little distance where you see a little carriage is waiting. The carriage looks a bit like a rickshaw but with the seat facing backwards so Buford can see behind him and not the back of the driver or the pony’s ass. He climbs onto the well cushioned and upholstered seat, drawing a wool blanket over his legs, then taking out a gold flask from his pocket and taking a quick drink out of it.

“Don't tarry!” he calls out to you, as the driver clicks his tongue, and the pony lurches forward.

Buford’s house is easy to spot, and therefore easy to find your way there should you choose to do so. Toppo Hill House is a hobbit hole at the top of the hill overlooking Gomwick, and even from down here you can see it is enormous in scope and quite splendid. It crowns the top of the hill, and is at least several stories of burrows with a pleasant little garden and gazebo at the very top.


This is where we can pick up next session, unless you want to do anything else beforehand, or you want to more things along a bit by email.

This is a bit of a long one, but this will wrap-up the Prologue of the campaign, and move us into the start of Chapter 1.

__

As you follow the strange bronze coin’s direction, pushing you northwards by instinct, you make your way out of the strange domain of The Hermit of the Woods.  As suddenly as it appeared, you find that the mist has vanished, and the crisp autumn air becomes more familiar and welcoming.

About an hour’s walk, you emerge out of the woods and back at the Old Road, and can see before you the mile or so of farmland that leads down the gentle slope towards Nettle’s Pond and the small village of Gomwick on its north shore. Even from here you can hear the distant sounds of the multitudes enjoying the final day of the Harvest Festival.

Your stomachs rumble. You realize now that you left all of your food atop the Hermit’s pumpkin table - that which you did not use to make your soup, that is - and have not eaten a single thing for what must be getting close to three hours!

Seeing the fall sunshine bathe the scene before you, and the vibrant canopy of red, orange and yellow trees of the woods behind you and in the distance, you begin to wonder if what you just experienced happened at all. It seems somewhat dreamlike, and preposterous now that you really dwell on it.  In fact, as you spoke with each other on your walk out of the woods, and realized that none of your impressions of the Hermit’s house’s exterior are even remotely similar, you consider whether all that happened was real or not.

However, you do remember each of your prophetic visions quite clearly: a plague that will decimate the population, a tidal wave that will drown the land, and undeath, patiently, but certainly, waiting.

The walk to Gomwick is interminable as your thoughts turn from what now lies behind you, to the promise of sustenance before you. You question whether or not walking all the way up the slope to Toppo Hill House is urgent enough to deny yourselves the simple necessity of a late lunch.

Fortunately, the decision does not need to be made, as you spy Buford’s small carriage parked outside The Happy Hobbit Inn, signifying his presence inside.  You enter the Inn, and it takes no time to spy the old hobbit holding court at a table in the beergarden in the back. Seeing you arrive, he politely shoos away all the others at his table, and invites you to sit and fill your bellies.  However, he dismisses any talk of your mission until you can speak in private.

Once you have satisfied your appetites sufficiently, he pays the barmaid, and calls for his carriage to take him home; as well as a wagon to pull you up behind him.

You arrive at Toppo Hill House, and retire to a drawing room where tea, wine, biscuits and cake are brought out to supply the conversation.

With intensity in his eyes, Buford listens to your report of meeting with The Hermit.  He answers some of your questions as they come up:

“His name? I do not know it! I don't think even he knows it anymore.  I’ve only ever known him as ’The Old Man in the Woods’ or ’The Hermit’, to be honest. I did not think of giving you a different name when I appointed this task to you, because I do not know what name to give otherwise. Since you did not ask, I did not feel I needed to elaborate, and I am truly sorry about that.”

“I met him many decades ago, when I was not much older than you are now. I stumbled upon his house when I was out exploring the woods. I shared what food I had with him, and so he invited me to stay and help him with his strange arcane machine. He asked me a simple question, one that I thought was cryptic and inane, but this caused me to see a vision -  a vision I have not been able to unsee despite the many years that have since passed. I cannot speak what I saw in that vision to you, I am sorry. But what I saw changed the course of my life. It compelled me to set out for the coast and live a life at sea, where I had a great many adventures, and made my great fortune. But also where I sacrificed and lost a good deal more…”

“A skeleton? What…?! That’s preposterous! Perhaps he grows hungry - I should see if I can have some food delivered for him to see him through the winter. But dead and walking as a skeleton? He is but a simple old man that lives in a cabin in the woods. A bit eccentric, yes, but he is a hermit.  He is one of the tall-folk, and he is very old, so perhaps that is why you were so shocked at his appearance and demeanour. It is my fault that I did not prepare you better. The only foible I can truly ascribe to the man is that he seems to have an insatiable appetite for pumpkin pie, and yet he refuses to come to town and enter into the pie-eating competition!”

“Tobias was a man that was always up to no good. He worked for me once. But then, he’s worked for just about every good gentlehobbit in this part of the shire. As farm labour, household servant, handyman…you name it, he’s done it. We’ve all given him plenty of opportunities to turn his life around, but inevitably he returns to the drink in an evil way, gambles what little coin he’s earned, then becomes violent and hateful and has to be turned away. He is truly unhobbitish. I wonder if he might not have had some goblin or elf somewhere in his ancestry. I do not know how or why he may have met such a terrible end out there in the woods by the Old Man’s cabin, but a few bears, boars, coyotes and other dangerous wild animals are known to sometimes wander those woods, and perhaps it was one of those that gored Tobias, and the Old Man had brought him in to his cabin to try to save him. The idea that the Old Man killed anyone, and then was trying to eat him… well, I think your imagination has run away with you and done several laps of the Shires!”

“I’d like my blowgun back, if you wouldn't mind. It is after all, quite sentimental for me.”

(If you have any other questions, or conversations you’d like to ask or have with Buford, please let me know.)

At the end of your conversation, he gives each of you a stack of 5 gold coins each, and says, “Thank you kindly for having done this task for me. I know it was a heavy thing of me to ask, and it cost you a morning’s enjoyment of the Festival. You are welcome here in my house tonight. In fact, I have already taken the liberty and spoken with Nali Stonecleft, and his grandsons already brought up your belongings, which you will find in your individual guest rooms where you slept last night. Your pony, as well, has been brought up and is enjoying his stay in my stable. I encourage you to go back to the festival and enjoy the final night’s festivities! Then come back and sleep comfortably here. Tomorrow morning we will say our goodbyes, and I will see you well provisioned for your journey back to your homes."

__

You can tell me what you want to do at the festival this final night, if anything. It will pass without much incident.  A few little events will occur, however:

• Turnip will find out that he missed the quarter-final of the Storytelling Competition, and was therefore disqualified. However, he is invited to sit and tell his stories for a group of older hobbits sitting at one of the campfires, and will then listen to these old-timers gripe about how "Tucker Tallowfoot ate 17 pies to win the trophy back in aught-eight ("You’re wrong! It was aught-six, and it was 21 pies!") And they were full-sized pies, mind you, not the little ones this Bucko Lunderfella or whatever his name is, ate this year.” He also listens to them complain about the apples these days being mealy and flavourless, and the girls not being as pretty as they used to be. At the end of the night, they declare Turnip to be a "fine fellow, indeed” and feel that with solid, well prepared young men like him the future of the Shires is in good hands.

• Wedge will be involved in at least three minor fights with inanimate objects he claims were looking at him funny. The third one sees him plunge his axe into the face of one of the wooden horses on the carousel, making several children cry and many parents upset. Nali Stonecleft takes Wedge aside, and steadies the young hobbit’s paranoid nerves with some strong moonshine, and then keeps an eye on him for the rest of the evening.

• Boffo returns to the festival as something of a conquering hero, his legend embellished and grown in his absence. Throughout the evening he is embroiled in romantic misunderstandings and comic situations involving both Daisy and Grunela. It is unknown what becomes of him as he is not seen by his friends until breakfast the next morning, but it is hard to say where he may have spent the night…

In the morning, you are treated to a splendid breakfast by Buford in his home, and afterwards leads you outside to Binny, your pony, that has been loaded with your belongings and supplies for the trip home.  You will be travelling on the North Road this time, with your planned stop at the Right Way Inn this evening, and arriving at your home tomorrow by lunchtime.

Buford bids you fond farewells, slipping each of you another gold coin as he shakes your hands, and reassures you to put the strange happenings you experienced out of your mind, for he will personally get to the bottom of whatever all this was, and it should no longer be a concern for you.

Your trip home is uneventful, the road busy with hobbits heading all along it to their respective villages and hamlets scattered among the Five Shires. Gomwick recedes behind you, its existence largely forgotten until next year’s Harvest Festival brings people back.


Over the winter months a few things will happen:

and gather near to me,
For I shall sing of autumn days
beside this old oak tree.
When pies were piled like haystacks,
and cider steamed the air,
And fate crept softly through the tents
at the Gomwick Harvest Fair.

My friend had reached the finals
in the contest of the pies,
Where hobbits bold did strive with forks
and a hunger in their eyes.
The crowd was loud, the trumpets bright,
the tables fit for kings,
Yet doom lay hidden in the shade
of lesser-spoken things.

For near the cider barrels, friends,
there whispered two in spite,
One, A. Lardy, a grumbling wretch,
whose orchards groaned with blight.
Seven years the fruit-crown lost,
to Odelay the Fair,
And envy gnawed his weary heart
like a weasel in its lair.

Now Odelay oft declared with pride
the secret of his trees:
His children sang among the boughs
like a gentle summer breeze.
Their voices, sweet as starlight spun,
he claimed would bless the fruit,
And by their songs the harvest swelled
from blossom unto root.

But Lardy scoffed at tales like this
with bitter curling lip.
“Lies and tricks and dismal tales,
a fraudulent fellowship.
No magic children, no singing trees,
no charms of branch and bough.
A cheat he is, and by my scheme
I mean to stop him now.”

With Lardy stood the Potty lass,
who muttered low and sly,
She listened to his wicked plan
to taint the apple pie.
A tincture brewed from parasols
of a green and poisonous shade,
A drop to sour the tasting feast
and see their rival fade.

I heard them speak in voices dark,
and fled without a sound,
To seek my true companions two
who swiftly gathered round.
We swore an oath in pumpkin light
to guard the feast that day,
For pies, like peace and honest hearts,
must never be betrayed.

Then through the throngs we stole apart
beneath the banners high,
While market folk with merry shouts
sent coloured leaves awry.
I tracked the rascal Chubbins T.,
a scoundrel thin and sly,
Who bore the fateful flask of doom
with gleam of cunning eye.

He ambled toward the bakers’ tent
all aloof and unperturbed,
Intent to spill his venom there
before the pies were served.
But swift as wind on autumn fields
I leapt to block his path,
And tumbled hard in feigned misstep
to Chubbins' pique and wrath.

Flour burst around us both
like winter’s early snow,
The tent was filled with startled cries,
amidst the ovens' glow.
And in that swirl my comrade brave
with fingers quick and light,
Drew out the flask from Chubbins’ coat
and vanished from his sight.

The villain saw his fortune fled
and into panic ran,
He bolted through the scattered crowds
no more to plot or plan.
And Lardy’s scheme was soon unveiled,
his heart of envy bared,
While Odelay won once again
at the Gomwick Harvest Fair.

So lift your cups and sing with me
of courage in the Shire,
Of pies unspoiled, of friendships true,
of feasting by the fire.
For though the shadows sometimes creep
through tents and autumn air,
The light holds fast when hobbits stand
at the Gomwick Harvest Fair.

*Boffo’s Level 0 Winter Story*

Boffo returned in the crisp days after Thanksgiving, following the familiar
path to the hobbit hole where his mama, grandmama, and great-grandmama
waited. Of their fifty‑three kin, he was the last still at home, and the
three women greeted him with such warm joy you’d think he’d hauled summer
back in his pack.

They fussed over his pie‑eating ribbons and trophy until the shine wore
thin. Boffo only smiled, loving them all the more for it. In his room, he
set his awards in a corner and decided that would be their final place.
Little hobbits soon gathered, wide‑eyed, asking how he’d earned such
treasures. Boffo only shrugged: “I love Miss Daisy’s pies.”

The leaves had fallen since they left, so Boffo raked his family’s hobbit
hole, then the neighbour’s, then the next. By week’s end, he had coaxed
nearly the whole village’s leaves into one tremendous pile on a fallow
field. The littlest hobbits vanished into it each day, entering after
breakfast and tumbling out at dusk.

At the first snowfall, Boffo decided he ought to learn how Miss Daisy made
her pies. He tried his best to recreate them, but the results were never
quite the same pie twice. A little dust, a few mystery specks, and plenty
of guesswork slipped into his crusts. Yet, the pies were always edible and
proudly homemade, and whenever Boffo served them, everyone somehow felt
they had gotten just the right-sized piece.

When winter froze the stream and the Miller's Pudding Hound couldn’t turn
the winter mill‑wheel anymore, Boffo stepped in without fuss. He simply
knocked on the miller's door and said, “Pie’s not pie without flour,”
before taking the hound's place at the wheel. Each morning he pushed it
round, grinding flour for the whole village; each afternoon he baked pies.
And so the village kept its flour through the cold months until spring
thawed the stream and the Pudding Hound's pups were born.

But winter dragged on, and the food stores thinned. No hobbit was in danger
of starving, heavens no! But only five meals a day makes for a gloomy
season. It just so happened that on one of Boffo’s nightly walks, for he
liked the crunch the snow made under his feet and didn't mind the cold, he
came upon a curious patch of nettles poking through the snow. They were the
tallest, thickest, and stinging‑iest, yet somehow the most succulent
nettles a hobbit ever met. They stung something fierce to pick, but Boffo
only grunted, “Pie’s pie, and this’ll do.” So he stepped into the patch and
saved the village’s stomachs, and all the hobbits enjoyed their afternoon
tea with nettle pie for the rest of the winter until spring finally stirred.

Snowdrops bloomed first, cheering every hobbit heart. Then came the
crocuses, bright enough to wake a sleepy village and perfect for
crocus‑bulb scones. After that came fried fiddleheads with sugar, then
blackthorn blossom tarts, then tulip‑bud cake, and then at last savory
allium sammiches—the true heralds of spring!

It was then that a letter arrived for Boffo. He squinted at it, tongue out
in deep concentration, before handing it to his great-grandmama. She read
it, then passed it to his grandmama, who passed it to his mama, who finally
read it aloud so Boffo could hear what the letter had to say. As she read,
Boffo squinted harder and harder; he squinted from one eye to the next. And
when she finally read, “I Remain At Your Service, The Honourable Horace
Buford Hockwallop Niss, Esquire,” Boffo kept right on squinting, and he
didn’t stop for the rest of the day.

The next day Boffo was packed and ready. He hugged his mama, then his
grandmama, then his great grandmama. “I’ll come home again,” he said with a
nod. And off he went.
