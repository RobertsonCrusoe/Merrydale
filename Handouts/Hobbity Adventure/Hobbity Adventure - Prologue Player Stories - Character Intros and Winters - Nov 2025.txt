TURNIP'S INTRODUCTION
Stephen Robertson — 6 November 2025
===================================================================

Turnip "Stockwell" Bramblebrook is a prepper among hobbits, perennially
carrying almost-but-not-quite-the-right-thing-for-the-job.

He chose his own nickname, but everyone still calls him Turnip.

WEDGE'S INTRODUCTION
Les Blackwell — 6 November 2025
===================================================================

William Wedgerton at your service, sirs. Everyone calls me Wedge, which is
what they called my daddy and his daddy. Reused but useful, which is what
I say about the rummage the village pays me to remove and sort or dispose
of.

WEDGE'S WINTER STORY
Les Blackwell — 18 November 2025
===================================================================

During a get-together at Wedge's messy little home sometime in the deep
of winter, over a steaming cup of tea and the requisite number of cakes,
Wedge ruminates upon the adventuring life with his colleagues.

"Only a few years ago, on a night quite like this, all cold and snowy, and
best used for staying indoors and warming the belly and the heart, my older
sister, Willow, began her labours with her first child. As it happened, I
was visiting her and having a cup of tea, just like we're doing now, when
the weather turned foul, and her husband, such as he is, was waylaid by the
storm and drink. Our mother had gone out to fetch him, she having been
nested next to Willow for some weeks in anticipation of this very moment,
and was likewise laid by.

"That left just Willow and myself in her modest home when the show began,
and without recounting the steps that brought me to the foot of the bed,
and the dispensation of modesty required by both of us, I can tell you that
I was... front and centre as it were... when she issued forth her eldest
child, and it was I that not only supported the effort, but pulled young
Odo into the world, amidst the wet and fleshy chaos of the moment.
Everything turned out fine, as you know, minus a bit of scandal about my
role as midwife to my own sister, but fortunately, her husband's truancy
was of greater transgression, and my experience is now a footnote in the
entire affair.

"I say all this, because I have been thinking about the Gomwick Fair last
year, and the dispensation of modesty and abandonment of manners that were
required to pull the whole thing off. And I think that is the very nature
of adventure and adventuring; we put aside our manners and do a sticky job
that others don't want to, or can't do. All to great reward. A risk and a
return, just like we saw at the Spottle table. Just like we've seen in the
very trappings at Toppohill; that is the finest house I've ever seen, and
it's inhabited by the most impertinent hobbit I ever met, saving that
villain, Chubb, who I've tried to forget these last seasons. But that
master Niss is cheeky; sending us to feed a monster from our nightmares,
without telling us we're meant to feed it, or that IT is a monster. He
meant to leave those bits out, or we would never had the courage for the
errand. But now we do, and on top of the coin and nightmares we all
brought out of the situation, I think we've all been left with a bit of
a... desire... for further impertinence. I at least do, and I mean to feed
that desire, and find myself a home..." — and he pauses to look about his
small, dark and messy rooms — "...like Toppohill."

Wedge spends the seasons reading any books he can find that are as close to
forbidden lore and adventures as he can get, as well as practicing petty
thefts, which he always undoes by way of replacing the pilfered items after
he's had a moment to enjoy his clandestine victories.

BOFFO'S WINTER STORY
Todd Burry — 20 November 2025
===================================================================

Boffo's Level 0 Winter Story

Boffo returned in the crisp days after Thanksgiving, following the familiar
path to the hobbit hole where his mama, grandmama, and great-grandmama
waited. Of their fifty-three kin, he was the last still at home, and the
three women greeted him with such warm joy you'd think he'd hauled summer
back in his pack.

They fussed over his pie-eating ribbons and trophy until the shine wore
thin. Boffo only smiled, loving them all the more for it. In his room, he
set his awards in a corner and decided that would be their final place.
Little hobbits soon gathered, wide-eyed, asking how he'd earned such
treasures. Boffo only shrugged: "I love Miss Daisy's pies."

The leaves had fallen since they left, so Boffo raked his family's hobbit
hole, then the neighbour's, then the next. By week's end, he had coaxed
nearly the whole village's leaves into one tremendous pile on a fallow
field. The littlest hobbits vanished into it each day, entering after
breakfast and tumbling out at dusk.

At the first snowfall, Boffo decided he ought to learn how Miss Daisy made
her pies. He tried his best to recreate them, but the results were never
quite the same pie twice. A little dust, a few mystery specks, and plenty
of guesswork slipped into his crusts. Yet, the pies were always edible and
proudly homemade, and whenever Boffo served them, everyone somehow felt
they had gotten just the right-sized piece.

When winter froze the stream and the Miller's Pudding Hound couldn't turn
the winter mill-wheel anymore, Boffo stepped in without fuss. He simply
knocked on the miller's door and said, "Pie's not pie without flour,"
before taking the hound's place at the wheel. Each morning he pushed it
round, grinding flour for the whole village; each afternoon he baked pies.
And so the village kept its flour through the cold months until spring
thawed the stream and the Pudding Hound's pups were born.

But winter dragged on, and the food stores thinned. No hobbit was in danger
of starving, heavens no! But only five meals a day makes for a gloomy
season. It just so happened that on one of Boffo's nightly walks, for he
liked the crunch the snow made under his feet and didn't mind the cold, he
came upon a curious patch of nettles poking through the snow. They were the
tallest, thickest, and stinging-iest, yet somehow the most succulent
nettles a hobbit ever met. They stung something fierce to pick, but Boffo
only grunted, "Pie's pie, and this'll do." So he stepped into the patch and
saved the village's stomachs, and all the hobbits enjoyed their afternoon
tea with nettle pie for the rest of the winter until spring finally stirred.

Snowdrops bloomed first, cheering every hobbit heart. Then came the
crocuses, bright enough to wake a sleepy village and perfect for
crocus-bulb scones. After that came fried fiddleheads with sugar, then
blackthorn blossom tarts, then tulip-bud cake, and then at last savory
allium sammiches — the true heralds of spring!

It was then that a letter arrived for Boffo. He squinted at it, tongue out
in deep concentration, before handing it to his great-grandmama. She read
it, then passed it to his grandmama, who passed it to his mama, who finally
read it aloud so Boffo could hear what the letter had to say. As she read,
Boffo squinted harder and harder; he squinted from one eye to the next. And
when she finally read, "I Remain At Your Service, The Honourable Horace
Buford Hockwallop Niss, Esquire," Boffo kept right on squinting, and he
didn't stop for the rest of the day.

The next day Boffo was packed and ready. He hugged his mama, then his
grandmama, then his great grandmama. "I'll come home again," he said with a
nod. And off he went.
