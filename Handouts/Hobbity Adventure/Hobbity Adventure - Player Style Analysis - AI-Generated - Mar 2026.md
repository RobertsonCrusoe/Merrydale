THE DM

Gus is running a tight ship disguised as a loose one. His pre-session email
was already evidence of this, but in play it's even clearer. He gives the
players enough rope to hang themselves, then lets the world respond
naturally. Grover isn't a villain or an ally -- he's a credible constable
dealing with credible problems, and Gus plays him with just enough warmth
(the cake at breakfast, the genuine concern about the troglodytes) to keep
the players second-guessing themselves. The Zacharias scene is beautifully
staged -- the paranoia about being overheard, the fishing charade, the slow
reveal down by the lakeshore. Gus knows when to let the players talk
themselves into a plan and when to nudge. He's also clearly enjoying the
comedy of your party's antics while making sure there are real
consequences. The constabulary is a timeout couch, not a dungeon -- but you
still got tied up and detained.


TURNIP -- THE PRINCIPLED NUISANCE

Turnip is the most hobbity hobbit at the table, and you play that
completely straight, which makes him both endearing and infuriating in
equal measure. You demand an apology from the constable who just released
you. You casually mention breaking into Zacharias's house while introducing
yourself. You offer to help carry his fishing gear to the mud room "we saw
last night when we were in the house." You have no guile whatsoever, and
the comedy comes from the fact that you genuinely don't understand why any
of this is a problem -- because by hobbit standards, it isn't. The failed
wisdom check where you blow past Zacharias's fishing code-talk is perfect
Turnip: high intelligence, low wisdom, says the quiet part loud. You're
also the one asking the most probing narrative questions -- why does he
think it's the priests specifically, where did the story about the inn
originate, who told whom -- but you ask them at exactly the wrong social
moments. You play Turnip like someone who's genuinely good but
constitutionally incapable of reading a room.


WEDGE -- THE FIELD MARSHAL

Wedge is clearly the operational leader and he knows it. He drives the
tactical planning, makes the decisive calls ("let's spill our guts to
Belba," "let's go tonight," "100% let's go"), and when the party dithers he
just moves things forward. He's the one who frames the key questions to
Zacharias -- who do you trust, what material support can you give us, what's
your honest read on Grover. He thinks in terms of strategy and leverage.
He's also the one most willing to play the social game, doing the
diplomatic heavy lifting with NPCs while the other two do their thing. His
weakness, which he half-acknowledges, is that his confidence sometimes
curdles into abrasiveness -- he's the one who's been antagonizing Grover
most directly, and his "I've made a horrible miscalculation" moment is
genuinely self-aware. He plays like someone who ran logistics or managed
people in a past life. He's also the one most likely to bulldoze past
something when he's ready to move on. Les plays Wedge like a competent NCO
who's slightly too sure of his own read on every situation.


BOFFO -- THE QUIET PROFESSIONAL

Boffo talks the least but when he speaks it lands. "Except for that guy
with the hammer" -- that's the kind of dry, deadpan observation that
recenters a conversation. He's the one who says Grover is on the up and up
while the other two are suspicious, and his reasoning is simple and sound:
the constable has done everything he said he would, he's just grumpy. When
Les reveals the ESP scroll, Todd immediately and correctly says save it for
the priests. He doesn't grandstand, doesn't try to drive the narrative,
just makes the right call when it counts. He also gets the best throwaway
comedy lines -- putting on half-moon reading glasses to look at the note
intently before handing it to someone who can actually read. Todd plays
like someone who's happy to let others take the spotlight but has the best
instincts at the table.
