Collector’s Preface

To the reader who holds this volume, I offer a confession: I am a man possessed. For the better part of twenty years, I have wandered the roads between Scardale and Shadowdale, through Dagger Falls and into the hill country beyond, collecting scraps of song and verse from tavern-keepers, soldiers, priests, and the occasional obliging bard. The result of that labour, such as it is, you hold in your hands.

I am Tammen Gale, of the Scardale Gales, a family once of some mercantile distinction, now of somewhat reduced circumstances. The occupation and its aftermath, which I shall not dwell upon, left my family with little but a name and a modest house on Chandler’s Row. What it left me, specifically, was an ear tuned to the songs of other people’s sorrows and triumphs, for we had few enough of our own to sing about. I trained as a bard in the old tradition — not at any great college, but under Aldus Greymere, a half-blind Oghmanite who could still pick out a false note at forty paces and who insisted that every song worth remembering was a piece of history with the dust shaken off. I tried, in my younger years, to be a performer rather than a collector. Cormyr cured me of that ambition, and I have been the better for it — or so I tell myself.

Old Aldus was right, of course. The songs collected here are not mere entertainments, though several of them entertain mightily. They are the living memory of Daggerdale and its people during a period of extraordinary trial: from the liberation of Dagger Falls in 1372 DR to the present struggle against the Netherese shadow. In these verses one hears the authentic voice of the Dalelands: defiant, grieving, comic, profane, and shot through with a stubborn insistence that courage matters and that the names of the brave deserve to be remembered.

The collection is arranged roughly by chronology, beginning with the great Harper ballads of the liberation era and proceeding to more recent compositions. The centrepiece of this volume, and its reason for being, is the Saga of Maturin the Grey, composed by no less a figure than Dorian of Sembia — the Count of Castle Daggerdale himself — whose manuscript was recovered at some peril from the library of the castle after his disappearance. That a warrior-count of such renown should also have been a poet of genuine feeling is one of the more remarkable facts of recent Dalelands history. His saga is presented here in full, exactly as found, with all its rough edges and idiosyncratic spellings preserved, for I hold that a collector who polishes his sources is no better than a forger.

The companion pieces to the Maturin saga — the stirring Ballad of Maturin and the Lion, Boregar the Wanderer’s rollicking account of the Battle of Dagger Falls, and Maturin Horgath’s own immortal drinking song — were gathered from Harper sources and from the oral tradition of the Dalelands, where they are still sung in taverns from Anathar’s Dell to the banks of the Ashaba.

The Ballad of the North, by the remarkable Oberon of Netheril, belongs to a parallel tradition: the songs of the Neverwinter expedition against the Zhentarim wizard Malkor. I have included it here because the Daggerdale and Neverwinter campaigns were twin prongs of a single great effort, and because the ballad is too fine a thing to leave out of any collection that aspires to completeness.

The later pieces present a different character. The compositions of Beliard Emmarask — diplomat, courtier, and bard of extraordinary and, I confess, enviable gifts — belong to the new era of Daggerdale, the age of Castle Radiance and the Free Defense League. His performance at the tavern of Quick Axes in Dagger Falls, which I was fortunate enough to witness some few years ago, remains one of the most remarkable displays of bardic craft I have encountered. His lay is a meditation on Daggerdale itself: its landscapes, its dangers, its stubborn beauty. The other Emmarask pieces were obtained through channels I am not at liberty to disclose, but I vouch for their authenticity.

Finally, by way of appendices, I have included several pieces from the Eastwitch country, a region far removed from the Dalelands in both geography and character, but whose traditions of dark balladry and frontier song deserve wider currency. Dunlogan’s Song, in particular, is a masterwork of frontier verse, and I should be remiss to exclude it from any anthology of this kind. The lighter songs of the young bard Bin Fletcher offer a welcome counterpoint, and a reminder that even on the edge of the Wild, there is room for laughter. A further appendix collects three sea shanties from the southern archipelagos, transcribed by a correspondent during the festival of Raidfest. These rough and bawdy sailors’ songs belong to a different world entirely, but they are too good to leave out.

I have added brief contextual notes before each piece, but I have not presumed to alter so much as a comma of the poems and songs themselves. These are the words of their authors, not mine, and they deserve to be met on their own terms.

If this little volume finds its way to the shelves of Candlekeep, or even to a few appreciative tavern-keepers who might be moved to have their house bards learn a verse or two, I shall consider my labours well repaid.

Tammen Gale Scardale Town, Marpenoth 1484 DR

I. The Saga of Maturin the Grey

by Dorian of Sembia, Count of Castle Daggerdale

Notes found in the library of Castle Daggerdale after the disappearance of Dorian of Sembia.

Of all the compositions to emerge from the liberation of Daggerdale, none is more ambitious or more personal than this long narrative poem by Dorian of Sembia — warrior, exile, and ultimately Count of Castle Daggerdale. Written in the voice of Maturin Horgath, the Paladin of Ilmater whose fall and redemption form the poem’s great arc, the saga recounts the full sweep of the campaign: the council at Waterdeep, the long march east, the battles with Grimwald’s hunters, the horrors of Anauroch, and the first terrible encounter with Grandfather Lion. The recurring refrain shifts between speakers — brother, father — suggesting that the saga was intended for performance in sections, perhaps at different occasions. It is presented here complete and unaltered.

Prologue

“Well, where have you been, Maturin, my son? What were your deeds? What have you done? Did you cross the seas of blue? Did you fight the battles true? Did you see the righteous through? Come now, Maturin, tell me of you!

Introduction

“Well, brother, I’ll tell you where I have been, I’ll tell you of heroes and horrors I’ve seen. This is the story of many a man, Of Nobles, and rascals, and a failed Zhent plan. This is a story of monsters and war. This is the story of my long journey far. A Council we held in the Jewel of the North, Heroes, three groups, set out and went forth. Ours was the longest, a trip to the Dales, Not destined to see the waves’ happy sails. For us was the desert, the great Anauroch, No pleasantly swaying ships on the dock. Eastward we strode, with our armour a’ gleaming, With courage and honour, our hearts were yet beaming There was Raven the Ranger, leading the way, (Not long it would be ‘ere the close of his day). He was a Raventree noble, with a hunger for fight, Yet doomed nonetheless, but not by Ravensblight. There was Boregar the Wanderer, accompanying too, The roads were unsafe - what else could he do? A fat man was he, but fattest his heart, A wounded companion for miles he’d cart. His staff was a whirlwind of parries and blows, Villains he vanquished in rows upon rows. Sparrow the mageling, priest of the Lady. He’d always a spell or a trick at the ready. But true was his quest, and truer his aim, He didn’t seek gold, nor glory nor fame. He ventured with us, pursuing his goal, With spells and his wits, he’d vanquish Yarkul. Also with us was a man who was bold, He could turn himself purple, he could turn into mould. Hanibal ‘the Wild’ was the name of this man, Of reason and safety, he was not the greatest fan. Nevertheless, he was one of our mages, Wild or not, he was one for the ages. Corrina the gnome, small and brokenhearted, She’d left her true love, and sadly departed. She managed the horses, and spoke to the beasts, And could turn our poor rations into sumptuous feasts. Of illusions she weaved, and knew more than most, Of Dragons, and demons, and Riders, and ghosts. So this was the troupe, that took up the quest. For hundreds of miles we would walk from the West. We would venture to mountain and desert and town, To take from the Zhents Randal’s lost crown. Remember their names, O Brother, my dear, For this is the story, of which you will hear.

Canto I

“Well, brother, I’ll tell you where I have been, I’ll tell you of heroes and horrors I’ve seen. This is the story of many a man, Of Nobles, and rascals, and a failed Zhent plan. This is a story of monsters and war. This is the story of my long journey far. We started our quest, with a price on our heads, The blood that was spilled turned the trail to red. Grimwald the Sheriff longed for our death; He would draw from our Ranger his life’s final breath. But we battled, and jousted, and fought tooth and nail, And in Secomber-town, his hunters would fail. The sheriff was brave, and quick with his gun, But his days as a villain were soon to be done. We vanquished his forces, brought him to his knees, We thwarted his effort to collect on his fees. Grimwald was judged, and brought to his end, But the loss of our Ranger, his death could not mend. For Raven had died, from a shot to his chest, I hope that in slumber, he’s found final rest. And Hanibal, my friend, vanished from sight, I know that he died, turning wrong into right. To be claimed by our foes, these were the first, But I thank Great Ilmater that they suffered no thirst. For the desert awaited, and soon we’d not drink, Its hotter, and harsher, than you’d ever think, Anauroch, O Anauroch, where I met my fate, It was there that He stalked and He hunted in wait... But Raven and Hanibal, great men that had died, For their souls and their families, I cried and I cried.

Canto II

So, Brother I’m telling you, of where I have been. I’m telling of heroes, and horrors I’ve seen. This is the story, of many a man, Nobles and rascals, and a failed Zhent plan. This is the story of monsters and war, This is the story of my journey so far. In Secomber-town, we bolstered our ranks, And cleaned off the blood on the river’s wide banks. Help we would need, and so help we sought, From a Chauntean Druid (or so we first thought). But, he was no Druid, we could see his plate mail, But rather a bandit, expelled from his Dale. Dorian the warrior, joined in our fight, His honour I hoped would soon come to light. But alone he was not, for he came with our Dalak, A Dwarf with an axe, who missed his dear Balak. Dalak was proud, with a look that was savage, He’d rant and he’d drink and soon he would ravage. O Dalak the Dwarf, the greatest of cobblers, Many a Zhent with his axe he would clobber. So these were our allies, and help for the quest, And so we departed again from the west. Dalak, and Sparrow and Dorian too, With Boregar, Corrina and myself we were true. Eastward we went, onwards our path; We’d show those foul villains the weight of our wrath! But trouble awaited, from foes we’d not seen, In the mountains we stumbled on Hill Giants mean. To defeat we came (almost), as spells started flying, And a Demon appeared, while the heroes fell dying! But our end was not there, for it was not written, Nay, to the desert we’d go, where we’d get bitten. O Anauroch, Anauroch, where I met my fate, ‘Twas there where He hunted and prowled in wait. So, Father pray, listen up and hear this brave tale, For soon we shall hear of Ilmater’s great wail.

Canto III

So, Brother I’m telling you, of where I have been. I’m telling of heroes, and horrors I’ve seen. This is the story, of many a man, Nobles and rascals, and a failed Zhent plan. This is the story of monsters and war, This is the story of my journey so far. The desert sands welcomed us with hatred and fear, But not yet was the time when I’d get this grey tear. But all our great endings must have a foul start, And soon was to be the first test of my heart. O, Anauroch, cursed Anauroch, damned be your sands, You gave us the plague that I spread with my hands. We went out of the heat to a canyon so right, But we heard a great roar that gave us a fright! Grandfather Lion, Hunter and King, I knew he was evil, so I took the first swing. The battle was bloody, the most horrible so far, His infectious claws; my soul they would mar! The day was a loss, and we were all broken, The beast, all he’d wanted, was a horse as a token. All of us were bloody and battered and dying. But all I could hear was the Gods’ mournful crying. Our task was not finished, rather only just begun, I’d meet him once more before our quest was done...

II. The Ballad of Maturin and the Lion

A Harper composition, sung in the Dalelands c. 1373 DR

Where the Saga tells Maturin’s story in his own voice, this companion ballad recounts it in the third person, in the formal style of the Harper tradition. It is the more widely known of the two compositions, sung from Shadowdale to Berdusk, and its escalating refrain — from “I will endure” to “He will endure” to the thunderous communal “We will endure” — never fails to bring an audience to its feet. The six cantos trace the full arc: the ride from Waterdeep, the lycanthropic horror in Anauroch, Shildevan’s prophecy, the liberation of Dagger Falls, the desert campaign of the Eastern Storm, and the climactic single combat between Maturin and Grandfather Lion.

I

Maturin was a Paladin who rode out of the East An exile from Damara to secure his nation’s peace A soldier of Ilmater and a healer of the poor, He made his way to Waterdeep, on Faerûn’s western shore. ‘Gainst slavery and suffering, Ilmater’s son was called, With five brave friends from Waterdeep, to ride for Dagger Falls. But a villain’s bounty on their heads brought Grimwald and his men, ‘Til a bitter night in Secomber, where the Sheriff met his end. House Raventree there lost a son, and Hanibal was gone, But with Dorian and Dalak now, the party soldiered on. The road they took grew grimmer still, through Loudwater and Llorkh, Towards destiny and danger, towards wailing Anauroch. I will endure. I will endure. Maturin and the Lion.

II

The desert burned away their flesh, their horses died of thirst, They were poisoned by the Zhentarim, they were hunted, they were cursed. The Grandfather of Lions came upon them in the dunes, And dread grew on them daily, with the waxing of the moon. The full moon rose, all Hell broke loose, and in the dying light From heroes in the desert’s sun, came lions in the night. The Green-Eyed God grinned on the scene of murder, strife and gore As hapless slaves were hunted down, as Zhents were rent and torn. Maturin woke beneath the sun; his wounds had all been healed. A night beyond remembrance, the corpse-strewn ground revealed. His noble steed fled from his touch into the barren sands. Ilmater had forsaken him; he knew then that he was damned. I will endure. I will endure. Maturin and the Lion.

III

In that bloody night of carnage, two more friends had disappeared, Three men and a raging dwarf pressed forward, through their fears ‘Til mad and roaming Shildevan, a prophet and a saint, Came to them at the next full moon, to cleanse the lion’s taint. ‘You will hearken to your duty, you will go into the East, But when Dagger Falls is free then you must answer to my geis. Boregar, you’ll set to rights the wrongs you’ve left behind. Dorian, you’ll a pauper be, ‘til you’ve foiled Beholder-kind. Maturin, Ilmater weeps to see your low disgrace, But all may be redeemed and if you would once more take your place, Return unto the desert, for alone to face your foe In single combat slay the Lion, and thereby save your soul.” I will endure. I will endure. Maturin and the Lion.

IV

Two months in the desert, then another two of war But none of these brave heroes forgot what they were fighting for. The mines were closed, the slaves were freed, and Yarkul put to death. But yet there lurked in Dagger Falls an unseen, silent threat. Maturin’s dreams were clouded then, the God of Lies appeared, “Go into the desert, now, your fate awaits you there.” His prayers went still unanswered, and he was plagued anew with doubt, But Daggerdale could not be free ‘til the evil was cast out. The walking dead and demon-knights and all things dark and weird, Came boiling from dark temple crypts, but the Heroes persevered. Victory at Harvestide was the conclusion of their quest. But a desert wind rose up that night, and Maturin could not rest. I will endure. I will endure. Maturin and the Lion.

V

They rode into the desert then, five brothers of the sword: Boregar and Garrik, Count Dorian the Lord, Gaebril the Crusader and Maturin the Grey No foe could stand before them; no blade could block their way. That Eastern Storm whipped through the sands, a purifying gale. It swept through Zhentish citadels, it cleansed the desert trails. The disciples of the God of Lies were slaughtered to a man, But their quarry still eluded them, the Tyrant’s caravan. At length they reached the ruined place where they had fought and killed, Midwinter was upon them then and they sheltered from the chill. Maturin stared into the dunes, hand clenched upon his sword, And somewhere in the desert night, they heard a lion roar... I will endure. I will endure. Maturin and the Lion.

VI

The Lion came into the keep, the winter stormed without Maturin stood before it and he gave a mighty shout “I’ve sworn by all that’s holy to end your foul plague. Forsake your Green-Eyed Master now and answer to my rage.” The Lion roared, its claws lashed out, its fierce teeth opened wide Maturin raged with passion, and he fought with all his might And in that clash of silver, muscle, steel, tooth, and blood, A man warred with a lion; god made war with god. Abruptly then, as silence fell, both bodies fell to ground. Maturin’s friends rushed to his side, and on seeing him, they found A small grey tear beneath one eye, and a smile ‘pon his face, He whispered out a prayer then, and I swear they heard him say... I will endure. I will endure. Maturin and the Lion. He will endure. He will endure. Maturin and the Lion. We will endure. We will endure!! Maturin and the Lion.

III. The Ballad of Dagger Falls

by Boregar the Wanderer

One of many Harper ballads about the Liberation of Dagger Falls, c. 1372 DR

Boregar the Wanderer — Harper agent, lycanthrope survivor, and wielder of what may be the most feared quarterstaff in the Dalelands — composed this rousing piece in a style deliberately modelled on an old marching song. Its irreverent tone and infectious chorus made it an immediate favourite among the soldiers and freemen of the liberated Dale. The reference to “guns” is a Mistledale colloquialism for crossbows.

“In 1372 we took a little trip, Across the desert Anauroch, to take away the whips, We had the hero Dorian, and Maturin the Grey, With Sparrow and a Dwarven Dread The Fat Man led the way “We lost our guns but the Zhents they kept a’comin’ There wasn’t quite as many as there was a while ago With one heroic strike, we sent the Zhents a’runnin’ Across the mighty desert to the nine hells far below. “Ol’ Sparrow said we could take them by surprise If he killed their mighty wizard while the sleep was in his eyes The Harpers helped to destroy the snakes with wings And we cleared the Town of Dagger Falls of all the evil things. “We lost our guns but the Zhents they kept a’comin’ There wasn’t quite as many as there was a while ago With one heroic strike, we sent the Zhents a’runnin’ Across the mighty desert to the nine hells far below. “We freed the slaves and returned them to their towns But the slabs were off for Anauroch b’fore the snows came down Shar had sent a paladin, with Garrik it made five To free the sands of Anauroch and leave no Zhents alive “We lost our guns but the Zhents they kept a’comin’ There wasn’t quite as many as there was a while ago With one heroic strike, we sent the Zhents a’runnin’ Across the mighty desert to the nine hells far below. “O we fought through the guards, We fought through the gen’rals, We fought all the leaders of each mighty Zhent outpost We fought so hard that the Zhents’ll not forget us The Five brave fighters who came on them in the snow.”

IV. The Ballad of the North

by Oberon of Netheril

A composition on the travails and fortunes of the heroes who thwarted the Zhentarim wizard Malkor, spring 1372 DR

Oberon of Netheril is one of the most extraordinary figures in recent bardic history, a man (if man he truly be) some five thousand years old, rescued from magical stasis in an ancient Netherese dungeon. His ballad of the Neverwinter expedition against the Zhentarim wizard Malkor Githeas is sung to a melody of great beauty and melancholy, and its chorus is among the most recognizable in the North. The piece was composed in Neverwinter in the aftermath of the battle, and is still performed at the annual commemoration beneath the great statue on the Southern High Road Gate.

Chorus

Ah, for just one time, I would ride for Neverwinter To smite the hand of Luskan, reaching from the Trackless Sea; Tracing one warm line through Frontier both wild and bitter As Neverwinter’s Heroes did for thee.

Verses

I have a tale to tell you, unmatched in all Faerûn A tale of woe and glory, a tender tragic tune. You who would be heroes, who would set aright the wrong Listen to my words and hear my song. Northward from Roark’s Tavern ‘tis there ‘twas said to lie, The road to Neverwinter for which the Harpers died: The Road Mage and Bloodshoulder, fairest Laurel Dawn. The Zhents’ deceit flows thick and black and on. Beljuril and Rufus, the last of that great band, Set out with Mae and Oliver, and Fazil the Grand. Sheriff Grimwald’s men came after, tracked us through hill and vale, ‘Till Selûne brought down thunder on their tails. Then through the night, at Leilon’s gates, through wizard’s base deceit We ran afoul of folly, as orcs rained down like sleet. Locked inside a prison, facing oil and smoke and fire Atemnon planned a ghastly fun’ral pyre. Beshaba’s breath was on us, as we came out of that place. But likewise had the Sheriff’s men felt Maevri’s lethal taste The city fell behind us, as the Red Shields led the way But darkness was to rise against the day. Insidious enchantments corrupt the best of men And Shar’s brood came before us, rising upwards from the plain. In defiance and in glory, Selûne’s blade in his hand, Rufus led the charge, and we scattered them like sand. At Neverwinter Harbour, hidden safe from sight, We found the nest of Malkor and made ready for to fight. But not illusions, songs, or arrows, nor prayers, art, or faith Could stop that madman’s rain of fire and death. To sea we put to hound the fiend, who’d cut so many down, We swore that Rufus’ killer had seen the last of solid ground. Forsaken then by Luskan, his ship was set ablaze, An arrow’s lonely flight cut short his days. The world of men is fleeing; I’ve seen thousands turn to dust. But legends live forever, and our songs recall the just. Endings are beginnings, and Neverwinter’s free Now sing of heroes, death and hope with me.

[all sing together]

Ah, for just one time, I would ride for Neverwinter To smite the hand of Luskan reaching from the Trackless Sea. Tracing one warm line through Frontier both wild and bitter As Neverwinter’s heroes did for me.

V. Oberon’s Lament for Old Netheril

by Oberon of Netheril

This short, fierce poem accompanies the Ballad of the North as a second specimen of Oberon’s art, and a markedly different one. Where the Ballad is narrative and communal, the Lament is private and philosophical: a meditation on the difference between minor and major poetry, and an implicit claim for the tradition to which Oberon himself belongs. Coming from a man who watched the fall of Netheril and slept through five thousand years of history, the final image of mad blind hands reaching into the night to drag forth “mad, monstrous nightmare shapes” carries a weight that no younger poet could give it.

The little poets sing of little things: Hope, cheer, and faith, small queens and puppet kings; Lovers who kissed and then were made as one, And modest flowers waving in the sun. The mighty poets write in blood and tears And agony that, flame-like, bites and sears. They reach their mad blind hands into the night, To plumb abysses dead to human sight; To drag from gulfs where lunacy lies curled, Mad, monstrous nightmare shapes to blast the world.

VI. Dalak’s Homemade Beer

A drinking song composed by Maturin Horgath during the long night of his fall from grace

That a paladin of Ilmater should compose a drinking song is remarkable enough; that the song should be this good is little short of miraculous. Maturin wrote it during the dark period of his lycanthropic curse, when his prayers went unanswered and his god seemed to have turned His face away. The song commemorates the dwarven warrior Dalak Quickaxe’s legendary homebrew, a concoction of such ferocity that even Boregar the Wanderer, a man of heroic appetites, could not keep it down. It is best sung loudly, with a full tankard, and with diminishing accuracy as the verses progress.

First Verse

O, the year was 1372 (I wish I’d never tried it now) And Anauroch’s sun might’ve been the shade To the scummiest brew that’s ever been made...

Chorus

Tyr damn us all! I was told This ale was worth its weight in gold. We’d feel no pain, shed no tears But it’s a foolish man who shows no fear At a glass of Dalak’s homemade beer.

Verses

Dalak Quickaxe brought forth the jug (I wish I’d never tried it now) For three brave men, all warriors who Would taste for him the gut shakin’ brew.

[chorus]

Dorian was the first to drink (I wish I’d never tried it now) The brew brought forth such chokes and gags, Like a lion had ripped his throat to rags.

[chorus]

Boregar next, belly hard and vast (I wish I’d never tried it now) He was seven foot tall, and eight foot wide He growled “Pass that jug or I’ll tan your hide!”

[chorus]

He swallowed down then spewed it up (I wish I’d never tried it now) Dalak was smashed with a gutful of dregs And his breath set fire to both me legs.

[chorus]

A Paladin drunk is a Paladin not (I wish I’d never tried it now) But Ilmater’s away, and I’m pretty sure There ain’t a hangover I can’t endure.

[chorus]

So here I lie with me twenty-third beer (I wish I’d never tried it now) The Gutshaker’s great, but soon it’ll be Grandfather, Ilmater, the desert and me.

[chorus]

VII. Three Ravens

From an old Mistledale poem

This grim little verse circulates in several forms throughout the Dalelands. The version presented here is, I am told, a particular favourite of Beliard Emmarask — which surprised me when I learned of it. It is a far cry from the comic odes and patriotic lays for which he is known, and it suggests a depth of feeling for the older poetic traditions of this region that one would not expect from a Cormyrean. Its theme — the faithlessness of the living and the indifference of nature to the fallen warrior — is characteristic of a strain of Dalelands poetry far older than the Harper tradition. Perhaps Emmarask understands the Dales better than I give him credit for. He would not be the first outsider to do so.

Raven doth to ravens fly, Raven doth to ravens cry: Brethren, where is carrion tender? What shall be the day’s provender? Ravens answer raven thus: Aye, we know of meat for us; On the fallow, by the willow Lies a knight, a clod his pillow. Why he died, who dealt the blow, That his hawk alone can know, And the sable mare who bore him. And his bride who rode before him. But the hawk now sails the air, And the foe bestrode the mare, And the bride a wreath is wreathing For a new love, warm and breathing.

VIII. Beliard’s Lay at Quick Axes

Performed by Beliard Emmarask at the tavern of Quick Axes, Dagger Falls

8 Flamerule, 1480 DR

I first heard Beliard Emmarask sing some twenty years before this performance, in a music hall in Suzail. I had gone to the capital as young Dalesman bards sometimes do, convinced that talent and persistence would open doors that birth and connections had not. They did not. I spent three years in Suzail, singing in taverns that emptied before I finished, entering competitions I did not win, and learning, with a slowness that shames me in retrospect, that I was not what Cormyr wanted. Emmarask was. He was young then, callow and sharp-tongued, the sort of performer who wandered into a room and owned it without appearing to try. He sang a love ballad that evening, something slight and fashionable, and he sang it with a carelessness that was worse than arrogance, because it was genuine: the boy simply did not know how good he was. I remember thinking, with a bitterness I am not proud of, that the gods distribute talent without the slightest regard for who will use it well. I went home to the Dales shortly after, and began collecting other men’s songs instead of writing my own.

Twenty years later, I found myself in the great taproom of Quick Axes in Dagger Falls on a night when the Cormyrean caravans had come in and the hall was packed to the rafters. Emmarask — no longer young, no longer callow, but unmistakable — had talked his way into the evening’s entertainment, displacing, I later learned, the hired minstrel who had failed to appear. After a comic ode to the caravan-master Denelius Ormath that had the room in stitches, he mounted the table and began to sing.

The room fell silent — three hundred souls, traders and soldiers and dwarves and carters, all of them holding their breath. His voice was low and rich and full of a sorrow that had nothing theatrical about it. The song was Daggerdale itself: its roads and rivers, its dangers and beauties, the weight of its history and the stubbornness of its people. When he finished, a crash from the kitchen broke the spell, and three elves walked in from the night, and the evening took a different turn entirely. But the song stayed with me. It has stayed with me ever since, and I think it always will. If I cannot sing like Beliard Emmarask, I can at least ensure that his songs survive him.

IX. Silver Talons

by Beliard Emmarask

Composed on the evening of 8 Flamerule, 1480 DR

This short, enigmatic poem reached me through the same channels as Emmarask’s other later compositions. I am told it was written on the same evening as his performance at Quick Axes, though its character could not be more different. Unlike the public lay, which is a meditation on Daggerdale’s landscape, this piece is darker and more allusive: a private work never intended for the tavern stage. Its references to “silver talons” and ravens freed from a cage suggest a political subtext that I confess I have not been able to fully decode, though the final image — of something caged now flying free over the “wooded dales” and the “sandless sea” — is unmistakably hopeful. It is the work of a man who knows more than he is saying, which is, as I have come to understand, Emmarask’s natural condition.

A dagger falls in Dagger Falls A stranger’s blood to spill — A death unstaid by haggard calls To conscience good or ill. No stone unturned, no task undone, No hand be left to chance No night god’s knight nor sun god’s son Can stay Rauthauvyr’s lance. The ravens from their cage fly free, Their silver talons bright The wooded dales, the sandless sea, Are theirs once more tonight.

X. Ode to Denelius Ormath

by Beliard Emmarask

Performed at Quick Axes, Dagger Falls, 8 Flamerule, 1480 DR

Strictly speaking, this comic piece has already been mentioned in context as part of Beliard’s performance at Quick Axes, but it deserves separate recognition as a specimen of the bard’s gift for flattery so extravagant as to become its own form of satire. Denelius Ormath is a caravan-master of some local reputation, a man whose leadership of the Cormyrean trading convoy to Dagger Falls had apparently been an ordeal that few of his charges would care to repeat. Beliard’s ode transformed this unpromising material into a triumph of audience manipulation, goading Ormath into buying ale for the entire taproom. The piece is given here in its verse form only; the prose patter that surrounded it was improvised and is lost.

Who thunders ‘cross the Thunder Peaks? Why, Denelius Ormath! Who knows what every merchant seeks? Why, Denelius Ormath! Will he be stayed by bandit swarms? Not Denelius Ormath! By orc marauders? Or fire or storms? Not Denelius Ormath! Who carts the cargo and lugs the loads? It’s Denelius Ormath! He’s the iron captain of the roads, is Great Denelius Ormath!

Appendix I: Songs of Eastwitch

The pieces that follow belong to a tradition far removed from the Dalelands, both in geography and in character. The Eastwitch country is a frontier settlement on the edge of the Wild, a place where civilization clings by its fingernails to a hostile landscape, and where the old darkness of the forest presses in on every side. Its poetry reflects this duality: where the Daggerdale tradition celebrates heroism and resistance, the Eastwitch songs range from the haunted and elegiac to the raucously comic, and are concerned alike with the terrible intimacy of violence in small communities and with the defiant good cheer of those who live in its shadow. I include them here because they are fine poems that deserve to be more widely known, and because the collector’s art, like the bard’s, sometimes requires a change of key.

XI. Dunlogan’s Song

Part I: Lyrics by Benjamin Wrightson, 1684

Part II: Verses added in 1690, author unknown

Dunlogan’s Song is a masterwork of frontier balladry, and I do not use the word lightly. Benjamin Wrightson’s original composition tells the story of Ridge Dunlogan, a farmer on the Heights whose death at the jaws of a wolf pack left his five young daughters orphaned, and of the monstrous Zebulon Kavenaugh, who married the eldest girl for her land and met a justice more terrible than any court could deliver. The anonymous second part, added six years later, shifts from narrative to elegy, and its closing image of Ruth Ann Dunlogan’s ghost humming to her father’s pipes is among the most haunting passages in the literature of the frontier.

Part I

In the autumn bleak of ‘sixty-four At the farm up on the Heights, Good Ridge Dunlogan hung pipes that play For miles on wind-swept nights. In the slightest breeze, the pipes would moan Through the orchards fair and long; That ghostly air still sounds today, And sings Dunlogan’s Song. The few who’ve heard would fain forget That weird and woeful wail, And if you’d learn their lone lament, Turn your ears unto this tale. Good Ridge Dunlogan, of Eastwitch born Was a stoic soul indeed, With an orchard large and sheep to tend, And five small mouths to feed. His days were long but he seldom frowned When labour bent his back, His toiling ceased one autumn night, When he fell to a ravening pack. The baleful beasts bayed, ferociously foaming, Their wicked eyes windows to Hell. As Dunlogan fought, far away in the gloaming, His pipes played their master’s death knell. Thus Ruth Ann Dunlogan, young, winsome and wan Was orphaned by wolves at fifteen, Charged with four sisters, a flock and a farm, In a year when the harvest was lean. A matriarch maiden, Ruth saw to her sisters, But the unwanted charge wore her raw. When she turned to her kinfolk, as the fox to the coop, Came the wretch Zebulon Kavenaugh. At Zebulon’s birth, his mother’d expired And his father, struck senseless by loss Hideously hanged himself high by the neck As his babe bayed beneath Canaan’s Cross. Thus orphaned, unwanted and scorned by the world The boy cringed and crept through his life. He grew up as cruel as a midwinter storm He grew up as sharp as a knife. Fortune recoiled from that evil-eyed son, No gesture came gracious or kind, Naught came from friends, so friends came to naught In Zebulon Kavenaugh’s mind. Zebulon courted his young cousin Ruth And he hungered for Dunlogan’s land He flatter’d with falsehoods, ‘till, in spite of herself Ruth reluctantly gave him her hand. Lonely and lean was the small ceremony, For the Heights are a solid day’s ride, Kavenaugh’s kinfolk cared naught for the nuptials; Ruth’s sisters played maids for the bride. Five wedding bells chimed as the five sisters wept Five mockingbirds sang from their perch And high on the barn, old Dunlogan’s pipes Conducted the desolate dirge. His concern so settled, the registrar rode out As soon as the couple was wed, Then Zebulon Kavenaugh, with a leer in his eye Pulled Dunlogan’s daughter to bed. Refusing her husband, Ruth hied from the house, To take to the trees in her flight But Zebulon Kavenaugh ran her to ground, That violent conjugal night. The sinister spouse’s degenerate thirst Was slaked on his newlywed bride, But his lot had been cast ‘neath Saint Canaan’s Cross And Fortune was not on his side. The deed duly done, ‘neath a mist-covered moon, The pipes pining low in the air. That unearthly music was joined in lament As Ruth Ann wept cold bitter tears. But at the wan woman’s wailing, the gale grew fierce, The pipes sounded shrill through the Wild, And from the dark drew a drove of dastardly orcs, To doom the cruel groom and the child. Zebulon cowered, Ruth screamed in fear, The stench of the beasts choked their chests, But a westerly wind met the gathering storm, A grim wind of steel and of death. A Warden’s black blades begged no pardon that day, Not from flesh, nor from bone, nor from blood. The screams of Or’uk joined in Dunlogan’s Song, As it wailed on the winds through the wood. The Warden’s dread dance did deliberately slow, As his gaze caught Zebulon’s eye. With a face quite as cold as a corpse in the snow He strode to the sad scoundrel’s side. Frantically the fiends fled, fully afraid As the stalwart stood guard o’er the knave, But he strangely stayed silent as they bore off the bride To a fate in their festering cave. The rapist was rescued; the lass led away! Surely a more terrible theme, Cannot been found in the foulest of fables, Yet such was the grim Warden’s scheme. Zebulon Kavenaugh looked up in dread, To look on the conquering Shade, And he flinched at the eyes that flamed fierce in the foxlight, As the forester made known his fate. “You’ve murdered that maiden,” the Warden spoke softly, “You’ll bear the full weight of your wrong, And I’ll punish you best by permitting you breath So long as the winds play this song. “For your woman’s been won by those wretched Or’Uk She’ll be worked as a slave and to breed. Such suffering she’ll know to the edge of her grave For your sin of ungovernable greed. “Hark to the music that haunts the Heights here And weep when it whispers its song And if ever you tamper with Dunlogan’s pipes You’ll die seven deaths before dawn.” Thus Zebulon Kavenaugh’s sole gift in life – The only he ever received, Was the gaunt Warden’s sentence of sorrowful song On the night he was wed, and bereaved. Cravenly Kavenaugh fled through the night His features grown haggard and drawn. And from that day forward, he hid on the Heights And hearkened to Dunlogan’s Song.

Part II

Verses added in 1690, author unknown

The pipes on the barn up at Dunlogan’s Farm Still sound their sad song in the south, Though Kavenaugh’s form is now food for the worm, And the tale turned to rumour from truth. Though wise folk will race now away from that place Where Dunlogan’s pipes gently play, His girl Ruth, it is sworn, still walks in some form ‘Round the farm of her childhood days. In ethereal guise her spectre now flies, Through the trees by the house on the Heights, And once in a while, her ghostly face smiles As she hums to her father’s sad pipes. She surveys the lands that Dunlogan’s hands Once laboured to nurture and grow, And there’s a tear in her eye, at the memory why, It was lost to them long years ago. The dead will know best why they never can rest When grief and sorrow run strong. And for Ruth Ann Dunlogan, the sorrow unspoken Sings softly in Dunlogan’s Song.

Fin.

XII. Eugenia’s Lament

Sometimes known as Woman’s Scorn

A folk poem of the Eastwitch country

This spare, bitter lyric tells of a woman courted and abandoned by a Warden of Eastwitch, one of the grim forest rangers who patrol the frontier between civilization and the Wild. The poem’s power lies in its restraint: the speaker’s grief is conveyed almost entirely through understatement and irony, and the shift to third person in the final stanza, where “her lips grew pale and wan,” has the quality of a death observed from a great distance. The identity of Eugenia, if she existed, is unknown.

A Warden courted me Nine months and longer He fairly won my heart His heart was stronger With his axe-blade in his hand He looked quite clever And if I was with my love I’d live forever. But where is my love gone With his eyes like weather And his strange brown token on Decked out in wolf’s leather I’m afraid the darkling Wild Has robbed me of his beauty And if I was with my love I’d do my duty. Strange news is come to town Strange news is carried Strange news flies up and down That my love is buried. I wish his brethren joy Though they can’t hear me And may the Wild reward them well For the slighting of me. They don’t remember when He lay beside me And he said he’d marry me And not deny me. No witness have I none Save the Wild murky And may It reward them all For the slighting of me. Her lips grew pale and wan It made a poor heart wonder To see the Wildwood yon Twist her love asunder.

XIII. The New Wardens of Eastwitch

by Bin Fletcher

A favourite at the Green Grizzly

Bin Fletcher is the youngest composer represented in this collection, and perhaps the most improbable. A boy performer of the Eastwitch country, naïve and irrepressibly cheerful, Bin possesses a natural bardic gift that manifests less as polished craft than as an infectious, guileless enthusiasm. This rollcall verse celebrates his companions among the Wardens of Eastwitch, the grim frontier rangers who patrol the boundary between civilization and the Wild. Each Warden receives a couplet suited to his character, and the closing lines have the quality of a toast or a battle-cry. It is a favourite at the Green Grizzly tavern, where Bin is something of a local celebrity.

Brave Bin Fletcher, Warden of the Dale With a jaunty bit of playin’, I’ll spin a Warden tale. Turik Grimwood, Warden of the Thicket He’s always usin’ Warden ways to aggravate the wicked. Gen’ral Clem MacDiarmid, Warden of the Field He’ll strategize a thousand ways to bring his foes to heel. Ol’ Doc Miller, Warden of the Hamlet He’s sewin’ up the Wardens’ wounds as good as near as dammit. Wyatt and his buddy Zieg, Warden Wonder Twins, Wyatt cuts the mustard, and Zieg ain’t what he seems. Dargar Fiddich, Warden of the Rocks. If you’re needin’ any wardin’, Dargar’s got the chops. It’s ill luck to our enemies, and Fortune to our friends, When the goodly folk of Eastwitch call the Wardens for defense!

XIV. The Tale of the Fletcher Expedition

by Bin Fletcher

Performed at the Green Grizzly, Eastwitch

This longer performance piece was sung by Bin Fletcher at the Green Grizzly tavern on the evening that he and his companions — Dargar Fiddich, Wyatt Fletcher, and Zieg — returned from their expedition into the Wild, where they had discovered the fate of an earlier Fletcher expedition and fought a running battle with orcs. The song was preceded by Bin’s characteristic call to drink, which I include here as it is integral to the performance. The verses that follow recount the battle itself, with Bin’s customary generosity toward his companions and rather less modesty about his own contributions than the facts may warrant. The aside about Zieg and his brew refers to an incident earlier in the evening that had already become tavern legend by the time the song was finished.

So raise your tankard, raise your cup, raise your mug to young Bin ‘Cause I want to see the bottom of them before I even begin Now raise your cups again because you know I really don’t believe That those drinks are really empty now raise your cups to Zieg! Appendix II: Songs of Freeport

The three shanties that follow were not collected by me at all, but were transcribed by a correspondent — a priestess of Selûne, no less — who had occasion to witness a remarkable evening of song on a beach somewhere in the southern archipelagos, during the festival known as Raidfest. These are sailors’ songs, rough and bawdy and magnificent in their way, and they belong to a tradition as old as the sea itself. I include them because a collection of songs that confines itself to landlocked heroism is only half the story, and because the Gaffer’s whisky shanty, Zechary Mudge’s cautionary ballad of the Waterdeep girls, and the enigmatic Salazar’s hanging song are too good to leave mouldering in a priestess’s correspondence.

XV. The Gaffer’s Tune

A whisky shanty, sung at Raidfest

The Gaffer is a figure of some notoriety in the southern seas, a man whose singing voice is, by all accounts, as terrible as his looks, but whose enthusiasm for whisky and for song is beyond question. This shanty, a call-and-response drinking piece of the classic form, was sung around a beach fire while the meat was grilling. It requires no further commentary.

Whisky is the life of man, Whisky Johnnie. Oh! Whisky is the life of many, Whisky for my Johnnie. I’ll drink whisky when I can, Whisky Johnnie. I’ll drink it out of an old tin can, Whisky for my Johnnie. Whisky gave me a broken nose, Whisky Johnnie. Whisky made me pawn my clothes, Whisky for my Johnnie. Whisky drove me around the jungles of Chult, Whisky Johnnie. I was gone many a month, but bonnie twasn’t me fault, Whisky for my Johnnie. I thought I heard the old man say, Whisky Johnnie. ‘I’ll treat my crew in a decent way,' Whisky for my Johnnie. A glass of grog for every man, Whisky Johnnie. And a bottleful for the chanteyman, Whisky for my Johnnie.

XVI. Zechary’s Song

Sometimes known as The Waterdeep Girls

Sung by Zechary Mudge at Raidfest

Zechary Mudge is a scoundrel of the seafaring kind, and this bawdy ballad of a sailor’s misadventure in Waterdeep is apparently a favourite of his. The street names and taverns are authentic — Sail Street, Belnimbra’s Street, the Dripping Dagger — which lends the piece a documentary quality beneath its comic surface. The ‘Dhavian girls’ of the final verse are the women of the City of Splendors, though the song has clearly been adapted to suit whichever port the singer finds himself in.

As I walked out on Sail Street, a fair maid I did meet Who asked me please to see her home she lived on Belnimbra’s Street And away, you Johnny, my dear honey Oh you Waterdeep girls, you love us for our money I said, “My dear young lady, I’m a stranger here in town I left my ship just yesterday, from Candlekeep I was bound.” I took her out to Tiffany’s, I spared her no fib I bought her two gold earrings, they cost me five nibs. She said, “Come with me, dearie, I’ll stand you to a treat I’ll buy you rum and brandy, dear, and tab-nabs for to eat.” And when we reached the Dripping Dagger, boys, the drinks was handed round That liquor was so awful strong, my head went round and round. When the drinking it was over, we straight to bed did go And little did I ever think she’d prove my overthrow When I came to next morning, I had an aching head And there was I, Jack-all-alone, stark naked on the bed I looked all around the room, but nothing could I see But a lady’s shift and apron which now belonged to me Everything was silent, the hour was eight o’clock I put my shift and apron on and headed for the dock My shipmates seein’ me come aboard, these words to me did say “well well, old chap, you’ve lost your cap since last you went away.” “Is this the new spring fashion the ladies wear ashore? Where is the shop that sells it? Have they got any more?” The Old Man cried, “why Jack, my boy, I’m sure I could have sold A better suit than that, by far, to buy for eighty gold.” So come all you bully sailormen, take warning when ashore Or else you’ll meet some charming girl who’s nothing but a whore Your hard-earned cash will disappear, your rig and boots as well For ‘Dhavian girls are tougher than the other side of Hell. XVII. Salazar’s Shanty

Sometimes known as Hanging Johnnie

Sung by Salazar at Raidfest

The last song of the evening belonged to Salazar himself, a man apparently unaccustomed to adulation of any shape, whose performance of this grim little work-song was so well received that the rest of the whisky was emptied after it was done. The song is a hauling shanty, used to time the pulling of ropes, and its cheerful litany of people the singer would like to hang gives it a quality somewhere between gallows humour and genuine menace. Who Salazar is, and what he hangs for, my correspondent does not say.

They call me Hanging Johnnie, Horray, Hooray! They call me Hanging Johnnie, Hang, boys, hang. They say I hang for money, Horray, Hooray! But saying so is funny; Hang, boys, hang. I’d hang the highway robber, Horray, Hooray! I’d hang the burglar jobber; Hang, boys, hang. I’d hang a noted liar, Horray, Hooray! I’d hang a bloated friar; Hang, boys, hang. Come hang, come haul together, Horray, Hooray! Come hang for finer weather, Hang, boys, hang. I’d hang a brutal mother, Horray, Hooray! I’d hang her and no other; Hang, boys, hang. I’d hang to make things jolly, Horray, Hooray! I’d hang all wrong and folly; Hang, boys, hang. They call me Hanging Johnnie. Horray, Hooray! They call me Hanging Johnnie, Hang, boys, hang.
